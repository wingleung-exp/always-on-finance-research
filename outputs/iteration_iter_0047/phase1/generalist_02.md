# Inflation Regimes — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The current inflation episode is a compound analogue matching elements of five distinct historical episodes, with no single analogue achieving similarity above 7.0/10 — making single-analogue narratives systematically misleading.** The five relevant episodes are: 1966-69 (first wave/premature pivot), 1946-48 (post-supply-shock demand re-acceleration), 1994-95 (OER-masking soft landing), 2004-07 (expectations drift under measured tightening), and 1951-53 (post-Korean War supply normalization with fiscal expansion). The KB's existing emphasis on 1966-69 is directionally correct but insufficiently hedged.

2. **The base rate for "last mile" disinflation succeeding without recession is 1/7 across all post-1945 episodes where core inflation stalled in the 2.5-4.0% range with unemployment below 4.5%, and the single success (1994-95) required conditions unlikely to be replicated — namely a concurrent productivity acceleration that was already measurable in real time.** This is more pessimistic than the KB's existing 0/5 finding for services specifically, because it includes the 1994-95 episode as a partial success and still produces a near-zero base rate for the current configuration.

3. **The "mission accomplished" trap has a historical activation rate of 4/6 episodes during the goods-disinflation-to-services-persistence transition, but the *sequencing* of the trap matters more than its occurrence: in episodes where shelter deceleration led the headline decline (1983, 1995, and now), policy pivoted prematurely within 2-4 quarters of headline reaching the "comfort zone," and in 2/3 cases this required a subsequent correction.** The 1995 exception succeeded because productivity growth was already running at 2.5%+ — a condition absent today.

4. **The 1946-48 analogue is underweighted in the KB and is the most relevant for the *goods re-inflation* channel.** Post-WWII supply normalization produced rapid goods disinflation (1946 price controls lifted → spike → normalization by mid-1947), followed by demand-driven goods re-acceleration in 1947-48 as fiscal transfers and pent-up demand reasserted. The current tariff/deglobalization vector creates an analogous goods re-inflation pathway that compounds the services persistence problem rather than offsetting it.

5. **Cross-analogue duration analysis yields an expected persistence of 18-36 months for the "stall zone" (core PCE 2.5-3.5%) from the point of initial goods disinflation completion, with a fat right tail extending to 48+ months in the 1966-69 and 2004-07 cases.** Markets are pricing the left tail of this distribution (resolution within 12 months).

6. **The policy error taxonomy across analogues reveals two distinct failure modes: Type I (premature easing into an inflation stall, base rate 3/5 when fiscal is expansionary) and Type II (insufficient tightening masked by lagging indicators, base rate 2/5). The current configuration is uniquely exposed to both simultaneously** — Type I via the Fed's revealed reaction asymmetry and AI optimism easing trap, and Type II via shelter OER lag and covenant-lite transmission delay creating false signals of progress.

7. **A previously unidentified pattern: in 4/5 post-war episodes where services inflation persisted above 3.5% while goods disinflation was completing, the *credit cycle* extended rather than contracted for 12-18 months, because falling headline inflation improved financial conditions even as the underlying inflation regime remained unresolved.** This "financial conditions paradox" is operating now and explains the apparent inconsistency between tight policy and loose financial conditions flagged in the KB's cross-asset pricing inconsistency concept.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Compound analogue framework:**
The five-analogue set was selected by scoring each historical episode against seven dimensions of the current situation: (a) initial shock type (supply, demand, or mixed), (b) fiscal stance during disinflation, (c) labor market tightness, (d) services inflation level, (e) policy stance relative to neutral, (f) expectations anchoring status, (g) structural change overlay.

No episode scores above 7.0 because the current situation combines four structural novelties absent in every analogue: (1) 3.0% mortgage lock-in effect, (2) >90% covenant-lite credit, (3) AI investment cycle, (4) simultaneous deglobalization and fiscal expansion at 6-7% GDP deficit. The KB's existing 6.5-7.0 similarity score for 1966-69 is the ceiling, not the floor.

**Claim 2 — Last-mile base rate:**
The seven episodes where core inflation stalled in the 2.5-4.0% range with sub-4.5% unemployment:
- 1966-69: Stall → second wave. Failure.
- 1951-53: Stall → Korean War fiscal resolution. External resolution, not policy success.
- 1956-58: Stall → recession (1957-58). Required recession.
- 1968-70: Stall → recession (1969-70). Required recession.
- 1989-91: Stall → recession (1990-91). Required recession.
- 1994-95: Stall → soft landing. Success (productivity-assisted).
- 2006-08: Stall → financial crisis. Required crisis.

The single unambiguous success (1994-95) coincided with labor productivity growth accelerating to 2.5%+ that was visible in contemporaneous data. Current productivity growth of ~1.5% with AI effects not yet in the pipeline (per the KB's 5-8 year timeline) removes this escape valve.

**Claim 3 — Mission accomplished sequencing:**
The episodes where goods disinflation completing led to premature "mission accomplished":
- 1966-67: Goods deflation in late 1966 → Fed eased early 1967 → services re-accelerated by late 1967. Gap: ~3 quarters.
- 1983-84: OER deceleration drove headline CPI from 5% to 3.5% → "inflation conquered" → services ex-shelter still 4%+. Masked for ~6 quarters before becoming apparent.
- 1995-96: OER deceleration → headline below 3% → Greenspan kept rates steady → productivity saved the cycle. Gap: ~4 quarters but *outcome was benign*.
- 2004-06: Core goods deflation from China trade → headline well-behaved → expectations drifted → not apparent until late 2007. Extended masking.

The current episode maps to the 1983 and 1995 variants where OER is the dominant headline driver. The critical discriminant is whether productivity growth is present (1995 → success) or absent (1983 → masked persistence).

**Claim 4 — 1946-48 goods re-inflation:**
Post-WWII sequence: price control removal (1946) → supply shock spike → supply normalization by mid-1947 → demand-driven re-acceleration Q3 1947-Q4 1948 as consumer spending absorbed wartime savings. The goods price index fell ~10% from the 1946 peak to mid-1947, then re-accelerated 8%+ through 1948.

The current parallel: pandemic supply shock → supply normalization driving goods CPI to -1/+0.5% → tariff/deglobalization vector creates a *new* goods re-inflation channel independent of the supply chain normalization. The 1946-48 episode shows that goods disinflation completion is not a permanent state — demand-side or policy-driven shocks can reverse it. The KB correctly identifies 0.3-0.8pp tariff impact but doesn't contextualize this as a *regime echo* of the 1946-48 pattern.

**Claim 5 — Duration analysis:**
Stall zone durations from the point of goods disinflation completion:
- 1966-69: 30 months (mid-1966 to early 1969)
- 1951-53: 18 months (resolved externally by Korean War end)
- 1983-86: 24 months (resolved by Volcker persistence + oil price collapse)
- 1994-96: 15 months (resolved by productivity + measured Fed response)
- 2004-07: 36+ months (never resolved; terminated by financial crisis)

Median: 24 months. Mean: 25 months. The 1994-95 left-tail outcome (15 months) required both productivity acceleration *and* no adverse supply shocks. The 2004-07 right-tail outcome (36+ months) involved expectations drift analogous to the current Michigan 5Y-10Y pattern.

**Claim 6 — Dual policy error exposure:**
Type I errors (premature easing): 1967, 2001, 2019 — all occurred when fiscal was expansionary (2/3) or neutral (1/3). Current fiscal at 6-7% GDP deficit matches the expansionary condition 2/3 of the time.

Type II errors (insufficient tightening masked by lags): 1966-67 (WPI masking), 2006-07 (core goods masking). Both involved measurement lags creating false signals of progress. The current OER lag and covenant-lite delay create analogous masking.

Unique dual exposure: In no prior episode did *both* error types apply simultaneously with comparable force. The combination of the Fed's revealed dovish asymmetry (Type I risk) with the OER lag and covenant-lite transmission delay (Type II risk) creates a compound probability that exceeds the simple base rate of either error alone.

**Claim 7 — Financial conditions paradox:**
Episodes where services inflation >3.5% coexisted with falling headline and loosening financial conditions:
- 1967-68: S&P rose ~20% while services CPI remained 4%+
- 1984-85: Corporate spreads tightened 80bp while services ex-shelter ran 4%+
- 1995-96: Financial conditions loosened materially while services measured ~3.5%
- 2005-06: Credit spreads at historic tights while core services ran 3.5%+

In 4/5 cases, the financial conditions loosening extended the credit cycle by 12-18 months, which *delayed but amplified* the eventual correction. The exception (1995-96) again required productivity to validate the loosening ex post. Current financial conditions (HY spreads ~300bp, equity multiples at ~21x forward) follow the same pattern.

## Connections to Other Topics

**To credit markets:** The financial conditions paradox (Claim 7) directly connects to the KB's `credit_maturity_wall` and `covenant_lite_transmission_delay` concepts. The historical pattern suggests the current credit cycle extension is not anomalous but is the *expected* outcome during the goods-disinflation masking phase — and that the eventual correction is more severe for being delayed. The 2005-06 parallel is particularly informative: historically tight spreads during a services inflation stall preceded the worst credit repricing in 80 years.

**To rates/monetary policy:** The dual policy error exposure (Claim 6) reinforces and extends the KB's `fed_reaction_asymmetry` and `taylor_rule_coefficient_decline` concepts. The historical base rate for Type I errors under expansionary fiscal is 2/3, which directly calibrates the probability weight on the `ai_optimism_easing_trap` pathway.

**To FX/EM:** The 1946-48 and 1951-53 analogues both featured USD weakness during the goods re-inflation phase as real rate differentials compressed. If the goods re-inflation channel activates via tariffs, the USD may weaken despite rising nominal rates — a non-consensus outcome that would amplify imported inflation.

**To equities:** Across the five analogues, equity markets rallied during the stall zone in 4/5 episodes (median +18% from stall onset), with the rally ending 3-6 months *before* the inflation regime resolved — either via recession or second wave. The current equity rally is consistent with this pattern, not contradictory to the inflation persistence thesis.

**To commodities:** The `agricultural_supply_shocks` and `export_ban_reflexivity` concepts in the KB gain additional weight from the 1946-48 analogue, where food price re-acceleration was the primary driver of the second goods inflation wave. The post-2005 biofuel structural floor (correlation 0.4-0.6 with energy) was absent in all historical analogues, creating a novel upward convexity in the commodity-to-inflation transmission.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the 1994-95 analogue more applicable than the base rate suggests?** If current productivity growth is being undermeasured (as it was in 1995-96, where initial estimates of ~1.5% were later revised to ~2.5%), the escape-valve mechanism may be available. What real-time productivity indicators would confirm or refute this?

2. **How does the 3.0% mortgage lock-in effect alter the housing channel compared to all historical analogues?** This is genuinely unprecedented at this scale. In every prior analogue, housing activity responded to rate changes within 2-4 quarters. The lock-in effect may be extending the lag to 6-8+ quarters, which would alter both the OER resolution timeline and the monetary transmission mechanism in ways no analogue captures.

3. **What is the correct compound probability when both Type I and Type II policy errors are possible simultaneously?** Independent probabilities would give ~40% for at least one error, but the errors may be positively correlated (OER masking creates false signals that encourage premature easing), pushing the compound probability higher. Historical sample size is insufficient to estimate this directly.

4. **Does the fiscal deficit at 6-7% GDP create a genuinely novel inflation floor that invalidates all analogues?** In no prior episode was the structural fiscal deficit this large during a disinflation attempt. The `kalecki_fiscal_channel` mechanism in the KB suggests 100-150bp of spread compression, but the second-order effect on aggregate demand and services inflation persistence is not well-calibrated historically because this fiscal stance is unprecedented during non-recession, non-war periods.

5. **At what point does the compound nature of this episode render the analogue methodology itself unreliable?** If the honest answer is "this is a 5-6/10 match to everything and a 9/10 match to nothing," the base-rate approach may produce false precision. The epistemically honest conclusion may be that the distribution of outcomes is wider than any single analogue framework suggests — and that portfolio positioning should reflect genuine uncertainty rather than a point estimate from an imperfect historical match. What alternative frameworks (structural models, simulation-based approaches) should be weighted more heavily at this point in the analysis?
