# Growth Models — Geopolitical Risk & Political Economy Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Fiscal consolidation is politically constrained to <1pp GDP through 2027, making the KB's >2pp threshold trigger unlikely absent a bond market crisis.** The political economy of U.S. fiscal retrenchment faces insurmountable headwinds: 2026 midterm elections, TCJA extension pressure from Republican base, defense spending escalation from great power competition, and entitlement spending locked by demographics. DOGE-style cuts target discretionary spending (~$1.7T) but the politically achievable savings are 0.3-0.5pp GDP at most, concentrated in low-multiplier categories.

2. **The tariff regime represents a structural supply shock with asymmetric escalation risk, and the KB underprices its growth model implications.** The current tariff architecture (Section 301/232 tariffs, entity list expansion, CHIPS Act restrictions, outbound investment screening) is bipartisan consensus, not reversible by election outcomes. The escalation ladder includes secondary sanctions on Chinese semiconductor supply chains, rare earth export controls, and potential SWIFT-adjacent restrictions on Chinese financial institutions. This is not episodic noise — it is a permanent downward revision to global potential GDP growth of 0.3-0.5pp annually via allocative inefficiency.

3. **Immigration policy reversal is the single most politically determined variable in the U.S. growth model, with a policy-driven potential GDP reduction of 0.2-0.4pp annually already underway.** The KB correctly identifies immigration as a decaying tailwind but underweights the political economy mechanism: immigration restriction is the one policy area where executive authority is broadest, populist demand is strongest, and bipartisan opposition is weakest. Net immigration is likely to decline from ~3.3M (2023) to 0.5-1.0M by 2026-2027 under current policy trajectory, with concentration of losses in the exact sectors (construction, healthcare, food services) where labor market tightness most directly feeds inflation.

4. **Central bank independence is under episodic but escalating threat, introducing a fat-tailed risk to the rate path assumptions embedded in every KB concept.** The political economy of monetary policy has shifted: populist pressure for lower rates from both parties, explicit presidential commentary on Fed decisions, and potential appointment of politically sympathetic Fed governors in 2026. While base case remains institutional resilience, the tail scenario (politically constrained Fed unable to tighten into inflation) would invalidate the maturity wall analysis, the NKPC framework, and the rates-vs-equity divergence simultaneously.

5. **The stagflationary/disinflationary bifurcation identified in the KB is primarily a geopolitical variable, not a macroeconomic one, and the probability weights should be updated for current geopolitical trajectory.** The KB assigns 15% to the stagflationary path — this underweights the cumulative probability of: (a) tariff escalation beyond current levels (35-40%), (b) Middle East conflict disrupting energy supply (15-20%), (c) Taiwan Strait crisis disrupting semiconductor supply (5-10% over 2-year horizon), and (d) secondary sanctions on Russian energy creating supply displacement (20-25%). These are partially correlated — a Taiwan crisis triggers all channels simultaneously.

6. **The Kalecki fiscal channel operates through a political economy feedback loop that the KB treats as one-directional.** The deficit sustains profits → profits sustain employment → employment sustains political viability of incumbents → incumbents resist fiscal consolidation. This creates a structural bias toward deficit persistence that makes the >2pp fiscal consolidation scenario primarily a bond market vigilante scenario rather than a policy choice scenario. The binding constraint is not political will to cut but financial market tolerance for continued deficits — monitor the term premium as the market's enforcement mechanism.

7. **U.S.-China technology decoupling is a structural drag on AI productivity materialization that the KB's AI productivity lag concept does not account for.** Export controls (October 2022 semiconductor rules, expanded January 2025) restrict access to advanced compute for ~18% of global GDP. Reciprocal Chinese restrictions on critical minerals (gallium, germanium, rare earths) and component supply create supply chain friction that raises the cost curve for AI deployment globally. The geopolitical fragmentation of the AI ecosystem delays aggregate productivity gains beyond even the historical GPT diffusion base rates.

8. **Europe's growth model is undergoing a geopolitically-forced structural shift that will create divergent growth paths with direct implications for cross-asset positioning.** European defense spending ramp (+1-2pp GDP over 5 years), energy transition acceleration driven by Russian gas decoupling, and industrial policy responses to U.S. IRA/CHIPS Act create a fiscal expansion channel in Europe that runs counter to the U.S. fiscal consolidation narrative. This produces a rare regime of transatlantic growth convergence that the KB's U.S.-centric framework misses.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Fiscal consolidation politically constrained):**
- CBO baseline projects deficits of 5.5-6.5% GDP through 2034 even with current law (TCJA expiration). Full TCJA extension adds ~$4.6T over 10 years, or ~1.5pp GDP annually.
- Historical base rate: fiscal consolidation >2pp GDP has occurred only twice in post-WWII U.S. — 1993-2000 (bipartisan, peace dividend, tech boom revenue surge) and 2011-2014 (sequestration, required crisis to trigger). Current polarization makes bipartisan consolidation structurally harder.
- DOGE announced targets of $2T in savings are aspirational. Actual achievable discretionary cuts face legal, contractual, and political barriers. GAO estimates suggest ~$100-200B in genuine waste/duplication — 0.4-0.8pp GDP at best.
- 2026 midterms create a 12-month window where neither party will champion austerity. The political economy window for consolidation only opens post-2026, narrowing to 2027-2028 before the next presidential cycle constrains action.

**Claim 2 (Tariff regime as structural supply shock):**
- U.S. effective tariff rate has risen from ~2% (2017) to ~10-15% (current), with sector-specific rates far higher (Chinese EVs: 100%, steel: 25%, semiconductors: various). This is the highest sustained tariff level since Smoot-Hawley.
- Bipartisan consensus: Biden administration retained and expanded Trump-era tariffs. Neither party platform advocates meaningful tariff reduction. This removes the historical mean-reversion assumption embedded in most growth models.
- IMF estimates global tariff escalation reduces world GDP by 0.5-1.5% over medium term through allocative inefficiency, reduced specialization, and investment uncertainty. The distribution is uneven — small open economies and trade-dependent sectors bear disproportionate impact.
- Entity list expansion (now >600 Chinese entities) and outbound investment screening (effective January 2025) restrict capital flows and technology transfer, creating parallel innovation ecosystems with duplicated R&D costs.

**Claim 3 (Immigration as politically determined growth variable):**
- Executive authority over immigration is constitutionally broad: border enforcement, asylum processing, visa issuance rates, and enforcement priorities can shift net immigration flows by millions without legislative action.
- CBP encounter data shows sharp decline from peak: monthly encounters dropped from ~250K (December 2023) to ~100-120K (late 2025). Work authorization processing has slowed significantly.
- The sectors most affected by immigration restriction — construction (10-15% immigrant workforce), food services (20-25%), healthcare support (15-20%) — are precisely those where wage inflation most directly feeds services CPI.
- The Solow accounting from the KB (3-4M workers × ~$80K/worker = 0.3-0.4pp GDP growth) runs in reverse: a 2-2.5M reduction from peak net immigration mechanically reduces potential GDP growth by 0.2-0.3pp.

**Claim 4 (Central bank independence risk):**
- Presidential commentary on Fed decisions has escalated in frequency and specificity. While jawboning is not new (LBJ, Nixon, Trump first term), the current political environment combines it with: (a) congressional proposals to audit the Fed, (b) legal challenges to Fed regulatory authority, and (c) upcoming vacancy cycles enabling politically aligned appointments.
- Historical precedent: Burns-era Fed (1970-1978) demonstrated how political pressure produces accommodative bias, contributing to the stagflation of the 1970s. The institutional safeguards are strong but not impregnable.
- The tail risk is not immediate politicization but gradual erosion: a Fed that leans accommodative by 25-50bp relative to optimal due to political pressure produces cumulatively large inflation persistence effects through the NKPC convexity mechanism.
- Market pricing: 5Y5Y inflation breakevens at ~2.3% embed a modest independence risk premium but likely underweight the tail scenario.

**Claim 5 (Stagflationary probability underweighted):**
- The KB's 15% stagflationary probability was likely estimated before the current tariff escalation trajectory solidified as bipartisan consensus. Conditional on tariffs remaining at or above current levels (which I assess at >80% probability), the stagflationary scenario probability rises to 25-30%.
- Supply shock correlation: geopolitical shocks are positively correlated — a Taiwan crisis triggers semiconductor supply disruption AND energy supply disruption (China retaliates with energy/mineral restrictions) AND financial market contagion AND trade collapse. This correlation structure means the sum of individual probabilities understates the compound probability.
- Historical base rate for supply-driven inflation episodes (1973-74, 1979-80, 2021-22): each was triggered by geopolitical events (oil embargo, Iranian revolution, COVID + supply chain). The current geopolitical environment has more simultaneously active risk nodes than any period since 1979-80.

**Claim 6 (Kalecki political economy feedback loop):**
- The sectoral balance identity is mechanical, but the political economy of maintaining deficits is behavioral. Elected officials face asymmetric incentives: deficit reduction produces diffuse, delayed benefits but concentrated, immediate costs (job losses in affected sectors, reduced transfer payments).
- The bond market is the only disciplining mechanism: the term premium is the market's real-time assessment of fiscal sustainability. The KB's identification of the term premium steepener as the dominant curve driver is consistent with markets beginning to price fiscal risk — but at ~100bp, this is well below the levels (~200-300bp) that historically forced fiscal consolidation (Greece 2010, Italy 2011, UK 2022 gilt crisis).
- The political economy equilibrium is: deficits persist until term premium rises enough to create a fiscal crisis, which then forces consolidation that is larger and more disruptive than gradual adjustment would have been. This is a Minsky dynamic applied to sovereign fiscal policy.

**Claim 7 (Tech decoupling drags AI productivity):**
- October 2022 semiconductor export controls restrict China's access to advanced AI chips (A100/H100 and successors). This fragments the global AI ecosystem, forcing parallel R&D tracks and reducing the efficiency of global knowledge spillovers.
- Chinese reciprocal restrictions on critical minerals (gallium, germanium processing — China controls ~80% of global supply) and rare earth elements (China controls ~60-70%) raise the input cost curve for advanced semiconductor manufacturing globally.
- The historical GPT diffusion base rates in the KB (electricity ~30 years, IT ~15 years) were estimated for an integrated global economy. A fragmented global economy has slower technology diffusion by construction — knowledge barriers, duplicated infrastructure, incompatible standards.

**Claim 8 (European growth model structural shift):**
- Post-Ukraine defense spending pledges commit NATO Europe to 2-3% GDP defense spending, up from ~1.5% average. This is a fiscal expansion of 0.5-1.5pp GDP phased over 5 years — the largest European fiscal stimulus since post-WWII reconstruction, funded by new EU-level debt instruments.
- Energy transition investment: EU Green Deal Industrial Plan, REPowerEU, and national programs commit ~€600B through 2030. This is partially offsetting the energy cost shock from Russian gas decoupling.
- The net effect: European potential growth may be revised upward from ~1.0-1.2% to ~1.3-1.7% over the medium term, driven by defense and energy investment. This produces transatlantic growth convergence that challenges the strong dollar / wide current account deficit Mundell-Fleming equilibrium described in the KB.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary policy / rates strategy:** Central bank independence risk (Claim 4) directly modifies the rate path assumptions underlying the maturity wall analysis, the NKPC framework, and the cross-asset divergence. A politically constrained Fed changes the entire term structure of growth expectations.

- **Credit markets:** The fiscal consolidation constraint (Claim 1) is bullish for the Kalecki profit channel and therefore for corporate credit in the near term — but the mechanism that sustains credit quality (persistent deficits) simultaneously builds the term premium that threatens the maturity wall refinancing. Credit analysts should model the interaction between these two channels explicitly.

- **FX markets:** European growth model shift (Claim 8) directly challenges the strong dollar consensus. Transatlantic growth convergence + European fiscal expansion + potential U.S. fiscal consolidation = narrowing growth differentials that favor EUR. The Mundell-Fleming equilibrium described in the KB may be shifting.

- **Inflation / commodities:** Immigration reversal (Claim 3) + tariff regime (Claim 2) are both supply-side inflation channels that interact with the NKPC convexity. They make last-mile disinflation harder and shift the probability distribution toward the stagflationary branch of the bifurcation.

- **Equity markets:** AI productivity lag (KB concept) + tech decoupling drag (Claim 7) compound to extend the timeline before AI capex translates to aggregate earnings growth. This reinforces the Minsky classification concern — the longer the lag, the more downstream AI investment migrates from hedge to speculative finance.

- **Emerging markets / trade:** Tariff architecture and sanctions escalation reshape global trade flows, creating winners (Vietnam, India, Mexico as near-shoring beneficiaries) and losers (trade-dependent East Asian economies). This has direct implications for EM growth models and EM credit.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the term premium threshold that forces involuntary fiscal consolidation?** The UK gilt crisis (2022) occurred at ~150bp premium expansion in weeks. The U.S. has deeper markets and reserve currency status. Is the threshold 200bp? 300bp? And over what time horizon?

2. **How does the 2026 midterm cycle constrain both fiscal AND monetary policy simultaneously?** If neither party will champion austerity pre-midterms AND the Fed faces political pressure to ease, what is the combined effect on the growth/inflation trade-off?

3. **Is the U.S.-China technology decoupling an escalating staircase or approaching an equilibrium?** If export controls continue tightening (plausible), the AI productivity drag compounds over time. If a stable equilibrium emerges, the drag is one-time and adaptation reduces the ongoing cost.

4. **What is the probability of a simultaneous geopolitical shock — a Taiwan crisis contemporaneous with the maturity wall peak (2025-2027)?** This is the scenario where multiple KB concepts — maturity wall, stagflationary bifurcation, leading indicator degradation — all converge negatively. Markets have essentially zero probability priced for this compound scenario.

5. **How does the European defense/fiscal expansion interact with ECB monetary policy?** If Europe is expanding fiscally while the ECB is trying to reach its inflation target, this creates a European version of the U.S. fiscal-monetary tension — with potentially different institutional dynamics given the ECB's mandate structure.

6. **Are sanctions and export controls producing a measurable drag on global TFP, or is the allocative efficiency loss too diffuse to detect in current data?** This matters for whether the structural supply shock from geopolitical fragmentation is a 0.1pp or 0.5pp annual GDP drag — an order of magnitude difference with enormous compounding implications.

7. **What is the political economy of tariff de-escalation?** The KB identifies tariffs as the key swing variable for the stagflationary/disinflationary bifurcation, but the political economy analysis suggests de-escalation is structurally harder than escalation (concentrated losers from de-escalation lobby harder than diffuse winners). What event or crisis could trigger de-escalation?
