# Growth Models — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The current fiscal-dominated expansion most closely resembles the 1966-1967 Vietnam War mid-cycle slowdown, the 1983-1986 Reagan deficit expansion, and the 2005-2007 late cycle — but no single analogue captures the full structural picture.** Similarity scores: 1966-67 (0.72), 1983-86 (0.65), 2005-07 (0.58), 1998-2000 (0.52), 2015-16 (0.48).

2. **The base rate for large peacetime fiscal deficits (>5% GDP) producing extended expansions that eventually end in disorderly fiscal adjustment is 3-for-4 in the post-war record (1966-69→1970 recession, 1983-86→1987 crash, 2003-07→2008 crisis; exception: 2020-21 COVID recovery).** The current 6-7% deficit without wartime or crisis context is genuinely unprecedented in scale.

3. **The base rate for maturity walls producing systemic credit events is 0-for-4 in modern markets (2012, 2014, 2019, 2022), but all prior walls were defused under ZIRP/low-rate conditions unavailable today.** The relevant analogue set is actually the 1989-1991 S&L/LBO refinancing wall and the 2001-2002 telecom debt maturities — both of which produced significant credit stress with systemic spillovers.

4. **Leading indicator degradation of this magnitude — yield curve inverted 22-24 months, LEI down 40+ months, Sahm Rule triggered, all without recession — has no precedent in the post-war record.** The closest partial analogue is 1966-1967, when fiscal expansion overrode contractionary monetary signals for approximately 18 months before the indicators eventually reasserted in the 1969-1970 recession.

5. **Historical GPT diffusion base rates (electricity ~30 years, IT/Internet ~15 years, with acceleration trend) suggest AI productivity gains materializing at the aggregate level in the 2030-2035 window, not 2026-2028.** The base rate for technology-driven productivity booms being preceded by capex busts is 2-for-3 (1990s railroad overbuilding→eventual transportation revolution, 2000 telecom bust→eventual broadband productivity gains; exception: electrification which diffused without a bust).

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Analogue 1: 1966-1967 Vietnam War Mid-Cycle Slowdown (Similarity: 0.72)

**Why selected:** Fiscal deficit running 3-4% GDP (large for the era) overwhelmed Fed tightening. Leading indicators deteriorated — industrial production fell, credit spreads widened, profits decelerated — but no recession materialized. Private sector was roughly in balance.

**What happened:** The economy slowed to near-stall speed in Q1 1967 (0.2% annualized GDP) but avoided recession. The Fed reversed tightening in late 1966, cutting the discount rate. Fiscal spending continued to accelerate through 1967-1968. The expansion persisted until December 1969 — a full 3 years after the initial scare.

**Sequence:** Leading indicator deterioration (mid-1966) → credit tightening/mini-crisis (Aug-Nov 1966) → Fed reversal (Dec 1966) → re-acceleration (1967-68) → inflation acceleration (1968-69) → eventual recession (Dec 1969-Nov 1970).

**Key difference from today:** The 1966-67 fiscal deficit was 3-4% GDP vs today's 6-7%. Inflation was rising from 2% toward 4% then vs stalling at 2.5-3% now. No maturity wall overhang. No private credit opacity. The analogue supports the "extended mid-cycle" scenario but warns that the eventual resolution was stagflationary (1969-1970: recession + 6% inflation).

### Analogue 2: 1983-1986 Reagan Deficit Expansion (Similarity: 0.65)

**Why selected:** Large structural fiscal deficit (5-6% GDP), strong dollar, wide current account deficit, immigration contributing to labor supply (pre-IRCA surge), technology investment cycle (PC revolution). Mundell-Fleming dynamics confirmed — exactly as the KB's fiscal_deficit_kalecki_channel describes for the current period.

**What happened:** GDP growth averaged 4.5% from 1983-1986 despite the fiscal deficit being "unsustainable" according to contemporary consensus. The dollar peaked in February 1985 (Plaza Accord). The expansion ran until July 1990 — 7 years from the fiscal expansion's start. The October 1987 crash was a sharp equity correction (-22% in one day) but did not trigger recession.

**Sequence:** Fiscal expansion → strong growth + strong dollar (1983-85) → trade deficit widens → Plaza Accord/dollar reversal (1985) → tax reform (1986) → equity crash (Oct 1987) → S&L crisis builds (1988-89) → recession (Jul 1990).

**Key difference from today:** The 1983-86 expansion started from deep recession with massive slack. Today's expansion is running near potential with 4.0-4.2% unemployment. Interest rates were falling from Volcker highs vs today's rates elevated after a hiking cycle. The analogue supports extended expansion but warns that the credit market vulnerability (S&L then, cov-lite/private credit now) was the eventual transmission mechanism.

### Analogue 3: 2005-2007 Late Cycle (Similarity: 0.58)

**Why selected:** Credit market distortions masking true risk (CDOs/synthetic structures then, cov-lite + EBITDA addbacks + private credit opacity now). Maturity wall concerns. Yield curve inversion. Housing capex cycle then vs AI capex cycle now. Leading indicators flashing warnings that were dismissed.

**What happened:** GDP growth was 2.7% in 2006, 2.0% in 2007. Yield curve inverted June 2006-June 2007 (12 months). Subprime stress appeared in early 2007 but was dismissed as "contained." Recession began December 2007 — 18 months after first curve inversion.

**Sequence:** Yield curve inversion (Jun 2006) → early subprime stress (Feb 2007) → BNP Paribas/liquidity freeze (Aug 2007) → Fed begins cutting (Sep 2007) → recession onset (Dec 2007) → full crisis (Sep 2008).

**Key difference from today:** The private sector was in deficit at 3-4% GDP then vs surplus of 2-3% now. This is the single most important structural difference and argues against a 2008-style outcome. Household leverage was the vulnerability then; corporate leverage is the vulnerability now. The Godley sectoral balance framework correctly flagged 2005-2007 as dangerous and would not flag the current configuration as equivalently dangerous.

### Analogue 4: 1998-2000 Technology Bubble (Similarity: 0.52)

**Why selected:** Technology capex boom (internet then, AI now). Productivity narrative justifying elevated multiples. Strong corporate profits. Labor market tight with immigration supplementing supply.

**What happened:** GDP growth was 4.4% in 1998, 4.7% in 1999, 4.1% in 2000. S&P peaked March 2000. NASDAQ peaked March 2000 and fell 78%. Recession began March 2001.

**Sequence:** LTCM crisis/Fed easing (Sep 1998) → blow-off top in equities (1999-Mar 2000) → telecom/dot-com capex bust (2000) → profit recession (Q3 2000) → recession (Mar 2001) → 9/11 deepening (Sep 2001).

**Key difference from today:** Private sector was in deep deficit (~5% GDP) in 1999-2000 vs surplus now. The technology capex boom was financed by speculative/Ponzi financing structures broadly, not just in the downstream ecosystem as today (where hyperscaler capex is hedge-financed from cash flows per the KB's Minsky classification). This analogue is more relevant for the AI-specific risk than the macro cycle risk.

### Analogue 5: 2015-2016 Manufacturing Recession (Similarity: 0.48)

**Why selected:** ISM manufacturing below 50 for extended period, credit stress in specific sector (energy), leading indicators deteriorating, no recession. Mid-cycle slowdown that resolved with continued expansion.

**What happened:** GDP growth slowed to 1.7% in 2016. Energy HY defaults spiked to 15%+. Manufacturing contracted. But services and consumption held. No recession. Expansion continued until February 2020 (COVID).

**Sequence:** Oil price collapse (Jun 2014) → energy credit stress (2015) → ISM manufacturing contraction (2015-16) → global growth scare (Jan-Feb 2016) → Fed pause/reversal → re-acceleration (2017-18).

**Key difference from today:** Fiscal was not the dominant driver — the deficit was only 2.4% GDP in 2015. The Fed was at zero bound with QE still recent. This analogue supports the "extended mid-cycle" scenario but through different mechanisms.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Rates/Yield Curve:** The term_premium_steepener concept in the KB maps directly to the 1966-67 analogue, where the curve also re-steepened on fiscal supply rather than growth expectations. In 1966-67, this false signal of mid-cycle health persisted for ~18 months before the inflationary consequences overwhelmed it. The current cross_asset_growth_rate_divergence has a partial analogue in 1999-2000, when equities priced permanent productivity gains while rates markets were more skeptical — rates proved correct within 12-18 months.

**Credit Markets:** The covenant_lite_default_delay and ebitda_addback_distortion concepts map most directly to the 2005-2007 CDO/synthetic credit distortion. The key structural parallel: both involve systematic optimism bias in credit metrics that delays recognition of deterioration. The base rate for "credit opacity that delays then amplifies stress" is 2-for-2 in the modern era (CDOs in 2007, synthetic CDOs in 2008). The private_credit_rate_insensitivity concept has no clean historical analogue — the scale of rate-insensitive credit deployment is structurally novel.

**Inflation/Monetary Policy:** The nkpc_convexity_last_mile has a direct analogue in 1966-1969, where fiscal expansion kept the economy above potential and inflation gradually accelerated from 2% to 6% despite Fed tightening. The sacrifice ratio for the last mile proved much higher than expected. This is the strongest historical warning in the KB.

**Fiscal Policy:** The fiscal_deficit_kalecki_channel at 6-7% GDP without wartime or crisis context is genuinely unprecedented. The closest analogues (1966-69 at 3-4%, 1983-86 at 5-6%) were both smaller in scale. The Kalecki profit identity is an accounting relationship that holds by construction, but the behavioral elasticities of the pass-through from fiscal to profits are regime-dependent and poorly estimated across these small samples.

## Open Questions

1. **Which analogue is playing out?** The signpost that most clearly distinguishes the 1966-67 "extended but inflationary" outcome from the 2005-07 "credit-driven downturn" outcome is the private sector financial balance trajectory. If the surplus erodes from 2-3% toward zero, the 2005-07 analogue gains weight. If the surplus holds, the 1966-67 path of extended-but-inflationary expansion is more likely.

2. **Is the 1966-67 analogue's resolution (stagflation) the base case for how this cycle eventually ends?** The 1966-67 expansion was extended by fiscal for 3 years beyond the initial indicator deterioration, but ended in a stagflationary recession (1969-1970). If the current fiscal dominance extends the cycle similarly, the resolution window is 2027-2028 rather than 2025-2026.

3. **Has the maturity wall analogue set been properly specified?** I have 0-for-4 under ZIRP conditions and 2-for-2 under elevated rate conditions, but the sample is too small to draw statistical inference. The critical question is whether private credit refinancing channels (which have no historical analogue at current scale) can defuse the wall even at higher rates.

4. **Is the leading indicator degradation temporary (overridden by fiscal, will reassert) or permanent (structural economic shifts)?** The 1966-67 analogue supports "temporary" — indicators reasserted within 3 years. But the structural shifts (services-dominant economy, private credit opacity, cov-lite structures) argue for some permanent component. The resolution of this question is testable: if fiscal consolidation >2pp GDP occurs and leading indicators begin to correlate with recession risk again, the "temporary override" hypothesis is confirmed.

5. **What is the base rate for technology capex cycles (AI) producing productivity booms vs capex busts first?** The historical record (railroads, telegraph, electricity, radio, PCs, internet, telecom) shows 4-for-7 producing busts before booms, with the busts concentrated in cases where speculative/Ponzi financing dominated (per the KB's Minsky classification). The hyperscaler hedge-financing structure argues against a 2000-style bust in the core AI complex but leaves the downstream ecosystem (AI startups, GPU leasing, data center REITs) vulnerable — consistent with the KB's minsky_ai_capex_classification.
