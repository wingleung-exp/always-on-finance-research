# Growth Models — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## MACRO REGIME ASSESSMENT

The U.S. economy occupies a mid-to-late cycle expansion characterized by a closing-to-closed output gap, inflation sticky above target, and an unusually dominant fiscal impulse that obscures the true private-sector growth trajectory. Real GDP growth of approximately 2.0-2.5% headline masks a private-sector contribution of only 1.0-1.5% once the Kalecki-identifiable fiscal channel (6-7% of GDP deficit contributing an estimated 1.5-2.4pp) is stripped out. Unemployment at ~4.0-4.2% sits within the NAIRU confidence interval of 4.3-7.3% (Staiger, Stock, and Watson 1997), making definitive slack assessment impossible — a point I return to below. The New Keynesian Phillips Curve's convexity near potential explains the stalling of disinflation at ~2.5-3.0% after the rapid decline from 5.6%: the easy supply-chain normalization gains are exhausted, and last-mile disinflation now requires either meaningful output sacrifice (sacrifice ratio potentially 4-5x at this stage) or a favorable supply shock that has not materialized.

## Key Claims

1. **The output gap is approximately zero to slightly positive, but this assessment carries an irreducible uncertainty of ±1.0-2.0 percentage points, rendering real-time cycle-phase identification unreliable.** The Orphanides and van Norden (2002) result — that real-time output gap estimates are revised by 1.0-2.0pp on average — is among the most robust findings in applied macroeconomics. The CBO's real-time estimates in 2006-2007 provided no warning of the collapse ahead. This does not invalidate the output gap as a conceptual framework (contra the Post-Keynesian strong-form claim that hysteresis makes it "theoretically incoherent"), but it does mean that any policy prescription or market positioning predicated on a precise gap estimate is building on sand.

2. **The fiscal deficit at 6-7% of GDP is the single most important variable sustaining this expansion, operating through both the Kalecki profit identity and the Mundell-Fleming open-economy channel.** Through the Kalecki-Levy identity (Profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports − Household Saving + Dividends), the ~$1.8-2.0T federal deficit mechanically sustains corporate profits regardless of the private sector's organic momentum. Through Mundell-Fleming, this fiscal stance — combined with restrictive monetary policy — produces the predicted strong dollar, wide current account deficit (~3-3.5% GDP), and capital inflows. The sectoral balance identity confirms the private sector is in surplus at 2-3% of GDP, a condition that has historically been inconsistent with near-term recession (contrast with the 5% private deficit in 1999-2000 and 3-4% deficit in 2005-2007 per the Godley framework). This is the load-bearing variable: fiscal consolidation exceeding ~2pp of GDP would mechanically erode the private surplus, reduce aggregate profits, and expose the economy's true stall speed.

3. **The economy's effective stall speed is higher than conventional estimates suggest — approximately 1.5-2.0% GDP growth — due to EBITDA addback distortions and the approaching maturity wall.** Standard stall-speed analysis assumes reported leverage ratios are accurate. With EBITDA addbacks inflating reported metrics by 15-40% (ECB and Fed analyses), true leverage for the leveraged loan universe is 7.5-8.5x on cash EBITDA versus reported 6x. At 9-10% all-in borrowing costs, interest expense at true leverage consumes 70-80% of cash EBITDA. This means the economy needs to maintain substantially above-trend growth merely to prevent cascading coverage deterioration. The $900B-$1.1T maturity wall (2025-2027) with 300-500bp coupon step-ups adds a deterministic headwind that is manageable at current growth rates but becomes a solvency cliff if growth downshifts to 0-1%.

4. **Traditional leading indicators are temporarily overridden by fiscal dominance rather than permanently degraded, and will reassert predictive power when fiscal impulse contracts.** The yield curve inverted for 22-24 months without recession; the Conference Board LEI declined for 40+ consecutive months without recession; the Sahm Rule triggered in July 2024 without recession. The New Keynesian interpretation is that these indicators were correctly reading private-sector contractionary signals that were overwhelmed by unprecedented peacetime fiscal stimulus. The ISM Manufacturing PMI spending extended periods below 50 while GDP grew above trend is consistent with a services-dominant economy where manufacturing-centric indicators capture only a fraction of aggregate activity. I assign ~65% probability to fiscal override as the primary explanation, ~25% to permanent structural degradation, and ~10% to indicator design obsolescence.

5. **The immigration labor supply expansion (CBO: ~3.3M net immigration in 2023, 3-4M workers above pre-pandemic trend) provided an estimated 0.3-0.4pp annual GDP growth via Solow accounting and was the primary disinflationary supply shock — but this is now a decaying tailwind with an internal consistency problem in the consensus narrative.** By standard Solow growth accounting, more workers at average productivity mechanically raises output. The concentration in lower-skill service occupations (hospitality, construction, healthcare) relieved precisely those sectors with the most acute post-COVID wage pressure. However, immigration cannot simultaneously raise potential GDP (an extensive growth argument) AND keep the labor market loose enough to suppress wage inflation (a slack argument) without specifying the precise sectoral mismatch channel. The disinflationary impact requires that immigrants increased labor supply in high-wage-pressure sectors more than they increased demand — plausible but imprecisely estimated.

6. **AI has not produced a measurable structural break in aggregate total factor productivity, and historical GPT diffusion base rates strongly suggest the consensus 2-3 year timeline for aggregate productivity gains is unrealistic.** Aggregate nonfarm TFP growth remains at ~1.5-2.0%, within normal cyclical variation. Firm-level gains are real (14% in customer support, 30-40% in writing tasks), but aggregate productivity = (micro gains) × (adoption rate) × (reallocation efficiency) × (complementary investment). Each multiplicative term is well below 1.0. The yield curve has not endorsed the structural productivity story — if markets genuinely priced sustained productivity acceleration, r-star would be revised higher and term premium would compress. Instead, term premium remains elevated, suggesting fiscal supply concerns dominate.

7. **The cross-asset growth rate divergence — equities pricing 5-6% NGDP versus rates pricing 3-4% — must resolve, and historical base rates slightly favor the rates market.** S&P 500 at ~21x forward P/E with ~10% implied EPS growth embeds 5-6% nominal GDP. The rates curve with forwards embedding 75-100bp of easing prices 3-4% NGDP. Historical base rates favor the rates market ~60-65% of the time as leading indicator, though post-COVID rates credibility has deteriorated. The term premium steepener (ACM and Kim-Wright decompositions) creates a false mid-cycle signal in the curve shape, further confusing cross-asset signal extraction.

8. **The 1946-1948 post-war normalization is a more instructive analogue for the current cycle than any post-1970 recession.** Post-WWII featured: massive fiscal retrenchment, supply-side normalization, pent-up household demand with elevated savings, inflation that spiked then fell rapidly, and a brief mild recession (1948-49) despite dramatic fiscal withdrawal. The parallel is striking but imperfect — today's fiscal consolidation will be far less aggressive.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Output gap measurement error):** Orphanides and van Norden (2002, "The Unreliability of Output-Gap Estimates in Real Time") documented mean absolute revisions of 1.0-2.0pp across multiple estimation methods and countries. Staiger, Stock, and Watson (1997) showed the NAIRU 95% confidence interval spans approximately 4.3-7.3%. With unemployment at 4.0-4.2%, we cannot distinguish between a modest positive gap and a modest negative gap with any statistical confidence. This does not validate the Post-Keynesian strong-form hysteresis claim — the weight of evidence from Blanchard and Summers (1986) through Ball (2014) suggests hysteresis operates partially and on multi-year horizons — but it counsels epistemic humility in cycle-phase identification.

**Claim 2 (Fiscal dominance via Kalecki-Mundell-Fleming):** The Kalecki profit identity is an accounting tautology. The behavioral question is the fiscal multiplier, estimated at 0.8-1.2 given the current state of the cycle (near full employment, restrictive monetary policy, open economy with flexible exchange rate). Fiscal demand contribution of 1.5-2.4pp implies private-sector organic growth of 1.0-1.5% — consistent with the "mid-to-late cycle stripped of fiscal" characterization. Mundell-Fleming predictions (strong dollar, wide current account, capital inflows) are confirmed in the data. The private sector surplus of 2-3% GDP derives mechanically from the sectoral balance identity (Government deficit = Private surplus + Current account deficit; 6-7% = 2-3% + 3-3.5%) and provides a structural buffer absent in prior late cycles.

**Claim 3 (Elevated stall speed):** ECB analysis and Fed supervisory data confirm the 15-40% EBITDA addback range. Arithmetic: if true leverage is 7.5-8.5x and all-in cost is 9-10%, interest expense = 67-85% of cash EBITDA. The maturity wall of $900B-$1.1T in 2025-2027 is contractual fact; average 400bp step-up yields ~$36B incremental annual interest expense, a 14% increase for HY with 3-5pp FCF margin erosion. However, the 0-for-4 base rate of maturity walls producing systemic stress in modern markets (2012, 2014, 2019) counsels humility — though prior walls were defused under ZIRP conditions unavailable today.

**Claim 4 (Leading indicator override):** The New Keynesian framework predicts indicator failure when exogenous fiscal shock overwhelms normal cyclical forces. ISM-GDP divergence is consistent with a services-dominant economy (~78% of GDP) where manufacturing PMI captures a narrowing window of aggregate activity. Weighting fiscal override at ~65% because: (a) timing of failure coincides with pandemic fiscal expansion; (b) indicators correctly read private-sector weakness (1.0-1.5% growth is recessionary); (c) historical fiscal dominance episodes (wartime economies) showed similar distortion.

**Claim 5 (Immigration supply shock):** Vacancy decline from ~12M to ~8M without unemployment rising is the key empirical signature of a rightward Beveridge curve shift. Solow accounting: 3-4M workers × ~$80K average output ≈ 0.3-0.4pp GDP growth annually. The sectoral concentration in high-wage-pressure services explains the outsized disinflationary effect. The internal consistency problem is resolved only if the labor supply shock was sectorally concentrated and not offset by proportional demand from immigrant consumption — plausible but imprecisely measured.

**Claim 6 (AI productivity lag):** David (1990) on electrification, Brynjolfsson, Rock, and Syverson (2021) on IT provide the GPT diffusion base rates. Electricity: ~30 years; IT/Internet: ~15 years. Aggregate nonfarm TFP at 1.5-2.0% shows no structural break. The term premium decomposition has not endorsed the productivity story — elevated term premium reflects fiscal supply, not productivity optimism compressing risk premia.

**Claim 7 (Cross-asset divergence):** S&P earnings yield ~4.8% minus 10Y Treasury ~4.3% implies ERP of ~50bp — historically compressed. Rates forwards embedding 75-100bp of easing imply growth deceleration. These cannot both hold at stated magnitudes. The term premium steepener creates a false mid-cycle signal in curve shape. Historical base rate of ~60-65% for rates accuracy is the weakest evidentiary link — post-COVID rates performance has been poor.

**Claim 8 (1946-48 analogue):** Post-WWII fiscal retrenchment (deficit from ~21% GDP to surplus), supply normalization, excess savings deployment, rapid disinflation, and brief mild recession parallel current dynamics. Key difference: today's consolidation will be far less aggressive. The analogue is underappreciated but inherently low-confidence — structural differences (financial system, globalization, demographics) are substantial.

## POLICY STANCE EVALUATION

**Monetary Policy:** Taylor Rule: r* + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*). Using r* ≈ 0.5-1.0% (Laubach-Williams), core PCE ≈ 2.7%, π* = 2.0%, output gap ≈ 0 to +0.5%, the Rule prescribes nominal rates of approximately 3.9-4.8%. With fed funds at ~4.25-4.50%, policy is at the restrictive end of the Taylor range but not dramatically misaligned. Real fed funds of ~1.5-1.8% above most r* estimates confirms restrictive stance. However, private credit's rate-insensitive deployment (~$200-300B/yr from committed capital pools) creates a leak in the IS curve, weakening transmission and implying effective restrictiveness is less than the rate level suggests.

**Fiscal Policy:** At 6-7% of GDP, the structural deficit is ~3-4pp above what cyclically adjusted models prescribe at full employment — the most aggressively pro-cyclical fiscal stance in peacetime history. Ricardian equivalence predicts partial offset (estimated 30-50% household saving response), meaning the fiscal multiplier is dampened but not eliminated.

## GROWTH AND INFLATION OUTLOOK

**Base Case — Extended Muddle-Through (50% probability):**
- Real GDP: 1.8-2.3% through Q4 2026
- Core PCE: 2.4-2.8%, sticky above target
- Fed: holds at 4.25-4.50% through H1 2026, begins gradual 25bp cuts in H2
- Unemployment: drifts to 4.2-4.5%
- Fiscal deficit: 5.5-6.5% GDP (modest consolidation, no structural reform)
- Private-sector growth: 1.0-1.5%, sustained by Kalecki channel

**Disinflationary Downshift (25% probability):**
- Real GDP: 0.5-1.5%
- Core PCE: 2.0-2.3%
- Fed: cuts 100-150bp over 12 months
- Unemployment: rises to 4.5-5.0%
- Trigger: fiscal consolidation >2pp GDP or maturity wall crystallization at current rates

**Stagflationary Path (15% probability):**
- Real GDP: 0.5-1.0%
- Core PCE: 3.0-3.5%
- Fed: faces impossible tradeoff, cannot cut despite weakness
- Trigger: tariff escalation or de-globalization supply shock
- Worst outcome for 60/40: positive stock-bond correlation

**Re-acceleration (10% probability):**
- Real GDP: 2.5-3.0%+
- Core PCE: 2.8-3.2%
- Fed: forced to hold or hike
- Requires: AI productivity surprise OR fiscal expansion (full TCJA + infrastructure)

## MARKET IMPLICATIONS

**Rates:** Taylor Rule and term premium diagnostics point to a curve appropriately priced for base case but mispriced for tails. Disinflationary downshift → 100-150bp front-end rally. Stagflationary path → 50-100bp long-end selloff from term premium expansion. Asymmetry favors conditional steepeners: receive front end (growth downshift triggers cuts), pay long end (fiscal supply sustains term premium). Private credit rate insensitivity means cuts will be less stimulative than historical norms.

**Equities:** At 21x forward P/E, equities embed re-acceleration or unusually extended muddle-through. Compressed ERP (~50bp) leaves minimal margin for disappointment. Kalecki framework: as long as deficit sustains at 6-7% GDP, profits are mechanically supported. Risk is non-linear — fiscal consolidation >2pp triggers profit decline → coverage deterioration → credit acceleration within 4-6 quarters. Immigration reversal creates relative value: overweight capital-intensive/productivity-benefiting sectors vs. labor-intensive services facing renewed wage pressure.

**Credit:** Maturity wall is dominant risk factor. HY OAS at 350-420bp consistent with mid-cycle but not priced for true leverage concealed by addbacks. Non-linear character (manageable at 2%+ GDP, cliff at 0-1%) means credit is a short-gamma position. Covenant-lite (>90% of leveraged loans) delays default recognition 12-24 months. Monitor gap between reported and cash EBITDA coverage ratios.

**FX:** Mundell-Fleming confirms strong dollar under tight monetary/loose fiscal. Dollar weakens under fiscal consolidation (narrows current account) or Fed easing outpacing other central banks. Base case: range-bound with modest weakening bias as Fed eventually cuts.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Labor Market Dynamics:** Immigration labor supply story is the primary bridge. Beveridge curve shift connects to labor market structure. Immigration reversal under policy tightening is the key variable for wage dynamics — if labor supply shock unwinds, Phillips curve re-steepens, complicating last-mile disinflation and forcing NKPC convexity reassessment.

**Monetary Policy:** Private credit rate insensitivity ($200-300B/yr) represents structural IS curve weakening affecting monetary transmission. Taylor Rule prescriptions assume stable transmission; if coefficient has fallen, effective neutral rate is higher. Connects to r-star estimation debate — Laubach-Williams estimates may be stale if private credit has permanently altered interest sensitivity of investment.

**Equity Cycles:** Kalecki profit identity links fiscal stance to aggregate earnings sustainability. Cross-asset growth divergence is the most actionable equity positioning signal. Minsky financing classification of AI capex (hyperscaler hedge finance vs. downstream speculative/Ponzi) connects growth assessment to tech valuations.

**Inflation Regimes:** NKPC convexity at last mile is the direct bridge. Sacrifice ratio of 4-5x at last mile means the Fed faces a stark tradeoff: accept 2.5% as new equilibrium or engineer demand destruction. Stagflationary vs. disinflationary bifurcation determined by whether supply-side shocks (tariffs/deglobalization) or demand weakness dominates.

**Fiscal Policy:** The fiscal deficit is the single most important variable. TCJA expiration, DOGE, and debt ceiling dynamics flow through Kalecki identity to profits and through sectoral balance identity to private-sector resilience. The ~2pp consolidation threshold is the most important and most imprecisely estimated number in this entire framework.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the precise fiscal consolidation threshold that triggers cycle downshift?** The ~2pp GDP heuristic is widely cited but imprecisely estimated. Behavioral pass-through elasticity from fiscal deficit to corporate profits depends on import leakage, household saving response, and monetary offset. This is the most important number I cannot pin down.

2. **Is the business cycle framework itself applicable, or has the U.S. entered a fiscal dominance regime where NBER dating conventions are misleading?** If fiscal policy has structurally replaced the private credit cycle as the primary driver, the entire indicator framework needs recalibration. The 1946-48 analogue suggests this is not unprecedented, but 6-7% deficits at full employment with no war has no post-war precedent.

3. **How quickly will immigration policy tightening reverse labor supply gains?** If 3-4M excess workers were absorbed over 2-3 years, reversal affects the stock slowly even if gross flow terms are sharp. Net impact depends on separation rates, sector-specific substitutability, and enforcement mechanics.

4. **Can private credit's rate insensitivity be quantified precisely enough to adjust the Taylor Rule framework?** If ~$200-300B/yr is rate-insensitive and represents ~15-20% of total credit creation, aggregate interest sensitivity of investment is reduced by 15-20%, implying neutral rate is ~50-75bp higher than standard estimates. Highly uncertain calculation.

5. **Will the 2025-2027 maturity wall be the first to produce systemic stress, breaking the 0-for-4 modern base rate?** Key conditional: rate environment at refinancing time. Prior walls defused under ZIRP; amend-and-extend at 5%+ all-in is fundamentally different. Base rate counsels humility.

6. **Does the term premium steepener diagnostic produce actionable trades, or is model dependence too high?** ACM vs. Kim-Wright methodologies can produce materially different estimates. If decomposition is model-dependent, the "false mid-cycle signal" thesis weakens.

7. **What is the probability AI productivity gains materialize on a 3-5 year horizon rather than the 15-25 year GPT base rate?** If generative AI diffuses faster than prior GPTs (near-zero deployment cost, universal applicability), the base rate analogy overstates the lag. Genuinely uncertain with first-order implications for r-star, ERP, and long-run potential GDP.
