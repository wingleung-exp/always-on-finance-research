# Growth Models — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The U.S. economy is operating under a fiscal dominance regime where traditional NBER business cycle dating conventions are misleading.** The private sector, stripped of fiscal transfers, is growing at only 1.0-1.5% — consistent with stagnation or early recession. The 6-7% GDP fiscal deficit is not stimulus; it is the *structural floor* preventing a balance sheet recession. Conventional cycle-phase identification (mid vs. late) is a category error when the government sector is supplying the majority of aggregate demand growth.

2. **The sectoral financial balances reveal a deceptively fragile equilibrium: Government deficit (~6-7% GDP) = Private surplus (~2-3% GDP) + Current account deficit (~3-3.5% GDP).** While the private sector surplus appears to provide resilience relative to 1999-2000 (-5% GDP) or 2005-2007 (-3-4% GDP), this surplus is *entirely a fiscal artifact*. It exists only because the government is running historically unprecedented peacetime deficits. Any fiscal consolidation >2pp GDP mechanically destroys the private surplus, and the speed of that destruction is the single most important variable for growth forecasting over the next 12-18 months.

3. **Potential GDP is demand-determined through hysteresis — the output gap is not merely poorly measured but theoretically incoherent.** The strong-form Post-Keynesian position holds that actual output determines potential output, not the reverse. The immigration-driven labor supply expansion (3-4M workers above pre-pandemic trend) and the absence of aggregate AI productivity gains are both consistent with this view: labor supply responded to labor demand, and productivity growth responds to investment, which responds to demand. The CBO's potential GDP estimates are ex-post rationalizations, not independent benchmarks.

4. **The 1946-48 post-war normalization is the most analytically productive historical analogue for the current macro configuration.** The post-WWII period featured: (a) massive fiscal retrenchment from wartime deficits, (b) a private sector flush with savings (war bonds, deferred consumption), (c) pent-up investment demand, and (d) structurally elevated inflation from supply-side bottlenecks. The current parallels — large fiscal deficits with political pressure to consolidate, elevated private savings buffers, and sticky inflation from supply restructuring — make this a far better analogue than the standard late-cycle comparisons to 1999 or 2007. Critically, the 1946-48 transition included a sharp but short recession (November 1948 - October 1949) triggered by fiscal withdrawal, despite private balance sheet strength.

5. **The Minsky Financial Instability Hypothesis classifies the current credit ecosystem as predominantly "speculative" finance with Ponzi pockets, not yet at systemic Minsky moment risk — but the margin of safety is narrower than it appears.** Hedge finance (income covers both principal and interest) is concentrated in hyperscaler AI capex and investment-grade corporates. Speculative finance (income covers interest but not principal, requiring continuous refinancing) characterizes the $900B-$1.1T maturity wall borrowers facing 300-500bp coupon step-ups. Ponzi finance (income covers neither, requiring asset appreciation or additional borrowing) is emerging in downstream AI ecosystem financing, covenant-lite leveraged loans with true 7.5-8.5x leverage, and PIK-toggle private credit structures. The transition from speculative to Ponzi is non-linear and typically accelerates once it begins.

6. **The credit impulse — the change in the change of credit — is the correct leading indicator for this cycle, not the level of credit or default rates.** Default rates are structurally lagged by 12-24 months due to covenant-lite structures, and private credit opacity ($1.7T invisible to standard indices) further degrades signal quality. The credit impulse captures the flow dynamics that drive aggregate demand in an endogenous money framework. Current credit impulse is decelerating but positive, suggesting growth deceleration without imminent collapse — but this reading is itself contaminated by the same opacity that degrades default rate signals.

7. **The immigration "double-counting" problem is real and reveals an internal inconsistency in the consensus narrative.** Immigration cannot simultaneously (a) raise potential GDP (extensive growth via more workers at average productivity) AND (b) keep the labor market loose enough to suppress wage inflation. If immigrants are adding to potential GDP, they are adding to actual output, which is demand-creating. If they are suppressing wages, they are reducing the labor share of income, which is demand-destroying. The Kalecki profit equation resolves this: immigrant workers add to output while their below-average wages compress the household sector's claim on that output, transferring income to the profit share. This is a distributional, not an aggregate, effect — and it is now reversing under immigration policy tightening.

8. **Treasury debt maturity management functions as an implicit fiscal-monetary coordination signal.** The current barbell approach (heavy issuance in bills and long bonds) signals: (a) confidence in sustained nominal GDP growth sufficient to service the debt, and (b) willingness to accept duration risk on the government balance sheet rather than pushing it to the private sector. From an endogenous money perspective, Treasury issuance is a reserve-draining operation — it does not "borrow" from the private sector but rather offers a safe savings vehicle. The maturity structure affects term premium composition and the yield curve signal, directly connecting to the term premium steepener diagnostic.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Fiscal Dominance Regime):**
The Kalecki profit identity (Profits = Investment + Govt Deficit + Net Exports - Household Saving + Dividends) is an accounting tautology. With the government deficit at $1.8-2.0T annually, fiscal demand contribution is estimated at 1.5-2.4pp of GDP growth. Stripping this contribution reveals private-sector growth of only 1.0-1.5%. This is not "mid-to-late cycle with fiscal support" — it is fiscal dominance. The traditional indicators (yield curve, LEI, Sahm Rule) were reading the private-sector signal correctly; the fiscal firehose overwhelmed them. Godley's sectoral balances framework predicted this diagnostic precisely: when the government runs massive deficits, the private sector accumulates financial assets whether or not the underlying private economy is healthy. The leading indicator degradation documented in the KB (yield curve inverted 22-24 months, LEI down 40+ months, Sahm Rule false positive) is not indicator failure — it is the indicators correctly reading private sector weakness that fiscal is masking.

**Claim 2 (Fragile Sectoral Balance):**
The sectoral balance identity is arithmetic, not theory. Government deficit 6-7% = Private surplus 2-3% + Current account deficit 3-3.5%. This can be verified in the Fed's Financial Accounts (Z.1). The vulnerability is that the private surplus *depends entirely* on the fiscal deficit. Godley's analytical work at the Levy Economics Institute in the late 1990s showed that the private sector deficit of 5% GDP in 1999-2000 was unsustainable — and it was. Today the surplus appears healthy, but it is a fiscal byproduct. The Mundell-Fleming predictions (strong dollar, wide current account deficit, capital inflows) are confirmed in the data, validating the open-economy extension of the sectoral balance framework.

**Claim 3 (Demand-Determined Potential GDP):**
The Orphanides and van Norden (2002) result — real-time output gap estimation has mean absolute revisions of 1.0-2.0 percentage points — has been replicated exhaustively and is not in dispute. The NAIRU confidence interval of 4.3-7.3% (Staiger, Stock, Watson 1997) spans the entire policy-relevant range. But the Post-Keynesian critique goes deeper: these are not merely measurement problems but conceptual ones. If hysteresis is operative (and the post-2008 experience strongly suggests it is — potential GDP estimates were revised down repeatedly to match actual GDP), then "potential" GDP is simply wherever actual GDP happens to be, plus or minus cyclical variation. The immigration effect supports this: workers migrated to the U.S. *because* labor demand was strong, raising measured potential GDP as a consequence of actual demand, not as an independent supply-side input.

**Claim 4 (1946-48 Analogue):**
The post-WWII analogue is under-discussed because most market participants rely on post-1970 comparisons. Key structural parallels: (a) Federal deficit peaked at 30% of GDP in 1943, then consolidated rapidly to surplus by 1947-48 — the current political environment (DOGE, TCJA expiration, debt ceiling) contemplates fiscal consolidation from 6-7% deficit, a proportionally smaller but still Keynesian-contractionary move; (b) household savings rate was elevated post-war (~12-15%) from forced saving during the war, analogous to today's private sector surplus; (c) inflation was supply-driven (price control removal, supply chain conversion) not demand-driven, paralleling today's tariff/de-globalization supply shocks; (d) the 1948-49 recession was mild and short precisely because private balance sheets were strong — the Godley framework would have read the private surplus as a cushion. The key difference: the post-war era had no maturity wall, no covenant-lite structures, and a much smaller financial sector relative to GDP.

**Claim 5 (Minsky Classification):**
Minsky's taxonomy is behavioral, not merely financial: hedge units can service debt from operating income, speculative units can cover interest but must roll principal, Ponzi units require asset price appreciation or external financing to remain solvent. Applying this:
- *Hedge:* Hyperscaler AI capex (MSFT, GOOG, AMZN, META) funded from $200B+ annual cash flows. Investment-grade corporates with net leverage <3x and stable interest coverage >6x.
- *Speculative:* The $900B-$1.1T maturity wall borrowers at reported 6x (actual 7.5-8.5x) leverage requiring refinancing at 300-500bp higher coupons. These units can service current interest but cannot repay principal without refinancing. At 9-10% all-in cost and 8x actual leverage, interest consumes 70-80% of cash EBITDA — a thin margin of safety.
- *Ponzi:* AI startup ecosystem reliant on venture debt with negative cash flow. Leveraged loan borrowers where EBITDA addbacks inflate reported earnings by 15-40%, meaning actual cash flow may not cover even current interest. PIK-toggle private credit structures that defer interest payments, effectively borrowing to pay interest.

The Minsky cycle predicts that stability itself is destabilizing: the prolonged period of low defaults and tight spreads encourages lenders to ease terms (already visible in covenant-lite dominance >90%), shifting the system toward more fragile financing structures.

**Claim 6 (Credit Impulse as Leading Indicator):**
In the endogenous money framework (drawing on Schumpeter, Minsky, and contemporary circuit theorists), credit creation by banks is the primary driver of aggregate demand growth. The credit impulse — the second derivative of credit — leads GDP growth by approximately 2-4 quarters. This framework is theoretically superior to models that treat money as exogenous because it correctly captures the causal chain: banks create deposits when they lend, they do not lend out pre-existing deposits. The KB documents that default rates are lagged 12-24 months by covenant-lite structures and that $1.7T in private credit is invisible to standard indices. This means the credit impulse signal is the least-degraded available leading indicator, though it too suffers from private credit opacity. Flow-of-funds data from the Z.1 release provides the most comprehensive credit impulse measure.

**Claim 7 (Immigration Double-Counting):**
The Kalecki profit equation resolves the apparent contradiction. Let S = household saving, I = investment, G-T = government deficit, X-M = net exports, then Profits = I + (G-T) + (X-M) - S. Immigration adds workers who produce output (raising GDP) while earning below-average wages (compressing S relative to GDP, which *raises* the profit share). This is why corporate profits have been robust despite immigration-driven labor supply expansion — the Kalecki framework predicts exactly this distributional outcome. But the effect is asymmetric on reversal: immigration reduction raises the average wage (raising S) while reducing output growth, compressing profits from both sides. The CBO's 3.3M net immigration estimate for 2023 is now being reversed by policy, making this a live growth headwind.

**Claim 8 (Treasury Maturity Signal):**
From the endogenous money perspective, government debt issuance is not "borrowing" but rather offering the private sector a term deposit at the central bank (as Mosler and Wray have argued). The maturity structure choice reflects the government's preference for interest rate exposure and its signal about the nominal GDP trajectory needed to stabilize the debt/GDP ratio. A barbell strategy (bills + long bonds) signals: comfort with the current level of nominal GDP growth and willingness to lock in long-term rates. This connects directly to the term premium steepener: the ACM and Kim-Wright decompositions showing term premium dominance in the 2s10s slope are consistent with fiscal supply pressure (Treasury issuance) rather than growth expectations driving the curve.

| 2. Fragile sectoral balance | **9/10** | The sectoral balance identity is definitional. The fragility assessment (surplus depends on deficit) is mechanical. The only question is speed of adjustment under consolidation. |

But the mainstream view (supply-determined potential) has institutional support and the empirical evidence is contested. The hysteresis literature is growing but not yet consensus. |

| 5. Minsky classification | **7/10** | The framework is sound and the classifications are defensible. Uncertainty centers on the aggregate size of Ponzi finance and the tipping point dynamics. The $900B-$1.1T maturity wall is factual; the 7.5-8.5x true leverage estimate carries ±1x uncertainty. |
| 6. Credit impulse as leading indicator | **6/10** | Theoretically correct within the endogenous money framework, but signal quality is degraded by private credit opacity and data lags in the Z.1 release. Practically useful but not as clean as the theory suggests. |
| 7. Immigration double-counting | **8/10** | The Kalecki resolution is mechanically correct. The distributional analysis (profit share vs. wage share) is well-established. The asymmetric reversal prediction is logically sound but untested at this scale. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**Labor Market Dynamics:** The immigration double-counting problem (Claim 7) directly connects to labor market structure. The Kalecki distributional resolution predicts that immigration reversal will compress both labor supply AND the profit share, creating simultaneous labor market tightening and earnings deterioration — a stagflationary combination. The wage-price spiral rejection in the KB is consistent with the Post-Keynesian view that inflation is fundamentally a distributional conflict, not a monetary phenomenon.

**Monetary Policy Transmission:** The fiscal dominance regime (Claim 1) implies that monetary policy is subordinate to fiscal policy in determining aggregate outcomes. Private credit's rate insensitivity ($200-300B/yr deployed from committed capital pools) further weakens the monetary transmission mechanism. The endogenous money framework predicts that central bank rate changes affect the *distribution* of income (between debtors and creditors) more than the *level* of activity — especially when fiscal is dominant. This connects to the central bank balance sheet normalization research: QT is a reserve-draining operation that interacts with Treasury issuance in determining the term premium, not a separate policy instrument.

**Equity Market Cycles:** The cross-asset growth-rate divergence (equities pricing 5-6% NGDP, rates pricing 3-4%) maps directly onto the sectoral balance analysis. Equities are pricing the fiscal-sustained economy; rates are pricing the private-sector-only economy. The Kalecki profit identity suggests equities will remain correct *as long as the fiscal deficit persists*. Fiscal consolidation >2pp GDP would validate the rates market within 4-6 quarters via the profit channel. The Minsky classification of AI capex connects to equity valuation: hedge-financed hyperscaler capex supports multiples, but downstream Ponzi-financed AI ecosystem is a valuation trap.

**Inflation Regimes:** The NKPC convexity near potential output (Claim 3's connection) is rendered analytically hollow if potential output itself is demand-determined. The Post-Keynesian alternative is the conflict theory of inflation: inflation results from incompatible claims on output by workers, firms, government, and the foreign sector. The current sticky inflation at ~2.5-3% reflects an unresolved distributional conflict between profit margins and real wages, not an output gap. Tariff/de-globalization escalation would intensify this conflict (stagflationary path), while demand weakness would resolve it via margin compression (disinflationary path).

**Rates/Equity Correlation:** The stagflationary vs. disinflationary bifurcation is fundamentally about the inflation regime. In the Post-Keynesian framework, the correlation regime depends on whether the binding constraint is supply (positive correlation — stagflationary) or demand (negative correlation — disinflationary). The fiscal dominance regime currently suppresses the demand-deficiency path, making the supply-shock/stagflationary path the tail risk to monitor.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the precise fiscal consolidation timeline and magnitude?** DOGE spending cuts, TCJA expiration dynamics, and debt ceiling resolution will determine whether the 2pp GDP threshold is breached. The political economy of fiscal consolidation is inherently unpredictable. This is the single most important variable for the next 12-18 months.

2. **How quickly does the private sector surplus erode under fiscal consolidation?** The sectoral balance identity tells us it must erode, but the speed depends on behavioral responses: does the household sector attempt to maintain spending by drawing down savings (accelerating erosion) or cut spending immediately (cushioning the surplus but deepening recession)? The Godley models assumed relatively slow adjustment (4-8 quarter lags), but the post-COVID economy has shown faster behavioral adaptation.

3. **Is the private sector surplus distribution load-bearing?** If the 2-3% GDP surplus is concentrated in the top income decile (plausible given wealth concentration trends), the aggregate figure overstates resilience. The bottom 60% of households may already be in financial deficit, meaning the Minsky moment arrives for them well before the aggregate surplus turns negative. Distributional flow-of-funds data is needed but is only available with significant lags.

4. **Can the credit impulse be meaningfully measured given private credit opacity?** The theoretical argument for credit impulse as the correct leading indicator is strong, but the practical measurement problem is severe. $1.7T in private credit with manager-smoothed NAVs and PIK toggles may represent a positive credit impulse that is entirely fictitious — the accounting equivalent of Ponzi finance at the fund level.

5. **What is the aggregate size and systemic interconnection of Ponzi finance units?** The Minsky framework identifies the categories but does not automatically quantify them. If Ponzi finance is <5% of the credit system, the system can absorb localized failures. If it is >15%, the self-reinforcing feedback loops (asset price declines → margin calls → forced selling → further declines) become systemic. The opacity problem means we genuinely do not know the answer.

6. **Does the 1946-48 analogue predict the *depth* or only the *direction* of adjustment?** The post-war recession was mild (GDP declined ~1.7%, unemployment rose to 7.9%) because private balance sheets were genuinely strong and the supply-side reconversion created enormous pent-up demand. Today's economy has a private surplus but also a $900B-$1.1T maturity wall, covenant-lite structures with hidden leverage, and $1.7T in opaque private credit — none of which existed in 1948. The analogue may be correct on direction (fiscal withdrawal → recession) but wrong on depth (deeper than 1948-49 due to financial fragility).

7. **Is immigration reversal a supply shock, a demand shock, or both?** The Kalecki framework classifies it as both — but the relative magnitude matters. If the supply effect dominates (fewer workers → lower potential GDP), the output gap closes without a demand recession, suggesting inflationary pressure. If the demand effect dominates (fewer workers → less spending → lower aggregate demand), the output gap widens, suggesting disinflationary pressure. The answer depends on the marginal propensity to consume of immigrant workers relative to the firms that employ them, which is empirically uncertain.

8. **When does the Minsky cycle transition from "speculative" to "Ponzi" become self-reinforcing?** Minsky himself was vague on timing, noting only that stability breeds instability over "a long time." The current cycle has features that could accelerate the transition (algorithmic trading, passive investment flows, mark-to-model private credit valuations) or decelerate it (fiscal floor on profits, strong household balance sheets, Fed willingness to intervene). Identifying the tipping point ex ante remains the central unsolved problem in Minskyan economics.
