# Growth Models & Cycle Positioning — Cross-Asset Strategist (Generalist) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Cross-asset markets are pricing mutually inconsistent growth regimes.** Equities (S&P ~21x fwd P/E, ~10% EPS growth) imply 5-6% nominal GDP, while rates (forwards embedding 75-100bp of easing) imply 3-4% nominal GDP. Credit spreads (HY OAS 350-420bp) sit between the two, leaning toward the equity narrative. This divergence is the single most actionable signal in the current environment — it must resolve, and historically rates markets have been the better leading indicator ~60-65% of the time.

2. **The U.S. may be in a fiscal dominance regime that renders traditional business cycle dating conventions misleading.** With the fiscal deficit at 6-7% GDP in a non-recessionary environment — unprecedented in peacetime — the Kalecki profit identity mechanically sustains corporate earnings and aggregate demand. Private-sector GDP growth stripped of the fiscal contribution is only ~1.0-1.5%, consistent with early deceleration or near-stall speed. The "business cycle" may be better understood as a "fiscal cycle" — NBER dating conventions, built for private-sector-driven cycles, may systematically miscategorize the current phase.

3. **The correlation regime is unstable and regime-conditional, making the stagflationary vs. disinflationary bifurcation the dominant portfolio construction variable.** The current environment exhibits characteristics of a transitional regime: stock-bond correlation has been oscillating between positive (stagflationary episodes driven by tariff/supply shocks) and negative (flight-to-quality episodes). The tariff/de-globalization trajectory is the key swing variable that determines whether the 60/40 portfolio's core diversification assumption holds or breaks.

4. **The 1946-48 post-war normalization is the most underappreciated historical analogue for this cycle.** Post-WWII featured: massive fiscal deficit winding down, pent-up demand and supply chain normalization, inflation surge followed by disinflation, labor market adjustment from wartime to peacetime, and degraded leading indicators in a structurally disrupted economy. The COVID fiscal response (~25% of GDP in cumulative support) and subsequent normalization bears stronger resemblance to post-war adjustment than to any post-1960 business cycle.

5. **The term premium steepener creates a false positive in cycle identification, making the yield curve shape unreliable as a growth signal.** ACM and Kim-Wright decompositions confirm that the current positive 2s10s slope is driven primarily by term premium (fiscal supply, duration risk) rather than rate expectations. A genuine mid-cycle expectations steepener (like 2015-2017) has fundamentally different growth implications. Any cross-asset framework that uses yield curve shape without decomposing into expectations and term premium components is using a corrupted input.

6. **The maturity wall ($900B-$1.1T, 2025-2027) is a deterministic growth headwind whose severity is a non-linear function of the macro regime at the time of refinancing.** At consensus GDP (2.0-2.5%), the 300-500bp coupon step-ups are manageable (10-15bp GDP drag). At a growth downshift to 0-1% — which is the private-sector growth rate *without* fiscal support — the maturity wall becomes a solvency cliff, especially given EBITDA addback distortions that mask true leverage of 7.5-8.5x vs. reported 6x.

7. **Immigration as a growth driver is a decaying tailwind that cannot be replaced by AI productivity gains on any policy-relevant timeline.** The 3-4M workers above pre-pandemic trend contributed ~0.3-0.4pp of GDP growth annually through extensive Solow accounting. This is now reversing under policy tightening. AI has not produced measurable aggregate TFP gains (still ~1.5-2%), and historical GPT diffusion base rates (electricity ~30 years, IT ~15 years) suggest material aggregate impact is years away. The consensus narrative that implicitly relies on one replacing the other embeds a timing gap.

8. **Private credit's rate insensitivity (~$200-300B/yr deployed from committed capital pools) represents a structural leak in the monetary policy transmission mechanism that makes the cross-asset growth rate divergence more persistent.** The Fed's ability to slow credit creation and hence growth is impaired when a significant share of credit flows from deployment-driven rather than price-sensitive capital. This simultaneously supports the equity narrative (credit availability sustains activity) and undermines the rates narrative (the Fed needs to be more aggressive to achieve the same tightening).

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Cross-Asset Divergence):** The equity-implied nominal GDP of 5-6% derives from the relationship between S&P earnings growth expectations (~10%) and the historical earnings/GDP beta (~1.5-2.0x). The rates-implied figure of 3-4% derives from the path of policy rate expectations embedded in OIS forwards (75-100bp of easing priced implies the Fed expects growth to soften). Credit at 350-420bp HY OAS (40th-55th percentile) is consistent with ~2% GDP growth — closer to the equity view. The historical base rate of ~60-65% for rates as the better leading indicator is derived from post-1985 episodes of equity-rates divergence, but the post-COVID rates track record (inversion without recession for 22-24 months) weakens this prior.

**Claim 2 (Fiscal Dominance):** The Kalecki profit identity (Profits = Investment + Govt Deficit + Net Exports − Household Saving + Dividends) is an accounting identity — mathematically certain, not a behavioral theory. With the deficit at ~$1.8-2.0T (6-7% GDP), the fiscal contribution to corporate profits is the dominant term. The ~1.5-2.4pp GDP contribution from fiscal demand means stripping it out leaves private GDP growth at ~1.0-1.5%. This is below the economy's estimated stall speed of ~1.5-2.0% (after adjusting for EBITDA addback distortions). The private sector financial surplus of 2-3% GDP — mechanically linked via the sectoral balance identity (Govt deficit = Private surplus + CA deficit) — provides resilience that prior late cycles (1999-2000: -5% private deficit; 2005-2007: -3-4% deficit) lacked.

**Claim 3 (Correlation Regime Instability):** The stagflationary path (tariff escalation, supply shocks → positive stock-bond correlation, both falling) vs. the disinflationary path (demand weakness → negative stock-bond correlation, bonds hedge) produces radically different portfolio outcomes. The 5Y5Y inflation breakeven at ~2.3% and Michigan 5-10Y expectations at ~2.5% are elevated enough that supply shocks could tip expectations into a self-reinforcing stagflationary spiral, while weak enough that a demand shock could re-anchor them. The current scenario probability distribution (45% extended mid-cycle, 30% disinflationary, 15% stagflationary, 10% re-acceleration) implies the highest-impact tail is the 15% stagflationary scenario given its disproportionate effect on traditional portfolio construction.

**Claim 4 (1946-48 Analogue):** Post-WWII fiscal deficit declined from ~22% of GDP (1945) to ~3% (1947) to surplus by 1948. Inflation spiked to ~20% in 1947 then fell sharply. Leading indicators were unreliable during the structural transition. The economy experienced a brief, mild recession in 1948-49 as fiscal withdrew — precisely the kind of "fiscal cliff" scenario that the current Kalecki framework predicts. Key difference: the private sector in 1946 had massive forced savings (war bonds, rationing), providing a buffer analogous to the current private sector surplus. Key similarity: traditional cycle identification was degraded by the dominance of fiscal flows over private-sector dynamics.

**Claim 5 (Term Premium Decomposition):** The ACM (Adrian, Crump, Moench) and Kim-Wright models, while model-dependent, directionally agree that term premium accounts for the majority of the current 2s10s positive slope. In a genuine mid-cycle expectations steepener (2015-2017), the decomposition showed expectations driving the slope — markets expected rate hikes on strong growth. The current decomposition shows expectations for easing (weak growth) offset by rising term premium (fiscal supply, duration risk). This means the yield curve *shape* signals early/mid-cycle, but the *decomposition* signals late-cycle with fiscal distortion. Any cross-asset model using the raw curve shape is receiving a corrupted growth signal.

**Claim 6 (Maturity Wall Non-linearity):** The $900B-$1.1T in maturities is contractual fact. The average 400bp coupon step-up on this amount yields ~$36B in incremental annual interest expense. For the HY universe specifically, this represents a ~14% increase in debt service and 3-5pp FCF margin erosion. The non-linearity emerges because EBITDA addbacks inflate reported metrics by 15-40% — a deal at reported 6x leverage is actually 7.5-8.5x on cash EBITDA. At true leverage, 9-10% all-in cost implies interest expense consuming 70-80% of actual cash EBITDA. The critical conditional: prior maturity walls (2012, 2014, 2019) were defused by amend-and-extend under ZIRP conditions that no longer exist. Base rate of systemic stress from maturity walls is 0-for-4, but the conditions differ materially.

**Claim 7 (Immigration-AI Timing Gap):** CBO data shows net immigration of ~3.3M in 2023, adding 3-4M workers above pre-pandemic trend. At ~$80K average output per worker, this adds ~0.3-0.4pp to GDP growth annually via Solow accounting. Aggregate nonfarm TFP remains at ~1.5-2% with no structural break visible from AI adoption. Firm-level gains are real (14% in customer support, 30-40% in writing tasks) but aggregate productivity = micro gains × adoption rate × reallocation efficiency × complementary investment. The transition gap between decaying immigration tailwinds and materializing AI productivity gains could leave potential GDP growth lower than consensus for 3-5+ years.

**Claim 8 (Private Credit Transmission Leak):** Direct lending funds deploy from 5-7 year committed capital pools. A fund with $5B in dry powder and a 3-year deployment window is functionally insensitive to a 150bp rate hike. At ~$200-300B/yr in deployment, this represents a meaningful share of incremental credit creation that bypasses the Fed's transmission mechanism. The implication: the IS curve is flatter (less rate-sensitive), fiscal policy is relatively more potent, and the Fed needs to be more aggressive to achieve equivalent tightening — consistent with the "higher for longer" rates path.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Labor Market Dynamics:** Immigration labor supply expansion (claim 7) directly connects to wage dynamics, Beveridge curve shifts, and NAIRU estimation. The reversal of immigration under policy tightening should produce upward pressure on wages in lower-skill service sectors (hospitality, food services, construction, healthcare) — the sectors that benefited most from immigration-driven labor supply relief. This creates a potential inflationary impulse that interacts with the NKPC convexity (claim 3) and the stagflationary scenario.

**Equity Cycles & Valuation:** The cross-asset growth divergence (claim 1) directly determines whether current equity multiples (~21x fwd P/E) are sustainable. If rates markets are correct (3-4% NGDP), equity multiples need to compress 15-20% to equilibrium. If equities are correct (5-6% NGDP), rates are too low and will sell off, raising the discount rate and partially offsetting the growth benefit. The credit-equity basis (gap between equity-implied and actual credit spreads) is currently compressed — credit is validating the equity view, which is unusual in late-cycle environments and may reflect the private sector surplus buffer.

**Monetary Policy Transmission:** Private credit rate insensitivity (claim 8) implies the Fed's effective policy rate needs to be higher to achieve equivalent tightening compared to prior cycles. This connects to the "higher for longer" rates debate and explains why the economy has been more resilient than traditional monetary policy models predicted. The fiscal dominance regime (claim 2) further complicates monetary transmission — the fiscal "spigot" partially offsets monetary tightening, requiring the Fed to either accept slower transmission or compensate with more aggressive rate policy.

**Inflation Regimes:** The NKPC convexity (last-mile disinflation requiring disproportionate output sacrifice) intersects with fiscal dominance. If the fiscal deficit prevents sufficient demand destruction to achieve 2% inflation, the Fed faces an impossible trinity: cannot cut (inflation), cannot hike aggressively (fiscal dominance political economy), cannot hold (financial stability costs of higher-for-longer). This maps directly to the stagflationary vs. disinflationary bifurcation.

**Rates-Equity Correlation:** The term premium steepener (claim 5) is the mechanism through which the rates-equity correlation regime is being determined. In a term-premium-driven steepening, the rates-equity correlation tends to be *negative* (higher rates = lower equities, driven by fiscal supply concerns). In an expectations-driven steepening, the correlation tends to be *positive* (higher rates = higher equities, both reflecting stronger growth). Misidentifying the steepener type leads to incorrect correlation assumptions in portfolio construction.

**Fiscal Policy:** The Kalecki profit identity and sectoral balance framework (claim 2) make fiscal policy the master variable for this cycle. The timing and magnitude of potential fiscal withdrawal (DOGE spending cuts, TCJA expiration dynamics, debt ceiling constraints) is the single largest source of macro uncertainty. A fiscal consolidation >2pp GDP would mechanically reduce private surplus, erode corporate profits, and potentially trigger the maturity wall solvency scenario — all simultaneously.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the 60-65% historical base rate for rates market accuracy sourced and robust?** This claim underpins the thesis that the cross-asset divergence will resolve toward the rates view. If the post-2020 rates track record (prolonged inversion without recession) has structurally impaired this base rate, the optimal bet shifts toward the equity narrative.

2. **What is the fiscal consolidation probability distribution and timeline?** DOGE spending cuts, TCJA expiration, and debt ceiling dynamics each have uncertain timing and magnitude. The Kalecki framework is only actionable if we can estimate when and how much fiscal withdrawal occurs. The 2pp GDP threshold for cycle downshift is a heuristic — what is the actual fiscal multiplier in both directions?

3. **Does the private sector surplus distribution matter?** The aggregate 2-3% GDP surplus may be concentrated in the top income decile (via equity/asset gains) while lower deciles run deficits. If so, the aggregate surplus overstates resilience — a equity market correction could erode the surplus rapidly while lower-decile stress is already building.

4. **Can correlation regime shifts be traded, or only hedged?** The stagflationary/disinflationary bifurcation is analytically clear but practically difficult to position for when the probability-weighted expectation is close to 50/50 on which path materializes. What is the cost of holding optionality (straddles, swaptions, correlation swaps) vs. the expected payoff from regime identification?

5. **How quickly could the fiscal dominance regime end?** Political economy suggests deficit reduction is unlikely to be voluntary — it would require either bond market discipline (a "gilt crisis" moment), legislative constraint (debt ceiling), or a forced austerity program. The speed and nature of the exit matters enormously: gradual fiscal consolidation may allow adjustment, while a sudden fiscal cliff would trigger the Kalecki profit collapse → maturity wall solvency → credit cycle acceleration chain.

6. **Is the business cycle framework itself obsolete for this regime?** If fiscal flows dominate private-sector dynamics, NBER-style business cycle dating may misclassify the current environment. The 1946-48 analogue suggests we may need a "fiscal transition" framework rather than a business cycle framework — but this analogue is speculative and requires deeper comparative analysis.

7. **What is the correct cross-asset expression for the "rates are right, equities are wrong" view with hedged stagflationary tail risk?** The naive trade (short equities, long duration) works in the disinflationary scenario but catastrophically fails in the stagflationary scenario. A hedged expression requires explicit correlation regime conditioning — potentially long inflation breakevens + short equity vol + tactical duration, but the carry costs and roll dynamics need quantification.

8. **Does hysteresis invalidate the output gap concept entirely, or merely degrade its real-time measurement?** If demand determines supply through hysteresis effects, then potential GDP is endogenous and the output gap framework is conceptually — not just empirically — flawed. This has profound implications for the NKPC-based disinflation analysis and the Fed's policy rule calibration.
