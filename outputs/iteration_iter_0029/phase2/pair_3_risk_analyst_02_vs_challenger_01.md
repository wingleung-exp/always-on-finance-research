## DISAGREEMENTS

**2. Covenant-lite: fragility or flexibility?**

**3. Private credit opacity: shock absorber or amplifier?**

**5. China corporate credit: largest mispricing or adequately compensated?**

## REFUTED_CLAIMS

**Agent A, Claim 3a — "15-25% probability of a major forced divestiture that materially impairs a large-cap issuer's credit profile over 3 years":**
This number is fabricated. No methodology is provided. The base rate of forced divestitures that materially impair investment-grade credit profiles is effectively zero in the modern antitrust era — AT&T/Baby Bells (1984) didn't impair AT&T's credit. The Google cases may produce behavioral remedies or asset sales, but translating that into a 15-25% probability of credit-material divestiture requires a model that Agent A doesn't offer. Confidence should be ~2/10, not the implicit 6/10 assigned.

**Agent A, Claim 5 — Election cycle mispricing (confidence 5/10, but even that is generous):**
Agent A claims markets are "ignoring" 2026 midterms. Markets don't trade midterm elections because midterms have near-zero base rate of producing credit-relevant policy changes. Agent A acknowledges "midterms historically produce limited market impact" but then argues "current polarization" makes this time different. This is a common analytical error: identifying a plausible mechanism (polarization → policy divergence) without establishing that the mechanism has been activated. The US policy process has structural gridlock features (filibuster, judicial review, executive-legislative friction) that dampen midterm impact on corporate credit regardless of polarization. Agent A's own 5/10 confidence signals this claim is speculative.

**Agent B, Claim 6 — Private credit opacity as "systemic shock ABSORBER":**
The headline framing doesn't survive scrutiny against the KB's evidence. The KB establishes that credit impulse R² collapsed from 0.38 to 0.12 precisely because private credit is excluded from the monitoring apparatus. This isn't "absorption" — it's blindness. Agent B's own caveat ("amplifies sudden liquidity events") contradicts the headline claim. The Japanese life insurance analogy is instructive but misleading: Japanese life insurers operated under regulatory forbearance with no mark-to-market requirement and no retail/semi-liquid redemption pressure. The $300B+ in semi-liquid private credit vehicles has quarterly redemption features that break the analogy. Agent B correctly assigns 4/10 confidence, but the claim as stated ("absorber, not amplifier") is too binary for a mechanism that is regime-dependent.

**Agent B, Claim 1 — "The bearish case for corporate credit has become the consensus view among sophisticated allocators":**
Partially refuted by internal inconsistency. If the bearish case is truly the consensus and positioning is net short, then who is holding the $1.7T of private credit and the $1.3-1.5T of CLOs? Someone is massively long credit at the structural level even if survey respondents report bearish sentiment. The distinction between stated positioning (FMS surveys) and revealed positioning (actual allocations) is crucial. Institutional mandates, insurance regulatory requirements, and pension fund liability-matching force persistent credit longs regardless of macro views. Agent B conflates tactical positioning sentiment with structural credit exposure. The tactical contrarian argument has merit; the claim that "the bearish case is the consensus" overstates it.

**Agent A, Claim 2 — Geopolitical repricing is "in early innings":**
This is asserted without a framework for measuring what "middle innings" or "late innings" would look like. The claim that "markets still treat [fragmentation] as episodic risks" requires evidence about credit spread attribution to geopolitical factors, which Agent A doesn't provide. The academic references (Caldara & Iacoviello, Ahir/Bloom/Furceri) show elevated uncertainty indices, but elevated uncertainty indices coexist with compressed credit spreads — which either means markets are mispricing (Agent A's thesis) or that uncertainty indices don't map linearly to credit risk (the simpler explanation that Agent A doesn't consider).
