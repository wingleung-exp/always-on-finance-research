## ANALOGUE DECOMPOSITION

### Analogue 1: The Greenspan Conundrum (2004-2006)

**Context**: Fed raised Fed Funds from 1.00% to 5.25% in 17 consecutive 25bp hikes over two years. Greenspan testified in February 2005 that the failure of long-term rates to rise was a "conundrum."

**Sequence**: 
- 10Y yield stayed roughly 4.0-4.5% throughout, declining in real terms as break-evens rose
- Goldman FCI showed minimal tightening through the first 200bp of hikes
- Credit spreads compressed (IG CDX ~35bp, HY ~250bp) even as policy rates rose
- Mortgage origination volume remained elevated through 2005
- Housing prices continued rising through mid-2006
- The S&P 500 rose ~20% during the hiking cycle

**Duration**: The broken transmission persisted for approximately 24 months before unwinding catastrophically in 2007-2008.

**Key drivers**: Global savings glut (Bernanke's framing), regulatory arbitrage through structured credit, and the "search for yield" compressing risk premia.

**Resolution**: The eventual transmission was *discontinuous* — when it came, it arrived via credit market seizure (subprime) rather than the orthodox interest-rate channel. The accumulated imbalances from the period of broken transmission made the eventual correction far worse than it would have been had transmission worked normally.

### Analogue 2: Mid-1960s Stop-Go (1966-1970)

**Context**: Fed tightened in 1966 (credit crunch, mini-recession), then eased under fiscal pressure from Vietnam War spending and Great Society programs. Federal deficit expanded from 0.5% to 2.8% of GDP (modest by today's standards but large at the time).

**Sequence**:
- 1966: Fed funds to 5.75%, bond market "massacre," credit crunch
- 1967: Fed eased aggressively, fiscal expansion continued
- CPI accelerated from 1.6% (1965) to 3.4% (1967) to 6.2% (1969)
- Fed tightened again 1968-69, but fiscal impulse partially offset
- Penn Central bankruptcy (1970) demonstrated financial fragility from stop-go
- Required Volcker's 1979-82 extreme tightening to ultimately resolve

**Duration**: The stop-go dynamic lasted roughly 15 years (1966-1981) before decisive resolution.

**Key drivers**: Political pressure, fiscal dominance from war spending, the belief that unemployment was a greater risk than moderate inflation.

**Resolution**: Only resolved through the Volcker shock — accepting a severe recession (unemployment >10%) to re-anchor expectations. The total cost of the stop-go period was enormous: by 1980, real returns on Treasuries had been negative for over a decade.

### Analogue 3: 2018-2019 QT / Repo Crisis

**Context**: Fed began QT in October 2017 at $10B/month, ramping to $50B/month. Total balance sheet reduction ~$650B (from $4.45T to $3.8T). Simultaneously raised Fed Funds to 2.50%.

**Sequence**:
- 2017-2018: QT proceeded smoothly, reserves declined from $2.2T
- Q4 2018: Equity selloff (~20%), but Fed attributed it to rate hikes not QT
- January 2019: Fed pivoted to patience, halted hiking
- September 16, 2019: Repo rate spiked to 10% when reserves hit ~$1.5T
- Fed immediately intervened with repo operations, then began "not-QE" T-bill purchases
- QT formally ended in August 2019 (two months before the repo crisis forced action)

**Duration**: QT lasted ~23 months before reserve scarcity emerged as a binding constraint.

**Key drivers**: Reserves distribute unevenly across the banking system; aggregate reserve levels masked individual bank scarcity. JP Morgan's reserve position and SLR constraints were critical. Tax payment flows (September quarterly) were the proximate trigger.

**Resolution**: The Fed was forced to permanently expand its balance sheet floor. The episode demonstrated that the Fed cannot determine the reserve scarcity threshold *ex ante* — it discovers it by hitting it.

### Analogue 4: 1995-1998 Greenspan Soft Landing

**Context**: Fed hiked 300bp in 1994-95 (3.00% to 6.00%), then cut 75bp in three moves (July-January 1995-96). Economy avoided recession, equity markets surged.

**Sequence**:
- 1994: Bond market rout, Orange County bankruptcy, EM stress (Tequila crisis)
- H1 1995: Growth slowed, manufacturing contracted
- July 1995-January 1996: Three 25bp cuts to 5.25%
- 1996-1998: Real GDP growth accelerated to 4%+, S&P 500 rose ~100%
- Productivity boom (internet adoption) amplified the wealth effect channel
- Asian crisis (1997) and LTCM (1998) created intermittent volatility but didn't derail

**Duration**: The "soft landing" period of mid-cycle easing lasted ~6 months; the subsequent expansion ran 4+ years.

**Key drivers**: Productivity growth genuinely lifted r*, allowing the Fed to hold rates restrictive in nominal terms while the real economy expanded via supply-side improvement. Technology-driven wealth effects amplified consumption through a functioning asset-price channel.

**Resolution**: Ultimately ended with the dot-com bust (2000-2002), suggesting the AI parallel could have a similar terminal dynamic. The soft landing succeeded *during* the technology boom but sowed seeds for the eventual correction.

### Analogue 5: Japan's QE Exit Attempts (2001-2006 / 2024-2026)

**Context**: BoJ accumulated the largest central bank balance sheet relative to GDP in history. First QE (2001-2006): BoJ expanded reserves to ¥30-35T, then unwound over 2006. Current: BoJ holds ¥590T+ in JGBs (~130% of GDP), $450B in equity ETFs.

**Sequence (2001-2006)**:
- March 2001: BoJ launched QE, targeting reserve balances
- 2001-2006: Gradual expansion of reserves, JGB purchases
- March 2006: Ended QE, drained reserves over ~6 months
- July 2006: First rate hike to 0.25%
- CPI briefly turned positive but economy remained fragile
- February 2007: Hiked to 0.50%, then crisis forced reversal

**Sequence (2024-2026)**:
- March 2024: Ended negative rates and YCC
- 2024-2025: Gradual normalization to ~0.50%
- JGB market functioning impaired by BoJ's 50%+ ownership share
- BoJ facing "stock problem" — unwinding positions would crater both JGB and equity markets

**Key drivers**: Balance sheet dominance over market price discovery; the BoJ owns so much of the JGB market that QT creates outsized price impact. The "convexity event" risk — where rising yields trigger leveraged position unwinds.

**Resolution**: The 2006 exit was cut short by the GFC. The current exit remains in early stages. There is no historical precedent for successfully unwinding a central bank balance sheet of this magnitude relative to GDP.

### Critical Differences That May Alter Outcomes

**1. The nature of the transmission break is different from 2004-2006.** In the Conundrum era, the break was in the *term structure* (long rates didn't rise). Today, the break is in the *asset price channel* itself — equities are generating a wealth effect that actively offsets monetary tightening. The KB estimates the AI rally produced $300-750B in wealth effects, exceeding the tightening impulse. This is a more fundamental impairment: in 2004-06, the transmission mechanism was bypassed; today, it is *reversed* in its most powerful channel.

**2. Fiscal arithmetic is qualitatively different from the 1960s.** The deficit at 6.5% of GDP with 125% debt/GDP and net interest approaching 3%+ of GDP creates a self-reinforcing dynamic absent in the 1960s (when debt/GDP was ~40% and falling). The KB's "Fed asymmetric constraint" concept — a ceiling at ~5-5.5% where doom-loop dynamics activate — has no parallel in the 1960s. The stop-go risk is therefore *structurally embedded* rather than the product of political choices.

**3. QT operates at unprecedented scale relative to 2018-19.** The Fed's balance sheet peaked at ~$8.9T vs. $4.45T in 2017. Even after substantial runoff, reserves and the balance sheet are much larger. However, the *distribution* of reserves is likely more concentrated post-SVB (banks hoarding liquidity buffers), meaning the effective scarcity threshold may be *higher* than in 2019 despite larger aggregate reserves. The KB identifies this as a knowledge gap — and it is one that the historical analogue framework cannot resolve, because the structural conditions (post-SVB regulatory posture, bank reserve management under Basel III endgame uncertainty) are genuinely novel.

**4. The technology/productivity parallel to 1995-98 is the most seductive and most dangerous analogue.** The narrative fit is excellent: AI is to 2024-26 what the internet was to 1995-98. But the base rate from this analogue is concerning — the 1995-98 soft landing was followed by the 2000-02 bust, and the total cycle (boom-to-bust) delivered negative real returns for investors who entered near the late-cycle peak. Moreover, the 1995-98 Fed faced neither fiscal dominance nor broken transmission — the traditional toolkit was fully operational.

**5. Japan's experience is structurally cautionary but poor as a point analogue.** The BoJ's challenges stem from a balance sheet 2-3x the relative size of the Fed's, in an economy with deflationary not inflationary biases. The relevant lesson is narrower: central banks that accumulate massive balance sheets *never* fully normalize them. The Fed's balance sheet floor will be permanently elevated relative to pre-QE levels, and QT will end not when the balance sheet reaches an "optimal" size but when reserve distribution triggers stress.
