# Rates-Equity Correlation — Credit Cycle Analyst (credit_analyst_01) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **IG credit spreads are the single most informative real-time diagnostic for the stock-bond correlation regime, and their current tightness (~90-110bp OAS) represents the most dangerous cross-asset inconsistency in the framework.** IG OAS at or near cycle tights prices a near-zero recession probability and implicitly assumes durable negative stock-bond correlation (bonds as hedge, low required risk premium). Yet the correlation regime is empirically unstable and oscillating. The KB correctly identifies IG spreads widening >75bp from trough as the top-ranked leading indicator for correlation flips (18-month lead, 6/7 hit rate). The actionable point is that current IG tights simultaneously validate the current regime AND maximize the signal value of any widening from here.

2. **The credit spread decomposition reveals a structural anomaly: the non-default component of HY spreads has compressed to ~60-65% of total OAS (vs. historical 70-80%), implying that the risk appetite / liquidity / technical compression is near exhaustion while the default component remains stable.** Decomposing HY OAS (~310-370bp as of early 2026) into its constituent parts — expected default losses (~120-140bp based on Moody's trailing and forward default rates), recovery uncertainty (~30-40bp), and the residual non-default component (liquidity premium, risk appetite, supply/demand technicals at ~140-180bp) — shows the non-default component has collapsed from its typical ~200-280bp range during normal mid-cycle conditions to near the lower bound. This means further spread tightening requires either (a) even lower default expectations or (b) unprecedented risk appetite. Neither is sustainable. The risk is asymmetrically skewed toward widening.

3. **The credit cycle is in late-mid cycle, not early-late cycle, and the standard credit-to-correlation transmission mechanism is being delayed by 12-24 months due to three structural distortions: covenant-lite dominance (>90% of leveraged loans), EBITDA addbacks (15-40%), and private credit opacity ($1.7-1.8T mark-to-model).** These distortions do not eliminate the credit cycle — they extend it and compress the eventual recognition phase. Historically, the credit cycle has led the correlation regime flip by 12-18 months (IG widening precedes the macro deterioration that triggers demand shocks and negative correlation). The current structural distortions mean this lead time may extend to 24-36 months, but the eventual correction will be sharper and more synchronized. True leverage in the leveraged loan universe is 7.5-8.5x (vs. reported ~6x), and this hidden leverage will amplify the spread widening when it materializes.

4. **The CLO OC test mechanism creates a discrete, non-linear contagion channel from credit markets to the rates-equity correlation regime.** The 7.5% CCC-bucket overcollateralization test in CLO structures (current utilization 4-6.5%) is one downgrade wave away from breach. Breach would trigger: cash flow diversion from equity tranches → CLO equity destruction → forced loan selling → new CLO formation shutdown → removal of ~65-70% of leveraged loan demand → spread widening cascade → banking channel stress. This is the credit market's equivalent of the Treasury basis trade fragility — a leverage structure with no historical precedent at current scale (~$1.0-1.1T CLO market) during a correlation regime transition. Critically, a CLO OC breach would produce simultaneous credit, equity, and duration stress — mechanically forcing positive stock-bond correlation through the deleveraging channel.

5. **The $900B-$1.5T sub-investment-grade maturity wall (2025-2028) is a time-stamped, foreseeable catalyst for credit cycle deterioration that interacts with the correlation regime through two distinct channels: the fundamental earnings channel (ROIC-WACC compression via refinancing at 200-400bp higher costs) and the market structure channel (forced selling, rating migration, fallen angel risk).** The maturity wall's interaction with the correlation regime is asymmetric: orderly refinancing is correlation-neutral, but any disruption (recession coincidence, spread blowout, CLO market shutdown) would produce a sharp positive correlation spike as credit, equity, and rates sell off simultaneously. The base rate for maturity walls producing systemic stress is 0/4 without recession coincidence and 2/2 with recession coincidence. This conditionality is the critical credit cycle input to the correlation regime forecast.

6. **The term premium / credit premium divergence (ACM term premium +80-130bp adjusted vs. HY OAS near cycle tights at ~310-370bp) is an internal market inconsistency that has historically resolved via credit widening in 2 of 3 prior episodes (2013 Taper Tantrum, 2018 Q4, 2023 Q3).** The rates market is pricing fiscal supply risk and elevated inflation uncertainty (term premium rebuilding). The credit market is pricing a benign fundamental outlook with no recession and ample liquidity. These cannot both be correct over a 12-24 month horizon. If the term premium is right (rates market pricing positive correlation risk), HY OAS should be 100-150bp wider (~420-520bp) to reflect the correlation-adjusted risk premium. If credit is right (fundamentals benign), the term premium should compress back toward 30-50bp. The KB's three-way cross-asset disagreement concept captures this at the macro level, but from a credit perspective, the resolution pathway matters: 2/3 historical analogues resolved via credit widening, which itself is the #1 signpost for correlation regime flips.

7. **The Kalecki-Levy profit identity is the structural explanation for why the credit cycle has been able to sustain tight spreads despite restrictive monetary policy, but the identity operates on aggregate profits and provides zero protection to firm-level cash flow distress.** Fiscal deficits at 6-7% GDP channel ~$1.5-1.8T annually into aggregate corporate profits via the sectoral balance identity. This supports IG and upper-HY cash flows and keeps aggregate default rates suppressed. But the identity is quality-differentiated: a B-rated borrower at 8x true leverage refinancing at 400bp higher does not benefit from aggregate demand floors. The credit quality distribution is therefore wider than in any prior cycle — aggregate metrics (overall HY default rate, average OAS) look healthy while the tail is deteriorating. Distressed ratio (HY trading >1000bp) at ~4-6% is at the upper end of expansion-phase readings and rising, even as aggregate spreads are tight.

8. **Private credit's growth to $1.7-1.8T AUM has structurally degraded the credit spread signal's reliability as a correlation regime indicator.** Mark-to-model NAVs (autocorrelation 0.5-0.7 vs. ~0.05 for public markets) suppress measured volatility by 2-3x and delay the recognition of stress by 12-18 months. PIK toggle usage rising from ~2% to 5-8% is a stress concealment mechanism. This means the #1 correlation signpost (IG widening >75bp from trough) may trigger later and more abruptly than in prior cycles, compressing the early warning window from the historical 2-5 months to potentially weeks. The implication for the correlation regime is that private credit opacity extends the period of apparent stability (supporting the narrative that tight spreads validate benign fundamentals) but amplifies the eventual repricing.

9. **Credit cycle positioning provides a falsifiable test for the correlation regime forecast: if the next 12 months see (a) IG OAS widen >75bp from trough to ~165-185bp, (b) the HY-IG differential exceeds 350bp (currently ~220-260bp), and (c) the distressed ratio exceeds 10%, then the probability of correlation regime flip to sustainably negative rises from the current ~30-35% to ~60-70%.** This is because the credit market's transmission to the real economy (via tighter lending standards, reduced issuance, rising funding costs) creates the demand shock that is the fundamental driver of negative stock-bond correlation. Conversely, if credit remains tight (IG <120bp, distressed ratio <6%), the correlation regime will continue to oscillate in the transitional zone, unable to resolve because the demand shock catalyst is absent.

10. **The fallen angel channel (IG-to-HY downgrades) is an underappreciated amplifier of correlation regime transitions.** Rating migration trends show upgrade/downgrade ratios deteriorating from ~1.5x (early 2024) to ~0.9-1.0x (early 2026). If a recession or spread blowout forces a wave of fallen angels (estimated $200-400B of BBB debt at risk based on leverage ratios and interest coverage deterioration), the mechanical selling by IG-mandated investors and the absorption challenge for HY/CLO structures would produce simultaneous credit, equity, and duration stress. Each major fallen angel wave (2002, 2008-09, 2020) coincided with sharp negative stock-bond correlation as the Fed responded to the demand destruction. The fallen angel channel is the credit market's mechanism for converting gradual credit deterioration into a discrete correlation-flipping event.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: IG Spreads as Correlation Diagnostic
- The KB's `correlation_signpost_hierarchy` places IG credit spreads widening >75bp from trough as the #1 leading indicator (18-month lead, 6/7 hit rate across 13 post-1960 transitions).
- Current IG OAS at ~90-110bp implies a benign credit outlook inconsistent with ACM term premium at 80-130bp.
- The credit market's institutional structure — IG investors are the most sophisticated, information-efficient segment — makes this signal high-quality.
- Only Tier 2b (yield curve un-inversion) is currently active in the signpost hierarchy. Credit has not yet flagged.
- **The absence of credit market stress is the single strongest evidence against an imminent correlation flip.**

### Claim 2: Spread Decomposition
- HY default rate forecasts (Moody's trailing 12M at ~3.0-3.5%, forward 12M at ~3.5-4.5%) imply ~120-140bp of expected default loss embedded in spreads.
- Recovery rate assumptions have declined from historical ~60-70 cents to ~40-50 cents for leveraged loans (covenant-lite effect), adding ~20-30bp to the default loss component.
- The residual non-default component (~140-180bp) is at the lower bound of the post-GFC range, implying risk appetite exhaustion.
- Historical analogues: non-default component at similar levels preceded the 2007 (compressed to ~150bp before blowing out to 1000bp+) and 2018 Q4 (compressed before partial reversal) episodes.
- The asymmetric risk profile (limited further tightening potential, significant widening potential) aligns with the KB's finding that the transitional correlation regime is vulnerable to sudden shifts.

### Claim 3: Credit Cycle Positioning (Late-Mid Cycle)
- **Supporting late-mid (not late) cycle:**
  - New issuance quality: leveraged loan issuance remains robust at ~$300-400B annually, but repricing activity (extending maturities at lower spreads) has dominated true new-money issuance — a mid-cycle pattern.
  - Leverage ratios by rating cohort: BB leverage stable at ~3.5-4.0x; B leverage rising from ~5.5x to ~6.5x (reported, ~7.5-8.5x true); CCC leverage at ~8-10x (reported).
  - Interest coverage ratios: aggregate ICR stable at ~3.0-3.5x, but B-rated ICR declining from ~2.5x to ~2.0x, approaching the 1.5x distress threshold.
  - Rating migration: upgrade/downgrade ratio deteriorating from ~1.5x to ~0.9-1.0x but not yet inverted (inversion is a late-cycle signal).
- **Structural delay mechanisms:**
  - Covenant-lite (>90%) eliminates maintenance tests that historically forced early distress recognition. In the pre-covenant-lite era, leverage covenant breaches at 5.5-6.0x forced restructuring conversations. Now, borrowers can operate at 8x+ without triggering any covenant.
  - EBITDA addbacks of 15-40% inflate reported metrics. ECB and Fed analyses confirm true leverage is ~1.5-2.0x higher than reported.
  - Private credit ($1.7-1.8T) absorbs distressed borrowers through amend-and-extend, delaying public market recognition.
- **Credit cycle lead time to correlation:** historical 12-18 months, extending to 24-36 months under current structural distortions.

### Claim 4: CLO OC Test Contagion
- CLO structures have contractually specified 7.5% CCC-bucket OC tests. Current utilization at 4-6.5% means one coordinated downgrade wave (e.g., 5-10 large issuers) could breach the threshold.
- Breach triggers are non-linear and contractual — not discretionary. Cash flow diversion is automatic.
- CLOs represent ~65-70% of leveraged loan demand. Shutdown of new CLO formation would remove the marginal buyer, repricing the entire $1.3T+ leveraged loan market.
- The CLO market at ~$1.0-1.1T has never been tested through a full cycle at this scale. The 2020 near-breach was contaminated by $1.6T of Fed emergency intervention.
- CLO AAA spreads have a secondary connection to the rates-equity correlation through the BOJ normalization channel: Japanese institutional demand for CLO AAAs adds a cross-border vector (identified in KB's `boj_normalization_shock`).

### Claim 5: Maturity Wall Interaction with Correlation Regime
- $900B-$1.5T sub-IG debt maturing 2025-2028 at contractually determined dates.
- B-rated borrowers face 200-400bp higher all-in refinancing costs (from ~4-5% to ~7-9%).
- Base rate evidence: 2/2 maturity walls coinciding with recession produced systemic outcomes; 0/4 without recession were orderly. The recession question IS the correlation question.
- The maturity wall peaks in 2025-2026 for HY bonds and 2026-2027 for leveraged loans — this is the window of maximum vulnerability.
- If the maturity wall coincides with a growth scare (which the signpost hierarchy would detect through the credit channel), the resulting default wave would create the demand shock that flips correlation negative.
- KB concept `corporate_debt_maturity_wall` estimates ~$50-75B incremental annual interest expense for the broader corporate sector. For the leveraged universe specifically, the impact is more concentrated: ~$25-40B of incremental interest expense on ~$900B-1.5T of maturing debt, representing a 20-30% cash interest increase for the median B-rated issuer.

### Claim 6: Term Premium / Credit Premium Divergence
- ACM term premium at +80-130bp (adjusted) reflects the rates market pricing fiscal supply risk, duration uncertainty, and inflation persistence.
- HY OAS at ~310-370bp reflects the credit market pricing benign fundamentals, ample liquidity, and a favorable refinancing environment.
- Three prior episodes of similar divergence:
  - **2013 Taper Tantrum:** term premium spiked ~100bp; HY OAS widened ~100bp with 2-3 month lag; SPX pulled back ~5-6%.
  - **2018 Q4:** term premium volatile; HY OAS widened ~200bp; SPX fell ~20%. Resolution via credit widening.
  - **2023 Q3:** 30Y yields hit ~5.0%; HY OAS widened ~50-75bp before Fed pivot narrative compressed both.
- In 2/3 cases, resolution came via credit widening (not term premium compression), confirming that credit is the more likely adjustment leg.
- The Kalecki identity provides a structural explanation for why this divergence can persist longer than pure financial logic suggests: fiscal deficits simultaneously support corporate profits (keeping spreads tight) and increase Treasury supply (pushing term premium higher). The question is whether the Kalecki support is durable or whether it merely extends the divergence before a sharper resolution.

### Claim 7: Kalecki Quality Differentiation
- The Kalecki-Levy identity (Profits ≡ Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports − Household Savings) is accounting arithmetic, not theory.
- At 6-7% GDP fiscal deficit, ~$1.5-1.8T flows to aggregate corporate profits annually.
- But the identity operates on aggregate, not firm-level, cash flows. This creates a quality bifurcation:
  - **IG / upper HY:** direct Kalecki beneficiaries. Top-line growth from fiscal stimulus, manageable leverage, refinancing at affordable costs. Default rates near zero.
  - **B/CCC / leveraged loans:** Kalecki floor is irrelevant at firm level. A company at 8x true leverage with interest expense consuming 70-80% of actual cash EBITDA cannot survive a refinancing at 400bp higher regardless of aggregate profit growth.
- The distressed ratio (HY >1000bp) at ~4-6% is elevated relative to aggregate spread tightness, confirming the quality bifurcation.
- This quality bifurcation maps directly onto the KB's `equity_concentration_correlation_illusion`: mega-cap equities (net cash, Kalecki beneficiaries) mask the stress in the broader leveraged universe, just as tight aggregate HY OAS masks the tail deterioration.

### Claim 8: Private Credit Opacity
- Private credit AUM at $1.7-1.8T (from ~$200B a decade ago) has absorbed a significant share of distressed and lower-quality credit that would previously have shown up in public HY and leveraged loan markets.
- Mark-to-model NAVs with autocorrelation of 0.5-0.7 (vs. ~0.05 for public markets) mean that measured private credit volatility is suppressed by 2-3x.
- PIK toggle usage (rising from ~2% to 5-8%) allows borrowers to defer cash interest — a stress concealment mechanism that delays default recognition by 12-18 months.
- The credit impulse R-squared (credit conditions → real economic activity) has declined from 0.38 to 0.12, partially attributable to private credit growth.
- **Implication for the correlation signpost hierarchy:** The IG widening signal (Tier 1, 18-month lead) should still function because IG markets remain fully public and mark-to-market. But the secondary signals (default rates, distressed ratios, HY OAS levels) are now contaminated by private credit opacity, reducing their lead time and increasing the probability of sudden, rather than gradual, spread widening.

### Claim 9: Falsifiable Credit-Based Correlation Test
- **Condition A (IG OAS >165-185bp):** IG widening >75bp from trough is the #1 signpost. Current ~90-110bp + 75bp = 165-185bp threshold.
- **Condition B (HY-IG differential >350bp):** The HY-IG spread differential is a pure risk appetite gauge, stripping out the duration/rates component. Current ~220-260bp; >350bp signals genuine credit discrimination and flight-to-quality within credit. This level was breached in every recession since 1990.
- **Condition C (distressed ratio >10%):** The percentage of HY trading >1000bp OAS above 10% historically precedes default wave onset by 6-12 months and indicates sufficient stress in the tail to produce meaningful demand destruction.
- **All three conditions met simultaneously → 60-70% probability of demand shock sufficient to flip correlation negative.** The logic: credit tightening → lending standards tighten → capex/hiring decelerate → growth scare materializes → Fed responds → negative correlation reasserts.
- **None of the three conditions currently met → the credit cycle is not yet producing the demand shock catalyst that historical base rates require for a durable correlation flip.**

### Claim 10: Fallen Angel Channel
- BBB-rated debt represents ~50% of IG outstanding (~$4-4.5T). Of this, an estimated $200-400B sits in the BBB- tranche with leverage and coverage ratios that would warrant BB or lower ratings in a stressed environment.
- IG-mandated investors (insurance companies, pension funds, index-tracking funds) face mechanical selling requirements on downgrade to HY. Estimated forced selling volume: 30-50% of the fallen angel notional within 3-6 months of downgrade.
- HY index absorption capacity: the HY market at ~$1.3-1.5T can absorb ~$50-100B of fallen angels before spread dislocation; beyond that, spread widening becomes non-linear.
- Historical fallen angel waves: 2002 (WorldCom, ~$60B), 2005 (GM/Ford, ~$80B), 2020 (~$200B+). Each produced significant market stress and coincided with negative stock-bond correlation episodes.
- The fallen angel channel converts gradual fundamental deterioration (slow leverage creep, ICR erosion) into a discrete, observable event that triggers the correlation signpost hierarchy.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Monetary Policy Transmission & Central Bank Strategy
- **The credit cycle is the primary transmission mechanism for monetary policy to the real economy.** Broken monetary transmission (KB: interest rate channel at ~60% effectiveness) manifests most clearly in credit markets through the mortgage lock-in effect (reducing the rate channel) and covenant-lite structures (delaying the credit channel).
- **The Fed's effective Taylor Rule coefficient (φ_π ~1.0-1.2) has direct implications for the credit cycle.** A lower-than-textbook coefficient means the Fed is less likely to tighten sufficiently to trigger a credit cycle turn, extending the period of tight spreads and delaying the correlation signpost trigger.
- **Forward guidance asymmetric attenuation** (KB: dovish guidance losing ~50% potency under positive correlation) has credit market implications: when the Fed eventually cuts, the expected credit market rally may be muted if cuts are interpreted as validating inflation concerns rather than supporting risk appetite. This would keep the IG OAS widening signpost from being reversed by Fed easing.

### Inflation Regime Identification & Dynamics
- **Services inflation persistence** is the primary inflation variable for the credit cycle because it determines the Fed's ability to cut in response to credit stress. Core services CPI at ~3.5-4.0% constrains the Fed's reaction function, creating the conditions for a "credit cycle without a Fed put" — the most dangerous configuration for the correlation regime.
- **Phillips Curve convexity near the zero output gap** (KB concept) amplifies credit cycle dynamics: small negative demand shocks produce outsized credit spread widening because the economy is at maximum sensitivity. This is why the maturity wall + growth scare combination is so potent.
- **Inflation expectations anchoring** is the credit market's free option: as long as expectations remain anchored (5Y breakevens at 2.3-2.5%), the Fed can eventually cut to support credit if needed. De-anchoring would remove this option, locking in a positive correlation regime through the credit channel (Fed unable to cut → credit cycle turns → defaults rise → but correlation stays positive because the Fed is trapped).

### Volatility Regime Analysis
- **Credit spread volatility is lower-frequency than equity or rates vol** but transmits through the same structural amplifiers (vol-targeting, risk parity, CLO structures). A vol regime shift in credit (from ~40-60bp annualized IG OAS vol to ~120-180bp during stress) would amplify the correlation transition.
- **VIX-MOVE correlation** typically rises during credit stress episodes, producing the positive stock-bond correlation that the KB's framework predicts. The credit market is the intermediating variable: credit stress → VIX rises AND MOVE rises → positive correlation.
- **Implied credit vol** (CDX option market) is currently near cycle lows, creating the same asymmetric risk profile as the spread decomposition analysis: limited further compression, significant widening potential.

### Equity Market Cycles & Valuation Regimes
- **The mega-cap / leveraged credit bifurcation** (iter_0035 finding) is the equity-credit bridge. The S&P 500 at 21-22x forward P/E is primarily a mega-cap net-cash story. The S&P 493 at ~15-17x forward P/E is the leveraged credit universe's equity sibling, and this segment is already pricing closer to positive correlation assumptions.
- **Earnings quality rotation** (KB: `correlation_earnings_quality_rotation`) has a credit cycle explanation: positive correlation regimes punish low-quality earnings because discount rate volatility exposes firms with weak cash conversion, which are precisely the firms with the highest credit risk. The quality factor is a cross-asset arbitrage of credit cycle information.
- **The ROIC-WACC compression** (KB: ~3.5-5.0pp, tightest since 2007-08) is the equity market's version of the credit spread compression: both reflect the same underlying dynamic (tight financial conditions met by resilient aggregate earnings from the Kalecki channel), and both carry the same asymmetric risk profile.

### Fiscal Policy
- **The Kalecki-Levy identity is the bridge between fiscal policy and the credit cycle.** Fiscal deficits at 6-7% GDP sustain aggregate corporate profits, keeping aggregate default rates suppressed and IG/upper-HY spreads tight. This is the credit market's version of the "corridor of instability" — fiscal support prevents the credit cycle from turning (which would trigger the correlation signpost), but fiscal supply pressure on term premium keeps the rates market pricing a different regime.
- **Fiscal consolidation is the only pathway that would simultaneously resolve the term premium / credit premium divergence AND allow the credit cycle to run its natural course.** But the KB correctly identifies that fiscal consolidation is politically unavailable through at least 2028.

## Open Questions

1. **Has private credit absorption ($1.7-1.8T) permanently degraded the IG OAS signal's lead time, or merely delayed it?** If the signal is delayed but still valid, the 18-month lead time may extend to 24-36 months, still useful for portfolio construction. If the signal is permanently degraded (false positives/negatives from private credit distorting the public market), the entire correlation signpost hierarchy needs recalibration.

2. **What is the actual CCC-bucket utilization across the CLO universe as of March 2026?** This requires Intex/LCD data and is the single most operationally critical data point for the CLO contagion channel. The difference between 4% and 6.5% utilization is the difference between 2-3 downgrades away from breach and 1 downgrade wave away.

3. **Does the SEC central clearing mandate for Treasuries (expected Q2 2026) interact with the credit cycle transmission mechanism?** Central clearing could force position adjustment in the $800B-$1T basis trade, which would affect repo markets, which would affect dealer willingness to intermediate credit markets. This is a second-order credit-to-correlation channel that has not been analyzed.

4. **Can a maturity wall produce systemic outcomes WITHOUT a coincident recession?** The 0/4 base rate (orderly without recession) may not hold if: (a) the wall is larger than prior episodes, (b) private credit has absorbed the marginal quality borrowers, or (c) CLO structures amplify the stress. This is the critical conditional for whether the maturity wall is merely a headwind or a catalyst.

5. **What is the threshold credit quality above which the Kalecki aggregate profit floor becomes irrelevant?** If the threshold is B+, the at-risk population is ~$500-700B of leveraged loans and HY bonds. If the threshold is BB-, the at-risk population shrinks to ~$200-300B. This boundary condition determines whether the default cycle produces sufficient demand destruction to trigger the correlation signpost.

6. **How quickly can the fallen angel channel convert from gradual deterioration to a discrete event?** The rating agencies' typical 6-12 month review cycle creates a lag between fundamental deterioration and downgrade action. If the agencies accelerate (as they did in March 2020), the forced selling wave compresses into weeks rather than months, amplifying the correlation transition.

7. **Is the credit market's pricing of the term premium / credit premium divergence rational or complacent?** The Kalecki identity provides a structural justification for tight spreads despite elevated term premium. If the Kalecki channel is truly durable, the divergence can persist for years and the credit signpost may never trigger. This is the most important unresolved question for the entire correlation framework: is the credit market correctly pricing a novel structural support, or is it repeating the same complacency that preceded 2007 (when credit was "too tight" for 2-3 years before the correction)?

8. **What is the cross-border CLO AAA demand sensitivity to yen carry unwind?** Japanese institutional demand for CLO AAAs creates a secondary channel connecting BOJ normalization to US leveraged credit markets. If this channel is material (~$100-150B of Japanese CLO AAA holdings), then BOJ tightening simultaneously affects both the rates-equity correlation (through Treasury selling) and the credit cycle (through reduced CLO demand).
