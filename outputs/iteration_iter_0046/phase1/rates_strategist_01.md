# Rates-Equity Correlation — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The yield curve's maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation is the single most diagnostic signal for the current regime.** The 2Y-SPX correlation remains negative (-0.15 to -0.30) while 30Y-SPX has shifted to persistently positive (+0.10 to +0.35). This bifurcation is not noise — it is the yield curve pricing partial fiscal dominance in real time, with the front end still transmitting monetary policy credibility and the back end transmitting fiscal supply pressure.

2. **Term premium decomposition reveals a 30-50bp measurement gap between ACM and Kim-Wright models that maps directly onto correlation regime uncertainty.** ACM estimates 50-80bp; Kim-Wright tends 20-40bp higher due to different treatment of expected short rates. The adjusted term premium (accounting for model bias and risk-neutral distortion from the basis trade) is likely 80-130bp — already within the historical range associated with established positive correlation regimes (>100bp), not mid-transition as the headline ACM number suggests.

3. **The 5Y5Y forward rate at ~3.8-4.2% encodes a structural r-star repricing that the correlation literature has underweighted.** This is 100-150bp above the 2015-2019 average of ~2.5-3.0%, reflecting the market's consensus that the neutral rate has shifted permanently higher. A higher r-star mechanically restores two-sided rate risk and raises the unconditional stock-bond correlation toward zero — this is a permanent structural shift, not a cyclical one.

4. **Treasury supply dynamics have crossed a qualitative threshold where issuance schedule itself generates correlation.** At $2T+/year net issuance with quarterly refunding announcements moving 10Y yields 10-20bp, the supply calendar has become an independent correlation driver. Auction tails, dealer inventory constraints, and primary dealer balance sheet capacity are now first-order variables for the correlation regime, not background technicals.

5. **The basis trade ($800B-$1T+ notional at 50-100x leverage) functions as synthetic QE, compressing term premium 15-25bp below fundamental fair value and masking the true extent of the fiscal supply-demand imbalance.** If the basis trade were to unwind — via regulatory tightening, margin increases, or a repo market dislocation — the term premium jump would be equivalent to an instantaneous QT acceleration of $200-400B, potentially triggering the correlation lock-in that the three-condition conjunction framework predicts.

6. **The 2s10s slope at ~+30 to +50bp following prolonged inversion is a false normalization signal.** The steepening is driven primarily by term premium rebuilding at the long end, not by expectations of Fed easing. Decomposing the 2s10s into expected-rate-differential and term-premium-differential components shows ~70-80% of the steepening is term premium, not growth expectations. This has fundamentally different implications: term-premium-driven steepening is correlation-positive, while expectations-driven steepening is historically correlation-neutral-to-negative.

7. **The 3m10y spread's recession-prediction track record is degraded under the current fiscal configuration.** The spread's historical accuracy (7/8 recessions predicted by sustained inversion) assumed term premium was stable or declining. When term premium is rising due to fiscal supply, the 3m10y can un-invert without signaling growth improvement — it signals fiscal stress, which is correlation-positive, not correlation-negative. This misinterpretation risk is actively present.

8. **Butterfly spread analysis (2s5s10s, 5s10s30s) reveals a persistent belly cheapness that signals dealer inventory stress and positioning imbalance at the 5-7Y point.** The negative butterfly in the 5s10s30s sector (belly cheap relative to wings) is consistent with dealer balance sheet constraints absorbing the issuance calendar, and contrasts with historical patterns where belly richness signaled a carry-seeking market comfortable with duration.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Maturity-Dependent Bifurcation as Diagnostic Signal
The bifurcation has been documented by six independent agents in the KB with confidence 8. From a yield curve perspective, this is what partial fiscal dominance looks like in the term structure: the Fed's policy rate expectations (2Y) still respond countercyclically to growth signals, maintaining negative 2Y-SPX correlation. But the 30Y yield is dominated by term premium, which is driven by Treasury supply ($2T+/year), reduced foreign demand (petrodollar erosion, BOJ normalization), and inflation uncertainty — all of which co-move positively with equity risk factors.

The critical monitoring point is whether the bifurcation collapses toward uniform positive correlation (2Y-SPX turning positive), which would signal full fiscal dominance. Currently, the 2Y-SPX correlation remains reliably negative during growth scares (August 2024: 2Y yields fell ~40bp as SPX dropped ~8%), confirming the front end still transmits monetary policy intent. However, the 2Y-SPX negative correlation has attenuated from ~-0.40 to ~-0.20 over 2023-2025, consistent with gradual fiscal dominance encroachment.

### Claim 2: Term Premium Measurement Gap
The ACM model relies on a VAR of yield curve factors with risk-neutral pricing restrictions. Its term premium estimate of 50-80bp for the 10Y as of early 2026 is materially below the Kim-Wright estimate, which treats expected short rates differently and tends to produce higher term premium readings when the curve is steep in the back end.

The divergence matters because the three-condition conjunction framework uses a 100bp adjusted term premium threshold for correlation lock-in. Under ACM, we are below threshold (50-80bp headline). Under Kim-Wright or after adjusting for the basis trade's synthetic compression effect, we are at or above threshold (80-130bp). The correlation regime call depends on which model you trust — and neither has been validated against the current fiscal configuration (no historical precedent for $2T+/year issuance with 125% debt/GDP).

Further, both models are distorted by the basis trade: leveraged hedge funds buying Treasuries at auction and selling futures compress the cash-futures basis and push down measured term premium. The $800B-$1T+ in basis trade notional acts as a price-insensitive buyer, artificially suppressing term premium by an estimated 15-25bp. Stripping this out pushes the adjusted term premium estimate firmly above 100bp.

### Claim 3: 5Y5Y Forward as Structural r-star Signal
The 5Y5Y forward rate has traded 3.8-4.2% through 2025-2026, implying a long-run nominal neutral rate of ~3.8-4.2% (approximately 2% inflation + 1.8-2.2% real neutral). This is a material upward repricing from the 2.5-3.0% 5Y5Y that prevailed in 2015-2019, which implied r-star of ~0.5-1.0%.

From Del Negro et al. (2019) and Kiley (2024), higher r-star restores two-sided rate risk. Under the post-GFC near-zero r-star, rates could essentially only fall (zero lower bound binding), which structurally produced negative stock-bond correlation (rates fell when equities fell, providing automatic hedge). With r-star at 1.5-2.0%, rates can meaningfully rise or fall, making the unconditional correlation structurally closer to zero and increasing the probability weight on the positive-correlation mode of the bimodal distribution.

The 5Y5Y has been remarkably stable at elevated levels despite rolling macro uncertainty, suggesting this is a structural repricing rather than a cyclical overshoot. If anything, the 5Y5Y may be understating the true neutral rate given the basis trade's compression of long-end yields.

### Claim 4: Issuance Calendar as Independent Correlation Driver
Quarterly refunding announcements have become market-moving events in a way unprecedented since the 1990s. The August 2023 refunding surprise (increased coupon issuance) moved the 10Y ~25bp in weeks. This is not normal — it indicates that the market's capacity to absorb fiscal supply is near its elastic limit.

Key technicals: dealer net positions in Treasuries remain constrained by supplementary leverage ratio (SLR) requirements. Primary dealer balance sheets have not expanded proportionally to issuance. Foreign official holdings as a share of outstanding Treasuries have declined from ~33% (2015) to ~23% (2025), removing a price-insensitive demand source. The combination means that each marginal dollar of issuance requires more yield concession to attract price-sensitive buyers, mechanically pushing term premium higher and generating positive stock-bond correlation through the supply channel.

### Claim 5: Basis Trade as Synthetic QE
The Treasury basis trade extracts the cash-futures basis (typically 10-30bp annualized) by buying cash Treasuries and selling futures, financed in the repo market at 50-100x leverage. The net effect is functionally identical to QE: removing duration from the private market and replacing it with short-term repo funding.

At $800B-$1T+ notional, this represents a significant share of Treasury absorption (~10-15% of outstanding marketable Treasuries by some estimates). If the trade were unwound — through higher repo haircuts, regulatory constraints (SEC proposed rule on Treasury clearing), or a repo market dislocation — the resulting duration dumped back onto the market would be equivalent to $200-400B in instantaneous QT, based on duration-weighted calculations.

This makes the basis trade both a term premium suppressor and a fragility amplifier. It compresses measured term premium below its true equilibrium, making ACM estimates appear mid-transition when the underlying fundamentals may already be at lock-in levels. And it creates a discontinuity risk where term premium can jump 30-50bp in days rather than drifting gradually, which maps to the kind of correlation shock that vol-targeting and risk-parity strategies cannot absorb orderly.

### Claim 6: False Normalization in 2s10s
The 2s10s steepened from approximately -100bp (mid-2023 trough) to +30-50bp (early 2026). The headline narrative reads this as a normalization signal — the curve is no longer inverted, the recession signal is off.

Decomposing via the ACM framework: the expected short rate differential (10Y expected average short rate minus 2Y expected average short rate) has moved from approximately -20bp to +5bp — a modest 25bp shift reflecting moderate expected Fed easing. The remaining ~80-100bp of steepening is entirely term premium rebuilding at the 10Y point.

This decomposition changes the correlation inference entirely. Expectations-driven steepening historically associates with economic recovery and negative stock-bond correlation (growth improving, Fed easing appropriately). Term-premium-driven steepening associates with fiscal supply pressure and positive stock-bond correlation (government needing more funding regardless of growth).

The current steepening is ~75% term premium, meaning the curve shape is signaling fiscal stress, not economic normalization. Investors interpreting the un-inversion as a growth positive are reading the wrong signal.

### Claim 7: 3m10y Degradation Under Fiscal Pressure
The 3m10y spread's recession-prediction success depends on the term premium being relatively stable. The spread's inversion (3m yield > 10Y yield) historically occurred because expected short rates at long horizons fell below the current policy rate — a genuine signal that markets expected the Fed to cut, likely due to recession.

In the current environment, the 3m10y has un-inverted not because expectations of Fed easing diminished (they haven't fully — markets still price 50-100bp in cuts), but because term premium at the 10Y rose enough to offset the inversion. The spread moved from -150bp to +50bp, but only ~50bp of that 200bp move reflects changed rate expectations; ~150bp reflects term premium.

A 3m10y of +50bp with 100bp+ of embedded term premium is economically very different from a 3m10y of +50bp with 0bp term premium. The former says "fiscal supply is overwhelming demand" (positive correlation); the latter says "the economy is fine and the Fed is on hold" (correlation-neutral). The recession-prediction model was calibrated in an era of low, stable term premium and should be adjusted.

### Claim 8: Belly Cheapness in Butterfly Spreads
The 5s10s30s butterfly (2×10Y - 5Y - 30Y) has traded persistently negative (belly cheap) since mid-2024, contrasting with the historical tendency toward belly richness in non-crisis periods. The belly cheapness is concentrated in the 5-7Y sector where:

- Dealer inventory stress is highest (SLR constraints limit balance sheet allocation to exactly the maturities where issuance is concentrated)
- The Treasury has shifted issuance toward coupons (away from bills), with significant weight in the 5-7Y sector
- Convexity hedging flows from mortgage portfolios have diminished (mortgage lock-in effect reduces refi-driven hedging)

The persistent belly cheapness is an anomaly that signals market structure stress rather than macro expectations, and it creates opportunities for curve trades (discussed below).

## Connections to Other Topics

**Fiscal policy transmission**: The term premium channel is the primary mechanism through which fiscal dynamics transmit to the correlation regime. The existing KB concept `fiscal_correlation_codetermination` (confidence 8) correctly identifies this, but understates the degree to which supply technicals (auction performance, dealer positioning, foreign flows) are the operational transmission mechanism — not just the "stock" of fiscal deficits but the "flow" of issuance and its market absorption.

**Monetary policy effectiveness**: The `broken_monetary_transmission` concept (confidence 7.5) is reinforced by my finding that the asset price channel is counterproductive. But from a yield curve perspective, the break is more nuanced: transmission is maturity-dependent. The front end transmits normally (2Y responds to Fed guidance); the long end is increasingly insensitive to monetary policy and responsive to fiscal dynamics. This means the Fed's effective toolkit shrinks as you move out the curve — the `toolkit_bandwidth_compression` concept is confirmed from the yield curve perspective.

**Central bank balance sheet**: QT's interaction with fiscal issuance is additive at the long end. The Fed's ~$60B/month Treasury runoff adds to Treasury's new issuance, creating a "double supply" headwind at the long end that does not affect the front end. QT cessation (likely late 2026 or 2027) would remove one leg of this double supply, potentially reducing term premium by 15-30bp and partially collapsing the maturity bifurcation. This is one of the few time-bound catalysts for regime change.

**Global rate correlations**: BOJ normalization (`boj_normalization_shock`, confidence 4.67) matters for US term premium through the demand channel. Each 25bp BOJ hike reduces carry incentive for Japanese institutions to hold USTs, incrementally shifting the supply-demand balance at the long end. The 7-18bp per 25bp BOJ hike estimate (from quant_researcher_01) is consistent with my term premium framework. The EUR bund spread also matters — ECB balance sheet normalization removes another price-insensitive demand source.

**Commodity/inflation dynamics**: The yield curve's breakeven inflation term structure (2Y breakevens vs. 10Y breakevens vs. 5Y5Y forward inflation) provides a high-frequency readout of how the market decomposes inflation expectations across horizons. Currently, near-term breakevens (~2.5-2.8%) exceed long-run breakevens (~2.3-2.5%), consistent with residual supply-side inflation expected to fade — which supports the transitional correlation thesis rather than permanent positive lock-in.

**Credit markets**: IG credit spread term structure mirrors the Treasury term premium pattern. Short-dated IG spreads (1-3Y) remain near-cycle-tights (~60-70bp), while long-dated IG spreads (10Y+) have widened ~20-30bp from tights, reflecting the same fiscal supply/duration premium dynamic that is reshaping the Treasury curve. This reinforces the maturity-dependent story across fixed income.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the basis trade unwind be modeled as a discrete event or a continuous process?** If discrete (repo market stress, regulatory shock), the correlation impact would be sudden and potentially systemic. If continuous (gradual position reduction), the term premium adjustment may be absorbed without correlation disruption. The distinction determines whether we face a jump risk or a drift risk.

2. **What is the true elasticity of foreign demand to yield differentials?** The decline in foreign official holdings from ~33% to ~23% of outstanding is clear, but the marginal price sensitivity of remaining foreign buyers (sovereign wealth funds, Taiwanese insurers, Middle Eastern reserves) is unknown. If remaining foreign demand is price-elastic, higher yields self-correct the supply-demand imbalance. If price-inelastic (reserves managed for non-return objectives), yield increases don't attract incremental demand, and term premium has no natural ceiling.

3. **How does the repo market's capacity to fund the basis trade interact with the Treasury's issuance composition?** A shift toward more bill issuance (short-end) would ease long-end supply pressure but increase repo market collateral supply, potentially tightening repo conditions and stressing the basis trade — creating a paradoxical feedback loop where easing long-end supply pressure via bill issuance simultaneously undermines the synthetic QE that was compressing term premium.

4. **Is the current term premium level already pricing the fiscal trajectory, requiring fiscal surprise to generate additional positive correlation?** Markets have had $2T+ annual deficits for multiple years now. The efficient-market argument is that the fiscal supply effect is fully priced. The counter-argument is that fiscal trajectories are path-dependent and the debt sustainability arithmetic worsens nonlinearly at current debt/GDP — meaning each year of continued high deficits adds marginal surprise even if the annual deficit number is unchanged.

5. **How should the term premium threshold for correlation lock-in be recalibrated for current market structure?** The historical ~150bp+ threshold was established when risk parity, vol-targeting, and the basis trade did not exist at scale. These strategies are long-duration and short-correlation by construction. Their presence may lower the effective threshold — meaning correlation could lock in at 100-120bp of term premium rather than the historical 150bp+, because the leveraged strategies amplify the fiscal supply signal.

6. **What is the information content of the yield curve's local curvature (second derivative) versus its slope (first derivative) for correlation regime prediction?** Most existing analysis focuses on slope (2s10s, 3m10y). But curvature anomalies — persistent butterfly spread dislocations, kinks at specific maturities — may contain additional regime information by revealing positioning stress and supply absorption bottlenecks that slope measures miss.

7. **Does the maturity bifurcation collapse upward (full fiscal dominance) or downward (negative correlation reassertion) in the next regime transition, and what is the advance warning time from curve signals?** Historical evidence on the lead time from bifurcation collapse to regime resolution is limited to 3 episodes. The 2Y-SPX correlation turning positive would be the strongest signal of upward collapse, while 30Y-SPX correlation reverting negative during a growth scare would signal downward collapse. Monitoring both tails is essential.
