# Rates-Equity Correlation — Structured & Private Credit Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The CLO arbitrage engine is the hidden governor of correlation regime persistence.** CLO equity tranche IRRs have compressed to ~12-14% (from 17-20% in 2021-22) as liability spreads (CLO AAA at ~155-170bp) remain elevated relative to loan yields (~S+350-400bp). When CLO arbitrage economics deteriorate below the ~11-12% equity IRR threshold, new formation slows, removing the single largest marginal buyer of leveraged loans (~65-70% of institutional loan demand). This creates a nonlinear credit tightening channel invisible to standard FCI measures and directly amplifies positive stock-bond correlation through simultaneous equity and credit stress.

2. **Private credit's $1.7T+ AUM is absorbing maturity wall refinancing but embedding correlation risk in opaque, illiquid structures.** Private credit vehicles are absorbing an estimated 15-25% of leveraged loan and high-yield refinancing activity that would otherwise hit public markets. This substitution masks the true severity of the maturity wall by moving price discovery from transparent public markets to quarterly-marked NAVs, creating a "shadow maturity wall" where stress transmission is delayed by 2-4 quarters but not eliminated. NAV markdowns in private credit, when they come, will hit simultaneously with public credit stress, amplifying the positive correlation episode rather than diversifying it.

3. **The covenant-lite leveraged loan market (>90% of new issuance) delays the default transmission mechanism by 12-24 months, creating a false sense of security in the correlation signpost hierarchy.** With >90% of leveraged loans covenant-lite and EBITDA addbacks averaging 15-40% of reported EBITDA, the traditional credit cycle signal — rising defaults triggering negative correlation via recession recognition — is structurally delayed. Borrowers can amend-and-extend, toggle PIK, and draw on revolvers well past the point where economic distress would have triggered default under traditional documentation. This means the correlation signpost hierarchy's IG credit spread trigger (Tier 1: OAS widening >75bp from trough) may activate later than the 18-month historical lead time suggests.

4. **The $2.5T corporate maturity wall creates a time-stamped, two-channel correlation amplifier operating through both the fundamental earnings channel and the credit availability channel.** Refinancing $2.5T of ZIRP-era debt (weighted average coupon ~2.5-3.5%) at current spreads (5.5-6.5% all-in) mechanically increases interest expense by ~$50-75B/year, compressing ROIC-WACC spreads and creating ~$2.50-3.75 S&P 500 EPS headwind. But the credit availability channel is potentially more dangerous: if CLO formation slows and bank lending remains constrained by Basel III endgame rules, the marginal dollar of refinancing must come from private credit at even higher all-in costs (8-12%), creating a stratification where the weakest borrowers face the highest refinancing costs precisely when their earnings are most under pressure.

5. **Cross-border CLO demand, particularly from Japanese institutional investors, links BOJ normalization directly to US leveraged loan market liquidity.** Japanese banks and insurers hold an estimated $80-120B of CLO AAA tranches. BOJ normalization narrows the carry incentive for these cross-border investments: each 25bp BOJ hike reduces the hedged yield pickup by approximately 15-20bp after accounting for basis swap dynamics. A sustained Japanese repatriation from CLO AAA would widen liability spreads, compress CLO arbitrage economics, and reduce new CLO formation — transmitting BOJ tightening directly into US leveraged loan demand. This is a secondary but significant channel that the existing KB analysis of BOJ normalization (focused on UST selling) has not captured.

6. **The Treasury basis trade ($800B-$1T+ at 50-100x leverage) and the CLO warehouse pipeline ($40-60B at any given time) represent correlated leveraged structures that could unwind simultaneously.** Both rely on stable repo/warehouse financing, both are sensitive to rate volatility, and both involve leveraged carry positions in fixed income markets. A VaR shock that forces basis trade deleveraging would simultaneously stress CLO warehouse financing terms, potentially freezing new CLO issuance and removing the leveraged loan bid. This correlated unwinding risk is the most acute channel through which a correlation regime transition could produce a credit crisis rather than merely a market correction.

7. **Semi-liquid private credit vehicles ($200-400B AUM in interval funds, BDC non-traded, tender-offer structures) are the new "breaking the buck" risk.** These structures promise quarterly or semi-annual liquidity to retail and wealth management investors on a pool of fundamentally illiquid assets. Under stress, redemption queues form, NAVs gap down, and the behavioral response (accelerated redemption requests) forces fire-sale asset disposal. The $200-400B in semi-liquid private credit is not a systemic risk by size, but it is a contagion pathway: forced selling from semi-liquid vehicles establishes distressed marks that infect the broader private credit NAV complex, causing a reassessment cascade across the $1.7T universe.

8. **Reported EBITDA in leveraged credits systematically overstates true cash earnings by 15-40% through addbacks, creating a hidden vulnerability that will surface precisely when the correlation regime matters most.** Pro forma EBITDA addbacks — projected cost savings, synergies, run-rate adjustments — averaged 25-35% of reported EBITDA for new leveraged loan issuance in 2024-25. Actual realization rates for projected addbacks are historically 40-60%, meaning true leverage for the median leveraged loan is 0.5-1.5x higher than the reported leverage metric. When the maturity wall forces refinancing, lenders will demand more conservative underwriting, revealing the true leverage and potentially triggering a wave of forced equity contributions, restructurings, or defaults concentrated in 2026-2028.

9. **The three-way cross-asset disagreement identified in the KB extends to a four-way disagreement when private credit is included.** Equities price no recession (21-22x P/E), public bonds price positive correlation/term premium, public credit prices neither recession nor stress (IG OAS ~90-110bp, HY OAS ~350-400bp), AND private credit NAVs price neither defaults nor spread widening (marked at or near par with minimal loss reserves). Private credit's pricing inconsistency may be the most extreme because it involves the least frequent mark-to-market and the largest information asymmetry between managers and LPs.

10. **The correlation signpost hierarchy should be augmented with a credit plumbing tier: CLO formation pace, warehouse pipeline health, and private credit deployment pace as 6-12 month leading indicators.** When CLO creation falls below ~$25B/quarter (vs. ~$40-50B/quarter in 2024-25), it signals that leveraged loan demand is structurally insufficient, foreshadowing credit rationing for leveraged borrowers and eventual default cycle acceleration. Similarly, when private credit fundraising exceeds deployment by more than 2:1 for two consecutive quarters, it signals that deal quality is deteriorating and managers are warehousing risk.


## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: CLO Arbitrage as Correlation Governor

CLOs purchase ~65-70% of institutional leveraged loans. The arbitrage (loan portfolio yield minus liability cost minus management fees minus equity return) determines CLO creation pace. With leveraged loan yields at approximately S+350-400bp and CLO AAA liabilities at ~155-170bp (vs. ~100-110bp in early 2021), the equity tranche IRR has compressed from 17-20% to approximately 12-14%. This compression occurs because:

- **Loan yields have not widened commensurately** with the rise in risk-free rates; leveraged loans are floating-rate so they repriced higher on the base rate, but spread compression in 2024 offset much of the risk premium benefit.
- **Liability spreads remain elevated** due to reduced bank demand for CLO paper (regulatory capital treatment), reduced Japanese demand (BOJ normalization), and post-2022 investor caution.
- **Reinvestment period expirations** are concentrated in 2025-2027 (~$200-250B of CLOs), which means a large portion of the CLO stock cannot buy new loans, reducing demand at the margin.

When equity IRRs fall below the ~11-12% threshold, CLO equity investors (hedge funds, PE firms) redeploy capital elsewhere, CLO managers cannot close warehouses, and new formation drops precipitously. This happened in Q4 2022 (CLO formation dropped ~50% QoQ) and briefly in March 2020. The result is a sudden withdrawal of the leveraged loan bid, spread widening, and credit rationing for refinancing borrowers — all occurring without any change in fundamental default rates.

This is directly relevant to the correlation regime because the withdrawal of CLO demand is a credit supply shock that produces positive stock-bond correlation: equities fall (earnings and multiple compression from credit stress), bonds fall (the same term premium and supply dynamics that caused CLO liability spread widening also affect Treasuries), and the Fed cannot easily offset a structural credit supply contraction with rate cuts because the transmission mechanism runs through the securitization pipeline, not the policy rate.

### Claim 2: Private Credit Shadow Maturity Wall

Private credit AUM has grown from ~$700B (2019) to ~$1.7T+ (2025E), with direct lending comprising roughly 60-65% of the total. Private credit deployment has absorbed a significant share of leveraged refinancing:

- **Unitranche structures** at ~S+550-650bp (all-in 10-12%) are refinancing leveraged loans that cannot access the broadly syndicated market due to size, leverage, or complexity.
- **NAV markdowns lag reality** by 2-4 quarters because private credit marks are based on internal models, not observable market prices. The industry standard is to mark loans at par (or near-par) unless a specific credit event triggers a reassessment.
- **PIK toggle usage has increased** from ~5-8% of new private credit deals (2021) to ~12-18% (2025E), indicating that borrowers are increasingly unable to service cash interest — a leading indicator of stress that does not appear in headline default rates.

The shadow maturity wall manifests when private credit borrowers face their own refinancing needs: most private credit loans have 5-7 year maturities originated in 2020-2022, meaning a wave of private credit maturities hits in 2025-2029. These borrowers cannot easily refinance in public markets (too small, too leveraged), and the incumbent lender has every incentive to amend-and-extend rather than recognize a loss — perpetuating the NAV fiction until a liquidity event or fund wind-down forces recognition.

### Claim 3: Covenant-Lite Transmission Delay

The existing KB identifies the correlation signpost hierarchy as a high-confidence framework (confidence 8). However, this hierarchy was calibrated on historical episodes where leveraged credit markets had traditional covenants — maintenance covenants testing leverage, coverage, and capex on a quarterly basis. Today:

- **>90% of leveraged loans are covenant-lite**, with only incurrence-based covenants that test metrics only when the borrower takes a specific action (new debt, dividend, acquisition).
- **EBITDA addbacks** inflate reported leverage metrics by 15-40%, meaning a company reported at 5.0x leverage may be 6.0-7.0x on a clean EBITDA basis.
- **Amendment activity** is the new default proxy: when 40-50% of leveraged borrowers have sought amendments in the trailing 12 months (vs. ~20-25% in 2019), it signals pervasive stress that has not yet registered as defaults.

This structural delay means the IG credit spread trigger (Tier 1 in the signpost hierarchy, >75bp widening from trough) may not activate until stress has already progressed further through the real economy than historical precedents would suggest. The 18-month lead time may stretch to 24-30 months under current documentation standards.

### Claim 4: Two-Channel Maturity Wall Amplifier

The KB documents the maturity wall's earnings channel ($50-75B incremental interest expense, ~$2.50-3.75 S&P 500 EPS headwind). I add the credit availability channel:

- **CLO capacity constraints**: If CLO formation runs at $100-120B/year (below the $130-150B pace needed to absorb maturity wall refinancing plus new issuance), there is a structural shortfall in loan demand.
- **Bank lending constraints**: Post-Basel III endgame, bank balance sheet capacity for leveraged lending is approximately flat to declining. Leveraged lending guidance from regulators caps individual exposure at 6x leverage (on reported EBITDA), which, given addback inflation, effectively caps clean-EBITDA leverage at 4.5-5.0x.
- **Private credit as lender of last resort**: Private credit fills the gap but at all-in costs of 10-12% (vs. 6.5-7.5% for BSL). The weakest borrowers face the highest refinancing costs, creating a self-reinforcing downgrade cycle.

The two channels interact: earnings compression from higher interest expense reduces EBITDA, which increases leverage ratios, which makes refinancing more expensive or unavailable, which triggers further earnings compression. This vicious cycle operates independently of the macroeconomic cycle and is entirely a function of the maturity schedule and rate environment.

### Claim 5: Japanese CLO AAA Demand Channel

Japanese megabanks (MUFG, SMFG, Mizuho) and insurance companies (Dai-ichi, Nippon Life) are among the largest holders of CLO AAA tranches globally, estimated at $80-120B. The carry trade economics are straightforward:

- **CLO AAA yield**: ~S+155-170bp = ~5.8-6.0% USD
- **Hedging cost**: ~3-month SOFR basis swap at ~120-150bp = ~4.3-4.5% hedged
- **JGB alternative**: ~1.0-1.5% (10Y JGB under BOJ normalization)
- **Net carry pickup**: ~280-350bp hedged

Each 25bp BOJ hike narrows this pickup by ~15-20bp (accounting for basis swap adjustment). If the BOJ normalizes to ~1.0-1.5% (from ~0.5%), the hedged carry pickup falls to ~200-280bp, which may be insufficient to justify the credit risk and complexity of CLO AAA for many Japanese institutions.

Reduced Japanese CLO AAA demand widens liability spreads, compresses CLO equity IRRs, and slows CLO formation — transmitting BOJ tightening into US leveraged loan demand with a 3-6 month lag through the warehouse-to-pricing pipeline.

### Claim 6: Correlated Leveraged Structure Unwinding

The Treasury basis trade ($800B-$1T+ at 50-100x leverage) and CLO warehouse pipeline ($40-60B outstanding) are both:

- **Repo/warehouse funded**: Both rely on short-term secured financing that can be pulled or repriced under stress.
- **Sensitive to rate volatility**: Basis trade P&L is a function of cash-futures basis convergence; CLO warehouse P&L depends on loan mark stability during the 3-6 month warehousing period.
- **Subject to forced unwinding**: Margin calls on basis trades and warehouse margin calls/NAV triggers on CLO warehouses can cascade simultaneously because they share funding market infrastructure (the same prime brokers and banks provide both).

A rate volatility shock that widens the basis trade spread beyond haircut capacity simultaneously increases CLO warehouse mark-to-market losses, freezing new CLO issuance. The March 2020 episode provides a partial preview: CLO warehouse freezes lasted 4-6 weeks and required Fed intervention (Term Asset-Backed Securities Loan Facility expansion). The next episode may not receive the same rapid policy response, particularly if the Fed is constrained by inflation persistence.

### Claim 7: Semi-Liquid Vehicle Breaking Point

Semi-liquid private credit vehicles include:

- **Non-traded BDCs**: ~$80-100B AUM, offering quarterly tender at NAV with ~5% quarterly limits
- **Interval funds**: ~$60-80B AUM, offering quarterly repurchase at NAV with ~5-25% limits
- **Tender-offer funds**: ~$60-100B AUM, with discretionary tender offers

These structures create a fundamental liquidity mismatch: underlying loans have 5-7 year maturities and no secondary market liquidity, while the vehicle offers quarterly liquidity windows. When redemption requests exceed tender limits:

1. **Queue formation**: Investors get pro-rata allocation, creating urgency to redeem early.
2. **NAV gap risk**: If forced to sell assets, transaction prices are 85-92 cents on the dollar (vs. NAV at 98-100), creating NAV decline that triggers further redemption requests.
3. **Contagion**: Distressed sales establish marks that infect the broader private credit NAV complex, since competing managers must justify their own marks relative to observable transaction prices.

The behavioral analogy is money market fund "breaking the buck" in 2008. The absolute AUM is smaller (~$200-400B vs. ~$3.5T in money market funds in 2008), but the contagion channel into the broader $1.7T private credit universe and the CLO market (which shares some of the same underlying borrowers) is potentially significant.

### Claim 8: EBITDA Addback Hidden Vulnerability

Pro forma EBITDA addback practices in leveraged lending have expanded significantly:

- **2019**: Average addbacks ~15-20% of reported EBITDA
- **2022**: Average addbacks ~20-30% of reported EBITDA
- **2024-25**: Average addbacks ~25-35% of reported EBITDA, with some deals exceeding 50%

Common addback categories include: projected cost savings from acquisitions (often unrealized), run-rate revenue assumptions (extrapolating recent growth), one-time charges (recurring "one-time" charges), and management fee addbacks. Historical realization rates:

- **Projected synergies**: 50-70% realized within 2 years
- **Cost savings**: 40-60% realized
- **Run-rate adjustments**: 30-50% realized

At a 50% average realization rate, a company reported at 5.0x leverage is actually at ~6.0-7.0x. This hidden leverage means:

- Rating agencies' assessments systematically understate true credit risk
- CLO OC tests (based on par/market value ratios) appear healthier than economic reality
- The "maturity wall" refinancing challenge is more severe than headline metrics suggest because true leverage is higher

When the maturity wall forces refinancing in a tighter credit environment, lenders will demand more conservative underwriting. The gap between reported and true leverage will be exposed, triggering a wave of mark-to-market adjustments across the CLO universe as rating agencies catch up with reality.

### Claim 9: Four-Way Cross-Asset Disagreement

Extending the KB's three-way disagreement:

| Asset Class | Implied Scenario | Pricing Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Equities | No recession, negative correlation | 21-22x P/E, ERP ~4.5-5.0% |
| Public Bonds | Positive correlation, term premium | 10Y at ~4.3-4.5%, ACM TP 50-80bp |
| Public Credit | Neither recession nor stress | IG OAS ~90-110bp, HY OAS ~350-400bp |
| **Private Credit** | **Neither defaults nor spread widening** | **NAVs at/near par, loss reserves ~1-2%** |

Private credit's pricing is potentially the most disconnected because:
- Mark-to-market occurs quarterly at manager discretion
- No secondary market for price discovery
- Manager compensation incentives favor higher marks (higher AUM, higher fees)
- LP reporting creates the illusion of stability with annualized volatility of 2-4% (vs. 8-12% for comparable public credit)

### Claim 10: Credit Plumbing Signpost Augmentation

The existing signpost hierarchy (IG spreads → ISM → yield curve → initial claims → Fed cuts) should be augmented with:

- **Tier 0.5 (24-month lead): CLO formation pace.** When quarterly CLO issuance falls below $25B for two consecutive quarters, it signals structural credit supply contraction. This preceded the 2007-08 crisis by ~18 months (CLO formation peaked in Q2 2007 at ~$120B, then collapsed) and the 2015-16 energy credit stress by ~12 months.
- **Tier 0.5 (18-month lead): Private credit deployment/fundraising ratio.** When the ratio exceeds 2:1 (fundraising outpacing deployment by 2x), it signals either deal quality deterioration or market overheating. Current estimates suggest the ratio is ~1.3-1.5:1, approaching but not yet at warning levels.
- **Tier 1.5 (12-month lead): Amendment and PIK activity.** When trailing-12-month amendment rates exceed 40% of the leveraged loan universe, and PIK toggle activation exceeds 15% of private credit deals, it signals pervasive stress invisible in headline default rates.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Monetary Policy Transmission
The CLO pipeline and private credit absorption directly affect monetary policy transmission — a key KB concept (`broken_monetary_transmission`, confidence 7.5). My analysis adds a specific channel: rate hikes transmit to leveraged borrowers primarily through the CLO arbitrage mechanism (liability costs rising → CLO formation slowing → loan demand falling), NOT through the direct interest rate channel (leveraged loans are floating rate, so borrowers immediately pay higher coupons). This distinction matters because the CLO transmission channel has a 3-6 month lag (warehouse-to-pricing pipeline) and is subject to nonlinear threshold effects (equity IRR floor), whereas the interest rate channel is immediate and linear. The partially broken monetary transmission diagnosed in the KB may be partially explained by the CLO mechanism: rate hikes do tighten credit conditions for leveraged borrowers, but through a lagged, nonlinear, and opaque channel that standard FCI measures do not capture.

### Inflation Regimes
The covenant-lite structure and EBITDA addback practices create a hidden linkage to inflation regimes. In a services inflation persistence environment (core services ex-shelter at ~3.5-4.0%), leveraged companies face wage cost pressures that compress true EBITDA margins. But because addbacks inflate reported EBITDA, the true margin compression is masked. This creates a scenario where inflation persistence simultaneously:
- Keeps the Fed from cutting (term premium stays elevated → positive correlation pressure)
- Erodes true earnings for leveraged companies (hidden by addbacks)
- Eventually forces recognition when refinancing demands more conservative underwriting

The inflation regime directly determines whether the maturity wall is manageable (inflation falls → Fed cuts → refinancing costs fall → no crisis) or catastrophic (inflation persists → rates stay high → refinancing squeeze → default cycle). The KB's `corridor_of_instability` concept (core inflation 2.0-3.5% × deficit 4.5-7.0% GDP) should be extended to include a credit dimension: within the corridor, leveraged credit stress builds silently; exiting the corridor in either direction resolves the credit stress question.

### Volatility Regimes
Rate volatility (MOVE index) is the critical transmission variable between the macro correlation regime and credit market plumbing. Elevated MOVE (>120, as in much of 2023-2025) directly affects:
- CLO warehouse financing terms (higher haircuts, wider warehouse spreads)
- Basis trade viability (wider basis but higher volatility of basis convergence)
- Private credit deployment pace (wider bid-ask spreads in new origination)

A volatility regime shift from the current elevated-but-not-crisis level to a genuine vol spike (MOVE >150) would be the trigger for the correlated leveraged structure unwinding described in Claim 6. Conversely, a sustained decline in rate volatility (MOVE <90) would restart the CLO arbitrage engine, absorb the maturity wall, and potentially support the negative correlation reassertion thesis.

### Equity Cycles
The KB's `roic_wacc_compression` concept (confidence 9) is directly amplified by the credit channel I describe. ROIC-WACC compression at ~3.5-5.0pp (tightest since 2007-08) interacts with the maturity wall because:
- Companies with the tightest ROIC-WACC spreads are precisely the ones most vulnerable to refinancing at higher rates
- The S&P 493 (ex-mega-cap) faces both WACC increases from refinancing AND ROIC compression from wage/input cost pressures masked by addbacks
- The equity concentration correlation illusion (KB confidence 6) extends to credit: mega-cap tech is net cash with no maturity wall exposure, so index-level credit metrics (interest coverage, net leverage) are distorted by the same concentration that distorts correlation measurement

### Fiscal Policy
The $2T+ annual Treasury issuance (KB `fiscal_correlation_codetermination`, confidence 8) competes for the same institutional investor capital that funds CLO liabilities and private credit vehicles. When Treasury yields rise to attract marginal buyers, CLO liability spreads must widen commensurately (or CLO AAA holders rotate into Treasuries), compressing CLO economics. This is a direct fiscal-to-credit transmission channel: fiscal expansion → higher term premium → wider CLO liabilities → slower CLO formation → tighter credit conditions for leveraged borrowers → maturity wall stress → positive correlation amplification.


## Open Questions

1. **What is the precise CLO equity IRR threshold below which formation materially slows?** I estimate ~11-12%, but this depends on the opportunity cost of CLO equity capital (which varies with PE fund return expectations, hedge fund strategy returns, etc.). A more precise calibration would require surveying CLO equity investors on their return hurdle rates.

2. **How much of the maturity wall has private credit already absorbed, and at what terms?** Published data on private credit deal terms is sparse and lagging. If private credit has already refinanced 20-25% of the maturity wall at 10-12% all-in costs, the remaining public-market maturity wall is smaller but the private credit stress buildup is larger. The net systemic risk may be unchanged, merely redistributed from visible to invisible venues.

3. **Will the Fed provide a CLO-specific backstop in the next credit stress episode?** The 2020 TALF expansion explicitly included CLO AAA as eligible collateral. If the Fed has this tool available, the worst-case CLO warehouse freeze scenario (Claim 6) is time-limited. But if inflation persistence prevents the Fed from acting quickly (the asymmetric reaction function identified in the KB), the freeze could last months rather than weeks.

4. **Is the EBITDA addback problem self-correcting as refinancing forces more conservative underwriting, or does it create a nonlinear recognition event?** If lenders gradually tighten standards, the adjustment is orderly. If the recognition happens suddenly (a large, high-profile default that exposes addback inflation), the repricing across the leveraged loan and CLO universe could be disorderly and amplify the correlation regime transition.

5. **How does the growth of private credit ETFs and interval funds change the behavioral dynamics of the asset class under stress?** Retail and wealth management investors in semi-liquid structures may behave differently from institutional LPs in closed-end funds. If the behavioral response is faster redemption requests at the first sign of NAV decline, the contagion pathway from semi-liquid vehicles to the broader private credit universe could be faster and more destabilizing than I estimate.

6. **Does the CLO demand channel for leveraged loans have a structural substitute?** If private credit, direct lending, and bank balance sheets can collectively replace CLO demand at the margin, then CLO arbitrage deterioration may be less significant for the correlation regime. But if CLOs remain the irreplaceable marginal buyer for broadly syndicated leveraged loans (as I believe), then CLO economics are the single most important variable for leveraged credit conditions and their correlation implications.

7. **What is the actual correlation between Treasury basis trade unwinds and CLO warehouse stress?** The March 2020 episode saw both occur simultaneously, but the sample size is n=1. A more systematic analysis of funding market stress transmission between repo (basis trade) and warehouse (CLO) would be needed to validate the correlated unwinding thesis.

8. **Can rating agencies catch up with EBITDA addback inflation before the maturity wall peaks?** Moody's and S&P have published increasingly skeptical research on addback practices, and some rating actions have begun incorporating addback adjustments. If rating agencies accelerate their reassessment, downgrades could front-run the maturity wall, potentially activating the IG credit spread signpost earlier than the documentation-delay argument suggests.
