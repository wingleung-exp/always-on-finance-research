# Demographics — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The output gap is structurally smaller than consensus estimates, and the mismeasurement is growing.** With labor force growth at ~0.3% vs. the 1.5% embedded in most potential output models, CBO-style potential GDP estimates are overstated by 0.5-1.0pp annually on a cumulative basis. The economy is running closer to — or above — capacity than standard gap measures suggest, which means monetary policy has been more accommodative in real terms than the headline Taylor Rule prescriptions imply.

2. **The New Keynesian Phillips Curve is steepening under structural labor scarcity, raising the sacrifice ratio for disinflation.** ULC > productivity in 12/16 recent quarters (already in KB) is not merely an inflationary signal — it reflects a structural flattening of the labor supply curve. The marginal cost channel that transmits the output gap to inflation operates with higher pass-through when firms face a permanently tighter labor pool. Empirically, the sacrifice ratio (output cost per 1pp inflation reduction) should be rising from the 1.5-2.5 range toward 3.0-4.0, making disinflation campaigns politically and economically costlier.

3. **Demographics is fundamentally a supply shock that the political economy transforms into simultaneous demand stimulus — the worst-case scenario for the NK inflation-output tradeoff.** The aging political economy ratchet (KB: confidence 7.5) generates fiscal expansion while the labor force contracts. This supply-constraining + demand-stimulating configuration produces the maximum inflationary pressure per unit of output gain. Within the NK framework, this resolves the aging-inflation/deflation binary (KB: 50/50) toward 60/40 inflationary, because the political economy demand channel has a more robust micro-foundation than the pure savings-glut channel.

4. **Taylor Rule prescriptions are informationally degraded to a ±200bp range, creating a structural policy uncertainty premium.** The Taylor Rule requires three inputs: the output gap (claim 1 shows this is mismeasured), the inflation gap (distorted by supply-shock composition), and r* (KB: bimodal distribution, 0.8-2.2pp cumulative range). Compounding these uncertainties, the Taylor-prescribed policy rate has a 95% CI of roughly 250-450bp in the current environment — an interval so wide it cannot distinguish between "too tight" and "too loose."

5. **Fiscal multipliers in aging economies are systematically higher than standard estimates, creating a procyclical fiscal bias.** Three NK mechanisms amplify multipliers under aging: (a) higher marginal propensity to consume among transfer-dependent retirees (0.7-0.9 vs. 0.3-0.5 for working-age), (b) monetary accommodation is more likely due to political economy ratchet, which the Woodford (2011) framework shows maximizes the multiplier, and (c) the economy operates closer to capacity (claim 1), where the NK model predicts fiscal spending displaces less private activity but generates more inflation.

6. **The Solow-Swan transition dynamics create a 10-15 year window of rising capital intensity that partially — but only partially — offsets the labor headwind.** Lower population growth (n declining from ~1.0% to ~0.3% in effective terms) raises the steady-state capital-labor ratio. During the transition, capital deepening contributes 0.3-0.5pp to labor productivity growth. But this effect is bounded: steady-state per-capita growth converges back to the rate of technological progress, and the aggregate growth rate is permanently lower by the full decline in labor force growth.

7. **Mundell-Fleming dynamics predict that asymmetric aging across economies creates persistent real interest rate differentials that drive capital flows and exchange rates — but simultaneous global aging collapses this mechanism.** In a standard IS-LM-BP framework, a country with worse demographics would have lower r* and a depreciating currency. Japan 1990-2020 conformed to this prediction. The novel condition (KB: simultaneous global aging, confidence 8.3) means Mundell-Fleming capital-flow equilibration has no destination, creating a "closed economy at global scale" where all the adjustment falls on domestic asset prices rather than exchange rates and capital flows.

8. **The demand-supply shock composition shift is the single most important NK variable for cross-asset pricing, and demographics is one of the primary — though not sole — drivers.** The 1998-2021 era featured demand-shock dominance (under the NK model: credible inflation targeting, high savings, favorable demographics). The reversal toward supply-shock dominance (labor scarcity, fiscal expansion against constrained supply, energy transition capex) produces positive stock-bond correlation as a model-predicted outcome, not merely an empirical regularity. The NK framework provides the micro-foundation the KB's correlation regime analysis (confidence 7) lacks: under demand shocks, the central bank can stabilize both output and inflation; under supply shocks, it faces a tradeoff, and asset prices move in tandem.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Output gap mismeasurement):**
CBO potential GDP estimates embed a production function approach where labor input is a primary determinant. With the working-age population growth rate declining from ~1.0% to ~0.3%, the cumulative output gap estimation error grows over time. Orphanides (2001) demonstrated that real-time output gap measurement errors of 2-3pp were common even without structural breaks in potential growth — adding a demographic structural break compounds this. The practical consequence: policies calibrated to a larger negative output gap (more accommodative) are actually calibrated to an economy at or above potential.

**Claim 2 (Phillips Curve steepening):**
The expectations-augmented Phillips Curve in NK form relates inflation to expected inflation plus a coefficient (κ) times the output gap, where κ depends on the slope of the marginal cost curve. Structural labor scarcity steepens this curve: Ball and Mazumder (2019) show that the Phillips Curve slope has varied historically with labor market tightness. The KB evidence of ULC > productivity in 12/16 quarters is consistent with a steeper curve. Practically, this means the Fed needs larger output gaps (deeper recessions) to achieve the same disinflationary effect — the KB's political economy ratchet (aging voters tolerate recession less) directly collides with this increased sacrifice ratio.

**Claim 3 (Supply + demand worst case):**
The NK model's central insight is that supply shocks create an inflation-output tradeoff while demand shocks do not. Demographics simultaneously constrains supply (labor force decline) while stimulating demand (fiscal transfers to aging voters). This is analytically equivalent to a permanent adverse supply shock combined with a permanent positive demand shock — the configuration that produces maximum inflationary pressure at any given output level. Blanchard (2023) notes this combination has been absent since the 1970s stagflation, which was driven by oil (external supply) + fiscal expansion (Vietnam/Great Society), not demographics. The demographic version is slower but more persistent.

**Claim 4 (Taylor Rule degradation):**
The standard Taylor Rule: i = r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*). With r* uncertain by ±1.0pp (KB: bimodal distribution), output gap uncertain by ±1.5pp (claim 1), and inflation gap measurement complicated by supply-shock composition, the propagated uncertainty in the prescribed rate exceeds 200bp. Laubach and Williams (2003, updated) show that r* estimation uncertainty alone generates ±100bp policy rate uncertainty. This is not a critique of the Taylor Rule per se — it is a statement about the information environment under demographic regime change. The practical consequence is that forward guidance loses credibility because the central bank cannot credibly communicate where "neutral" is.

**Claim 5 (Higher fiscal multipliers):**
Christiano, Eichenbaum, and Rebelo (2011) show multipliers approach 1.5-2.0 at the zero lower bound with monetary accommodation, vs. 0.5-0.7 under active monetary policy. The aging political economy ratchet (KB: confidence 7.5, ~1/20 counterexample rate) systematically pushes toward monetary accommodation. Additionally, Galí, López-Salido, and Vallés (2007) show that the fraction of "rule-of-thumb" (hand-to-mouth) consumers amplifies fiscal multipliers — retirees dependent on government transfers closely approximate this consumer type. As the transfer-recipient share of the population rises, aggregate MPC rises, and multipliers increase.

**Claim 6 (Solow transition dynamics):**
In the Solow model, the steady-state capital-labor ratio k* = (s/(n+δ+g))^(1/(1-α)). Reducing n from 1.0% to 0.3% raises k* by approximately 15-25% (depending on α and δ calibration). The transition to this higher k* generates temporarily elevated capital deepening. However, the steady-state growth rate of total output falls by the full decline in n. The practical implication: total returns to capital may be maintained during the transition (capital per worker rising) even as aggregate economic growth declines — consistent with the observation that equity markets can perform well during the early stages of demographic decline (Japan 2012-2024, South Korea post-2020).

**Claim 7 (Mundell-Fleming under simultaneous aging):**
In the Mundell-Fleming model with imperfect capital mobility, a country-specific negative supply shock (lower potential growth from aging) reduces the domestic natural rate, generating capital outflows and currency depreciation until the external balance adjusts. Japan's persistent yen weakness from 2012-2024 conformed to this prediction. But when all major economies face the same shock simultaneously — the N=0 condition the KB identifies — the Mundell-Fleming adjustment mechanism breaks down. The external adjustment channel is eliminated, and all pressure falls on domestic variables: asset prices, fiscal balances, and the domestic inflation-output tradeoff.

**Claim 8 (Shock composition as micro-foundation):**
The NK model predicts: under demand shocks, output and inflation co-move positively → stocks and bonds move oppositely (negative correlation). Under supply shocks, output and inflation move in opposite directions → stocks and bonds co-move (positive correlation). This provides the theoretical grounding for the KB's empirical observation (64% historical positive correlation, supply-shock eras). Demographics drives the shock composition toward supply dominance through three channels: (i) labor supply constraints make any positive demand impulse inflationary, (ii) fiscal transfers are demand shocks hitting supply-constrained economies, (iii) the central bank's inability to fully stabilize both output and inflation under supply shocks means both equities and bonds become sensitive to the same fundamental uncertainty.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Output gap mismeasurement | 7/10 | Directionally robust (Solow arithmetic + Orphanides evidence), but magnitude depends on TFP assumptions and the degree to which immigration/participation offset headline labor force decline. The effective dependency ratio gap (KB: 6.5/10) partially moderates. |
| 2. Phillips Curve steepening | 6/10 | Theoretically grounded in NK micro-foundations and supported by ULC data. However, the Phillips Curve has been notoriously unstable empirically (the "Phillips Curve puzzle" of 2010-2019), and the steepening claim rests on a structural break that requires more data to confirm. |
| 3. Supply-demand worst case | 7/10 | The logical structure is sound within the NK framework. The main uncertainty is whether the political economy demand channel actually materializes with the speed and magnitude the ratchet model predicts. Germany's Schuldenbremse (1/20 counterexample) introduces modest doubt. |
| 4. Taylor Rule degradation | 8/10 | This is nearly a mathematical consequence of compounding uncertainties in r*, output gap, and inflation measurement. The policy implication (reduced forward guidance credibility) is the strongest testable prediction. Already partially validated by the Fed's shift away from point-estimate forward guidance. |
| 5. Higher fiscal multipliers | 5/10 | Theoretically sound but empirically fragile. The literature on state-dependent multipliers is contested (Ramey 2019 vs. Auerbach-Gorodnichenko 2012). The aging → higher MPC channel is logical but not directly tested in a demographic context. |
| 6. Solow transition dynamics | 7/10 | Standard model prediction with well-understood mechanics. The main uncertainty is the magnitude and duration of the transition, not the direction. AI/automation enters through the g (technological progress) term and could dominate. |
| 7. Mundell-Fleming collapse | 6/10 | Directionally compelling and consistent with the KB's simultaneous aging concept (confidence 8.3). But the real world has many more frictions, policy tools, and adjustment mechanisms than the textbook model. India and Africa, while institutionally immature today, provide partial safety valves. |
| 8. Shock composition shift | 8/10 | The most robust claim because it connects the NK micro-foundations to the observable correlation regime shift. The theory is internally consistent and the empirical pattern is well-documented. The main limitation is that demographics is one of several drivers (deglobalization, energy transition), and isolating its contribution is impossible. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**Fiscal Dominance (sovereign_debt topic):**
The unprecedented fiscal configuration (KB: 6-7% deficit at sub-4% unemployment, confidence 9) is precisely the supply-demand worst case of claim 3 operationalized. The NK framework explains *why* this configuration is inflationary: fiscal expansion (demand) against a supply-constrained economy with structurally smaller output gaps. The post-WWII deleveraging impairment (KB: confidence 8) maps directly to the Solow steady-state prediction — lower n means the growth channel for deleveraging is permanently impaired.

**Rates/Equity Correlation (rates_equity_correlation topic):**
Claim 8 provides the NK micro-foundation for the negative correlation historical anomaly (KB: confidence 9). The 1998-2021 negative correlation was a specific prediction of the NK model under demand-shock dominance with credible inflation targeting — conditions now reversing. The correlation term premium feedback loop (KB: confidence 7) is an amplification mechanism operating on the NK fundamental.

**Corporate Credit (corporate_credit topic):**
The EBITDA addback fiction (KB: confidence 6-7) maps to claim 2 (steeper Phillips Curve): if the labor supply curve is steeper, labor cost synergies are harder to realize, and the embedded addbacks are fictitious for NK-structural reasons, not merely cyclical ones. The maturity wall interaction is amplified by claim 5 (higher multipliers) because fiscal expansion in aging economies generates more inflation than output, squeezing real margins for leveraged borrowers.

**AI and Tech Disruption (ai_and_tech_disruption topic):**
The ECB-Fed-BoJ feedback loop (KB: confidence 4) connects to claim 7 (Mundell-Fleming). AI capex differentials create r* differentials across economies, which under the Mundell-Fleming framework drive capital flows and exchange rates. The NK model adds: if AI primarily enters through the g (technology) term in the Solow model, it can offset the labor headwind in claim 6 — but only if adoption is fast enough to matter during the transition window.

**Energy Transition:**
The transition capex impact (KB: confidence 6, episodic 0.1-0.3pp) is a supply shock within the NK framework, reinforcing claim 8's shock composition shift toward supply dominance.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the Phillips Curve steepening be empirically distinguished from a level shift in inflation expectations?** Both produce higher inflation at any given unemployment rate, but the policy implications differ: a steepening requires larger output gaps for disinflation, while an expectations shift requires re-anchoring. The KB's 50/50 aging-inflation binary partially turns on this distinction.

2. **Does the Solow capital-deepening transition actually manifest, or does investment decline with expected growth?** The model predicts rising k*, but if firms anticipate lower demand growth (demographic headwind), investment may fall rather than rise, violating the Solow transition prediction. Japan's experience (low investment despite low labor force growth) partially supports the pessimistic case, though Japan had unique confounds.

3. **What is the empirical fiscal multiplier in an aging economy?** No country has yet experienced a large fiscal expansion during advanced aging with independent monetary policy. Japan's fiscal expansions coincided with extreme monetary accommodation and deflation. The state-dependent multiplier literature has not incorporated aging as a state variable.

4. **How should the NK framework handle the possibility that demographics changes the inflation target itself?** If aging electorates tolerate higher inflation (KB: erosion of CB independence), the effective inflation target shifts upward. The NK model is calibrated around a fixed target — endogenizing the target to demographic political economy is theoretically uncharted territory.

5. **Is the Mundell-Fleming "closed economy at global scale" prediction falsifiable on the 2026-2035 horizon?** The prediction is that cross-border capital flows become less effective at equilibrating r* differentials. The test would be persistent real rate differentials across major economies despite free capital mobility — currently partially observable in the US-Japan-Europe rate gaps.

6. **What is the minimum AI productivity contribution (entering through g in the Solow model) needed to fully offset the demographic labor headwind?** If labor force growth declines by ~1.0pp, maintaining the same aggregate growth rate requires ~1.0pp additional TFP growth — roughly doubling the historical 0.8-1.2% trend. Is this within the plausible AI productivity range (KB: 0.5-1.0% per Acemoglu)?
