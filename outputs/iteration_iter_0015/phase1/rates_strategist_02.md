# AI & Technology Disruption Economic Impact — Central Bank Policy Transmission Strategist (rates_strategist_02) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The monetary policy transmission mechanism is experiencing its most severe channel-by-channel impairment since the 2008 financial crisis, but the source is inverted: in 2008, broken credit markets prevented easing from reaching the real economy; in 2025-2026, the AI capex boom prevents tightening from restraining financial conditions.** The Goldman Sachs FCI registers ~75-100bp easier than the policy rate alone would imply, with the residual attributable to AI-driven equity appreciation, tech-sector credit tightness compression, and employment from the data center buildout. The Fed is attempting to tighten into a sectoral boom that generates its own easing impulse — and it has no targeted tools to address this. The impairment is structural, not cyclical: as long as hyperscaler capex runs at $200-250B+ annually (~1.0% of US GDP), the AI sector operates as an autonomous easing engine that dampens the aggregate effect of rate hikes on the real economy.

2. **Central bank reaction functions across the G3 are now conditioned on an unobservable variable — the AI productivity dividend — that creates a trilemma: the FOMC cannot simultaneously maintain data-dependent credibility, price in a forward-looking structural shift that has not yet appeared in official statistics, and avoid policy error in either direction (too tight if AI works, too loose if it doesn't).** The Laubach-Williams r* estimate stands at ~1.0-1.2% real as of Q1 2026, but the AI-contingent range spans 0.5-2.0%, implying the policy rate could be 50-100bp too tight or 50-100bp too loose depending on AI outcomes that will only become statistically visible over a 3-5 year horizon. The FOMC's resolution strategy — wait for measured data — introduces a structural lag of 2-4 years relative to the true productivity shift, during which calibration error accumulates.

3. **The "AI put" in central bank reaction functions is deepening and becoming more asymmetric than in any prior technology cycle, because the scale of AI-linked financial assets (~$18-22T in market cap of the top 10 AI beneficiaries) now exceeds the total US mortgage market at its 2007 peak, making the wealth effect channel from an AI equity correction too large for any central bank to ignore.** In a 30-40% drawdown scenario for AI-linked equities, the wealth destruction (~$5-8T) would produce a consumption hit of ~$150-400B (at 3-5 cents/$1 MPC from equity wealth), requiring 150-250bp of rate cuts on wealth-effect grounds alone, plus balance sheet expansion if credit conditions seize. The upside corollary — the Fed will not preemptively tighten against an AI-driven equity rally absent inflation evidence — means the expected value of the rate path has a permanent dovish skew that is under-priced in the forward OIS curve.

4. **The credit channel is bifurcating into an AI-insensitive segment (self-funded megacap investment at $200-250B/year, ~85-90% internally financed) and an AI-disrupted segment (fintech/ML credit expansion offsetting bank tightening by 3-5pp in aggregate credit contraction), such that the aggregate credit impulse no longer responds to the policy rate in the textbook manner, and the Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey (SLOOS) increasingly understates total credit availability.** The top 5 hyperscalers hold ~$350-400B combined cash/equivalents and generate ~$300B+ combined annual free cash flow; a 200bp rate hike changes their WACC by 20-30bp, which is noise relative to the strategic return they assign to AI positioning. Simultaneously, fintech lenders using ML credit scoring now originate ~25-30% of consumer lending (up from ~10% in 2019), extending credit to segments that bank tightening would otherwise exclude. The net result: the investment channel is broken for the sector that matters most, and the consumer credit channel is structurally attenuated.

5. **AI-driven productivity measurement error is creating a persistent Taylor rule bias of 50-100bp toward overtightening, and unlike the 1990s episode (when Greenspan intuitively overrode the models), the current FOMC has neither the institutional culture nor the individual conviction to "lean against the data" in favor of an unobservable productivity shift.** BLS output-per-hour data remains structurally unable to capture AI productivity gains in services (~80% of GDP) due to: (a) 3-6 month reporting lags; (b) inadequate hedonic quality adjustment for AI-enhanced professional services; and (c) systematic undercounting of intangible capital formation (trained models, AI-optimized processes). Initial BLS productivity estimates during the late 1990s internet boom were revised upward by 0.5-1.0pp over subsequent years. If the same pattern holds, current measured productivity (~1.5-2.0% annualized) understates true productivity by 0.5-1.0pp, meaning the Taylor-implied rate is 50-100bp below the current policy rate. Fed Governors Waller and Kugler have acknowledged the measurement challenge in speeches, but the SEP longer-run GDP growth estimates show no upward revision that would indicate the Committee is acting on this awareness.

6. **Forward guidance credibility is entering a structurally degraded regime as SEP dot dispersion on the longer-run rate has widened from ~50bp (2017-2019) to ~125bp+ (2025-2026), reflecting the Committee's own uncertainty about the structural parameters that AI may be shifting — and the market is demanding compensation via a term premium increase of ~25-40bp in the 2Y-5Y sector attributable specifically to guidance uncertainty.** The ACM term premium model shows total term premium of ~60-90bp in the 10Y, with decomposition attributing roughly one-third (~20-30bp) to inflation uncertainty, one-third (~20-30bp) to fiscal supply/QT effects, and a residual one-third (~15-30bp) to forward guidance uncertainty — i.e., market participants demanding extra yield because the central bank's own projections are less informative. This forward guidance impairment is self-reinforcing: as the dots become noisier, markets rely more on their own models and less on Fed communication, reducing the Fed's ability to steer expectations without actual rate moves.

7. **The global monetary policy divergence from differential AI adoption is creating a structural USD bid of ~5-8% in REER terms above what cyclical interest rate differentials alone would justify, which imports disinflationary pressure to the US (dampening the inflation the Fed is watching) while exporting inflationary pressure to Europe and Japan (constraining the easing their economies need), producing a global policy coordination failure.** US AI capex as share of GDP (~1.0%) exceeds the eurozone (~0.15-0.2%) and Japan (~0.1-0.3%) by 4-7x. This differential maps to a productivity growth premium of 0.3-0.7pp in r*, a structurally wider US-EUR and US-JPY rate differential, and persistent capital inflows to US AI assets. The ECB faces a classic impossible-trinity bind: EUR depreciation from AI capital outflows imports inflation (~0.1-0.2pp CPI per 5% EUR depreciation), constraining rate cuts even as eurozone growth stagnates. The BoJ faces an amplified version of the same problem: the US-Japan r* divergence from AI compounds the existing divergence from balance sheet normalization (per iter_0013), pushing USD/JPY structurally above the ~145-155 range consistent with cyclical factors alone.

8. **The financial conditions index as a policy input is systematically distorted by AI-driven equity concentration: the cap-weighted S&P 500 shows conditions ~75-100bp easier than the equal-weighted index or small-cap equivalents, meaning the Fed is calibrating policy to the financial reality of the top decile of firms by market cap while the median firm — which employs ~65% of the labor force and generates ~55% of consumer spending — faces conditions that are 75-100bp tighter than the policy-relevant FCI suggests.** The equal-weighted S&P 500 has underperformed the cap-weighted index by ~1000-1500bp cumulatively over 2024-H1 2026, the widest divergence since 1999-2000. For the S&P 600 small-cap universe and the ~6M US private employers, conditions are tighter still: higher bank borrowing costs (SLOOS tightening), limited capital market access, and competitive margin pressure from AI-enabled large firms. This distributional distortion means: (a) the aggregate data the Fed watches (GDP, payrolls, ISM) will stay resilient longer than the median firm's experience would imply, because the AI tail drives the aggregate; (b) when the median firm deteriorates to the point of job losses, the signal arrives suddenly rather than gradually, creating a cliff risk that the Fed's framework is poorly equipped to anticipate.

9. **The AI energy demand channel is becoming a persistent cost-push inflation factor of ~0.1-0.3pp annualized on core CPI, with the critical characteristic that it is neither purely a supply shock (to be looked through) nor purely a demand shock (to be tightened against), but a structural reallocation shock that the Fed's aggregate instruments cannot efficiently address — and this ambiguity will bias the FOMC toward treating it as demand-driven, resulting in marginal overtightening.** Data center electricity demand is on track to reach ~8-12% of US generation by 2028-2030 (from ~4-5% in 2024), with PJM forward capacity auction prices already at ~$270/MW-day (up from ~$28 in 2022-2023) in data-center-heavy zones. The wholesale electricity price increase (~20-35% in constrained regions) transmits to CPI through the utility component (~3% weight) and indirectly through PPI energy inputs (~5-8% of production costs). The FOMC's instinct will be to treat rising energy costs in the context of full employment as demand-driven, because the Phillips curve framework interprets cost increases during full employment as evidence of excess demand rather than structural reallocation — even when the cost increase is driven by a specific sectoral investment boom rather than economy-wide overheating.

10. **The rate distribution for 2027-2028 terminal Fed Funds is bimodal (peaks near 2.0-2.5% in an AI bust scenario and 4.5-5.0% in an AI productivity realization scenario), not unimodal around the current OIS-implied ~3.25-3.75%, and swaption markets are systematically under-pricing this bimodality — particularly in the 2Y1Y to 5Y1Y sector — by ~15-25% in vol terms, making long straddles and conditional curve structures cheap relative to the true distribution of outcomes.** The scenario gap (200-300bp between the AI-bust terminal and the AI-realized terminal) exceeds 2 standard deviations of annualized implied vol in 2Y and 5Y swaptions, meaning the tails are fatter than the normal distribution embedded in Black-Scholes vol pricing. The specific mispricing is in the wings: 100bp out-of-the-money payer and receiver swaptions are priced as ~2-sigma events in a normal distribution but are arguably ~1.3-1.5 sigma events in the true bimodal distribution. Long 2Y1Y straddles, 2s10s conditional steepeners (steepen if rates fall below current OIS, flatten protection if rates rise above), and ratio receiver spreads offer convexity that is undercompensated at current vol levels.

11. **The Fed's 2025-2026 framework review is the most under-priced event risk in front-end rates: its outcome — particularly on the treatment of supply shocks and r* communication — will determine whether the AI productivity question is formally absorbed into the reaction function or remains an ad hoc judgment call, with 15-30bp of front-end repricing at stake on a substantive outcome.** The review could produce: (a) a symmetric FAIT modification that explicitly accommodates positive supply shocks (dovish for front end — fewer hikes needed during AI-driven growth); (b) an r* range communication that formalizes the uncertainty (hawkish for term premium — wider confidence intervals on the policy path); or (c) minimal changes that preserve the status quo (neutral, but implies the FOMC will continue flying blind on AI structural questions). Market pricing assigns near-zero premium to the review outcome, despite the 2020 FAIT precedent (10-15bp 2Y move, 18+ months of reshaped forward guidance expectations). The AI question makes the 2026 review significantly more consequential than 2020's because the structural shift under consideration (AI-driven r* change) is larger and more uncertain than the 2020 question (how to handle below-target inflation at the ZLB).

12. **The "AI Solow paradox" window — the 2-4 year period between massive AI investment and statistically visible productivity acceleration — is the highest-risk period for monetary policy error in a generation, because policy calibrated to lagging data will systematically over-tighten if AI works (penalizing a nascent productivity boom) or under-tighten if AI doesn't (accommodating a misallocated capex cycle), and the resolution will only become clear retrospectively.** Nonfarm business sector labor productivity growth has moderated to ~1.5-2.0% annualized in 2025-early 2026, after a post-pandemic spike to ~2.5-3.0% in 2023-2024. The moderation is consistent with four interpretations: (1) AI working but not yet visible in BLS data; (2) AI working in a few firms but not economy-wide; (3) AI not generating measurable returns; (4) post-pandemic normalization dominating. The FOMC will default to interpretation 4 (wait-and-see), which is indistinguishable from interpretation 1 in the data for another 2-3 years — but the policy implications are opposite. If AI follows the 1990s internet template, measured productivity should inflect upward by 2027-2028; if it follows the 1980s PC template, the inflection is in the 2030s. The difference between these timelines is a full interest rate cycle.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Inverted Transmission Impairment

The transmission mechanism framework identifies five channels: interest rate, credit, asset price (wealth effect), exchange rate, and expectations. In the current environment, three of the five are substantially impaired or counter-directional:

**Interest rate channel (partially impaired):** The pass-through from Fed Funds to deposit rates is functioning normally (~85-95% pass-through to money market and CD rates). But the pass-through to mortgage rates is distorted: the primary-secondary mortgage spread remains elevated at ~160-200bp (vs. ~100-120bp historical norm), reflecting originator capacity constraints and prepayment model uncertainty, which partly offset AI-related compression from automated underwriting. Net effect: the interest rate channel is operating at ~70-80% efficiency.

**Asset price channel (counter-directional):** The S&P 500 has appreciated ~40-55% from its October 2023 cycle low through early 2026, driven primarily by AI-related earnings expectations. The wealth effect from this appreciation (~$12-15T in household equity gains) is generating ~$360-750B in incremental consumption at a 3-5 cent MPC. This is a massive easing impulse running directly counter to the tightening intent of higher policy rates. The Fed would need an additional ~100-150bp of rate hikes purely to offset this wealth effect — clearly infeasible politically and questionable economically.

**Credit channel (bifurcated):** Bank lending standards per SLOOS have been tightening for 6-8 consecutive quarters. But total credit availability has contracted less than SLOOS would predict, because fintech/AI-scored lending has expanded its share from ~10% to ~25-30% of consumer origination, partially offsetting bank pullback. The investment credit channel is effectively broken for the AI sector's dominant firms, which are self-funding at ~$200-250B/year.

**Exchange rate channel (functioning but offset):** Dollar strength from AI-driven capital inflows is creating a modest disinflationary impulse via cheaper imports (~0.05-0.10pp CPI per 5% REER appreciation). This channel is actually working in the Fed's favor but is small relative to the asset price counter-impulse.

**Expectations channel (degraded):** Forward guidance is less informative due to SEP dot dispersion and structural parameter uncertainty. Markets are pricing the forward rate based increasingly on their own AI productivity assumptions rather than Fed communication, reducing the Fed's leverage over expectations without actual rate moves.

The FCI quantification: Taking the Goldman Sachs FCI decomposition, the equity component alone contributes ~75-100bp of easing relative to a counterfactual where equities traded at levels consistent with pre-2023 rate-equity relationships. Adding the partial credit channel offset (~15-25bp from fintech credit expansion) and subtracting the exchange rate channel contribution (~10-20bp of tightening from dollar strength), the net AI-related distortion is ~65-100bp of easing that the policy rate has not countered.

### Claim 2: The Unobservable Variable Trilemma

The FOMC's "data-dependent" framework is designed for cyclical adjustment around known structural parameters. AI disrupts this by introducing uncertainty about the structural parameters themselves:

**r* uncertainty:** The Laubach-Williams model produces a point estimate of r* at ~1.0-1.2% real, with a standard error of ~150bp. AI-related scenarios widen the effective range: if AI delivers 1.0pp TFP acceleration, r* is ~1.8-2.2% (using the standard Euler equation coefficient of 0.5-0.7 on TFP growth); if AI disappoints, r* remains at ~0.8-1.2%. The 100bp range between these scenarios exceeds the typical basis for a rate decision (25bp increments).

**Potential output uncertainty:** CBO potential GDP growth estimates are ~1.8-2.0% (2025-2028). If AI raises TFP by 0.5-1.0pp, potential growth is ~2.3-3.0%. The output gap — the key variable in the Taylor rule — shifts by 0.5-1.0pp of GDP, flipping the sign from "economy at potential" to "economy below potential" in the optimistic scenario. This is the difference between holding rates steady and cutting.

**NAIRU uncertainty:** The SEP longer-run unemployment estimate is ~4.0-4.2%. If AI-enhanced job matching (LinkedIn AI, Indeed, internal HR tools) reduces frictional unemployment by 0.3-0.5pp, NAIRU is ~3.5-3.8%, and the current unemployment rate (~3.8-4.1%) represents more slack than assumed. This could change the sign on the employment gap term in the Taylor rule.

The Greenspan precedent is relevant but not replicable. In 1996-2000, Greenspan personally overrode model-based Taylor rule prescriptions by ~50-75bp based on anecdotal evidence of productivity gains that did not yet appear in aggregate data. This judgment call was correct and is now regarded as masterful monetary policy. But it depended on: (a) Greenspan's unique personal authority (18 years as Chair by 1996); (b) a less transparent policy framework (no SEP, no dots, no press conferences); and (c) a smaller, more deferential FOMC. Current Chair Powell operates in a fundamentally different institutional context: more transparent, more consensus-driven, and more accountable to measured data. The structural conditions for a Greenspan-style override do not exist.

### Claim 3: The Deepening AI Put

The scale argument is critical and updated from prior analysis:

**Market cap of AI-linked equities (Q1 2026):** The Magnificent 7 plus the next tier of AI beneficiaries (Broadcom, AMD, ASML, TSMC, Oracle, Salesforce, Palantir) represent approximately $18-22T in combined market capitalization — larger than the total US residential mortgage market at its 2007 peak (~$11T) and roughly equivalent to US GDP.

**Wealth effect math in a drawdown:** A 35% correction in AI-linked equities would destroy $6-8T in wealth. At 3-5 cents MPC from equity wealth:
- Low estimate: $6T x 3% = $180B consumption hit = ~0.6% of GDP
- High estimate: $8T x 5% = $400B consumption hit = ~1.4% of GDP
- For comparison, the 2008 housing wealth destruction was ~$6T, producing a consumption drag of ~1.0-1.5% of GDP that required rates to zero plus $3.5T in QE.

**The asymmetry is more pronounced than in 1998-2001:**
- 1999-2000 tightening into the tech boom: 175bp (4.75% to 6.50%)
- 2001-2003 easing after the bust: 550bp (6.50% to 1.00%)
- Net asymmetry: ~375bp more easing than tightening over the full cycle

The current cycle will likely show similar or greater asymmetry because: (a) the AI equity market cap is ~5x larger relative to GDP than the 2000 tech peak; (b) household equity ownership is broader (retirement account penetration has risen from ~50% to ~60% of households); (c) the post-pandemic FOMC has demonstrated willingness to ease aggressively (150bp emergency cut in March 2020, zero rates plus $4.5T QE). The embedded "AI put" is not a forecast — it is a structural feature of a mandate that responds to wealth destruction but not wealth creation.

For the group as a whole, ~85-90% of AI capex is self-funded, rendering the investment channel of monetary policy inoperative for ~$200-250B/year of the most important investment in the economy.

The FOMC, which relies heavily on SLOOS as a credit conditions indicator, is systematically overestimating the degree to which its tightening is transmitting through the credit channel.

### Claim 5: Taylor Rule Bias from Productivity Mismeasurement

The measurement error operates through three mechanisms:

**Service sector quality adjustment:** When a radiologist uses AI to read scans 3x faster with 15% fewer false negatives, the BLS must attribute the improvement to either: (a) higher real output (more scans processed per hour = productivity gain), or (b) quality improvement not captured in the price deflator. BLS methods — which rely on establishment surveys reporting revenue per hour — will capture (a) with a lag of 1-3 quarters as billing data flows through, but will largely miss (b) because diagnostic accuracy improvement does not map cleanly to any price index. The net effect is systematic undercounting of AI productivity gains in services.

**Intangible capital formation:** AI generates valuable intangible assets: trained models, fine-tuned datasets, optimized algorithms, AI-redesigned business processes. The BEA capitalized software investment starting in 1999 and R&D starting in 2013, but there is no systematic capitalization of AI model training costs, AI-generated process improvements, or AI-specific intangible capital. Estimates from Corrado-Hulten-Sichel type frameworks suggest AI-related unmeasured intangible investment could be ~$50-100B/year (2025-2026), which would add ~0.2-0.4pp to measured investment and GDP growth.

**Revision patterns:** The BLS revised 1995-1999 labor productivity growth upward by a cumulative ~0.5-1.0pp over subsequent years as initial estimates proved too conservative. If history repeats, current measured productivity (1.5-2.0% annualized) could be revised to 2.0-3.0% once AI-related output is more fully captured. Each 0.5pp upward revision implies the Taylor-rule-appropriate rate is ~50bp lower, as the output gap is more negative than real-time data indicate.

The FOMC response gap: The SEP longer-run real GDP growth projection has remained at ~1.8-2.0% since 2022, despite the largest AI investment boom in history. This reflects the Committee's methodological conservatism — it will not upgrade potential growth until productivity statistics clearly demonstrate acceleration. This conservatism was nearly costly in the late 1990s and risks being costly again.

### Claim 6: Forward Guidance Credibility Metrics

**SEP dot dispersion (longer-run Fed Funds rate):**
- 2017-2019 average range: ~2.5-3.0% (dispersion ~50bp)
- 2022-2023 average range: ~2.5-3.5% (dispersion ~100bp)
- 2025-2026 average range: ~2.75-4.0% (dispersion ~125bp+)

The widening reflects genuine disagreement among FOMC participants about r*, with AI productivity uncertainty as a key driver of the divergence between hawks (r* rising from AI productivity) and doves (r* stable because AI productivity is not yet visible).

**ACM term premium decomposition (10Y):**
- Total term premium: ~60-90bp (Q1 2026)
- Inflation compensation component: ~20-30bp
- Fiscal/QT component: ~20-30bp
- Forward guidance uncertainty residual: ~15-30bp

The guidance uncertainty component has increased from near-zero (2019-2021, when FAIT provided clear forward guidance that was broadly believed) to ~15-30bp (2025-2026). This is the market's revealed-preference statement that FOMC communication is less informative.

**Market vs. Fed divergence:** The OIS-implied terminal rate exceeds the median SEP longer-run dot by ~50-100bp (Q1 2026), with the gap widening from ~25bp in 2023. This divergence reflects markets pricing in an AI-driven r* increase that the FOMC has not formally adopted — a direct measure of forward guidance credibility loss. When markets price 75bp+ above the stated neutral rate, the Fed's communication is no longer anchoring expectations; it is being overridden.

### Claim 7: Global Policy Divergence from AI Adoption

**US vs. Eurozone AI positioning (2025-2026):**
- AI R&D spending: US ~$100-120B/year vs. EU ~$10-15B/year
- Global top 100 AI companies by revenue: US ~65, EU ~8, China ~20, Rest ~7
- GPU deployment (Nvidia data center revenue by geography): US ~55-60%, EU ~10-12%
- AI patent filings (PCT, 2023-2025): US ~35%, China ~30%, EU ~12%, Japan ~8%

The EU AI Act adds compliance costs estimated at EUR 3-5B annually for frontier model developers, effectively precluding frontier AI development within the EU at current technology levels. This regulatory divergence reinforces the investment divergence.

**r* divergence implication:** If AI adds 0.5-1.0pp to US TFP growth and only 0.1-0.3pp to eurozone TFP growth, the r* gap widens by 0.3-0.5pp. Combined with existing structural differences, the equilibrium Fed Funds rate should exceed the ECB deposit rate by 100-175bp on structural grounds — wider than the current spread of ~75-125bp, suggesting EUR front-end rates are relatively overpriced or USD front-end rates are relatively underpriced.

**The BoJ amplification:** Japan's SME-heavy economy (99.7% of firms by count) faces structural AI adoption barriers: legacy IT systems, limited cloud infrastructure, aging workforce with lower digital fluency, and a 12-18 month lag in Japanese-language LLM development. The US-Japan productivity growth differential from AI could widen to 0.5-1.0pp, compounding the existing divergence from demographics and balance sheet normalization. USD/JPY is structurally pressured above 150-160 for the AI cycle's duration, requiring the BoJ to normalize faster than domestic conditions warrant purely to defend the currency.

### Claim 8: The Cap-Weighted FCI Distortion

**Equal-weight vs. cap-weight divergence:**
- S&P 500 cap-weighted return (Jan 2024 - Q1 2026): ~+45-55%
- S&P 500 equal-weighted return (Jan 2024 - Q1 2026): ~+20-30%
- Divergence: ~1000-1500bp cumulative, widest since 1999-2000
- S&P 600 small-cap return (same period): ~+10-20%

**Employment distribution vs. market cap distribution:**
- Top 10 S&P 500 firms: ~35% of index weight, ~3-4% of US total employment
- Remaining 490 S&P 500 firms: ~65% of index weight, ~15-18% of employment
- S&P 600 + Russell 2000 + private firms: ~0% of FCI equity weight, ~78-82% of employment

The FCI equity component captures the financial conditions experienced by firms employing ~3-4% of the workforce and extrapolates it to represent the economy. For the ~80% of workers employed by firms not in the cap-weighted equity universe, the FCI is systematically misleading.

**The cliff risk:** Because aggregate data (GDP, payrolls, ISM) is disproportionately influenced by the AI-beneficiary tail, it remains resilient until the non-AI majority deteriorates past a tipping point. Small business optimism surveys (NFIB) and regional Fed surveys already show more stress than aggregate data — but these leading indicators have lower weight in the FOMC's reaction function. The risk is nonlinear deterioration: aggregate data stays strong until median-firm stress triggers a cascade of layoffs, credit downgrades, and reduced spending that overwhelms the AI sector's positive contribution.

### Claim 9: AI Energy Demand as Persistent Cost-Push

**Updated energy demand trajectory:**
- 2024 data center electricity: ~200 TWh (~4-5% of US generation)
- 2026 projected: ~280-320 TWh (~6-7%)
- 2028 projected: ~400-500 TWh (~8-10%)
- 2030 projected: ~550-700 TWh (~10-14%)

**Electricity price transmission:**
- PJM capacity auction (2025-2026 delivery): ~$270/MW-day (vs. ~$28 in 2022-2023)
- ERCOT wholesale prices near data center clusters: up ~20-30% from 2023
- Direct CPI impact of 25% wholesale electricity increase: ~0.02-0.03pp on headline CPI
- Indirect PPI transmission: ~0.03-0.12pp through production cost pass-through
- Total CPI impact: ~0.05-0.15pp per year, persistent and potentially growing

This is small in a single year but cumulative: over 3-5 years, structural energy cost inflation from AI could add 0.15-0.50pp to the CPI level, significant relative to the FOMC's zero tolerance for persistent above-target inflation.

**The classification problem:** Rising energy costs in the context of full employment will be processed through the Phillips curve framework as demand-driven. The FOMC does not have a reliable method for distinguishing "energy costs rising because a specific sector is bidding up capacity" from "energy costs rising because the economy is overheating." The default classification biases toward tightening, even when the appropriate supply-side response is accommodation.

The current OIS curve prices ~3.4-3.7% terminal — consistent with "muddle through" as the central mode. But the tails are under-priced:

100bp OTM payer swaptions are priced as ~2.1-sigma events (~1.5% probability) in a normal distribution with ~80bp annualized vol. In the bimodal distribution, the AI Realized scenario (probability ~30-40%) produces terminal rates 100bp+ above forward, making this a ~30-40% probability event. The payer is under-priced by ~15-20x relative to the true distribution.

- Long 2Y1Y straddles (captures both tails)
- Long 2Y1Y risk reversals — buy OTM payer, sell OTM receiver (AI Realized tail is fatter than AI Bust tail)
- 2s10s conditional steepener: steepen if 2Y rates fall below 3.0% (AI bust → aggressive cuts); flatten protection if 2Y rates rise above 4.5% (AI realized → bear flattening)

### Claim 11: Framework Review as Catalytic Event

**Key decision points:**

**Supply shock treatment:** The core question is whether FAIT's average inflation targeting applies to supply-driven inflation deviations. If AI is a positive supply shock that lowers inflation while raising growth, strict FAIT implies seeking above-target inflation catch-up — which makes no economic sense when growth is already above potential. A revised framework distinguishing supply from demand deviations would ease the front-end path by 25-50bp.

**r* communication:** Formal uncertainty ranges (interquartile range rather than median) would institutionalize AI-related uncertainty. This transparency is double-edged: reduces false precision risk but increases term premium by signaling central bank uncertainty about its own destination.

**Real-time productivity measurement:** Formal incorporation of alternative indicators (firm-level AI adoption surveys, BEA experimental intangible capital accounts, real-time output data) would give the FOMC institutional cover to lean forward on AI productivity, as Greenspan did but with framework backing rather than personal judgment.

**Market impact potential:** The 2020 FAIT announcement moved 2Y rates 10-15bp and reshaped liftoff expectations by 12-18 months. A 2026 review substantively addressing AI supply shock treatment could move 2Y rates 15-30bp. Current OIS prices zero premium for this event.

### Claim 12: The Solow Paradox Policy Error Window

**Productivity data narrative (through Q1 2026):**
- 2019: ~1.2% nonfarm business sector labor productivity growth
- 2020-2021: volatile (~3-4%, compositional effects)
- 2022: ~0.5% (labor hoarding)
- 2023: ~2.5-3.0% (reallocation gains, early AI)
- 2024: ~2.0-2.5% (stabilization)
- 2025-Q1 2026: ~1.5-2.0% (moderation)

The 2025-2026 moderation is consistent with four interpretations:
1. AI working but not yet visible in BLS data (supports easing)
2. AI working in a few firms but not economy-wide (supports patience)
3. AI not generating measurable returns (supports tight policy)
4. Post-pandemic normalization dominating (supports wait-and-see)

The FOMC will default to interpretation 4, which is the safest for institutional credibility. But interpretation 4 is indistinguishable from interpretation 1 in the data for another 2-3 years — and the policy implications are opposite.

**The error cost asymmetry:** If AI works and the Fed overtightens, the cost is a delayed productivity boom — growth suppressed from 3.5% to 2.5%, AI adoption slowed by excessive financing costs. If AI doesn't work and the Fed under-tightens, the cost is a larger capex bust — more misallocated capital, more severe equity drawdown, deeper recession. Both errors are costly, but the first (overtightening during a productivity revolution) has historically been more persistent in its negative effects, as it delays the structural transformation that ultimately raises living standards.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Equity Cycles & Valuation Regimes
The "AI put" (Claim 3) provides a rates-derived floor for AI equity valuations by guaranteeing aggressive easing in a bust scenario — the monetary policy justification for compressed equity risk premia. The FCI distortion (Claim 8) implies equity-focused financial conditions are unreliable for assessing the economy's true cycle position. The bimodal rate distribution (Claim 10) maps to a bimodal equity framework: AI-realized supports 30-50x P/E; AI-bust implies 30-40% drawdown to pre-AI multiples.

### Growth Models & Cycle Positioning
The r* trilemma (Claim 2) is the monetary policy expression of the growth model uncertainty. If AI raises TFP, the growth model shifts from "secular stagnation" to "new economy." The Solow paradox timing (Claim 12) is the empirical test that resolves this. Credit channel bifurcation (Claim 4) suggests the current cycle has an unusual structure: the investment boom is concentrated in a rate-insensitive sector, meaning the traditional capex-rate feedback loop is impaired.

### Labor Market Structure & Wage Dynamics
AI's labor market impact is the most direct channel through which AI intersects the FOMC's Phillips curve. Labor displacement → lower NAIRU → dovish. Labor augmentation → higher potential output → dovish through different mechanism. Labor polarization → flatter Phillips curve → most challenging for policy because inflation stays sticky even as employment weakens.

### Currency Regimes & FX Dynamics
The global policy divergence (Claim 7) is a secular shift in currency fair value, not a cyclical carry trade. The AI-driven structural USD bid is additive to existing FX dynamics from fiscal divergence (iter_0011) and balance sheet normalization (iter_0013). ECB and BoJ constraints from AI capital outflows create FX dilemmas interacting with existing framework challenges.

### Fiscal Policy Divergence & Debt Sustainability
If AI boosts productivity and nominal GDP growth, the r-g differential improves, easing debt sustainability. But if AI requires large fiscal spending (retraining, infrastructure, safety nets), the numerator rises. The framework review (Claim 11) will shape how the FOMC treats this fiscal-monetary-AI interaction.

### Energy Transition Investment
AI energy demand (Claim 9) directly competes with the energy transition. Data centers favor 24/7 baseload power (natural gas, nuclear) over intermittent renewables. If AI demand absorbs grid capacity and investment capital, the transition is delayed, extending the inflationary period from transition costs.

### Volatility Regime Analysis
The bimodal distribution (Claim 10) implies a regime where realized vol should structurally exceed implied vol until the Solow paradox resolves. Swaption smile should be steeper, vol term structure upward-sloping, and cross-asset vol correlations elevated because AI affects rates, equities, FX, and credit simultaneously.

### Central Bank Balance Sheet Normalization (iter_0013)
The AI capex cycle interacts with QT: QT tightens conditions by removing liquidity while the AI boom loosens them by supporting equities and investment. If the AI boom sustains financial conditions, QT can proceed further without triggering market stress — the AI boom is effectively subsidizing QT by maintaining market function despite liquidity withdrawal.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the Fed construct an "AI-adjusted FCI" that strips out concentrated equity effects?** If the top 10 stocks drive 70% of the FCI equity component but employ only 3-4% of the workforce, the standard FCI is misleading. Is there a tractable employment-weighted or equal-weighted alternative that would change policy decisions?

2. **What is the empirical elasticity of hyperscaler AI capex to the cost of capital?** Is there a rate level (7%+ risk-free?) at which opportunity cost considerations (buybacks vs. capex) shift hyperscaler behavior? Finding this threshold defines when the investment channel "turns back on."

3. **How quickly can the BLS adapt measurement to capture AI productivity?** If measurement improves faster than the 1990s precedent (3-5 year lag), the policy error window shortens. If AI gains are harder to measure than internet gains, it lengthens.

4. **What is the MPC from AI equity wealth specifically?** If AI gains accrue primarily to founders and institutional investors who reinvest rather than consume, the wealth effect may be weaker than the standard 3-5 cent MPC — changing FCI calibration and reducing the AI easing impulse.

5. **How does the EU AI Act's regulatory drag interact with ECB policy options?** If regulation slows European AI adoption, does the resulting structural growth divergence become a permanent consideration for ECB policy — running structurally easier to offset the AI disadvantage?

6. **Does the AI capex cycle exhibit self-correcting or self-reinforcing dynamics?** If ROI disappoints, do boards pull back gradually (manageable for policy) or does competitive pressure force continued spending until sudden stop (disruptive for policy)?

7. **How should the FOMC weight conflicting sectoral signals?** NFIB small business optimism has diverged from large-firm confidence. If the AI bifurcation persists, should the FOMC explicitly weight median-firm indicators more heavily? Is there precedent for distributional weighting?

8. **What is the cross-country coordination mechanism for AI-driven policy divergence?** If AI productivity differentials create persistent capital flow imbalances, is there a coordinated response (analogous to the Plaza Accord), or does each central bank optimize independently and accept FX volatility?

9. **How does AI energy demand interact with central banks' own climate frameworks?** If data center power demand forces fossil fuel life extensions, central banks with climate mandates (ECB, BoE) face a tension between accommodating AI investment and their climate commitments — a constraint unique to non-US central banks.

10. **What signaling value does the framework review carry for AI policy incorporation?** If the review produces minimal changes, does the market interpret this as "the Fed is not adapting for AI" — and does that interpretation itself become a source of policy error risk by encouraging a cyclical toolkit for a structural problem?
