# Growth Models & Cycle Positioning — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The mainstream concept of "potential GDP" and the "output gap" are theoretically incoherent and empirically unreliable; growth is path-dependent and demand-determined, not supply-constrained around a predetermined equilibrium trend.** The Post-Keynesian position, following Kaldor's cumulative causation and the Verdoorn-Kaldor law, holds that productivity growth is endogenous to demand growth — aggregate demand does not merely fluctuate around a supply-determined trend but *creates* the trend itself. The repeated ex-post revisions to CBO potential GDP estimates (which consistently close toward actual GDP) are not measurement errors but evidence that the concept itself is flawed.

2. **The current US growth cycle (2023-2026) is primarily sustained by an extraordinary private-sector-deficit-absorbing fiscal posture — the federal deficit at ~6-7% of GDP is performing the Kalecki profit-sustaining function, masking private sector financial fragility rather than reflecting genuine productivity-driven expansion.** Using the sectoral balances identity (Private Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance = 0), the US private sector can only run financial surpluses if the government runs deficits exceeding the current account deficit. The current constellation — large government deficits, persistent current account deficits, and a private sector near balance — is meta-stable but structurally dependent on continued fiscal largesse.

3. **Leading recession indicators (yield curve inversion, ISM, Conference Board LEI) have lost reliability not because of "post-COVID distortion" but because the underlying monetary architecture has shifted: endogenous credit creation and fiscal flows now dominate the business cycle in ways these indicators were never designed to capture.** The yield curve inversion signal, for instance, assumes a loanable funds model where banks intermediate between savers and borrowers. Under endogenous money theory (Moore 1988, Lavoie 2014), banks create deposits *ex nihilo* when they lend — the curve's slope reflects term premium and rate expectations, not credit supply constraints. Its recession-signaling power was historically mediated through bank lending channels that have been partially disintermediated.

4. **The "soft landing" narrative is misconceived because it presupposes a Wicksellian natural rate framework where the central bank merely needs to calibrate the policy rate to a pre-existing equilibrium.** In the Post-Keynesian view, there is no such equilibrium to land on. What looks like a "soft landing" is more precisely a Kalecki-sustained profit floor combined with fiscal stimulus at historically anomalous levels. The question is not whether the economy will "land softly" but whether the current fiscal-credit configuration is generating the conditions for a Minsky moment when the fiscal impulse inevitably decelerates.

5. **Productivity growth from AI and technology will be structurally mediated by the distribution of income and the effective demand regime — supply-side potential is irrelevant without the demand-side channels to realize it.** Following Kalecki's dictum that "workers spend what they earn; capitalists earn what they spend," AI-driven productivity gains that accrue primarily to capital owners and are not recycled into wage income or fiscal transfers will produce a paradox of potential: higher technical capacity alongside demand deficiency. The historical precedent is the 1920s, where rapid mechanization coexisted with stagnant wages and rising private debt, culminating in the 1929 collapse.

6. **The credit impulse — the change in the flow of new credit as a percentage of GDP — is the single most important leading indicator for real economic growth, superior to any output-gap or NAIRU-based framework.** Private credit creation drives spending, income, and profit realization in a monetary production economy. When the credit impulse turns negative (credit is still growing but at a decelerating rate), demand growth slows before any real-sector indicator registers the change. The credit impulse peaked in mid-2024 and has been decelerating through 2025, signaling growth deceleration masked by fiscal offsets.

7. **The US business cycle should be analyzed not as oscillation around equilibrium but through a Minsky cycle lens: the economy has transitioned from hedge-financing-dominated growth (2010-2019) to speculative-financing-dominated growth (2020-present), with emerging Ponzi characteristics in private credit, CRE, and leveraged lending.** The Minsky Financial Instability Hypothesis holds that stability breeds instability — the extended expansion has encouraged progressively riskier financial structures. The shift is observable in rising private debt-to-GDP ratios, declining interest coverage ratios in leveraged lending, and the proliferation of payment-in-kind (PIK) toggle features in private credit.

8. **Deglobalization and supply chain restructuring represent a structural shift from a Kaldor-Verdoorn growth regime (where trade expansion drives productivity through scale effects) to a costlier, less productive fragmented regime — this is a permanent negative supply shock that mainstream models will misidentify as cyclical and attempt to "tighten through."** The Post-Keynesian view recognizes that international trade is not merely about comparative advantage but about increasing returns, cumulative causation, and the balance-of-payments constraint (Thirlwall's Law). Deglobalization breaks these virtuous circles and structurally reduces the growth rate consistent with external balance.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Path-dependence and the incoherence of potential GDP:**
The CBO's potential GDP estimates provide the strongest evidence against themselves. Between 2007 and 2015, the CBO revised potential GDP downward by approximately $1.7 trillion — not because of identified supply-side deterioration but simply because actual GDP failed to recover to the prior trend. This is hysteresis in the purest Keynesian sense: demand shortfalls destroy capacity rather than being self-correcting deviations from it. Kaldor (1957, 1966) formalized this through the Verdoorn-Kaldor Law, showing that manufacturing productivity growth is a positive function of output growth — not exogenous. Ball (2014) provided rigorous econometric evidence that the post-2008 shortfall in potential GDP directly tracked the demand shortfall, with each 1% of lost demand producing approximately 0.5% permanent loss of potential. The implication is devastating for mainstream cycle analysis: if potential GDP is endogenous to demand, then the "output gap" is not a meaningful concept — closing a demand shortfall doesn't create inflationary pressure against a fixed supply ceiling; it *creates new supply capacity*. Godley and Lavoie (2007) formalize this in stock-flow consistent (SFC) models where investment (which is demand) creates capacity (which is supply), making the separation between "demand" and "supply" analysis fundamentally misguided.

**Claim 2 — Sectoral balances reveal fiscal-dependence of growth:**
The sectoral financial balances identity is an accounting truism: the private sector balance + government balance + foreign balance = 0. As of late 2025, the approximate configuration is: government deficit ~6.5% of GDP, current account deficit ~3.5% of GDP, leaving the private sector running a surplus of approximately 3% of GDP. This is a crucial data point. In the run-up to 2008, the private sector was running a deficit of ~3-4% of GDP (households borrowing to consume, corporations leveraging for buybacks), which Wynne Godley identified in his 1999 Levy Institute strategic analysis as unsustainable — years before the crisis materialized. The current configuration is more favorable for the private sector's aggregate balance, *but only because the government deficit is historically enormous*. If fiscal consolidation reduces the deficit by even 2 percentage points of GDP (as DOGE-style spending cuts or sequestration might produce), the sectoral identity forces either: (a) a private sector balance deterioration toward deficit (unsustainable debt accumulation) or (b) a current account improvement (requires either dollar depreciation or demand contraction). Neither is benign. This is the Kalecki profit equation at work: corporate profits = investment + government deficit + net exports - household saving. With investment moderate, net exports negative, and household saving low, government deficits are doing the heavy lifting for profit realization.

**Claim 3 — Structural shift undermining traditional leading indicators:**
Traditional leading indicators were developed in a specific monetary regime — one where the Federal Reserve controlled the money supply (monetarist framework) or where the yield curve reflected bank lending profitability (spread banking model). Neither regime obtains today. The yield curve inversion of 2022-2024 was the deepest and longest since the 1980s, yet no recession materialized by early 2026. Post-Keynesian analysis explains this without resorting to "post-COVID distortion": (1) Endogenous money means banks lend based on creditworthiness and demand, not curve profitability — the net interest margin channel is real but not decisive when fee income, private credit arms, and non-bank lenders dominate marginal credit creation. (2) The fiscal deficit offset overwhelmed the monetary tightening signal — the Fed was tightening via rates, but the Treasury was loosening via deficit spending, and the net impulse to demand was stimulative. (3) The Conference Board LEI includes components (manufacturing hours, building permits, stock prices) that are increasingly divorced from aggregate demand in a services-dominant, financialized economy. Bibow (2010) and Wray (2015) have argued that flow-of-funds-based indicators — particularly the credit impulse, private debt service ratio, and sectoral balance trajectories — provide superior cyclical information because they track the actual mechanisms of demand creation and destruction.

**Claim 4 — The "soft landing" as category error:**
The "soft landing" concept assumes the economy is an airplane that can be brought gently to a stable state. The Post-Keynesian economy is more like a bicycle — it must keep moving (growing) or it falls. There is no stable "neutral" state to land on because the Wicksellian natural rate does not exist as a stable, observable parameter. What the mainstream calls a "soft landing" is a period where fiscal stimulus offsets monetary tightening sufficiently to prevent debt-deflation dynamics from cascading. The relevant question from a Minskyan perspective is: during this period of apparent stability, are financial structures becoming more fragile? The answer is clearly yes. Private credit AUM has grown from ~$800B in 2019 to ~$1.8T by early 2026, with declining underwriting standards. CLO issuance has maintained high volumes with increasingly aggressive structures. Corporate leverage in the Russell 2000 is at historically elevated levels, with more than 40% of small-cap companies unprofitable. These are classic late-Minsky characteristics: the surface appears stable (profits are maintained via fiscal flows), but the subsurface financial structure is increasingly speculative and proto-Ponzi. Minsky (1986) emphasized that stability itself is destabilizing because it validates risk-taking: extended periods without recession encourage the very leverage and maturity mismatch that produce the next crisis.

**Claim 5 — AI productivity and the demand-side constraint:**
The Solow residual / total factor productivity framework treats technological progress as manna from heaven that automatically translates into higher output and living standards. The Post-Keynesian critique, following Kaldor and Robinson, insists that technical potential must be realized through effective demand. AI capex is currently running at approximately $200B+ annually from hyperscalers alone (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta), but the revenue realization from this investment remains highly uncertain. From a Kaleckian perspective, this spending is itself demand — it sustains profits in the semiconductor, data center, and infrastructure sectors regardless of whether AI delivers productivity gains. The risk is a classic investment-led boom that becomes self-referential: AI capex drives earnings that justify AI valuations that drive further AI capex. When (not if) the marginal return on AI capex disappoints relative to expectations, the spending contraction creates a demand gap that the productivity gains (which may be real but diffuse) cannot fill because they are spread across the economy while the demand contraction is concentrated. The historical parallel to the 1990s telecom buildout is instructive: massive infrastructure investment drove the boom, and the bust came not from technology failure but from the demand contraction when investment slowed. Moreover, if AI-driven productivity gains predominantly displace labor income rather than augmenting it, Kalecki's wage-profit dynamics imply that aggregate demand will be constrained by the shift from high-MPC wage income to low-MPC profit income — a structural underconsumptionist pressure.

**Claim 6 — Credit impulse as the superior leading indicator:**
The credit impulse framework, developed by Biggs, Mayer, and Pick (2009) and extended by Keen (2011, 2017), rests on a fundamental Post-Keynesian insight: in a monetary production economy, aggregate demand = income + change in debt. Therefore, the *acceleration* of credit (the credit impulse) is what drives changes in demand growth. When credit is growing at an increasing rate, demand exceeds income (boom). When credit growth decelerates, demand growth falls below income growth (slowdown). When credit contracts absolutely, demand collapses below income (bust). This framework correctly predicted: (a) the 2008 crisis (private credit impulse turned sharply negative in 2007), (b) the 2010-2011 European sovereign debt crisis (credit impulse collapsed in periphery), (c) the 2020 recovery (massive fiscal + credit impulse), and (d) the 2023-2024 resilience (fiscal impulse offset negative credit impulse from rate hikes). The current read: the bank lending credit impulse has been decelerating since mid-2024, but non-bank credit (private credit, direct lending) has partially offset this. The key monitoring variable is the *total* credit impulse including shadow banking — BIS data suggests this is still positive but declining, consistent with growth deceleration in H2 2026.

**Claim 7 — Minsky cycle classification of current expansion:**
Minsky (1986) classified borrowers into three categories: *Hedge* units (cash flows cover both principal and interest), *Speculative* units (cash flows cover interest but not principal, requiring debt rollover), and *Ponzi* units (cash flows cover neither, requiring asset price appreciation or new borrowing to service existing debt). The transition from hedge-dominated to speculative-dominated to Ponzi-dominated is the "financial instability hypothesis." Evidence for the speculative-to-Ponzi transition in the current cycle: (1) In leveraged loans, the share of covenant-lite issuance exceeds 90% — removing the early warning mechanisms that would normally force restructuring before Ponzi dynamics set in. (2) In private credit, PIK toggle features allow borrowers to capitalize interest rather than paying cash — this is the definitional Ponzi characteristic (borrowing to pay interest). (3) In commercial real estate, many office properties have market values below their outstanding debt, with owners making interest payments from reserves or additional borrowing in anticipation of a recovery that may not come. (4) In the Russell 2000, a significant share of companies depend on continued access to capital markets rather than operating cash flows for survival — they are speculative units at best, Ponzi units at worst. The aggregate private debt-to-GDP ratio has declined from its 2008 peak but remains historically elevated at approximately 150%, and — critically — the *composition* has shifted toward riskier structures even as the level has modestly improved.

**Claim 8 — Deglobalization as structural growth regime downshift:**
Thirlwall's Law (1979) states that a country's long-run growth rate is constrained by its balance of payments: growth = (export growth) / (income elasticity of demand for imports). Globalization relaxed this constraint by expanding export markets, enabling scale economies (Kaldor-Verdoorn), and providing cheap imported inputs that boosted real incomes. Deglobalization reverses each of these channels. Nearshoring and friendshoring increase production costs by 15-30% according to McKinsey and Kearney estimates, reducing the real income dividend from trade. Export market fragmentation (BRICS trade blocs, tariff escalation) reduces the denominator of the Thirlwall ratio. Supply chain redundancy (building parallel facilities in "friendly" jurisdictions) represents investment spending that maintains rather than expands capacity — it is maintenance capex disguised as growth capex. From the sectoral balance perspective, reduced current account deficits (if they materialize) would mechanically reduce the fiscal deficit required to sustain a given private sector balance — potentially easing one constraint. But the transition path is deflationary for global demand, as each country attempts to simultaneously export more and import less (a fallacy of composition that Keynes identified in the 1930s). The mainstream response to the resulting growth slowdown and inflation spike will likely be to identify a smaller "output gap" and declare the economy "at potential" — confusing a structural demand-side deterioration for supply-side normalization.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary Policy Transmission:** The Post-Keynesian critique of growth models is inseparable from monetary policy analysis. If money is endogenous (created by banks when they lend), then the central bank does not control the money supply and cannot steer the economy to a "potential GDP" path via interest rate manipulation. The transmission mechanism runs from credit conditions (bank lending willingness + borrower creditworthiness) to demand to output to employment — not from money supply to prices to output. This means the Fed's rate hikes work primarily by increasing debt service burdens (a balance sheet channel) and discouraging marginal borrowers, not by constraining a loanable funds supply. The growth model is inherently monetary.

**Credit Cycles:** Growth models and credit cycles are the same phenomenon viewed from different angles. In the Post-Keynesian view, the business cycle *is* a credit cycle. Aggregate demand = income + change in debt (Keen 2011). Growth accelerates when credit accelerates; growth decelerates when credit decelerates; recession occurs when credit contracts. The private debt-to-GDP ratio is therefore the fundamental state variable of the growth model — not TFP, not the capital stock, not the labor force participation rate. The current credit cycle position (decelerating bank lending, expanding but fragile non-bank credit, elevated private debt service ratios) maps directly onto the growth outlook.

**Fiscal Policy & Sovereign Debt:** The sectoral balances framework makes fiscal policy the central determinant of the private sector's financial health and hence the growth rate. Government deficits = private sector surpluses (adjusted for foreign balance). The mainstream obsession with government debt-to-GDP ratios is misplaced when applied to a currency issuer like the US — the binding constraint is inflation, not solvency. However, the *composition* of fiscal spending matters: investment spending with high Kalecki multipliers (infrastructure, green transition) sustains growth differently than transfer payments or tax cuts that may leak into financial asset accumulation. DOGE-style spending cuts are contractionary regardless of their "efficiency" rationale because they directly reduce the fiscal component of the Kalecki profit equation.

**Equity Cycles:** Equity valuations are not independent of the growth model — they are an artifact of it. Corporate profits are determined by the Kalecki equation (profits = investment + government deficit + net exports - household saving + capitalist consumption). The fiscal deficit is currently doing the heavy lifting for profit realization, meaning equity valuations embed an implicit assumption about the persistence of large deficits. If the growth model shifts (fiscal consolidation, credit contraction), profit realization falls first, and equity markets follow. The disconnect between equity valuations and underlying financial fragility is the classic Minsky paradox: the best time for profits and stock prices is the moment of maximum subsurface fragility.

**Demographics:** Demographic shifts directly alter the sectoral balance dynamics underlying growth. Aging populations increase the household saving rate (life-cycle hypothesis, with Post-Keynesian modifications for distributional effects), requiring either larger government deficits or larger current account surpluses to maintain private sector balance. Japan since the 1990s is the canonical example: an aging, high-saving private sector required persistent government deficits to prevent deflationary collapse. The Post-Keynesian growth model for aging economies is straightforwardly Kaldorian: slower labor force growth reduces demand growth, which reduces productivity growth (via Verdoorn's Law), which reduces potential growth — a demand-led secular stagnation that mainstream models misidentify as supply-determined.

**Labor Market Dynamics:** The Post-Keynesian rejection of NAIRU has direct implications for growth models. If there is no unique "natural rate of unemployment" toward which the economy self-corrects, then labor market tightness is not inflationary *per se* — it depends on the relative bargaining power of labor and capital, the markup pricing behavior of firms, and the wage-productivity growth relationship. The current labor market can sustain lower unemployment without accelerating inflation if (a) productivity growth absorbs wage growth, (b) profit margins compress rather than firms passing costs through, or (c) supply-side improvements (immigration, participation) expand capacity. The NAIRU framework's blind spot is that it treats the labor market as a constraint on growth rather than as an outcome of the growth process.

**Deglobalization:** As elaborated in Claim 8, deglobalization fundamentally alters the growth model by reimposing Thirlwall's balance-of-payments constraint that decades of trade liberalization had relaxed. This connects to inflation regimes (cost-push from supply chain restructuring), commodity supercycles (critical minerals bottlenecks in a fragmented trade regime), and sovereign debt (fiscal costs of industrial policy and supply chain subsidization).

## Open Questions

1. **What is the precise magnitude of the Kalecki fiscal multiplier in the current US economy — specifically, how much of current corporate profit growth is attributable to the fiscal deficit versus organic private demand?** The sectoral accounting is clear, but the behavioral pass-through (how quickly and completely deficit changes transmit to profits) has significant uncertainty. A structural VAR approach using flow-of-funds data could provide estimates, but the regime-contingent nature of the multiplier makes point estimates unreliable.

2. **Can Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis be operationalized into a quantitative fragility index that provides actionable timing signals, or is it inherently a qualitative structural framework?** Prior work (Minsky framework in knowledge base) suggests negative expected value as a timing signal but positive value as structural mapping. Is there a middle ground — e.g., monitoring the share of Ponzi units in leveraged lending as a quantitative proxy for Minsky stage position?

3. **How does the rise of non-bank credit creation (private credit, fintech lending, stablecoins) alter the credit impulse framework?** If a growing share of credit creation occurs outside the banking system and thus outside traditional flow-of-funds data, the credit impulse measure may be systematically underestimating total credit growth and therefore overestimating the economy's sensitivity to bank lending conditions. What is the total credit impulse including shadow banking, and how does it compare to the bank-only measure?

4. **Is the Verdoorn-Kaldor demand-led productivity mechanism strong enough in a services-dominant economy to generate meaningful hysteresis effects, or is it primarily a manufacturing phenomenon?** If demand-led productivity growth operates weakly in services (which is plausible — baristas don't get faster with more customers), then the Post-Keynesian growth model must rely more on the investment-capacity channel than the productivity-demand feedback loop. This would weaken Claim 1 somewhat.

5. **What would a Godley-style strategic analysis project for the US sectoral balances over 2026-2028 under different fiscal scenarios (DOGE cuts, status quo, further stimulus)?** This is the single most valuable analytical exercise for growth forecasting from a Post-Keynesian perspective. The scenarios should trace the forced adjustments in private sector balances under each fiscal path, identifying which configuration leads to unsustainable private sector deficit spending (the precondition for a Minsky crisis).

6. **How should the Post-Keynesian growth model incorporate AI-driven productivity changes that may be genuinely supply-side and exogenous to demand?** The framework's strength is endogenizing productivity to demand, but AI may represent a technological discontinuity large enough to break this relationship. If AI genuinely enables orders-of-magnitude productivity improvements in specific sectors, the growth model needs a mechanism for how this supply-side shock transmits through the demand structure — the distributional channel (who captures the gains) becomes the critical variable.

7. **Is the current private debt-to-GDP ratio of ~150% sustainable given the elevated interest rate environment, or does the debt service ratio imply an imminent credit crunch?** BIS data on debt service ratios should be the first-order monitoring variable, but the distribution matters enormously — aggregate DSR can be moderate while marginal borrowers (private credit, leveraged loans, CRE) face insolvency. What is the distributional structure of the debt service burden?
