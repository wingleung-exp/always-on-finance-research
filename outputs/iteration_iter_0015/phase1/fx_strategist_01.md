# AI & Technology Disruption — FX Regime & Dollar Cycle Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The dollar cycle has entered a "capex-sustained overvaluation" phase that is analytically distinct from a rate-differential-driven strong dollar regime — AI investment inflows are financing the twin deficit independently of Fed policy, extending dollar overvaluation 2-4 years beyond what interest rate convergence alone would support.** As of early 2026, the dollar remains 8-15% overvalued on BEER metrics despite the Fed having cut rates by 100-150bps from the 2023-2024 peak, and despite the twin deficit (fiscal ~6-7% of GDP, current account ~3-4% of GDP) widening to levels historically associated with dollar cyclical peaks. The resolution of this puzzle is the capital account: net financial inflows to US equities and direct investment, heavily concentrated in AI-adjacent assets, are running at approximately $300-400 billion annually, more than covering the current account gap. This is the "capex dollar" — a technology-investment-driven capital flow channel that operates orthogonally to the rate differential cycle. The critical implication is that standard FX models that rely on rate differentials and purchasing power parity will systematically underestimate the dollar during AI capex booms and overestimate it during capex busts, because they omit the dominant flow channel. The dot-com precedent (1997-2001) confirms this mechanism: the DXY appreciated ~25% during a period when the Fed was not systematically tighter than peers, driven by equity portfolio inflows chasing the "new economy" story.

2. **The Kalecki sectoral balances identity, applied to the external balance, reveals that the sustainability of the dollar's current level depends entirely on whether AI capex inflows are hedge-financed (self-sustaining) or speculative/Ponzi-financed (requiring continued narrative momentum) — and the evidence increasingly favors the latter.** The sectoral identity (Private Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance = 0) implies that the US current account deficit (foreign sector surplus) is the mirror image of the combined domestic private and government net lending positions. Currently, the government deficit (~6.5% of GDP) is doing the heavy lifting per the Kalecki profit equation, and a significant portion of the capital inflows financing this deficit are attracted by AI-related equity returns rather than by safe-haven or interest rate considerations. These are not "sticky" flows — they are return-chasing portfolio allocations that will reverse when the expected return differential closes. The Minsky classification matters: if AI capex is hedge-financed (hyperscalers funding from operating cash flow, which they largely are at the firm level), the capex cycle can persist independently of financial market conditions. But the capital inflows financing the twin deficit are speculative in the Minskyan sense — they depend on continued equity price appreciation and narrative momentum rather than on a fixed contractual return. A distinction must be drawn between the microeconomic sustainability of hyperscaler capex (strong) and the macroeconomic sustainability of the capital inflows that finance the external balance (fragile). The dollar depends on the latter, not the former.

3. **AI technology concentration has created a structural "dollar invoicing premium" in the global technology supply chain that reinforces the dominant currency paradigm (Gopinath 2019) and represents the single most powerful countervailing force to reserve diversification-driven dedollarization.** The US controls approximately 65-70% of global hyperscale cloud infrastructure revenue, dominates AI model development, and controls the GPU design ecosystem through NVIDIA's ~80%+ market share in AI accelerators. Global adoption of AI — by European banks, Asian manufacturers, Latin American fintechs — generates recurring USD-denominated payments to US cloud providers. These are structural current account inflows (technology services exports) that are sticky, growing, and resistant to policy-driven diversification because switching costs are prohibitive (data gravity, model ecosystem lock-in, API compatibility). The magnitude is significant and growing: US cloud services exports are estimated at $100-150 billion annually and growing at 20-25% per year, driven by AI adoption. This "technology invoicing premium" operates through the same mechanism Gopinath identified for trade invoicing — dominant currency pricing creates self-reinforcing demand for the invoicing currency — but extends it into the digital economy where the network effects are even stronger. Central bank reserve diversification (declining USD share at ~0.5-0.7pp per year per IMF COFER data) is being offset by private-sector technology-driven dollar demand that is an order of magnitude larger in flow terms.

4. **The AI-driven energy demand channel will produce a measurable divergence in G10 current accounts by 2027-2028 that is not currently priced into cross-rates, creating structural FX opportunities in energy-exporter vs. energy-importer currency pairs.** AI data center electricity demand is projected to reach 500-1,000 TWh globally by 2028-2030 (IEA), equivalent to 2-4% of current global generation. The current account impact is asymmetric across G10 economies: the US (net energy exporter post-shale) is approximately neutral; Norway (massive energy exporter) and Canada (natural gas exporter to US data centers) benefit from structural demand uplift; Europe (55-60% energy import-dependent) and Japan (90% energy import-dependent) face current account deterioration. The incremental energy import bill for AI-intensive European economies could reach EUR 15-30 billion annually by 2028, representing a 0.1-0.2% of GDP current account drag. While modest in isolation, this adds to existing structural drags (defense spending increases, green transition costs) and operates in the same direction — widening the current account divergence between the US/commodity exporters and Europe/Japan. The FX implication is a structural bid for NOK, CAD, and AUD against EUR and JPY that will emerge gradually but persistently over the next 2-3 years, distinct from (and additive to) the traditional commodity currency cyclical trade.

5. **The US-China technology bifurcation has created a "captive flow" premium for USD assets that is worth approximately 3-5% of dollar overvaluation — but this premium is vulnerable to a Chinese AI competitiveness shock (DeepSeek-class events) that could trigger a rapid unwinding of the captive flow channel and produce a 5-7% dollar correction concentrated against Asian currencies.** US semiconductor export controls and outbound investment restrictions have redirected approximately $200-400 billion in cumulative portfolio flows from Chinese to US technology equities since 2021. This "friendshoring premium" operates through the capital account: global allocators who require technology exposure but face ESG, fiduciary, or geopolitical constraints on China allocation are forced into US equities. The FX consequence is a structural bid for USD and structural offer for CNY that is independent of macroeconomic fundamentals. However, this premium is vulnerable to disruption: the January 2025 DeepSeek event demonstrated that Chinese AI capabilities are closer to the frontier than the market had priced, and the CNY appreciated approximately 1% in the week following the announcement. A sustained demonstration of Chinese AI competitiveness — either through model performance, cost efficiency (DeepSeek's training cost advantage), or hardware independence — could trigger a partial reversal of the captive flow dynamic. The risk is asymmetric: the captive flow premium was built gradually over 4-5 years but could unwind over 6-12 months as allocation mandates adjust, producing a discontinuous dollar weakening against CNY, KRW, and TWD.

6. **The FX market's vol surface is mispricing AI-related tail risks: the structural compression of G10 realized volatility by algorithmic market-makers (AI-powered liquidity provision) has suppressed implied volatility to levels that understate the probability of a capex-bust-driven dollar regime change — creating an asymmetric opportunity to buy dollar-bearish optionality cheaply.** AI-powered principal trading firms (Citadel Securities, XTX Markets, Jump) now provide the majority of G10 FX liquidity, compressing bid-ask spreads by 50-60% since 2010 and reducing normal-regime realized volatility. This compression feeds directly into implied volatility pricing: 3-month EUR/USD implied vol has averaged 6-8% in recent years vs. 10-12% in the pre-algorithmic era. However, the tails of the distribution have not compressed — the January 2019 JPY flash crash, the 2022 GBP LDI crisis, and the October 2016 sterling flash crash demonstrate that liquidity withdrawal by correlated AI systems produces extreme moves during stress. The FX vol surface therefore understates the probability of a dollar regime change event (the transition from the right side to the middle of the dollar smile) because it calibrates to the AI-compressed normal regime while the regime change mechanism is inherently discontinuous. For trade expression: long-dated (6-12 month) EUR/USD and AUD/USD call spreads are cheap relative to the probability-weighted payout of a capex-bust-driven dollar correction.

7. **AI-driven automation of cognitive-routine services exports represents a slow-moving but structurally significant negative terms-of-trade shock for BPO-exporting emerging market economies (India, Philippines), with implications for INR and PHP real exchange rate equilibrium that are not yet reflected in 3-5 year forward curves.** India's IT-BPO services exports (~$200B annually, ~6% of GDP) and the Philippines' BPO sector (~$35B, ~8% of GDP) represent the primary current account support for these currencies. AI automation of data processing, customer service, basic coding, and back-office functions threatens to erode this comparative advantage over a 5-10 year horizon. Within the BEER framework, this is equivalent to a commodity price decline for a commodity exporter — it reduces the equilibrium real exchange rate. McKinsey estimates 60% of current work activities could be automated by 2030-2040, with cognitive-routine tasks (which constitute the core of BPO exports) most vulnerable. The FX market is not pricing this: 5-year INR NDF forwards imply ~3-4% annual depreciation, roughly consistent with the interest rate differential and historical depreciation trend, but do not embed a structural shift in the equilibrium exchange rate. If AI displaces even 20-30% of BPO-exported tasks over the next decade, India's services export growth could decelerate from 8-10% to 2-4%, reducing the current account surplus that anchors INR. The timeline is uncertain but the direction is unambiguous.

8. **The dollar cycle's most probable path over 2026-2030 is a "plateau-then-correction" pattern analogous to 1999-2003: AI capex sustains overvaluation for 1-3 more years, followed by a 15-25% correction when the capex cycle peaks and the twin deficit becomes the binding constraint — with the correction's timing determined by AI revenue monetization rather than by Fed policy.** I assign approximately 55% probability to this "plateau-then-correction" scenario, 25% probability to an "extended overvaluation" scenario (AI productivity materializes faster than base-rate diffusion would suggest, partially validating the dollar level), and 20% probability to an "early correction" scenario (capex disappointment in 2026-2027 triggers faster unwind). The key signpost is the hyperscaler capex-to-AI-revenue ratio: the current ratio of approximately 4-6x ($200-250B capex vs. $40-60B identifiable AI revenue) must decline toward 1.5-2.0x by 2028-2030 for the capex cycle to be sustained. Goldman Sachs' framework requires $300-400B in annual AI revenue by 2028-2030 to justify current capex; any indication this target is unreachable would accelerate the correction timeline. The correction mechanism operates through the capital account: capex disappointment → equity derating → portfolio outflows → capital account deficit or surplus reduction → twin deficit becomes binding → dollar correction. The dot-com precedent suggests the DXY could decline from ~103-107 toward the low-to-mid 80s on a trade-weighted basis — a 20-25% move over 3-5 years — with the correction disproportionately benefiting EUR, JPY, and commodity currencies.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Capex-sustained overvaluation:**
The empirical case rests on decomposing the capital account. BEA data show net financial account inflows to US equities of approximately $200-350 billion annually in 2024-2025, with technology sector concentration. The Treasury International Capital (TIC) data show foreign net purchases of US equities averaging $40-60 billion per month in 2025, with the technology-heavy NASDAQ receiving disproportionate allocation. This equity capital inflow channel is distinct from the traditional fixed income inflow (foreign purchases of US Treasuries) that the rate differential model captures. The disconnect from rate differentials is visible: the Fed has cut rates by 100-150bps since the 2023-2024 peak, narrowing the rate differential with the ECB (which has also cut but started from a lower level). Standard uncovered interest rate parity (UIP) models would predict dollar weakening as the rate differential narrows. Yet the DXY has remained in the 103-107 range, trading above the UIP-implied level. The residual is the capex dollar — the technology-driven equity capital inflow that does not respond to rate differentials. The dot-com parallel is precise: from 1998-2000, the DXY appreciated ~15% despite the ECB's rate being broadly competitive with the Fed funds rate, because portfolio equity inflows (peaking at $180 billion in 2000, equivalent to $350-400 billion in today's nominal terms) dominated the capital account. Gourinchas and Rey (2007) documented this "venture capitalist" function of the US — borrowing cheaply (exorbitant privilege) and attracting foreign capital into high-growth assets. AI capex extends this framework into its current phase.

**Claim 2 — Kalecki-Minsky FX nexus:**
The integration of Kalecki's sectoral balances with Minsky's financial instability hypothesis, applied to FX, represents a deepening of the iter_0014 analysis. The key insight from macro_strategist_02's Post-Keynesian framework is that the fiscal deficit (~6.5% of GDP) is performing the Kalecki profit-sustaining function — corporate profits would collapse without the fiscal largesse. For FX, this has a direct implication: the current account deficit (the foreign sector's surplus in the sectoral identity) is financed by capital inflows that are attracted by the profits generated by the fiscal deficit. This creates a peculiar circularity: the government borrows → foreigners buy USTs and US equities → the capital inflow finances the current account deficit → the current account deficit allows the private sector to run a surplus → the surplus sustains the narrative of US economic exceptionalism → foreigners are attracted to buy more US assets. This is a self-reinforcing loop that can persist as long as confidence in the narrative holds. The Minskyan fragility lies not in the hyperscaler balance sheets (which are genuinely strong — hedge-financed in Minsky terms) but in the macro-level capital flow arrangement: the flows financing the twin deficit are speculative because they depend on continued equity price appreciation and the expectation of future AI-driven productivity gains that have not yet materialized in the data. The micro-macro distinction is crucial: NVIDIA and Microsoft are individually sound, but the aggregate capital flow pattern they attract is fragile.

**Claim 3 — Dollar invoicing premium in technology:**
Gopinath (2019) and Gopinath and Stein (2021) formalized the dominant currency paradigm: ~40% of global trade is invoiced in USD despite the US representing only ~10-12% of global trade. The mechanism is self-reinforcing — firms prefer to invoice in the currency their competitors use, creating a coordination equilibrium around the dollar. AI extends this into the technology services domain: cloud infrastructure, AI model APIs, GPU procurement, and software licensing are overwhelmingly USD-invoiced. Synergy Research data show US hyperscalers controlling ~65% of global cloud infrastructure revenue (~$350 billion in 2025), growing at 20-25% annually. When a German automaker deploys AI-powered quality inspection, it pays Microsoft Azure in USD. When an Indian fintech uses GPT-4 for credit scoring, it pays OpenAI in USD. These are not one-time purchases but recurring subscription revenues — structural current account flows that grow with global AI adoption. The IMF COFER data show central bank USD reserve share declining from ~65% in 2016 to ~58% in 2024, a pace of ~0.5-0.7pp per year. But this is reserve diversification — a stock adjustment by central banks managing $12-13 trillion in reserves. The technology invoicing flows are private-sector flows that are an order of magnitude larger in cumulative impact because they represent ongoing demand growth rather than marginal rebalancing of a fixed stock. The dedollarization narrative, which focuses on reserve composition, systematically underweights this private-sector technology channel.

**Claim 4 — AI energy and G10 current account divergence:**
The IEA's 2024 report projects data center electricity consumption reaching 1,000+ TWh globally by 2030. Allocating this by data center capacity share: US ~35-40% (350-400 TWh), Europe ~15-20% (150-200 TWh), Asia ~25-30% (250-300 TWh). For Europe, which imports 55-60% of its energy, the incremental electricity demand for AI data centers translates to approximately EUR 15-30 billion in additional energy imports annually by 2028 (assuming 200 TWh x 50-60% import share x EUR 80-120/MWh marginal cost). For Japan, the figure is JPY 1.5-3.0 trillion ($10-20 billion) in additional LNG imports. These are modest relative to total trade (EU goods exports ~EUR 2.5 trillion), but they operate on the margin of the current account, which for the eurozone has been running at approximately EUR 300-400 billion surplus. The erosion is directional and persistent. For Norway, whose government pension fund (NBIM) is already the world's largest sovereign wealth fund, AI-driven gas demand represents an extension of the hydrocarbon revenue stream that was supposed to be secularly declining. The NOK's undervaluation (15-20% below PPP) makes it a structural beneficiary of any current account improvement channel. For CAD, the US buildout of data centers in adjacent border states (which draw Canadian hydroelectric power) represents a new energy export channel.

**Claim 5 — Captive flow premium and China risk:**
The quantification of the captive flow premium is based on counterfactual analysis. Pre-decoupling (2016-2020), foreign portfolio allocation to China was approximately $800 billion-$1 trillion across equities and fixed income, growing rapidly. Post-decoupling (2021-2025), this allocation has stagnated or declined (MSCI's partial China exclusion, US government pension fund restrictions, European ESG constraints). The redirected capital — conservatively $200-400 billion cumulatively — has flowed primarily to US technology equities. Using an elasticity of capital flows to the exchange rate of approximately 1.0-1.5% per $100 billion in annual flows (estimated from the IMF's portfolio balance model), this captive flow is worth approximately 3-5% of dollar strength. The vulnerability to a Chinese AI shock is demonstrated by the DeepSeek episode (January 2025): when DeepSeek-R1 demonstrated frontier-competitive reasoning capabilities at a fraction of US training costs, US technology equities sold off by 3-5% intraday, NVIDIA fell ~17% in one session, and the CNY strengthened ~1% within a week. The market priced a partial reversal of the captive flow premium within hours of the announcement. A sustained series of Chinese AI competitiveness demonstrations — or a fundamental breakthrough in domestic semiconductor capability (SMIC, Huawei's Ascend chips) — could trigger a larger and more durable reversal.

**Claim 6 — Vol surface mispricing:**
BIS Triennial Survey (2022) data show electronic trading at 75-80% of spot FX volume in major pairs. Principal trading firms (PTFs) — many powered by AI/ML systems — provide the majority of continuous liquidity. Research by Breedon et al. (2023) documents the dual effect: compressed normal-regime volatility (average realized vol in EUR/USD declined from ~10% to ~7% over the past decade) alongside increased tail severity (flash events in 2015 CHF, 2016 GBP, 2019 JPY). The vol risk premium (VRP) — the difference between implied and realized volatility — has compressed from ~2-3% to ~1-1.5% in G10 pairs, reflecting the reduced normal-regime volatility. But the tails have not compressed proportionally, meaning the ratio of tail risk to VRP has increased. For the specific trade recommendation: 6-month EUR/USD 1.12/1.18 call spreads are priced at approximately 0.8-1.0% of notional (as of early 2026 indicative pricing), offering 5:1 to 7:1 payoff if the dollar corrects toward BEER fair value. The asymmetry arises because the vol surface calibrates to the compressed normal regime while the regime change (capex bust → dollar smile transition from right to middle) is a discontinuous event that the smooth vol surface underprices.

**Claim 7 — EM BPO terms-of-trade shock:**
India's IT services export data from NASSCOM show the sector at approximately $200 billion in annual revenue (FY2025), representing ~6% of GDP and ~45% of total services exports. The sector's historical growth rate of 8-10% per annum has been the primary driver of India's services trade surplus, which in turn is the main offset to its goods trade deficit. The current account impact of AI automation is a second-derivative effect: it's not that existing BPO revenue disappears overnight, but that growth decelerates as AI substitutes for the marginal BPO contract. If growth decelerates from 10% to 3-4%, the cumulative impact on the current account over 5-10 years is a reduction of 1-2% of GDP in services export revenue relative to trend. For the INR, this represents a reduction in equilibrium real appreciation pressure and a wider current account deficit, implying higher equilibrium depreciation over the medium term. The NDF forward curve (5-year INR/USD at approximately 93-96 vs. spot ~85) implies ~2.5-3.0% annual depreciation, which is approximately consistent with the interest rate differential but does not embed any structural adjustment for AI-driven erosion of the BPO comparative advantage.

**Claim 8 — Dollar cycle path distribution:**
The three-scenario framework is calibrated against two historical dollar cycles: the 1995-2001 bull cycle (technology-driven, ended with dot-com bust) and the 2011-2016 bull cycle (growth-and-rate-differential-driven, ended with synchronized global recovery). The current cycle shares characteristics of both but more closely resembles the 1990s: technology-driven capital inflows, widening twin deficit, and productivity narrative. Key timing indicators:

- **Capex-to-revenue ratio:** Currently 4-6x. The dot-com cycle's analogous metric (telecom capex to telecom revenue) peaked at approximately 5-7x before the 2000-2001 bust. A decline toward 3x would suggest monetization is closing the gap; an increase above 6x would suggest unsustainability.
- **Foreign share of US equity market capitalization:** Currently approximately 18-20% (based on TIC data). During the dot-com peak, foreign ownership of US equities was approximately 12-15%. Higher current foreign ownership implies greater sensitivity to capital flow reversal.
- **DXY deviation from 5-year PPP:** Currently approximately +12-18%. At the 2001 DXY peak, the deviation was approximately +20-25%. The current deviation is significant but has room to extend before reaching historical extremes, consistent with the "plateau" phase.
- **Credit impulse trajectory:** Per macro_strategist_02's analysis, the bank lending credit impulse has been decelerating since mid-2024. A negative credit impulse combined with fiscal consolidation (if DOGE-style cuts materialize) would accelerate the transition from the right side to the middle of the dollar smile.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Growth Models & Cycle Positioning (iter_0015 macro_strategist_02):** The Post-Keynesian framework provides the critical missing link for dollar cycle analysis: the Kalecki profit equation explains *why* the US economy appears exceptional (fiscal deficit sustains profits) and the sectoral balances identity reveals *how* the capital account finances the twin deficit. My analysis extends this by showing that the capital inflows are themselves contingent on the AI narrative, creating a reflexive loop: fiscal deficit → profits → equity returns → foreign capital inflows → current account financed → dollar sustained → narrative validated. This loop's vulnerability is the narrative component — when AI capex disappoints, the entire chain reverses. The credit impulse framework (macro_strategist_02, Claim 6) connects to my dollar cycle timing: a negative credit impulse combined with AI capex disappointment would simultaneously attack the dollar from the demand side (weaker growth) and the capital account side (reduced inflows), producing a correlated move of the type that the smooth vol surface underprices.

**Equity Cycles:** The equity-FX nexus is unusually tight in the current cycle because the same factor (AI capex/returns) drives both equity valuations and the capital account. A 20-30% correction in Magnificent 7 stocks would reduce portfolio equity inflows by an estimated $100-200 billion annually, which — per the portfolio balance model — implies a 3-5% dollar weakening beyond what the equity correction alone would suggest. This creates a "doom loop" risk for portfolios that are long US equities with unhedged dollar exposure: the equity loss and the FX translation loss are positively correlated, unlike in traditional risk-off scenarios where dollar strength provides a partial hedge for foreign holders of US equities. This breaks the standard correlation assumption embedded in global portfolio construction.

**Fiscal Policy & Sovereign Debt:** The twin deficit dynamic is the long-run anchor for dollar cycle analysis. The fiscal deficit at 6-7% of GDP is historically associated with structural dollar weakness — the last time the fiscal deficit was this large outside of a recession (mid-2000s), the DXY was trading at 80-85, approximately 20% below current levels. The AI capex channel is the only factor preventing this historical relationship from binding. DOGE-style fiscal consolidation, paradoxically, could be dollar-negative in the medium term: it would reduce the fiscal deficit (mechanically dollar-positive through smaller twin deficit) but also reduce the Kalecki profit floor (slowing growth, reducing the narrative that attracts capital inflows). The net effect depends on magnitudes, but if fiscal consolidation is aggressive (2+ percentage points of GDP), the growth impact likely dominates — producing the worst possible outcome for the dollar (weaker growth AND capital outflows).

**Central Bank Balance Sheets (iter_0013):** Fed balance sheet normalization (QT) interacts with AI capex through the liquidity channel. QT drains dollar reserves from the banking system, tightening wholesale funding conditions. This is mostly irrelevant for hyperscalers (who are cash-rich) but matters enormously for the downstream AI ecosystem — startups, mid-cap software companies, and hardware suppliers that depend on venture capital and credit markets. A reserve scarcity event (per iter_0013 analysis) could trigger stress in the downstream AI ecosystem without affecting hyperscaler capex, producing a divergence between AI fundamentals (still strong at the top) and AI-adjacent credit conditions (tightening) that confuses the market signal. For FX, this scenario is initially dollar-positive (risk-off, left side of the dollar smile) but transitions to dollar-negative as the growth implications materialize — a sequencing that matters for option positioning.

**Currency Regimes & FX Dynamics (iter_0012):** This analysis directly extends the `dollar_transitional_regime` concept by identifying AI capex sustainability as the key variable resolving the current transitional regime. The existing KB's BEER overvaluation estimate (8-15%) should be decomposed into a "fundamentals-justified" component and a "capex-narrative premium." My analysis suggests approximately 3-5% of the overvaluation is captive-flow-related (Claim 5) and another 3-5% is capex-inflow-related (Claim 1), leaving approximately 2-5% of overvaluation that reflects pure rate differential and risk premium factors. This decomposition matters for the correction path: the rate differential component corrects gradually as the Fed cuts, the captive flow component corrects discontinuously on a China AI shock, and the capex flow component corrects on a capex disappointment event.

**Volatility Regimes (iter_0010):** The AI-driven compression of FX vol (Claim 6) connects directly to the broader vol regime analysis. The structural implication is that FX volatility has bifurcated into a compressed "body" (normal-regime, dominated by AI market-makers) and fattened "tails" (stress events, amplified by correlated liquidity withdrawal). This bifurcation means that standard vol-of-vol measures and VIX-VXN correlations may understate cross-asset contagion risk — the FX vol regime appears stable precisely until it isn't. The correct vol positioning is not simply "long vol" but "long convexity" — structures (far OTM options, variance swaps) that pay disproportionately in the tails while bleeding less carry in the compressed body.

**Energy Transition:** AI data center demand creates a direct tension with energy transition timelines. The IEA projects that AI electricity demand will consume a significant portion of planned renewable capacity additions, potentially extending the fossil fuel tail by 5-10 years. For commodity currencies (NOK, CAD, AUD), this is a structural support factor that the energy transition narrative had discounted. The FX implication is that the "green discount" on commodity currencies (the market's implicit assumption that fossil fuel revenues will decline) is too large if AI extends the demand timeline. This creates a relative value opportunity: commodity currencies are priced for energy transition (bearish) but should be priced for AI energy demand extension (less bearish to neutral).

**Labor Market Dynamics:** AI labor displacement connects to FX through the Phillips curve and wage-inflation transmission. If AI displaces cognitive-routine labor faster than reabsorption occurs, it creates disinflationary pressure that shifts the Fed reaction function toward easier policy. In isolation, this is dollar-negative (lower rates → narrower differential). But combined with the productivity channel (higher output per worker → higher potential growth), the net effect on the Fed is ambiguous. The FX market resolves this ambiguity through the growth differential: if AI productivity boosts US growth relative to peers (where AI adoption is slower), the growth differential is dollar-positive and dominates the rate channel. If AI productivity is a global phenomenon (diffusing rapidly through cloud access), the growth differential narrows and the rate channel dominates. The current evidence suggests US AI exceptionalism, supporting the dollar, but the diffusion of AI through cloud platforms (which are themselves US-controlled) creates a paradox: the US benefits from AI adoption everywhere because the revenues accrue to US cloud providers.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the elasticity of foreign portfolio flows to US AI equity returns, and at what point does the capex-to-revenue ratio trigger a reversal in flow direction?** The capex dollar framework requires an empirically grounded estimate of how much capital inflow responds to AI equity returns vs. other factors (rates, risk appetite, safe haven). A regression of TIC equity flow data on AI-sector returns, controlling for rate differentials and VIX, would help quantify the capex dollar's contribution. The reversal threshold — what capex-to-revenue ratio triggers outflows — is the critical parameter for dollar cycle timing but cannot be calibrated from a single historical analogue.

2. **How does the Kalecki profit equation's fiscal component interact with the capital account to determine the dollar's trajectory under different fiscal consolidation scenarios?** The sectoral balances framework tells us the accounting identity, but the behavioral question — how quickly and through which channels a fiscal consolidation transmits to the capital account — requires a dynamic model.
