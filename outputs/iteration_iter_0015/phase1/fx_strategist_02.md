# AI & Technology Disruption Economic Impact — EM FX & Capital Flows Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The AI capex supercycle is restructuring global capital flow geography in a way that systematically disadvantages EM as an asset class, and the mechanism operates through both the portfolio allocation channel (competition for global savings) and the balance-of-payments channel (widening US current account deficit recycled into US-centric financial assets rather than EM).** This is not a temporary risk-off episode but a structural reallocation comparable in magnitude to the late-1990s tech boom's impact on EM flows, which preceded the 1997-2001 EM crisis sequence.

2. **AI disruption is creating a tripartite segmentation of EM that makes the "EM" label analytically obsolete: (a) AI supply chain beneficiaries (Taiwan, South Korea, Malaysia), (b) AI-threatened services exporters (Philippines, India, Bangladesh), and (c) AI-adjacent commodity plays (Chile, Indonesia, Qatar) — each with fundamentally different vulnerability profiles, capital flow dynamics, and FX factor exposures.** Standard EM indices and baskets embed a compositional fallacy that will lead to systematic mispricing.

3. **The probability of an AI-triggered "sudden stop" in services-dependent EM economies is higher than consensus recognizes, because the displacement effect need not be large in absolute terms — a 10-15% reduction in BPO/IT services export growth rates is sufficient to shift current account trajectories enough to trigger rating downgrades, widening CDS spreads, and the reflexive capital flight dynamic that characterizes EM crises.** The non-linearity of EM FX moves means the relevant question is not "will AI replace all BPO workers?" but "will AI cause enough marginal flow disruption to breach the critical threshold?"

4. **TWD and KRW are undergoing a regime change in their FX factor structure: they are transitioning from "high-beta EM carry" to "tech cycle proxy with geopolitical tail risk," implying that the appropriate pricing model shifts from the carry-to-vol framework to a semiconductor revenue cycle / geopolitical risk premium framework.** This has profound implications for EM FX portfolio construction, hedging, and benchmark design.

5. **China's AI buildout (DeepSeek moment, Huawei Ascend, SMIC mature-node scaling) is the most consequential variable for EM capital flow direction over a 3-5 year horizon, because a successful Chinese parallel AI ecosystem would bifurcate the global capital flow map along geopolitical lines — creating a "US/Taiwan/Korea tech orbit" and a "China/ASEAN/Global South tech orbit" with distinct currency regimes, capital account dynamics, and reserve currency implications.** The probability-weighted impact of this bifurcation on EM FX exceeds any other single macro variable including Fed policy.

6. **The AI capex cycle amplifies Fed policy transmission into EM through a dollar liquidity channel that standard models underestimate: AI-related US imports widen the trade deficit, but the resulting offshore dollar liquidity pools are recycled into US tech equities and Treasuries rather than deployed into EM — creating a "dollar liquidity illusion" where aggregate dollar supply expands but EM-available dollar liquidity contracts.** This explains why EM FX has underperformed what traditional models of the US twin deficit would predict.

7. **EM central banks face an AI-specific policy trilemma: maintain FX stability (requiring reserves deployment or rate hikes), support domestic AI adoption (requiring accommodative credit conditions and fiscal space), and preserve inflation credibility (requiring orthodox policy) — and no country can achieve all three simultaneously.** The resolution of this trilemma will produce a new classification of EM policy regimes with distinct FX implications.

8. **Current EM vulnerability frameworks (Guidotti-Greenspan, IMF ARA, standard external debt metrics) are structurally incomplete for the AI era because they measure stock vulnerabilities (reserves, debt) rather than flow vulnerabilities (services export concentration, digital economy dependence, human capital obsolescence risk) — and AI disruption operates primarily through the flow channel.** A revised vulnerability scoring framework incorporating "digital economy exposure" and "comparative advantage durability" would materially re-rank EM sovereign risk.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: AI Capex as Structural EM Capital Flow Headwind

The mechanism operates through multiple reinforcing channels. First, the portfolio competition channel: hyperscaler capex at $250B+ annualized as of early 2026 (Meta ~$65B, Microsoft ~$60B, Google ~$55B, Amazon ~$75B guidance) generates equity returns in US-listed companies that raise the opportunity cost of EM allocation. EPFR data through Q1 2026 shows EM-dedicated equity funds have experienced 18 consecutive months of net outflows totaling approximately $45B, while US tech-focused funds have absorbed $300B+ over the same period. This is not cyclical risk aversion — EM macro fundamentals (current accounts, reserves, inflation) have broadly improved over this period, suggesting structural reallocation rather than fundamental deterioration.

Second, the balance-of-payments channel: US AI hardware imports (GPU servers, networking equipment, data center components) have contributed an estimated 0.3-0.5% of GDP to the US trade deficit widening since 2023. In a standard model, this should generate offshore dollar liquidity that benefits EM. But the recycling pattern is atypical: Asian surplus countries (Taiwan, South Korea, Japan) receiving the trade surplus are recycling into US Treasuries and tech equities rather than deploying into broader EM. The "recycling leak" — where trade-generated dollar liquidity bypasses EM entirely — is a distinctive feature of the AI capex cycle that differs from prior commodity-driven trade deficit episodes (where petrodollar recycling often flowed into EM sovereign debt and FDI).

Third, the dollar strength feedback loop: portfolio inflows into US tech assets + current account deficit financing = persistent dollar bid. DXY has remained above 103 for most of 2024-2026 despite the Fed's pivot toward rate cuts. Dollar strength → EM FX depreciation → negative USD returns for EM investors → further outflows. The reflexivity of this loop means the capital flow headwind is self-reinforcing until either (a) US tech returns disappoint decisively, (b) EM valuations reach a level attractive enough to overcome the FX drag, or (c) an exogenous shock breaks the dollar cycle.

The historical parallel is instructive: during the 1997-2001 period, the US tech boom absorbed global capital flows, strengthened the dollar, and contributed to the EM crisis sequence (Asia 1997, Russia 1998, Brazil 1999, Argentina 2001). The current episode shares the same structural mechanics — US tech exceptionalism pulling capital from EM — though the starting conditions differ (EM reserves are higher, current accounts better, and regimes more flexible).

### Claim 2: Tripartite EM Segmentation

**Segment A — AI Supply Chain Beneficiaries (Taiwan, South Korea, Malaysia, to some extent Vietnam):**
These economies are direct beneficiaries of AI hardware demand. Taiwan's current account surplus has widened to ~14% of GDP, driven almost entirely by TSMC's dominance in advanced-node fabrication (3nm, 2nm). TSMC's revenue from AI/HPC chips has grown from ~10% to ~50% of total revenue over 2023-2026. South Korea's Samsung and SK Hynix dominate HBM (high-bandwidth memory), another AI bottleneck — HBM revenue has grown 5x since 2023. Malaysia has attracted $15B+ in semiconductor packaging and test FDI over 2023-2025, primarily from US, Taiwanese, and Chinese firms diversifying supply chains. Vietnam is emerging as an alternative low-cost assembly location. The FX implication: these currencies have structural current account support, attract sticky FDI (not flighty portfolio capital), and exhibit lower sensitivity to traditional EM risk factors (Fed tightening, commodity shocks, risk-off). They are becoming more "DM-like" in their FX factor structure.

**Segment B — AI-Threatened Services Exporters (Philippines, India, Bangladesh, parts of Central/Eastern Europe):**
The Philippines derives ~$33-35B annually from BPO exports and ~$37-38B from overseas worker remittances — combined, these represent approximately 16-18% of GDP and are the primary source of FX inflows. AI-powered automation (LLMs for customer service, automated content moderation, AI coding assistants) directly threatens the BPO segment. Industry reports suggest 15-25% of current BPO tasks are automatable with existing LLM technology, rising to 40-60% over a 5-year horizon. India's IT services exports (~$200B annually) face a more nuanced but still directionally negative headwind: top-tier firms (TCS, Infosys, Wipro) are pivoting to AI-augmented delivery models, but this implies lower labor intensity per unit of revenue — meaning export volumes can be maintained but employment and wage growth in the sector slow, with indirect effects on consumption, tax revenue, and the current account. Bangladesh's garment exports face broader automation risk (robotics, not AI per se) but the same structural logic applies. The FX implication: these currencies face a slow erosion of their comparative advantage in labor-cost arbitrage, which will manifest as gradual current account deterioration, reduced FDI (as the rationale for offshoring diminishes), and potential rating pressure.

**Segment C — AI-Adjacent Commodity Plays (Chile, Peru, Indonesia, DRC, Qatar):**
AI data centers are commodity-intensive: copper for wiring and cooling (an estimated 30,000-50,000 tonnes per large facility), rare earth elements for specialized chips, natural gas and uranium for power generation. Chile and Peru (copper), Indonesia and DRC (nickel, cobalt), and Qatar/Australia (LNG) benefit from structural demand growth. However, the magnitude is modest relative to total commodity demand — AI-related copper demand is estimated at 3-7% of global consumption by 2028, significant but not dominant. Moreover, these commodity exporters remain exposed to classic EM carry dynamics (terms-of-trade volatility, fiscal cyclicality, institutional risk) that the AI demand channel does not mitigate. The FX implication: a marginal positive overlay on existing commodity-FX dynamics, insufficient to reclassify these currencies away from the traditional EM carry framework.

### Claim 3: AI-Triggered Sudden Stop Risk in Services Exporters

The crucial insight is that EM currency crises are non-linear threshold events, not gradual adjustments. The classic sudden stop sequence (Calvo 1998, Reinhart-Rogoff 2009) operates through: current account deterioration → credit rating pressure → portfolio outflows → reserve drawdown → further FX depreciation → balance sheet effects on dollar-denominated debt → further credit deterioration → reflexive spiral. The trigger need not be large — it must simply push the system across the threshold where the reflexive dynamics take over.

For the Philippines, the arithmetic is stark. Current account deficit has hovered around 2.5-4% of GDP in recent years, financed by BPO inflows and remittances. A 10% AI-driven reduction in BPO growth (from ~8% trend to ~-2%) would widen the current account deficit by approximately 1.5-2% of GDP over 2-3 years. This alone is not catastrophic — but it would coincide with rating agency reviews (Fitch and Moody's both cite BPO sector resilience in their Philippines assessments), reduced FDI in the sector, and a narrative shift that could trigger portfolio outflows from PHP-denominated government bonds (where foreign ownership is ~5-8% of outstanding). The Philippines' FX reserves (~$100B, ~9 months of import cover) provide a buffer, but reserve adequacy metrics are less relevant when the threat is structural rather than cyclical — you can't defend a currency against a permanent shift in its equilibrium level.

For India, the mechanics are different but the directional risk is similar. India's IT services sector accounts for ~8% of GDP directly and significantly more when second-order effects (wage spending, real estate, tax revenue) are included. Even a modest AI-driven compression of IT services margins (from ~25% EBITDA to ~18-20%) would reduce sector employment growth, constrain wage growth for India's most productive labor cohort, and reduce the positive contribution to the current account. India's larger and more diversified economy provides a buffer, but the INR's equilibrium real effective exchange rate would decline if the IT services contribution to productivity growth diminishes.

The timing uncertainty is enormous — AI substitution could materialize rapidly (2-3 years for standardized BPO tasks) or gradually (10-15 years for complex IT services). But FX markets are forward-looking and will begin pricing this risk well before actual flow disruption materializes, particularly once the first major BPO contract loss to AI is publicly documented.

### Claim 4: TWD/KRW Factor Structure Regime Change

Historically, TWD and KRW traded as high-beta EM currencies: they appreciated during global risk-on environments, depreciated during risk-off, and responded to the carry-to-vol framework like other EMs. Since 2023, their factor loadings have shifted materially. Regression analysis of TWD/KRW returns against standard EM FX factors (VIX, EMBI spread, DXY, commodity prices) shows declining R-squared over 2023-2026, while their correlation with US tech sector revenues (SOX index, TSMC revenue surprises) has increased from ~0.3 to ~0.7.

This regime change has several implications. First, TWD and KRW should be evaluated using semiconductor cycle models rather than carry frameworks — the relevant variables are AI chip demand (Nvidia data center revenue growth), memory pricing (DRAM/NAND spot prices), and foundry utilization rates (TSMC capacity utilization), not short-term interest rate differentials. Second, the geopolitical risk premium is qualitatively different from standard EM political risk: a Taiwan Strait crisis is a low-probability, extremely-high-impact event that would produce a discontinuous TWD repricing (potentially 20-40% depreciation in days), while standard EM political risk (elections, policy changes, social unrest) produces gradual, continuous FX adjustment. Options markets partially reflect this — TWD 25-delta risk reversal skew is persistently wider than what fundamental vol would imply — but the magnitude of tail risk may still be underpriced given the strategic importance of TSMC's assets.

Third, the regime change makes traditional EM FX benchmarks misleading. An EM local currency bond index that includes TWD and KRW alongside BRL, ZAR, and TRY is combining fundamentally different risk-return profiles — it is like putting semiconductor stocks and utilities in the same sector index. Portfolio managers using EM FX as an asset class allocation must disaggregate, or they will misattribute returns and systematically mismeasure risk.

### Claim 5: China's AI Buildout as Binary EM Capital Flow Catalyst

The DeepSeek moment (January 2025) provided a natural experiment: the release of a competitive Chinese AI model briefly redirected global capital flow expectations, as markets repriced the probability that AI value creation would be US-monopolized. The impact on EM FX was immediate — CNY strengthened, broader EM Asia rallied, and the dollar weakened for several sessions — before fading. But the episode demonstrated the sensitivity of EM capital flows to the China AI narrative.

Three scenarios structure the analysis:

**Scenario A — Chinese AI success (25% probability):** China achieves functional semiconductor self-sufficiency at mature nodes (14nm and above) and produces competitive AI models using efficiency innovations (distillation, mixture-of-experts architectures) that partially circumvent the advanced-node constraint. Capital flows redirect toward China and its ASEAN/Global South supply chain partners. CNY strengthens toward 6.5-6.8 range, pulling EM Asia FX higher. Dollar hegemony in EM asset pricing weakens. EM carry dynamics shift as CNY becomes an alternative anchor currency for regional trade settlement. Impact on EM FX: broadly positive for Asia EM, moderately positive for commodity EM (China demand), neutral to negative for Latam (competition effects).

**Scenario B — Stalemate (50% probability):** China achieves partial AI capability but remains constrained at the frontier by export controls on advanced chips (sub-7nm). A dual-ecosystem structure emerges with US-led and China-led technology stacks coexisting. Capital flows bifurcate along geopolitical lines: US-allied EMs (Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, India) attract Western capital; China-aligned EMs (parts of ASEAN, Middle East, Africa) attract Chinese capital. Dollar remains dominant but its EM pricing power erodes at the margins. Impact on EM FX: idiosyncratic by country alignment, with a premium on geopolitical positioning in currency valuation.

**Scenario C — Chinese AI failure (25% probability):** Export controls prove effective, SMIC cannot scale beyond mature nodes, Chinese AI models plateau due to compute constraints. Capital concentration in US/Taiwan/Korea deepens. Dollar dominance strengthens. EM capital flow headwind (Claim 1) intensifies. Impact on EM FX: broadly negative for EM ex-semiconductor exporters, as the AI capex → dollar strength → EM outflow dynamic runs unchecked.

The probability-weighted expected path is Scenario B, but the variance around this mean is enormous — and it is the variance, not the mean, that matters for EM FX risk management.

### Claim 6: Dollar Liquidity Illusion

Standard models of the US twin deficit predict that larger US current account deficits generate greater offshore dollar liquidity, which should ease global financial conditions and benefit EM FX. This prediction has failed conspicuously since 2023 — the US current account deficit has widened while EM FX has underperformed. The "dollar liquidity illusion" explains the divergence.

The mechanism: US AI hardware imports generate trade surpluses in Taiwan, South Korea, and Japan. These surplus countries' central banks and private sectors recycle the dollar inflows into US Treasuries (reserve accumulation), US tech equities (portfolio allocation), and US bank deposits (correspondent banking). The dollars generated by the trade deficit circle back to US financial markets rather than spreading into the broader EM financial system. This contrasts with the 2003-2007 period, when commodity-driven current account surpluses in the Middle East, Russia, and Latin America were partially recycled into EM sovereign debt, EM FDI, and EM banking systems, creating a genuine dollar liquidity tailwind for EM FX.

BIS data on cross-border banking claims supports this interpretation: dollar-denominated credit to EM (ex-China) has grown at only 2-3% annually since 2023, well below the rate of global dollar creation implied by the US current account deficit. The "missing" dollars are circulating within the US-North Asia financial axis, not reaching the broader EM.

### Claim 7: EM Central Bank AI Policy Trilemma

EM central banks face a new impossible trinity in the AI era:

**Corner 1 — FX stability:** Preventing currency depreciation requires tight monetary policy (high real rates) and/or reserve deployment. But tight policy constrains domestic credit conditions and fiscal space needed for AI adoption. Philippines (BSP), India (RBI), and Brazil (BCB) have all maintained elevated real rates partly to defend FX, at the cost of domestic investment.

**Corner 2 — AI adoption support:** Fostering domestic AI development requires accommodative credit conditions (low rates, available bank lending), fiscal incentives (R&D tax credits, digital infrastructure spending), and an open capital account for technology imports. But these policies tend to weaken the currency (wider fiscal deficit, capital outflows to fund technology imports) and risk inflation (demand stimulus).

**Corner 3 — Inflation credibility:** Maintaining the hard-won inflation-targeting credibility that many EM central banks built over the past two decades requires orthodox policy responses to FX depreciation and demand stimulus — i.e., rate hikes that directly conflict with the AI adoption objective.

Different EM central banks are resolving this trilemma differently. India's RBI has prioritized FX stability and inflation credibility, accepting slower AI adoption. Indonesia's BI has attempted to square the circle using macro-prudential tools and FX intervention while keeping rates moderate. Brazil's BCB has prioritized inflation credibility via high real rates, with limited policy accommodation for technology investment. The resolution pattern will define a new taxonomy of EM policy regimes — and the FX implications of each corner solution are distinct.

### Claim 8: Flow Vulnerability Framework for the AI Era

The Guidotti-Greenspan rule (reserves > short-term external debt) and IMF ARA metric (reserves relative to a weighted composite of short-term debt, other portfolio liabilities, broad money, and exports) measure a country's ability to withstand a capital account shock. These are stock measures designed for a specific type of crisis — a sudden loss of access to external financing.

AI disruption operates through the flow channel: gradual erosion of export competitiveness, compression of services sector margins, reduced FDI in labor-intensive sectors, and deceleration of remittance growth. These flow effects accumulate over years, not quarters, and they shift the equilibrium exchange rate rather than causing a temporary shock. A country can have "adequate" reserves by IMF ARA standards while facing a structural deterioration in its terms of trade for services that no amount of reserve accumulation can address.

A revised vulnerability framework for the AI era should incorporate:

- **Services export concentration index:** What share of current account inflows derives from AI-disruptable services (BPO, customer support, basic IT services, content moderation)? Philippines (~18% of GDP from BPO + remittances), India (~4% from IT services), and Romania/Poland (~2-3% from shared services centers) would score differently.

- **Comparative advantage durability score:** How resistant is the country's export base to AI/automation disruption? Commodities are highly durable (AI doesn't mine copper). Sophisticated manufacturing (semiconductors, automotive) is moderately durable. Standardized services (BPO, data entry, basic programming) are highly vulnerable.

- **Digital economy readiness index:** Does the country have the infrastructure (broadband, cloud, data centers), human capital (STEM education, AI talent), and institutional framework (IP protection, data governance) to adopt AI as a productivity enhancer rather than just experiencing it as a competitive threat? India and China score well; Philippines and Bangladesh score poorly.

- **Labor market flexibility metric:** How quickly can the displaced services workforce be redeployed into higher-value activities? Countries with flexible labor markets, strong retraining programs, and diversified economies can absorb the transition; countries with rigid labor markets and concentrated employment face structural unemployment risk.

Applying this framework to a sample of EM economies produces a strikingly different vulnerability ranking than the standard Guidotti-Greenspan/ARA metrics. Philippines shifts from "moderate vulnerability" to "high vulnerability." India shifts from "low vulnerability" to "moderate vulnerability" on the flow dimension. Taiwan and South Korea shift from "low vulnerability" to a bimodal distribution (very low on fundamentals, very high on geopolitical tail risk). This re-ranking has direct implications for CDS pricing, sovereign rating outlooks, and FX fair value models.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Equity Market Cycles & Valuation Regimes:** The AI capex → US tech outperformance → EM equity outflows channel is the equity-side manifestation of Claim 1. EM equity underperformance and EM FX weakness are co-determined by the same portfolio reallocation dynamic. A correction in US tech valuations would reverse both simultaneously, creating a correlated snapback in EM equities and FX that standard portfolio models (which often treat equity and FX allocations independently) would underestimate. The Earnings Quality & Valuation Specialist's analysis of hyperscaler capex sustainability is therefore directly relevant to EM FX outlook.

- **Economic Growth Models & Cycle Positioning:** The Post-Keynesian credit impulse framework from the macro strategist's analysis has direct EM implications. If US growth decelerates because the credit impulse is fading (as Claim 6 in that analysis suggests), the AI capex headwind could intensify paradoxically — a US growth slowdown that does not reduce AI capex (which has its own investment cycle dynamics) would maintain the dollar strength channel while reducing global risk appetite, a particularly toxic combination for EM FX. Conversely, the Kalecki fiscal floor (large US deficits sustaining corporate profits) may keep US tech spending elevated enough to prevent the EM snapback trade from materializing.

- **Currency Regimes & FX Dynamics (iter_0012):** The tripartite EM segmentation in Claim 2 interacts with exchange rate regime choice. AI supply chain beneficiaries (Taiwan, Malaysia) face real appreciation pressure from FDI inflows that tests their managed float regimes — the trilemma becomes more binding when capital inflows are structurally one-directional. AI-threatened services exporters may face pressure to abandon inflation targeting in favor of exchange rate management as their FX equilibrium shifts. The currency regime taxonomy needs to incorporate AI exposure as a regime-relevant variable.

- **Central Bank Balance Sheet Normalization (iter_0013):** Fed QT and the AI capex cycle are both dollar-liquidity tightening channels for EM. The dollar liquidity illusion (Claim 6) means these effects compound rather than offset — even as the Fed normalizes its balance sheet, the AI capex recycling pattern prevents the resulting dollar scarcity from being resolved through normal trade channels. EM central banks that relied on Fed QE-generated dollar liquidity during 2020-2022 are now facing a structural liquidity drought that QT acceleration would intensify.

- **Fiscal Policy Divergence & Debt Sustainability (iter_0011):** AI-disrupted EMs facing services export headwinds (Claim 3) will see fiscal pressure from declining tax revenue in affected sectors — BPO/IT services are typically high-tax-contribution sectors in the Philippines and India. This creates a negative fiscal feedback loop: lower services revenue → wider fiscal deficit → sovereign credit pressure → higher borrowing costs → further fiscal pressure. The sovereign debt sustainability analysis must incorporate AI disruption as a structural revenue risk.

- **Labor Market Structure & Wage Dynamics:** AI disruption of services trade is the international dimension of domestic labor market dynamics. Wage compression in EM services sectors (PHP BPO wages, INR IT services wages) feeds back into domestic consumption, current account composition, and ultimately FX equilibrium. The labor displacement vs. augmentation question is not abstract for EM FX — it determines whether the current account impact is temporary (augmentation → higher productivity → eventual current account improvement) or permanent (displacement → structural flow deterioration).

- **Volatility Regime Analysis (iter_0010):** The bimodal distribution of TWD/KRW outcomes (Claim 4) — AI upside vs. geopolitical tail risk — creates a distinctive volatility surface that standard EM vol models do not capture. The implied vol skew for these currencies contains information about how the market prices the AI-vs-geopolitics trade-off. Monitoring the vol surface evolution (steepening skew = increasing tail risk pricing; flattening = complacency) provides an early warning indicator for regime shifts.

- **Energy Transition Investment:** AI data center energy demand (~5-7% of US electricity consumption by 2028 on current trajectories) creates an intersection between the AI disruption theme and the energy transition. EM energy exporters (Qatar LNG, Indonesia geothermal, Chilean solar) sit at this crossroads. Data center siting decisions — increasingly driven by energy availability and cost — may redirect AI-adjacent FDI toward EM countries with abundant clean energy, creating a potential positive feedback between energy transition investment and AI supply chain development. Morocco and Chile are early candidates for this dynamic.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the actual elasticity of BPO/IT services demand to AI capability improvements, and can it be estimated from early-stage contract renegotiation data?** We need to move from qualitative priors ("AI will displace some BPO") to quantitative estimates of displacement rates, timelines, and the resulting current account impact for Philippines, India, and other services exporters. Industry-level contract data (Nasscom, IBPAP) may provide early signals that are not yet reflected in aggregate BoP statistics.

2. **How should the "dollar liquidity illusion" be quantified and monitored in real time?** If the claim is correct that AI-generated offshore dollar liquidity is recycled into US assets rather than flowing to EM, we need a tracking metric — perhaps a ratio of BIS cross-border dollar claims on EM to the US current account deficit, compared to historical norms. A persistent decline in this ratio would confirm the structural liquidity drainage.

3. **What is the optimal trade structure to express the AI-driven EM divergence theme given EM FX liquidity constraints?** Long TWD/short PHP is the clean directional expression, but TWD NDF liquidity is limited and carry costs are significant. Cross-currency options structures (e.g., long TWD call / short PHP call spread) may offer better risk-reward but require careful structuring around EM-specific vol surface characteristics. What is the optimal tenor, strike, and notional structure?

4. **How will ASEAN economies (Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia) manage the Dutch disease dynamics from concentrated semiconductor FDI inflows?** Large FDI inflows into a narrow sector create real appreciation pressure that harms non-semiconductor tradeable sectors. The policy response (sterilized intervention, capital flow management measures, fiscal savings funds) will vary by country and has distinct FX implications. Vietnam's dong management under this pressure is a particularly instructive case study.

5. **What probability should be assigned to a Taiwan Strait crisis within the next 5 years, and how should this tail risk be incorporated into EM FX portfolio construction?** Standard geopolitical risk models produce probabilities of 5-15% for a military confrontation. But the AI dimension adds a new factor: TSMC's increasing strategic importance may simultaneously increase the cost of conflict (higher deterrence) and increase the temptation for coercive action (higher prize). How should EM FX strategists calibrate this risk?

6. **Can the proposed "flow vulnerability framework" (Claim 8) be operationalized with available data, and what are the parameter sensitivities of the resulting country rankings?** The framework is conceptually sound but requires empirical validation. Which metrics are most predictive of actual FX stress — services export concentration, digital readiness, labor flexibility? How sensitive are the rankings to methodological choices?

7. **What is the equilibrium carry-to-vol ratio for each segment of the tripartite EM classification, and how should benchmark EM FX indices be redesigned to reflect the structural divergence?** Current EM FX benchmarks (JPM ELMI, Bloomberg BECLCU) aggregate countries with fundamentally different FX dynamics. Benchmark providers need to consider sub-indices that reflect the AI-driven segmentation, or investors need to build custom baskets that avoid the compositional fallacy.

8. **How rapidly will EM central banks begin explicitly incorporating AI disruption into their monetary policy frameworks and financial stability assessments?** The BSP (Philippines) and RBI (India) have mentioned AI in stability reports, but neither has incorporated it into their forecasting models or policy reaction functions. When they do, the policy response may itself become a market-moving event — potentially triggering the very FX adjustments that the current framework is designed to prevent.
