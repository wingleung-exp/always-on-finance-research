# AI & Technology Disruption Economic Impact — Behavioral Finance Critic & Historical Falsifier Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The cross-asset strategist's "consistency failure" framework (generalist_01, claim 1) is itself internally inconsistent: it assumes equity markets, rates markets, and credit markets should price the same AI productivity story, when in fact they price different time horizons, different claimant priorities, and different tail risks — meaning the "inconsistency" is partly an artifact of treating three different information aggregation mechanisms as if they should converge to a single signal.** The claim that "these three signals cannot all be correct simultaneously" rests on an unstated assumption that all three markets operate on the same model of AI's economic impact. They do not. Equity prices an option on a 5-10 year revenue stream. Credit prices a 3-7 year default probability. Rates price a 1-3 year macro trajectory. These can coexist without formal contradiction if each market is correct about its own horizon: AI may not move TFP in the rates-relevant horizon (1-3 years), credit structures may survive disruption within their maturity windows (3-7 years), and equity may correctly capitalize a 10-year transformation. The "inconsistency" rhetoric carries an implicit narrative fallacy — the assumption that a single coherent story must explain all three markets.

2. **The AI productivity debate across all agents exhibits a systematic anchoring asymmetry: bears anchor to the Solow paradox and 15-30 year GPT diffusion lags, bulls anchor to ChatGPT adoption speed and hyperscaler capex figures, and neither side rigorously addresses why their chosen anchor is more informative than the alternative.** The prior iteration's challenger analysis (iter_0014, claim 2) correctly identified the contradictory deployment of electricity vs. internet analogies, but this critique applies symmetrically: citing 30-year electricity diffusion lags is no less selective than citing 7-year internet adoption curves. The relevant question — which historical analogy best matches AI's deployment characteristics — requires a principled argument about AI's specific economic properties (marginal cost of deployment, complementary investment requirements, regulatory barriers), not a debate over which historical episode to invoke.

3. **Confidence levels assigned by the generalist_01 analysis are systematically miscalibrated upward: 7-8.5/10 confidence on claims about a technology whose aggregate economic impact has not yet materialized is inconsistent with the base rate for forecasting accuracy on technology-economy interactions, which Tetlock's superforecasting data places at 25-40% hit rates on 3-5 year horizons.** Specific miscalibrations: claim 1 at 8/10 (cross-asset inconsistency is "empirically observable") conflates observing a price divergence with correctly diagnosing its cause and resolution; claim 4 at 8.5/10 (concentration as correlation bomb) conflates a structural observation (concentration is high) with a predictive claim (it will cascade) without adjusting for the base rate of concentration-driven cascades.

4. **The macro strategist's application of the Kalecki profit equation to AI (macro_strategist_02, claim 5) contains a logical gap: the argument that "AI-driven productivity gains that accrue primarily to capital owners will produce demand deficiency" assumes a closed-system income distribution, when AI's actual transmission includes at minimum four channels — capital income, labor income of AI-complementary workers, consumer surplus from lower prices, and government revenue from corporate taxation — and the net demand effect depends on magnitudes across all four, not just the labor share channel.** The 1920s analogy (rapid mechanization, stagnant wages, 1929 collapse) is historically misleading: the 1920s also featured restrictive immigration policy, a gold standard constraining monetary response, and agricultural price collapse — confounding factors that make the analogy a case study in the narrative fallacy rather than a rigorous precedent.

5. **The "AI capex as self-referential bubble" thesis (present in both generalist_01 and macro_strategist_02) suffers from a base rate problem: the historical frequency of "massive corporate capex cycle that produces no economic value" is lower than the analyses imply, because most large-scale infrastructure buildouts — railroads, telecom, electricity grids — did produce enormous economic value, just not for the investors who funded the buildout.** The distinction between "value creation for the economy" and "value capture by investors" is analytically critical and neither analysis makes it cleanly. The telecom fiber buildout of 1996-2001 destroyed ~$2 trillion of investor capital AND created the infrastructure for $20+ trillion of subsequent digital economy value. The relevant market question is not "will AI create value?" but "is the value creation priced into the right securities at the right multiples?" — a much narrower, more tractable question than the broad "AI bubble or revolution?" framing.

6. **The labor displacement discourse across all agents fails a basic calibration test: stated confidence intervals on displacement magnitude are far too narrow relative to the actual variance of expert forecasts.** The generalist_01's own evidence shows Acemoglu at 0.06pp/year productivity uplift vs. Goldman Sachs at 1.5pp cumulative over 10 years — a 25x range in productivity estimates from credentialed experts. Yet the analysis assigns 5.5/10 confidence to a specific cross-asset pathway (displacement dominates augmentation), which is simultaneously too high (the pathway is highly uncertain) and too narrow (it doesn't reflect the multimodal distribution of possible labor market outcomes). A properly calibrated assessment would present a probability distribution across multiple scenarios rather than a single pathway with a point confidence.

7. **The prior iteration's challenger analysis (iter_0014) identified seven biases in AI discourse but itself exhibits a meta-level confirmation bias: every piece of evidence cited supports skepticism, and no "steel-manning" of the bull case is attempted.** A properly calibrated challenger analysis must identify conditions under which the bullish thesis is correct and assign non-trivial probability to those conditions. Specifically: if AI's marginal deployment cost is genuinely near-zero (unlike electricity, railroads, or computing hardware), the historical GPT diffusion base rates may be systematically too slow. The prior analysis's own open question #4 (faster SME adoption via SaaS delivery) identifies this possibility but does not integrate it into the confidence assessments, which remain anchored to the skeptical base case.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: The "Consistency Failure" as Partial Artifact

**BIAS AUDIT of generalist_01 claim 1:**

The cross-asset strategist frames the equity-rates-credit divergence as a logical impossibility: "These three signals cannot all be correct simultaneously." This is stated with 8/10 confidence. Let me decompose why this framing is itself biased:

*Narrative Fallacy:* The analysis constructs a coherent story where three asset classes "disagree" about AI, when in fact each asset class prices a complex bundle of factors, of which AI expectations are only one component. Equity multiples for Mag 7 reflect not only AI productivity expectations but also monopoly rents from existing businesses (Google Search, Amazon e-commerce, Apple's ecosystem), capital return programs, and relative scarcity of high-quality growth in a low-growth world. Rates reflect not only AI expectations but also fiscal trajectory, Fed reaction function, global savings glut dynamics, and term premium. Credit reflects not only disruption risk but also capital structure, secured collateral, maturity profile, and covenant protections. Attributing the divergence primarily to "inconsistent AI pricing" is a simplification that supports a narrative but may not accurately describe the primary driver of the observed prices.

*Framing Effect:* Calling it a "consistency failure" implies one or more markets must be wrong. But the correct framing may be that markets are pricing different aspects of a complex, multi-horizon phenomenon. Consider: a 5-year IG bond on a legacy media company may correctly price a low default probability (the company has enough cash flow to service debt for 5 years even under disruption) while the equity correctly prices a declining terminal value (the business model is impaired beyond year 5). These are not inconsistent — they are different claims about different time horizons. The generalist acknowledges this in claim 5 (the "timing gap") but does not feed it back to revise the confidence on claim 1, which is a logical inconsistency within their own analysis.

*What the historical record shows:* Cross-asset "inconsistencies" of this type are common during technology transitions and often persist for years without dramatic resolution. During the 1990s internet boom, equity markets priced a technology revolution while bond markets priced modest growth and credit markets priced benign default conditions — from 1995 to 1999. The "inconsistency" was eventually resolved (in 2000-2002), but an analyst in 1996 identifying the same pattern would have been four years early on the convergence trade, losing significant capital to carry costs. The persistence of cross-asset divergence is itself an empirical regularity that undermines the urgency of the "inconsistency" framing.

**IMPROVED FRAMING:** "Cross-asset markets appear to price different AI outcome probabilities across different time horizons, creating potential relative value opportunities — but the divergence may also reflect legitimate differences in time horizon, claimant priority, and tail risk sensitivity across asset classes. The convergence timeline is highly uncertain (historical analogues suggest 3-7 years), and the direction of convergence is not deterministic."

### Claim 2: Anchoring Asymmetry in the Productivity Debate

**LOGICAL CONSISTENCY CHECK:**

The debate across agents follows a predictable pattern:

- **Bull case anchor:** ChatGPT reached 100 million users in 2 months. Hyperscalers are spending $200B+/year. Firm-level studies show 14-40% productivity gains in specific tasks.
- **Bear case anchor:** TFP shows no structural break. Historical GPTs took 15-30 years. Base rate for "transformative technology within 5 years" is 15-25%.

Both anchors are factually correct. The debate is about *which facts are more decision-relevant*, and this is where bias operates.

**The bull case anchoring error:** Extrapolating from user adoption speed to economic impact speed conflates two distinct processes. Email was adopted rapidly (1990s) but measurable productivity impact from email took another decade. Consumer adoption ≠ business process transformation ≠ measurable productivity gains. The jump from "ChatGPT adoption was fast" to "AI productivity impact will be fast" skips two steps in the causal chain.

**The bear case anchoring error:** Citing 30-year electricity diffusion lags ignores a critical structural difference: electricity required massive, location-specific physical infrastructure (power plants, transmission lines, factory redesign). AI software can be deployed at near-zero marginal cost to any internet-connected device. The physical infrastructure bottleneck was the primary driver of the electricity diffusion lag, and this bottleneck is substantially absent for AI (with the exception of data center/GPU capacity, which is a supply constraint on *training* and *inference provision*, not on *adoption*). The prior iteration's challenger analysis cites this in open question #4 but does not integrate it — a failure of updating that reveals anchoring to the skeptical position.

**The principled analytical approach:** Instead of arguing by analogy, decompose the AI productivity transmission into its constituent steps:
1. Technology availability → firm adoption decision (fast for software, slow for hardware-dependent processes)
2. Firm adoption → task-level productivity gain (evidence exists: 14-40% for specific tasks)
3. Task-level gain → firm-level productivity gain (requires process redesign, moderate speed)
4. Firm-level gain → sector-level gain (requires firm exit/entry dynamics, competition, slow)
5. Sector-level gain → aggregate TFP (requires labor reallocation, institutional adaptation, slowest)

Steps 1-2 may be genuinely faster for AI than historical GPTs. Steps 3-5 are likely governed by similar institutional frictions as prior GPTs. This decomposition suggests a middle-ground timeline (5-15 years for aggregate impact) that neither the 2-3 year bull anchor nor the 20-30 year bear anchor captures.

### Claim 4: The Kalecki-AI Demand Channel

**HISTORICAL FALSIFICATION of the 1920s analogy (macro_strategist_02, claim 5):**

The macro strategist claims: "The historical precedent is the 1920s, where rapid mechanization coexisted with stagnant wages and rising private debt, culminating in the 1929 collapse."

Testing against the record:

*What the 1920s actually showed:*
- Real wages DID rise during the 1920s — by approximately 20-25% over the decade (BLS data). The "stagnant wages" claim is partially false. What stagnated was the LABOR SHARE of income (wages grew slower than productivity), not absolute wages.
- The 1920s featured a gold standard that constrained monetary policy response to deflationary shocks — a regime feature entirely absent today.
- Immigration was sharply restricted by the 1921 Emergency Quota Act and 1924 Immigration Act, creating artificial labor scarcity that partially supported wages.
- The agricultural sector (still ~25% of employment) was in depression throughout the 1920s due to post-WWI European agricultural recovery — an exogenous sector-specific shock unrelated to mechanization.
- The 1929 crash was triggered by speculative leverage in equities and a Fed tightening cycle, not directly by an underconsumption dynamic from mechanization.

*The analogy's valid element:* The 1920s did show that productivity growth concentrated in capital-intensive sectors can coexist with financial fragility driven by credit expansion — a Minsky-consistent observation. But the causal chain (mechanization → wage stagnation → underconsumption → crash) is an oversimplification that omits at least four critical confounding variables.

*The broader logical gap:* The Kalecki demand-deficiency argument assumes AI gains accrue "primarily to capital owners" and are not recycled. But the mechanism of recycling in a modern economy includes at least: (a) corporate tax revenue redistributed as government spending (the Kalecki fiscal channel the same analysis champions), (b) lower prices for consumers (consumer surplus channel — Amazon's deflationary effect preceded its profitability by a decade), (c) wages for AI-complementary workers, which may rise substantially (complementary skill premiums rose during both the IT revolution and electrification), and (d) equity wealth effects via broad market index participation (401k/IRA holdings mean profit gains partially recycle through household wealth). Whether the net effect is demand-deficient depends on the magnitudes of all four channels, and the analysis addresses only one.

### Claim 5: The "Bubble vs. Revolution" False Binary

**LOGICAL CONSISTENCY CHECK:**

Both the generalist_01 and macro_strategist_02 analyses implicitly frame AI as either a bubble (capex retrenchment, demand gap) or a revolution (productivity acceleration, structural growth). This binary framing is itself a cognitive bias — the availability of two vivid historical templates (dot-com bust vs. internet transformation) creates a representativeness bias that collapses a continuous outcome space into two narratively convenient poles.

**Counterexamples to the binary:**

The railroad era (1840s-1890s) provides the most relevant historical analogy precisely because it falsifies the binary:
- Total investment in US railroads exceeded $4.6 billion by 1890 (equivalent to ~$150B in 2025 dollars). This was the largest capital investment in US history to that point.
- The majority of railroad companies went bankrupt — 192 railroads failed in the Panic of 1893 alone. Investor returns were catastrophically poor in aggregate.
- AND the railroad network produced enormous economic value: reduced transportation costs by 90%+, enabled national markets for goods and labor, triggered urbanization, and raised US GDP growth by an estimated 1-2pp annually for decades.
- The resolution: the economic value was real but was captured by users (farmers, manufacturers, consumers) rather than investors. The value-creating entity and the value-capturing entity were largely distinct.

**Application to AI:** The most historically consistent outcome is not "AI is a bubble" or "AI is a revolution" but "AI creates enormous economic value AND most AI investors earn poor returns because the value accrues to users, competitors drive margins to zero, and the initial capital investment exceeds the present value of the cash flows captured by first-movers." This outcome is underweighted in the discourse because it is narratively unsatisfying — it doesn't produce a clear "buy" or "sell" signal. But it is the modal historical outcome for GPTs.

The capex-to-revenue ratio of 4-6x cited by generalist_01 should be assessed against the railroad capex-to-investor-return ratio (estimated by Fogel at roughly 3-5x initial investment vs. net present value of investor cash flows over the full rail network lifecycle) and the telecom ratio (estimated at roughly 5-8x for the 1996-2001 fiber buildout vs. the NPV of telecom operator cash flows). The current AI ratio is within the historical range for "value-creating but investor-value-destroying" episodes.

### Claim 6: Labor Market Forecast Calibration

**CALIBRATION ASSESSMENT:**

The generalist_01 cites three productivity estimates:
- McKinsey: 0.1-0.6pp annual uplift (concentrated in 2030-2040)
- Goldman Sachs: 1.5pp cumulative over 10 years (~0.15pp/year)
- Acemoglu: 0.06pp/year

The range spans 10x (from Acemoglu's 0.06pp to McKinsey's upper bound of 0.6pp). Yet the analysis does not explicitly grapple with what this range implies for confidence levels. If three credentialed experts disagree by an order of magnitude on the same question, the appropriate response is to widen confidence intervals dramatically, not to split the difference and assign 7.5/10 confidence to a "timing gap" claim that presupposes the gains will eventually materialize.

**What proper calibration looks like:**
- Probability that AI produces a measurable (>0.3pp) TFP acceleration within 3 years (by 2029): 15-20% (consistent with GPT base rates)
- Probability within 5-10 years (by 2031-2036): 40-55% (conditional on continued investment and no regulatory disruption)
- Probability within 15-25 years (by 2041-2051): 65-80% (if AI is genuinely a GPT)
- Probability that AI produces NO measurable TFP acceleration (it is not a GPT, or gains are fully captured in measurement error): 15-25%

These probability estimates are inherently imprecise, but they correctly represent the multimodal, fat-tailed distribution that the expert disagreement implies. A point estimate of "timing gap = 3-7 years" with 7.5/10 confidence is overconfident because it collapses this distribution into a single interval.

### Claim 7: The Meta-Bias in Challenger Analysis

**Self-Audit (iter_0014 challenger analysis):**

The prior iteration's challenger analysis identified seven biases in AI discourse. I audit whether the challenger itself exhibited bias:

*Confirmation bias in evidence selection:* Every historical example cited (nuclear "too cheap to meter," Japanese 5th Gen Computing, AI winters) supports skepticism. No examples of rapid, high-return technology transitions are cited (mobile telephony reached global scale in ~15 years with enormous investor returns; cloud computing generated massive value for both investors and users within a decade of commercial availability). The omission of favorable precedents is itself a selection bias — the same bias the challenger accuses bulls of exhibiting.

*Anchoring to the skeptical position:* The confidence scores (7-9/10 on skeptical claims, with a cap at 8 "deliberately") reveal that the challenger is more confident in its skeptical claims than the generalist is in its neutral-to-bullish claims. But if the challenger's core thesis is that forecasting accuracy is low and uncertainty is high, then high confidence in skeptical claims is internally inconsistent. "We cannot know" and "I am 90% confident this analogy is deployed selectively" are not logically contradictory but operationally inconsistent — the challenger's epistemic humility applies selectively to others' forecasts, not to its own critique.

*The steel-man gap:* The strongest version of the AI bull case — that software AI can be deployed at near-zero marginal cost, making historical hardware-dependent GPT diffusion lags inapplicable — is acknowledged in open question #4 but not integrated into the confidence framework. A properly calibrated challenger would assign explicit probability (I estimate 20-30%) to the scenario where AI's software nature makes it genuinely faster-diffusing than any historical GPT, and would adjust confidence scores on all claims accordingly.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Equity Cycles (equity_cycles, confidence 5.0)
The calibration critique applies directly to equity cycle analysis. The current equity cycle's dependence on the AI narrative means that the cycle-timing question ("are we in 1995 or 1999?") is really a question about the accuracy of AI productivity expectations — and the calibration evidence suggests that forecasting accuracy on such questions is low enough (~25-40% hit rate on 3-5 year horizons) that both "1995" and "1999" framings should be held with much lower conviction than typically expressed. The knowledge base entry on cross_asset_pricing_inconsistency (confidence 7) documents that equities require ~6-7% nominal revenue growth for 10 years — a requirement that embeds an implicit AI productivity assumption whose calibration deserves direct scrutiny.

### Growth Models (growth_models, confidence 7.4)
The macro strategist's Post-Keynesian framework provides a genuinely useful critique of the Solow growth decomposition's treatment of AI-as-TFP. The demand-side mediation argument (productivity potential means nothing without demand to realize it) is logically sound. But the framework itself exhibits a form of anchoring bias: by treating demand as the binding constraint *by construction*, it pre-determines that AI cannot deliver supply-side growth unless demand conditions are met. This is a strong theoretical prior that may not survive contact with a technology that simultaneously creates demand (capex cycle) and supply (productivity gains). The Solow framework's limitation (cannot distinguish cyclical from structural TFP in real time) and the Post-Keynesian framework's limitation (structurally skeptical of supply-side shocks) are complementary blind spots that, taken together, suggest genuine agnosticism about AI's growth impact is the correct epistemic posture.

### Labor Market Dynamics (labor_market_dynamics, confidence 6.5)
The knowledge base entries on ai_immigration_segmented_labor (confidence 4) and labor_market_stagflation_fulcrum (confidence 8) reveal an important analytical gap: the AI labor displacement discourse operates at the aggregate level, but the labor market effects are likely to be highly segmented — white-collar displacement in some sectors alongside blue-collar shortage in others. This segmentation means that aggregate labor market statistics (unemployment rate, average hourly earnings) may entirely miss the distributional dynamics of AI's labor impact, creating a measurement problem that afflicts all macro analyses relying on headline labor data. The Phillips curve becomes even less reliable as a guide to policy when AI-driven displacement creates simultaneous excess supply in some labor markets and excess demand in others.

### Energy Transition (energy_transition, confidence 6.5)
The AI-energy nexus creates a genuine, novel cross-asset linkage that did not exist pre-2023. The generalist_01's treatment of this connection is among the most valuable contributions across the analyses because it identifies a new correlation structure. However, the power demand projections (150-300+ TWh, a 2x range) are presented with 8/10 confidence when the range itself suggests 6-7/10 is more appropriate. The Jevons paradox question (does efficiency increase or decrease total energy consumption?) is a genuinely open empirical question for AI specifically, and historical precedent is ambiguous: computing energy efficiency has improved by ~1000x per operation since 1990, yet total data center energy consumption has increased by ~100x over the same period. The net effect depends on the elasticity of AI demand with respect to compute cost — a parameter that is currently unmeasured.

### Credit Cycles & Financial Stability
The Minsky AI capex classification (knowledge base, confidence 5) correctly distinguishes between hedge-financed hyperscaler capex and speculative/Ponzi-financed downstream ecosystem. This is the most analytically productive framework for assessing AI capex risk because it separates the question of "will the technology work?" from the question of "will the financial structures supporting it survive a disappointment?" The former is a technology question; the latter is a financial stability question. The macro strategist's claim 7 (Minsky cycle at speculative-to-Ponzi transition) applies specifically to the downstream AI ecosystem but should not be conflated with the hyperscaler layer, which has genuinely different financial characteristics — a conflation that both the bull case (all AI capex is justified) and the bear case (it's all a bubble) tend to make.

### Fiscal Policy & Debt Sustainability
If AI does deliver productivity acceleration, the fiscal arithmetic improves (higher growth → higher tax revenue → lower debt/GDP). If it does not, the fiscal costs of AI-related industrial policy (CHIPS Act, data center subsidies, displaced-worker support) compound existing fiscal pressures. The conjunction fallacy applies to government budgeting: fiscal projections embedding optimistic AI productivity assumptions inherit the same calibration problems identified in the private sector. CBO projections of revenue from AI-related economic activity should be treated with the same skepticism as sell-side revenue forecasts for AI companies — both are subject to the same biases and the same base rates for technology forecasting accuracy.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the proper reference class for AI forecasting, and can this question be answered empirically rather than by analogy?** The debate between electricity (30-year lag) and internet (7-year adoption) analogies may be a false choice. A more productive approach would be to identify the specific structural features that determined diffusion speed for each historical GPT (marginal deployment cost, complementary investment requirements, regulatory barriers, network effects) and assess where AI falls on each dimension. This would produce a "synthetic analogy" that is less susceptible to cherry-picking than any single historical case.

2. **Can we decompose the Mag 7 valuation premium into its component drivers — AI expectations, monopoly rents, capital return, relative scarcity — to isolate the AI-specific pricing?** Without this decomposition, the "cross-asset consistency failure" claim is untestable because we cannot determine how much of the equity premium is AI-driven. A reverse-DCF exercise isolating the AI revenue component from existing business cash flows would allow a direct comparison between equity-implied and rates-implied AI expectations.

3. **What is the actual historical hit rate for "cross-asset inconsistency" trades, and what is the typical convergence timeline?** The generalist_01 proposes several relative value trades based on the observed inconsistency. But the track record of "cross-asset inconsistency convergence" as a strategy class is under-documented. How often do cross-asset inconsistencies converge within a reasonable investment horizon (1-3 years) vs. persist or resolve in unexpected directions? Without this base rate, the trade is compelling in theory but uncalibrated in practice.

4. **How should the challenger function evolve as more data accumulates?** The current iteration's analysis is anchored to early 2026 data showing no TFP structural break. If, by 2027-2028, TFP data shows a sustained acceleration, the challenger must be willing to update dramatically rather than finding new reasons for skepticism. Establishing ex ante tripwires (e.g., "4+ quarters of >2.5% labor productivity growth concentrated in high-AI-adoption sectors") provides a commitment device against the meta-confirmation bias identified in claim 7.

5. **What is the right framework for assessing AI's impact on market efficiency itself?** If AI tools improve the speed and accuracy of financial analysis, the very biases this analysis identifies may become less prevalent (AI-assisted forecasting may reduce anchoring, availability, and narrative biases). Alternatively, if all analysts use similar AI tools, it may increase herding and reduce the diversity of analytical perspectives. The impact of AI on the analysis of AI is a reflexive problem that no current framework adequately addresses.

6. **What is the empirical distribution of "technology creates value but destroys investor capital" episodes, and what determines whether investors or users capture the surplus?** The railroad example suggests that when a technology reduces costs in a competitive market, users capture the surplus. The monopoly platform examples (Google, Apple, Meta) suggest that when a technology creates network effects in a winner-take-all market, investors capture the surplus. Which pattern AI follows depends on the market structure of AI deployment — competitive (like railroads) or monopolistic (like platform tech) — and this structural question deserves more rigorous treatment than either the bull or bear case provides.

7. **How do we distinguish genuine cognitive biases from legitimate analytical disagreements in the AI debate?** Not every instance of bullishness is overconfidence; not every bearish argument is well-calibrated skepticism. The taxonomy of biases (anchoring, narrative fallacy, herding, etc.) risks becoming a rhetorical weapon deployed selectively against disfavored conclusions rather than a genuine diagnostic tool. Establishing shared criteria for what constitutes "biased" vs. "legitimately different priors" would improve the analytical quality of the debate.
