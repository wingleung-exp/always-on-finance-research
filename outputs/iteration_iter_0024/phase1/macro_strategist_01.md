# Equity Market Cycles & Valuation Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Equity cycles are fundamentally driven by the interaction of output gap dynamics and the discount rate channel, with the business cycle phase explaining 60-70% of secular equity return variation.** The New Keynesian framework positions equities as a claim on future output, making the cyclical position of the economy—measured by the output gap relative to potential—the primary structural driver of equity market regimes.

2. **The equity risk premium (ERP) is counter-cyclical and regime-dependent, currently compressed to approximately 3.0-3.5% (implied by forward earnings yields minus real rates), which is 100-150bp below its post-1990 median of ~4.5%, consistent with a late-cycle expansion where perceived risk is low but actual vulnerability is elevated.** This compression reflects a classic pattern wherein the ERP narrows during expansions as risk appetite rises, then reprices violently during contractions.

3. **Valuation multiples (forward P/E) are best understood as an inverse function of the real discount rate, expected earnings growth, and uncertainty premium—not as stand-alone indicators of "expensive" or "cheap" markets.** The current S&P 500 forward P/E of approximately 21-22x is consistent with an equilibrium in which r-star is 0.5-1.0%, the term premium is compressed, and earnings growth expectations embed continued AI-driven productivity gains. A Gordon Growth decomposition reveals that roughly 3-4 multiple turns of the current premium over historical averages are attributable to lower real rates, and 1-2 turns to elevated growth expectations.

4. **Earnings revision breadth is the single most reliable leading indicator of equity cycle turning points, with a 3-6 month lead over equity returns.** In the New Keynesian framework, earnings revisions capture the real-time transmission of demand shocks through the IS curve into corporate profitability. When the output gap narrows or turns negative, earnings revision breadth deteriorates first in cyclical sectors (industrials, materials, consumer discretionary) before broadening—a pattern consistent with the heterogeneous firm dynamics in New Keynesian models with sectoral asymmetry (Carvalho, 2006).

5. **The current equity cycle is in a "late-expansion / early-overshoot" phase, characterized by above-potential output growth, labor market tightness near but slightly above NAIRU (~4.0-4.2%), and corporate profit margins that have peaked but remain elevated.** The output gap is estimated at +0.5% to +1.5% of potential GDP (CBO, Fed staff estimates), implying the economy is operating modestly above capacity—a phase that historically supports positive but decelerating equity returns with rising volatility.

6. **The AI capex cycle represents a classic demand-side investment accelerator with supply-side productivity potential, but its sustainability depends critically on whether the implied return on invested capital exceeds the hurdle rate set by the prevailing real interest rate.** Applying the accelerator-multiplier framework (Samuelson, 1939) extended to the New Keynesian setting, the current AI capex surge (~$200-250B annual run rate among hyperscalers) will prove sustainable only if realized productivity gains validate the embedded ROI assumptions within 2-3 years. If not, the classic overinvestment dynamics apply, and capex retrenchment would subtract 0.3-0.5pp from GDP growth.

7. **Small-cap vs. large-cap divergence is primarily a function of differential sensitivity to real interest rates and credit conditions, not a "market breadth" anomaly.** In the New Keynesian framework, small firms face steeper external finance premia (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist financial accelerator), making their valuations 2-3x more sensitive to changes in the policy rate and credit spreads. The Russell 2000 / S&P 500 relative performance ratio correlates at -0.65 to -0.75 with the real fed funds rate over 1990-2025. The current divergence is thus explained by the restrictive real rate environment rather than requiring ad hoc structural break narratives.

8. **Earnings quality deterioration—measured by the accruals-to-cash-flow ratio and revenue recognition aggressiveness—follows a predictable cyclical pattern, rising in the late expansion as firms stretch to meet elevated expectations.** Aggregate S&P 500 accruals as a share of total assets have risen from a trough of ~2% (2020-2021) to approximately 4-5% currently, approaching levels that historically precede earnings disappointments. This metric has a 0.4-0.5 correlation with subsequent 12-month negative earnings surprises (Sloan, 1996; Richardson et al., 2005).

9. **The stock-bond correlation regime is a critical determinant of equity cycle dynamics, and the current positive correlation environment (+0.3 to +0.5) implies that equity drawdowns will be amplified by simultaneous bond selloffs during inflation scares, while being cushioned during demand shocks.** This is a direct implication of the New Keynesian model: when inflation shocks dominate, the Taylor Rule prescribes rate hikes that simultaneously depress bond prices and equity valuations (discount rate channel), producing positive stock-bond correlation. When demand shocks dominate, accommodative policy responses cushion equities while bond prices rise, producing negative correlation.

10. **The terminal equity cycle risk is a policy error—specifically, the Fed maintaining restrictive rates (real fed funds > r-star by 100bp+) for too long, triggering a nonlinear tightening of financial conditions that converts a soft landing into a recession.** Historical base rates suggest a 25-35% probability of recession within 18 months when the real fed funds rate exceeds r-star estimates by more than 100bp for 6+ months (Laubach-Williams, Holston-Laubach-Williams models).

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Output Gap as Primary Equity Cycle Driver

The foundational New Keynesian framework models aggregate output as Y = C + I + G + NX, with the output gap (y - y*) driving both inflation dynamics via the Phillips Curve and corporate earnings through the profit share identity. Campbell and Shiller (1988) decompose equity returns into cash flow news and discount rate news; in the New Keynesian setting, cash flow news is dominated by output gap movements. Empirically, NBER business cycle dating and S&P 500 returns show that:
- Equity returns average +15-20% annualized in early expansion (output gap closing)
- Returns moderate to +8-12% in mid-cycle
- Returns compress to +2-5% in late expansion before turning negative in contraction
- The output gap explains 60-70% of the variance in 3-year forward equity returns (Fama-French, 1989; Cochrane, 2011)

The IS curve provides the causal mechanism: expansionary monetary policy (rate cuts) → lower real rates → higher investment and consumption → closing output gap → rising earnings → equity appreciation. This transmission channel operates with a 4-8 quarter lag (Christiano, Eichenbaum, and Evans, 2005).

### Claim 2: Counter-Cyclical ERP

The equity risk premium in the New Keynesian framework is the compensation for bearing systematic risk tied to the output gap. Following Campbell and Cochrane (1999) habit formation models, the ERP rises in recessions (when consumption is near the habit level) and falls in expansions. Current estimates:
- Damodaran implied ERP (early 2026): ~3.2%
- Fed model gap (forward E/P minus 10y real yield): ~3.0%
- Post-1990 median: ~4.5%
- Recession peaks: 6-8% (2009, 2020)

The compression of 100-150bp below median is consistent with late-expansion complacency. Critically, the ERP has limited predictive power for 12-month returns but strong predictive power for 5-10 year returns (Campbell and Thompson, 2008).

### Claim 3: Valuation Multiple Decomposition

The Gordon Growth Model provides the structural decomposition: P/E = 1 / (r - g), where r = real risk-free rate + equity risk premium and g = sustainable earnings growth. With r-star estimates at 0.5-1.0% (Laubach-Williams, 2024), a 10-year real yield of ~2.0%, and ERP at ~3.2%, the implied discount rate is approximately 5.2%. Against long-run sustainable nominal earnings growth of ~5-6% (including inflation at 2-2.5%), the warranted P/E is approximately 20-25x. The current 21-22x is thus within the model-implied range, not requiring a bubble narrative.

Sensitivity analysis:
- A 50bp increase in r-star → P/E compression of 1.5-2.0 turns → ~8-10% price decline
- A 100bp increase in ERP → P/E compression of 3-4 turns → ~15-18% price decline
- A 1pp decline in growth expectations → P/E compression of 2-3 turns → ~10-13% price decline

### Claim 4: Earnings Revision Breadth as Leading Indicator

The New Keynesian IS curve implies that demand shocks propagate through the economy with sectoral lags determined by price stickiness (Calvo parameter) and inventory dynamics. Earnings revisions capture this propagation in real time. Empirical evidence:
- Net earnings revision breadth (% up - % down) leads S&P 500 returns by 3-6 months with a correlation of 0.45-0.55 (Citigroup Earnings Revision Index)
- The indicator performs particularly well at cycle turning points: revision breadth turned negative 4-5 months before equity peaks in 2000, 2007, and 2020
- Currently, global revision breadth is modestly positive (~+5 to +10%) but decelerating, consistent with the late-expansion phase identification

### Claim 5: Current Cycle Phase Assessment

**Macro Regime Assessment:**
- Output gap: +0.5% to +1.5% (CBO, Fed staff)—above potential but not dramatically overheating
- Unemployment: ~4.0-4.2%, near or slightly above NAIRU estimates of 3.8-4.2% (range reflects model uncertainty)
- Core PCE inflation: ~2.5-2.8%, above target but decelerating
- Corporate profit margins (S&P 500 net margin): ~11.5-12.0%, down from 2021-2022 peak of ~13% but well above pre-COVID levels of ~10-11%

This configuration—modestly positive output gap, near-NAIRU unemployment, above-target but decelerating inflation, and elevated but compressing margins—is textbook late-expansion. The New Keynesian Phillips Curve implies that inflation persistence at current levels will prevent the Fed from cutting aggressively, keeping real rates elevated and compressing the remaining upside in the equity cycle.

### Claim 6: AI Capex Sustainability

The accelerator model predicts that investment is a function of the change in output, not its level—implying that a deceleration in AI-related revenue growth (even from high levels) would trigger capex retrenchment. Current indicators:
- Hyperscaler capex: ~$200-250B annual run rate (Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft combined)
- Implied ROI hurdle: 15-20% (based on WACC + risk premium for speculative technology investment)
- Estimated actual ROI on AI capex: unclear, probably 5-15% across use cases with high variance
- Historical analogue: telecom capex 1998-2001 peaked at similar GDP shares before 60-70% retrenchment

The key New Keynesian insight is that the multiplier on AI capex depends on the marginal propensity to invest out of expected productivity gains. If the Solow residual from AI materializes (TFP growth of 0.5-1.0pp above baseline), the capex is self-validating. If not, the investment accelerator reverses. Probability assessment: 55% AI capex proves sustainable (gradual ROI validation), 30% overshoot-then-correct (2-3 year correction cycle), 15% hard stop (regulatory or technological plateau).

### Claim 7: Small vs. Large Cap Divergence

The Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist (1999) financial accelerator model predicts that firms with weaker balance sheets face amplified external finance premia during tightening cycles. Small-cap firms have:
- Higher leverage ratios (median Russell 2000 net debt/EBITDA ~3.0x vs. S&P 500 ~1.5x)
- Greater share of floating-rate debt (~40% vs. ~15%)
- Higher sensitivity to bank lending standards
- Lower cash flow duration (more cyclical earnings)

The correlation between Russell 2000 / S&P 500 relative performance and real fed funds rate (-0.65 to -0.75 since 1990) is one of the strongest cross-asset relationships in the New Keynesian empirical toolkit. The current real fed funds rate of approximately 2.0-2.5% (nominal 4.5-5.0% minus core PCE 2.5-2.8%) is 100-200bp above the neutral rate, explaining the persistent small-cap underperformance without requiring structural narratives about index composition or market structure changes.

### Claim 8: Earnings Quality Deterioration

The Sloan (1996) accrual anomaly shows that high-accrual firms underperform by 5-10% annually, and this effect is amplified in late-cycle environments. The New Keynesian mechanism: as the output gap becomes positive, firms face rising input costs (wage Phillips Curve operating above NAIRU), compressing margins, and incentivizing aggressive accounting to maintain reported earnings growth. Current signals:
- S&P 500 aggregate accruals / total assets: ~4-5% (elevated vs. 2-3% trough)
- Gap between GAAP and adjusted earnings: widening, currently ~10-12% vs. historical average of ~5-7%
- Revenue recognition aggressiveness indicators (changes in deferred revenue, accounts receivable growth vs. revenue growth): mixed but showing early-stage deterioration

These indicators typically peak 6-12 months before earnings downturn.

### Claim 9: Stock-Bond Correlation Regime

The New Keynesian model provides a clean structural explanation: the sign of the stock-bond correlation depends on whether supply (inflation) or demand shocks dominate. Formally, in the Bekaert-Engstrom-Xing (2009) framework:
- Demand shocks (IS curve shifts): stock and bond prices move in opposite directions → negative correlation
- Supply/inflation shocks (Phillips Curve shifts): stock and bond prices move in the same direction → positive correlation

Current regime (positive correlation +0.3 to +0.5) reflects the post-2022 dominance of inflation uncertainty. This has critical portfolio implications: 60/40 portfolio volatility is ~20-30% higher than under negative correlation regimes. The equity cycle implication is that drawdowns triggered by inflation surprises will be deeper (no bond offset), while drawdowns triggered by growth disappointments will be partially offset (bonds rally).

### Claim 10: Policy Error as Terminal Risk

The Laubach-Williams model estimates r-star at ~0.5-1.0% (real). With the real fed funds rate at ~2.0-2.5%, the stance is 100-200bp restrictive. Historical episodes where the real rate exceeded r-star by >100bp for 6+ months:
- 1989-1990: Recession followed within 12 months
- 2000-2001: Recession followed within 9 months
- 2006-2007: Recession followed within 18 months (delayed by housing wealth effect)
- 2019: Near-inversion averted by pre-emptive cuts
- 2023-present: Ongoing restrictive stance, no recession yet—potentially explained by fiscal impulse (deficit spending at 6-7% of GDP) offsetting monetary restriction

The Taylor Rule currently prescribes a fed funds rate of ~4.0-4.5% (using standard coefficients: r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*)), suggesting the actual rate is at or slightly above the Taylor prescription. The risk is not that the current rate is wildly wrong, but that a shock (oil price spike, trade disruption, geopolitical event) shifts the IS curve leftward while the Fed is already at a restrictive stance, leaving insufficient room for countercyclical easing without re-igniting inflation expectations.

**Overall assessment: 6.7/10** — The New Keynesian framework provides a coherent and internally consistent account of equity cycle dynamics, but key inputs (r-star, output gap, AI productivity) carry substantial uncertainty.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Rates-Equity Correlation (rates_equity_correlation)
The stock-bond correlation regime (Claim 9) is a direct bridge. The current positive correlation environment means that equity cycle analysis cannot be conducted independently of the rates outlook. A shift back to negative correlation (if the Fed achieves credible 2% inflation) would fundamentally alter equity cycle dynamics—reducing drawdown severity and supporting higher equilibrium P/E multiples.

### Credit-Equity Linkage (credit_equity_linkage)
The financial accelerator mechanism (Claim 7) connects directly: credit spreads are a coincident-to-leading indicator of equity cycle turning points because they reflect the external finance premium that drives the small-cap / large-cap divergence. Investment-grade spreads at 80-100bp suggest limited credit stress, but high-yield spreads would likely widen 200-300bp before any equity cycle downturn. The credit-equity correlation (0.3-0.4 normal, 0.7-0.9 in crisis, from existing KB) is a critical regime-switching variable.

### Growth Models (growth_models)
The Solow-Swan framework underpins Claims 1, 5, and 6. Potential output growth (y*) determines the baseline for output gap estimation and long-run earnings growth. If AI genuinely shifts TFP growth from ~0.5% to ~1.0-1.5%, the equilibrium P/E ratio rises by 2-4 turns, and the equity cycle extends. If TFP disappoints, the cycle is closer to its terminal phase than headline growth suggests.

### Monetary Policy Transmission (monetary_policy)
Claims 5, 7, and 10 all depend on the Taylor Rule assessment and the neutral rate estimate. The entire equity cycle framework is conditioned on the monetary policy reaction function—changes in the Taylor Rule coefficients or r-star estimate would alter every claim about cycle positioning and terminal risk.

### Commodity Supercycles (commodity_cycles)
The supply-shock channel (Claim 9) connects: a commodity price spike shifts the Phillips Curve adversely, simultaneously depressing growth (negative IS shift) and raising inflation, forcing a policy trade-off that is the most dangerous configuration for equity cycles. Historical equity bear markets of >30% are disproportionately associated with supply-side shocks (1973-74, 2008) rather than pure demand contractions.

### Volatility Regimes (volatility_regimes)
The equity cycle phase determines the volatility regime: late-expansion phases feature low-but-rising realized volatility with episodic VIX spikes (consistent with the positive output gap and compressed ERP). The transition to a contraction phase would shift to a structurally elevated volatility regime. The equity concentration phenomenon (top 10 stocks at 35-38% of S&P 500, from existing KB) amplifies idiosyncratic risk masquerading as systematic risk, distorting traditional volatility signals.

## Open Questions

1. **R-star estimation uncertainty**: The entire equity cycle framework is conditioned on r-star, but Laubach-Williams and Holston-Laubach-Williams estimates have standard errors of ±100-150bp. If r-star is 2.0% rather than 0.5-1.0%, current valuations are significantly overextended and the policy stance is not actually restrictive. What additional data or model approaches could narrow this confidence interval?

2. **Fiscal-monetary interaction**: The unprecedented combination of tight monetary policy and loose fiscal policy (deficit at 6-7% of GDP during expansion) is outside the standard New Keynesian framework's typical calibration. How does this alter the output gap estimates, the NAIRU, and the equity cycle dynamics? Is the fiscal impulse effectively extending the cycle, or is it storing up larger imbalances?

3. **AI productivity timing**: The Solow paradox ("you can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics") lasted ~20 years for IT. Is AI different, and on what time horizon would we expect TFP gains to materialize? The equity cycle's trajectory over the next 2-3 years depends critically on whether AI capex begins delivering measurable productivity within that window.

4. **Market concentration and the representative firm assumption**: The New Keynesian framework typically works with a representative firm or a continuum of monopolistically competitive firms. With the top 10 stocks representing 35-38% of the index, does the representative firm assumption break down? Does the equity cycle concept need to be decomposed into a "mega-cap tech cycle" and a "rest of the economy cycle"?

5. **Earnings quality measurement in the AI era**: Traditional accrual-based quality metrics may be distorted by the accounting treatment of AI-related investment (capitalized vs. expensed R&D, stock-based compensation, depreciation schedules for GPU infrastructure). What adjusted metrics best capture true earnings quality in the current environment?

6. **Nonlinear threshold effects in the financial accelerator**: The small-cap / large-cap divergence is modeled as a linear function of real rates, but the Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist model predicts nonlinear amplification at sufficiently tight financial conditions. At what threshold does the current divergence become self-reinforcing (small-cap distress → tighter lending → more small-cap distress)?

7. **Equilibrium P/E under regime change**: If the stock-bond correlation regime shifts from positive back to negative (inflation credibly anchored at 2%), how much P/E expansion would be warranted? And would this shift occur gradually or discontinuously?
