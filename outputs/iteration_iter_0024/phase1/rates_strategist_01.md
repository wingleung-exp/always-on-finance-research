# Equity Market Cycles & Valuation Regimes — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The yield curve is the single most reliable leading indicator of equity cycle turning points, but the mechanism operates through term premium shifts, not just slope inversion.** The 3m10y and 2s10s inversions have preceded every recession since 1970 with a 12-18 month lead, but the predictive power resides specifically in the expected-short-rate component compressing below the front end — pure term premium compression can invert the curve without signaling recession (as arguably occurred in 2019 and partially in 2022-2023 due to QT-era term premium dynamics).

2. **Equity valuation multiples are fundamentally a term premium derivative: the equity risk premium (ERP) and the bond term premium co-move because they share a common discount rate channel, but diverge when fiscal supply dynamics dominate.** The Shiller CAPE's inverse correlates ~0.6-0.7 with the 10-year real yield across regimes. When term premium rises due to Treasury supply rather than growth expectations, equities face a headwind that is structurally different from — and less responsive to — Fed easing than traditional monetary tightening.

3. **The current equity cycle (2022-present) is distinguished by a historically anomalous decoupling between financial conditions and the policy rate, mediated by the AI wealth effect, which has compressed the ERP to levels only seen in 1999-2000 and 1928-1929.** The S&P 500 ERP (earnings yield minus 10-year real yield) has narrowed to approximately 1.0-1.5%, compared to a post-2008 average of ~4.0%. This compression has occurred despite a term premium that the ACM model estimates at +50 to +80bp on the 10-year — elevated by post-GFC standards but below historical norms.

4. **Small-cap vs. large-cap divergence is primarily a term premium and credit channel story: small caps carry implicit duration and credit risk that makes them hypersensitive to the long-end term premium in a way mega-cap tech is not.** The Russell 2000's ~40% of constituents being unprofitable means the index functions partly as a credit instrument. When term premium rises on fiscal supply concerns, small caps face a double penalty (higher discount rate + tighter credit conditions) while mega-caps with net cash positions are insulated.

5. **The AI capex cycle represents a classic late-cycle capex boom visible in the yield curve through the 5y5y forward rate, which at ~3.8-4.2% is pricing a structurally higher neutral rate (r*) partly justified by AI-driven productivity optimism — but capex booms historically end when the yield curve steepens violently from the long end (bear steepening), not from Fed cuts.** The 2000 analogue is instructive: the telecom capex cycle peaked 12-18 months after the curve began bear-steepening on fiscal/supply concerns.

6. **Earnings quality deterioration is detectable in the term structure before it appears in reported earnings, through widening swap spreads, rising credit risk premium in the belly of the curve (5-7 year sector), and a flattening of the credit curve relative to the Treasury curve.** When corporate earnings quality degrades, firms extend debt maturity to lock in rates, creating supply pressure in the intermediate sector that manifests as credit curve flattening — a signal that currently (March 2026) shows early-stage deterioration.

7. **The post-2020 correlation regime — oscillating between -0.2 and +0.4 on a rolling basis — means that equity cycle analysis cannot be conducted independently from term premium decomposition, because equity drawdowns in positive-correlation regimes are amplified by duration losses in balanced portfolios, creating forced selling feedback loops absent in the 1998-2021 negative correlation era.** This has structural implications for equity cycle amplitude and recovery speed.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Yield Curve as Equity Cycle Leading Indicator (Decomposition Required)

The empirical record is well-established: the 3m10y spread has inverted before 7 of the last 7 recessions (and most equity bear markets) since 1969. However, the decomposition matters critically:

- **Kim-Wright term premium** on the 10-year fell to approximately -100bp in 2020 at peak QE, creating a mechanical flattening force independent of growth expectations. The 2022-2023 inversion occurred with term premium transitioning from deeply negative to roughly zero — meaning part of the inversion was "artificial" in the sense that it reflected normalization of QE-depressed term premium rather than purely a pessimistic expected rate path.
- **ACM model estimates** diverged from Kim-Wright by 30-50bp during this period, highlighting model dependence. The ACM model showed term premium turning positive in late 2023 (~+30bp) while Kim-Wright remained near zero.
- **Current state (March 2026)**: The curve has un-inverted (the Tier 2b signpost from prior research is active). The 2s10s is approximately +20 to +40bp, driven by a combination of Fed easing expectations pulling down the front end and persistent term premium supporting the long end. Historically, un-inversion followed by steepening has preceded equity market stress by 6-18 months (1990, 2001, 2007). This is the "steepening trap" — markets celebrate un-inversion as an all-clear, but the recession typically arrives during or after steepening, not during inversion.

### Claim 2: ERP as Term Premium Derivative

The theoretical link runs through the discount rate channel of the Gordon Growth Model: P/E ≈ 1/(r + ERP - g), where r is the risk-free rate inclusive of term premium. Empirically:

- From 1960-2024, the correlation between the inverse CAPE and the 10-year TIPS yield (available since 1997, imputed via Cleveland Fed models before that) runs approximately 0.6-0.7.
- **Critical distinction**: When the 10-year yield rises due to higher expected short rates (Fed hiking), equities typically absorb this via earnings growth expectations rising simultaneously (growth optimism). When the 10-year yield rises due to term premium (fiscal supply, inflation uncertainty), equities face a pure discount rate hit without offsetting earnings growth — this is strictly worse for equity multiples.
- The 2023-2024 experience was textbook: the October 2023 10-year selloff to 5.0% was largely term premium driven (ACM term premium surged ~100bp in 3 months), and equities corrected ~10%. The subsequent rally was driven partly by term premium retreating.
- The current fiscal backdrop — ~$2T+ annual net Treasury issuance, debt/GDP >120% — creates a structural bid for higher term premium that competes directly with equity multiples for capital allocation.

### Claim 3: ERP Compression to Historical Extremes

The arithmetic is straightforward:
- S&P 500 forward earnings yield: ~4.0-4.5% (based on ~$270-280 forward EPS, ~6100-6200 SPX level)
- 10-year TIPS yield: ~2.0-2.5%
- Implied ERP: ~1.5-2.0% (some estimates as low as 1.0% using trailing earnings)
- Post-2008 average ERP: ~4.0%
- 1999-2000 average ERP: ~1.0-1.5%
- Long-run (1900-present) average ERP: ~4.5-5.0%

The prior research confirmed the mechanism: Goldman Sachs FCI eased ~100bp equivalent despite 525bp of Fed hikes (1/7 historical precedent). The AI-driven wealth effect added $10-15T in market cap, overwhelming the monetary transmission mechanism. This is directly analogous to the tech bubble dynamic where Greenspan's "irrational exuberance" comment preceded the peak by over 3 years — compressed ERPs can persist far longer than rational-expectations models predict, but they compress the margin of safety for any exogenous shock.

### Claim 4: Small Cap Divergence as Term Premium/Credit Channel

- Russell 2000 floating-rate debt exposure: ~35-40% of Russell 2000 debt is floating-rate or matures within 2 years (vs. ~15-20% for S&P 500 constituents), creating direct sensitivity to the front-end rate path.
- The ~40% of Russell 2000 constituents that are unprofitable function as equity-linked credit instruments: their valuations depend on the expected terminal value discounted at rates that embed both term premium and credit risk premium.
- Since 2022, the Russell 2000/S&P 500 relative performance has tracked the 5-year BBB OAS with ~0.7 correlation — it is fundamentally a credit trade.
- When term premium rises on fiscal supply concerns (supply-side yield increase), small-cap credit conditions tighten even without Fed action. This creates the paradox where small caps can underperform during Fed easing cycles if the long end is simultaneously selling off on supply — precisely the bear-steepening scenario.

### Claim 5: AI Capex Cycle and the 5y5y Forward

- The 5y5y forward nominal rate currently trades at approximately 3.8-4.2%, implying a long-run neutral real rate of ~1.5-2.0% (assuming 2.0-2.2% long-run inflation expectations from TIPS breakevens).
- This is well above the 2012-2019 average of ~0.5-1.0% real neutral rate, suggesting the market is pricing either: (a) structurally higher productivity growth (AI bull case), (b) structurally higher fiscal deficits requiring higher equilibrium rates, or (c) de-anchoring of inflation expectations. The yield curve cannot distinguish between these.
- Historical capex cycle analogues: the telecom capex boom of 1997-2000 saw 5y5y forwards rise ~80bp as markets priced a "new economy" neutral rate; the unwind saw 5y5y forwards collapse ~200bp in 2001-2002 as the capex cycle reversed and growth expectations cratered.
- **Bear steepening as the termination signal**: In 1999-2000, the curve bear-steepened (long-end yields rising faster than short-end) as fiscal surplus expectations faded and Treasury supply concerns emerged simultaneously with capex overcapacity. The S&P 500 peaked within 6 months of sustained bear steepening. Current curve dynamics show nascent bear steepening pressure from fiscal supply, but this is partially offset by Fed easing pulling down the front end.

### Claim 6: Earnings Quality Signals in the Term Structure

- **Swap spread behavior**: Narrowing or negative swap spreads indicate dealer balance sheet stress and reduced credit intermediation capacity, which is a precursor to corporate funding stress. Swap spreads went negative in 2015 and 2023, both periods that preceded earnings quality concerns.
- **5-7 year credit curve flattening**: When companies anticipate earnings pressure, CFOs extend debt maturity (locking in "high but manageable" rates before conditions potentially worsen). This concentrates issuance in the belly of the curve, flattening the credit term structure relative to Treasuries.
- **IG credit spread as leading indicator**: The prior research established IG credit spreads >75bp from trough as a Tier 1 early warning signal (18-month lead, 6/7 hit rate). This directly maps to earnings quality because IG spread widening reflects market assessment of cash flow deterioration.
- Current observation: IG spreads remain compressed (~90-100bp OAS) but have begun widening from cycle tights, and 5-7 year IG issuance has been running at elevated levels — consistent with early-stage CFO hedging behavior.

### Claim 7: Correlation Regime and Equity Cycle Amplitude

Building directly on the extensive prior research (iterations 0018-0020):

- In the 1998-2021 negative correlation era, equity drawdowns were cushioned by Treasury rallies, reducing 60/40 portfolio drawdowns by ~30-40% relative to equity-only. This dampened forced selling from risk parity (~$200-400B AUM) and balanced mandates.
- In the current oscillating correlation regime (+0.05 to +0.15 average, per prior findings), duration provides inconsistent hedging. The August 2024 growth scare temporarily restored negative correlation, but the Q1 2025 tariff shock saw positive correlation (both equities and bonds selling off).
- **Implication for cycle amplitude**: Without reliable duration hedging, equity drawdowns trigger portfolio rebalancing that amplifies rather than dampens selling pressure. A 20% equity drawdown in a positive-correlation regime could generate 30-40% more forced selling than the same drawdown in a negative-correlation regime, due to the absence of the bond-rally-rebalancing cushion.
- The maturity bifurcation finding (2-year negative correlation, 30-year positive) suggests equities are more sensitive to long-end moves than short-end moves in the current regime — further evidence that term premium, not policy rate expectations, is the binding constraint on multiples.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Stock-Bond Correlation Regime Analysis (depth 6, confidence 6.2)**: This analysis is deeply dependent on the correlation regime findings. The oscillating correlation directly determines whether equity drawdowns are self-limiting (negative correlation) or self-reinforcing (positive correlation). The maturity bifurcation (2y negative, 30y positive) implies equity strategists should monitor the 30-year term premium as the marginal equity valuation driver, not the Fed funds rate.

- **Credit-Equity Linkage & Contagion Channels (depth 1, confidence 7.4)**: The small-cap analysis (Claim 4) is fundamentally a credit-equity linkage story. The Russell 2000 as a "credit instrument in equity clothing" bridges these topics directly. The IG spread early-warning framework from prior research provides the operational signpost for equity cycle deterioration.

- **Economic Growth Models & Cycle Positioning (depth 1, confidence 7.4)**: The yield curve's role as a business cycle leading indicator (Claim 1) connects directly to growth model frameworks. The decomposition into expected rates vs. term premium maps onto the distinction between potential growth expectations (expected rates → neutral rate → r*) and risk appetite/uncertainty (term premium).

- **Monetary Policy Transmission (from iter_0021)**: The "broken transmission" finding — financial conditions easing despite 525bp of hikes — is the proximate cause of the current ERP compression. If transmission repairs itself (e.g., AI-driven wealth effect reverses), the ERP could normalize violently. The fiscal constraint on Fed policy (implicit rate ceiling at ~5.0-5.5%) means the Fed cannot aggressively tighten to cool equity markets even if it wanted to — a structural asymmetry favoring equity bubbles.

- **Inflation Regime Identification (from iter_0023)**: The inflation regime determines whether yield curve movements are demand-driven or supply-driven, which per the Campbell-Pflueger-Viceira framework determines the equity-bond correlation and therefore the hedging properties of duration during equity drawdowns. The current transitional inflation regime (~55-60% supply-driven) is what creates the oscillating correlation that amplifies equity cycle dynamics.

- **Commodity Supercycles (from iter_0022)**: Commodity price cycles feed directly into the supply-shock channel that drives positive stock-bond correlation. An energy price spike would shift the shock mix toward supply-dominant, pushing the correlation positive and removing the duration hedge for equity portfolios — amplifying any equity drawdown.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the AI capex cycle fundamentally different from telecom?** If AI delivers genuine productivity gains (TFP growth of +0.5-1.0% annually), the elevated 5y5y forward and compressed ERP may be partially justified rather than purely speculative excess. The yield curve cannot distinguish between a justified structural shift in r* and a bubble — this is a fundamental limitation.

2. **What is the "true" term premium?** The ACM and Kim-Wright models diverge by 30-50bp in the current environment. This matters enormously: if ACM is right (+50-80bp), term premium is elevated but manageable; if Kim-Wright is right (~0-30bp), there is significant room for term premium to rise further, which would compress equity multiples from here.

3. **At what ERP level does the self-correcting mechanism engage?** An ERP of 1.0-1.5% has historically been unsustainable, but the 1990s showed this can persist for 3+ years. What triggers the snap-back — is it endogenous (earnings disappointment) or exogenous (geopolitical shock, fiscal crisis)?

4. **How does the Treasury buyback program interact with term premium and equity valuations?** Treasury's recent buyback program could dampen long-end volatility and suppress term premium, providing a stealth support to equity multiples that is not captured in standard macro models.

5. **What is the regime-switching probability?** The prior research established that only Tier 2b (un-inversion) of the 4-tier signpost hierarchy is active. What event or data sequence would activate Tier 1 (IG spreads >75bp from trough) and shift the balance of evidence toward a correlation flip that would fundamentally alter equity cycle dynamics?

6. **How should equity cycle analysis account for the concentration problem?** The top 7-10 stocks represent ~35% of S&P 500 market cap. These mega-caps have duration profiles, credit characteristics, and term premium sensitivities that are radically different from the median stock. Is "the equity cycle" even a coherent concept when the index is this concentrated, or do we need separate frameworks for mega-cap vs. the rest?
