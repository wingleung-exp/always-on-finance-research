# Demographics — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Demographics is the dominant slow-moving force reshaping the term structure, but its net effect on rates is ambiguous because it simultaneously depresses r* (savings channel) and elevates term premium (fiscal supply channel).** The market has not priced this internal tension — most rates models treat demographics as univocally rate-depressing.

2. **The pension-duration bid versus fiscal-supply collision is the defining curve dynamic of the next decade.** ~$55-60T in global pension assets (60% DB) creates mandate-driven demand for long duration, while aging-driven fiscal deterioration (2-4pp per OECD country crossing 35% dependency) expands Treasury supply. These forces reinforced each other from 2000-2020 and have now decisively diverged. The fulcrum is the 10Y-30Y segment.

3. **The lifecycle savings hypothesis ambiguity is the single most consequential unresolved question for rates markets.** Standard models (aging → precautionary savings → lower r*) predict a structurally flat or inverted curve with suppressed term premium. Goodhart-Pradhan (aging → dissaving → higher r*) predicts a structurally steep curve with elevated term premium. Current pricing implicitly assumes the standard model dominates, creating asymmetric risk if dissaving prevails.

4. **Simultaneous global aging eliminates the cross-border capital flow safety valve that historically moderated demographic rate effects.** When Japan aged alone, surplus savings flowed to younger, faster-growing economies. With all major economies aging concurrently, there is no demographic arbitrage: the global savings-investment balance shifts structurally, and the global term premium must reprice to clear a market with universally expanding fiscal supply and no marginal foreign buyer stepping in.

5. **Demographics tilts the inflation regime toward supply-shock dominance, which structurally lifts term premium by 50-150bp above the QE-era baseline.** Labor scarcity (ULC growing faster than productivity in 12/16 recent quarters), retiree dissaving, and politically-driven fiscal expansion all push toward supply-side inflation. The ACM term premium has already moved from -50bp (2020) to +50-100bp (early 2026), but the demographic component of this repricing is only beginning.

6. **The 5y5y forward rate is mispriced relative to the demographic trajectory.** If aging raises long-run neutral by even 25-50bp via the fiscal/dissaving channel while simultaneously increasing inflation uncertainty, the 5y5y forward — currently pricing ~3.5-3.8% nominal — should embed a higher term premium than is currently observed. The forward curve treats demographics as a known quantity already priced, but the regime-change nature of simultaneous global aging creates fat tails that forwards cannot capture.

7. **China's demographic cliff is the largest exogenous shock to the global term structure over 2026-2035.** Working-age population declining by ~100M by 2035 implies a structural reduction in China's current account surplus (or shift to deficit), reducing foreign official demand for USTs precisely when US fiscal supply is expanding. This is a term premium event, not an expectations event.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Ambiguous net rate effect:**
- Point estimates of aging's r* impact cluster at -0.03 to -0.05pp per 1pp dependency ratio increase, explaining 1.0-1.5pp of the ~3pp r* decline (1990-2020) — but partial R-squared is only 0.15-0.30, and the sample is confounded by globalization and post-GFC deleveraging occurring simultaneously.
- The field is structurally underpowered: needs 60-80 independent country-decade observations to detect a 0.5pp effect; has only 30-50.
- Meanwhile, the fiscal supply channel works in the opposite direction: pre-QE regression shows 20-40bp term premium per 1pp deficit/GDP above ~3-4% threshold. Every OECD country crossing 35% dependency has seen 2-4pp fiscal deterioration in 15 years.
- These two forces — downward pressure on expected short rates, upward pressure on term premium — produce a net effect on long yields that is genuinely uncertain.

**Claim 2 — Pension vs. fiscal supply collision:**
- UK DB pensions hold ~GBP 1.5T in gilts/LDI; US DB pensions ~$5T in fixed income. This demand is mandate-driven (ALM), not behavioral — it reverses only with regulatory change or the DB-to-DC transition.
- The UK LDI crisis (2022) demonstrated this structural bid is leveraged and fragile — a nonlinear risk.
- Fiscal supply is expanding: Japan +12pp fiscal deterioration over 30 years, France +5pp, UK +3pp from dependency ratio crossings.
- From 2000-2020, both forces compressed long yields (pension demand bid + QE-suppressed supply). Post-2020, supply is expanding via both QT and aging-driven deficits while pension demand persists. The curve kink at 10Y-30Y should widen.

**Claim 3 — Lifecycle savings ambiguity:**
- The standard model is supported by Japan's 30-year experience of low rates during aging — but Japan is non-generalizable (NIIP +70% GDP, >90% domestic ownership, persistent current account surplus).
- Goodhart-Pradhan is supported by 5 years of post-2020 evidence: Japan itself is now seeing 5%+ Shunto wages and nascent inflation after 27 years of deflation.
- Both mechanisms operate simultaneously with different lags; the answer depends critically on pension system design (DB vs DC, funded vs PAYG), which varies enormously.
- Confidence is only 5/10 in the KB — this is genuinely unresolved, not merely uncertain.

**Claim 4 — No demographic arbitrage:**
- All major economies aging simultaneously is unprecedented. Historical episodes (Japan aging 1990-2020) occurred alongside demographic dividends in China, India, and SE Asia, providing a surplus savings offset.
- The OECD-wide immigration restriction (US, Canada, Australia, UK, Italy, France, Netherlands, Sweden all tightening) compounds the effect by removing the labor mobility safety valve.
- Markets mispriced growth by 1-2pp annually for at least a decade across 7 prior demographic transitions — but those transitions were asynchronous, allowing capital reallocation. Synchronous aging removes this error-correction mechanism.

**Claim 5 — Inflation regime shift and term premium:**
- Structural labor scarcity: global labor supply roughly doubled 1990-2015, now reversing. US labor share recovered from 57% (2014) to 59-60% (2025).
- Political economy: rising median voter age (Japan ~55, Italy ~52, Germany ~49, US ~47) shifts electorate toward transfer recipients, producing fiscal expansion and eroding CB independence.
- The ACM term premium's move from -50bp to +50-100bp captures early repricing. The pre-QE fiscal sensitivity (20-40bp per 1pp deficit/GDP above threshold) applied to projected fiscal deterioration suggests an additional 40-80bp of term premium expansion is plausible over the next 5-10 years.

**Claim 6 — 5y5y forward mispricing:**
- The 5y5y forward is the market's proxy for long-run neutral nominal rates. If demographics raises inflation uncertainty (supply-shock dominance), the risk premium embedded in the 5y5y should be higher than during the 1998-2021 negative-correlation regime.
- R* estimation uncertainty already spans 480bp (combined 90% CIs from LW, HLW, Lubik-Matthes). Demographics adds a structural source of uncertainty on top of this.
- The demographic pricing paradox: demographics-as-trend is likely priced, but demographics-as-regime-change may not be, due to hyperbolic discounting at nonlinear transition points.

**Claim 7 — China's term premium shock:**
- China's current account surplus has historically funded ~$1T+ in foreign reserve accumulation, a significant share of which went into USTs.
- Working-age population declining by ~100M by 2035, birth collapse from 17M (2016) to 9M (2024), shift from investment to consumption growth — all point toward reduced external surplus.
- This removes a marginal buyer from the long end of the UST curve at exactly the time fiscal supply expands, compounding the term premium effect.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Ambiguous net rate effect | **7/10** | The tension between r* depression and term premium elevation is well-documented but the net effect is model-dependent. The sign of each channel is robust; the magnitude is not. |
| 2. Pension vs. fiscal collision | **8/10** | Both forces are quantifiable and directionally certain. The divergence is mechanical. The uncertainty is which dominates at which maturity. |
| 3. Lifecycle savings ambiguity | **5/10** | This is genuinely unresolved in the academic literature. N is too small, confounds are severe, and the answer is pension-system-dependent. |
| 4. No demographic arbitrage | **8/10** | Simultaneous global aging is an empirical fact. The implication for global capital flows is directionally clear though the magnitude depends on EM exceptions (India, SSA). |
| 5. Supply-shock inflation tilt | **6/10** | The channels are clear (labor scarcity, fiscal expansion, political economy) but the 27-year Japan counterexample warns against overconfidence. Japan's recent inflation break may validate the thesis or prove transient. |
| 6. 5y5y mispricing | **5/10** | This is a market view, not a fact. Requires an opinion on which lifecycle savings model dominates. The logic is sound but depends on claims 3 and 5 being correct. |
| 7. China term premium shock | **7/10** | The demographic arithmetic is near-certain. The transmission to UST demand depends on China's policy choices (reserve management, capital account), which are unpredictable. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**Fiscal Dominance:** Demographics is the structural accelerant of fiscal dominance. The dependency-ratio-driven fiscal deterioration (2-4pp per OECD country) directly feeds the `r_g_differential_debt_dynamics` identity. Labor force growth at 0.3% vs 1.5% caps the growth channel that historically deleveraged debt. The political economy of aging electorates provides the micro-foundation for `financial_repression_modal_outcome` — financial repression emerges as the path of least resistance, not as deliberate policy. Term premium is the primary market signal of this progression (`term_premium_fiscal_signal`).

**Rates-Equity Correlation:** The demographic regime shift from demand-shock to supply-shock dominance directly supports the thesis in `negative_correlation_historical_anomaly` that the 1998-2021 negative stock-bond correlation was the exception, not the rule. If demographics drives supply-side inflation, the correlation regime reverts to positive — with profound implications for 60/40 portfolios and risk parity.

**Monetary Policy:** Demographics compounds `r_star_estimation_uncertainty` by adding a structural source of r* ambiguity (lifecycle savings direction). If aging raises r* via dissaving while simultaneously expanding fiscal supply, the Fed faces a doubly constrained environment where neither rate cuts nor rate hikes clearly resolve the tension.

**Sovereign Debt:** `Japan_non_generalizability` is critical here. Japan's experience cannot serve as the template for other aging economies because its unique configuration (NIIP +70%, domestic ownership >90%, current account surplus) is precisely the set of conditions that aging erodes elsewhere. The US is the anti-Japan on every structural metric.

**Credit Markets:** The `maturity_wall_labor_squeeze` concept represents the credit-market transmission of demographic labor scarcity — firms cannot cut labor costs during refinancing stress, elevating default rates above historical models. This interacts with term premium: if demographic fiscal expansion keeps long rates elevated, the maturity wall gets more expensive to roll.

## Open Questions

1. **Which lifecycle savings model dominates at what stage of the aging process, and does pension system design (DB vs DC, funded vs PAYG) determine the answer?** This is the single most important unresolved question for long-run rates positioning. The current academic literature cannot resolve it with available data.

2. **Where on the curve does the pension-demand bid dominate versus the fiscal-supply expansion?** The 10Y-30Y butterfly may be the key diagnostic — if the 20Y cheapens persistently relative to the 10Y and 30Y, that signals fiscal supply overwhelming pension demand at the belly while pension ALM protects the ultra-long end.

3. **What is the term premium elasticity to China's reserve demand withdrawal?** If China shifts from ~$200B/year in UST accumulation to flat or negative, how much of that flows through to the 10Y term premium? The basis trade and hedge fund leveraged positioning may amplify or dampen this.

4. **Can AI-driven productivity growth offset the demographic constraint on potential GDP growth quickly enough to prevent r>g from becoming structural?** The base rate of technology-driven >10-year productivity accelerations is arguably 2 episodes in 150 years. If AI fails to deliver on this timeline, demographics locks in fiscal dominance.

5. **Is the OECD-wide immigration restriction cyclical (18-36 months) or structural (10+ years)?** Only 3/6 democracies have historically sustained high immigration for >10 years. If structural, the labor scarcity → inflation → term premium channel becomes self-reinforcing.

6. **At what term premium level does the demographic fiscal trajectory become self-defeating?** If term premium rises enough to push r decisively above g, the debt dynamics become unstable, which should push term premium higher still — a reflexive loop. What is the threshold, and is the UK Truss episode (100bp in 4 days) the template for the speed of adjustment?
