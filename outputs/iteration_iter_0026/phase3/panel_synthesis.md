## HIGH_CONFIDENCE

**1. Below-replacement fertility is near-universal and effectively irreversible.**
- **Confidence: 9/10**
- **Originating agents:** quant_researcher_01 (9/10), generalist_02 (7/10), challenger_01 (concedes facts)
- **Debate survival:** pair_2 confirmed unanimously. No agent disputes.
- **Surviving evidence:** 36/38 OECD countries below 2.1 TFR. 0/25 recoveries from TFR <1.5 back to replacement. UN median variant overestimates by ~0.3 children/woman (p<0.01) across 50 countries. Ultra-low outliers (South Korea 0.72, China ~1.0-1.1) show no floor. Pronatalist policy advocates bear the burden of explaining why they expect to be in the 16% recovery base rate — and even then, recovery means to ~1.6, not replacement.

**2. Dependency ratio crossings reliably predict fiscal deterioration in direction (not magnitude).**
- **Confidence: 9/10 direction; 5/10 magnitude**
- **Originating agents:** generalist_02 (8/10), quant_researcher_01 (9/10 direction), risk_analyst_02 (8/10), rates_strategist_01 (8/10), generalist_01 (implicit)
- **Debate survival:** Confirmed across pair_0, pair_2, pair_3. Challenger noted effective dependency gap but did not contest directionality.
- **Surviving evidence:** Every OECD country crossing 35% old-age dependency experienced 2-4pp structural fiscal deterioration within 15 years. 14 episodes, median 3.2pp, IQR 2.0-5.5pp. Critical decomposition from quant_researcher_01: utilization/cost uncertainty (+-3.0% GDP) dominates demographic uncertainty (+-1.5% GDP) by 2:1. The 95% CI on 2050 age-related costs spans 3-8% of GDP. A second threshold at ~45% (Japan, Italy) triggers qualitative shifts in monetary-fiscal coordination.

**3. The 1998-2021 negative stock-bond correlation was historically anomalous.**

The negative-correlation regime coincided with working-age growth, high savings, credible inflation targeting, and demand-shock dominance — conditions now reversing. The pattern is robust; the causal decomposition is not.

**10. Correlation assumptions — not levels — are the primary mispricing across asset classes.**

**12. The aging-r* empirical literature is structurally underpowered — magnitude claims are unreliable.**
- **Confidence: 8/10**
- **Originating agents:** quant_researcher_01 (8/10)
- **Debate survival:** pair_2 agreed as "a mathematical fact about sample sizes."
- **Surviving evidence:** Point estimates of -0.03 to -0.05pp per 1pp dependency ratio increase. But the field has 30-50 observations against 60-80 needed for 0.5pp effect at 80% power. Partial R-squared of 0.15-0.30 means 70-85% of r* variance is non-demographic. The sign (aging likely depresses r*) is more robust than the magnitude (0.8pp to 2.2pp cumulative range). Anyone claiming demographics is "the most important" secular r* determinant must explain why a variable with 15-30% explanatory power dominates the other 70-85%.

## REFUTED

**R1. "The 5y5y forward real rate encodes a demographic signal."**
- **Killed by:** challenger_01 (8/10), pair_2 debate
- **Reasoning:** No decomposition methodology exists to isolate demographics from expected short rates, term premium, inflation expectations, convexity hedging, and positioning. The partial R-squared of 0.15-0.30 means 70-85% of r* variance is non-demographic. This is attribution error — mistaking partial correlation during a specific period for a structural relationship. The claim should be dropped from the KB or heavily qualified to: "demographics may contribute to long-run r* through labor supply and productivity channels, but the effect is not identifiable in market rates at tradeable horizons."

**R2. "The Minsky financial instability cycle is demographically conditioned."**
- **Killed by:** challenger_01 (7/10), risk_analyst_02 (self-rated 5/10), pair_3 debate
- **Reasoning:** Financial crises have occurred across every conceivable demographic configuration: 1997 Asian crisis (young, growing populations), 2008 GFC (moderate US demographics), 1990 Japan bubble (before aging became severe, median age ~37), 2022 UK gilt crisis (moderately aging). The timescale mismatch (Minsky cycles 5-15 years, demographics 30-50 years) makes causal attribution nearly impossible. The hypothesis is unfalsifiable — any crisis can be retroactively attributed to "demographic conditioning." NOTE: generalist_02's observation that *recovery duration* correlates with demographic backdrop (Japan 30yr, Spain 10+yr, US 5yr, Nordics 3-5yr) survives as a separate, weaker claim — but the original Minsky conditioning thesis should be abandoned.

**R3. "Bank profitability decline is demographically determined."**
- **Killed by:** challenger_01 (7/10), pair_3 debate
- **Reasoning:** Swedish banks profitable despite aging population (Riksbank maintained positive rates). Switzerland similarly. Japan's bank profitability crisis is a BoJ zero-rate policy artifact, not a demographic inevitability. Japanese banks adapted by becoming the largest cross-border lenders in Asia. The claim conflates the *policy response to aging* with aging itself. NOTE: The *policy-contingent* version survives at lower confidence: if aging economies adopt zero-rate policies for extended periods, banking profitability suffers. And the three-phase *descriptive pattern* (loan growth stagnation → margin compression → credit quality deterioration) remains useful as a watchlist framework conditioned on monetary policy, not demographics alone.

**R4. "GLP-1 demand shock is entirely unpriced."**
- **Killed by:** pair_1 debate
- **Reasoning:** Walmart publicly flagged GLP-1 effects on food basket volumes in Q3 2024 earnings. Every food company analyst has been asked about GLP-1 demand impact since 2023. The direction may be underpriced; "entirely unpriced" is an extraordinary claim contradicted by visible market awareness. The underlying demand shock analysis (direction, processed food concentration) survives; the pricing claim does not.

**R5. "The term premium will expand 50-150bp above QE-era baseline due to demographics."**
- **Killed by:** pair_2 debate
- **Reasoning:** Derived by applying pre-QE regime coefficients (20-40bp per 1pp deficit/GDP) across a regime boundary where QE fundamentally changed market structure, with uncertain fiscal deterioration inputs (2-4pp with 95% CI spanning 3-8% GDP), and ignoring that the ACM term premium has already moved from -50bp to +50-100bp. Three layers of uncertainty multiplied through uncertain transmission mechanisms produce a range so wide as to be uninformative. The directional claim (term premium rising) has merit; the specific quantification does not.

**R6. "RMBS pipeline compression is primarily a demographic story."**

## KEY_DISAGREEMENTS

**D1. Is aging net inflationary or deflationary? (The single most important unresolved question)**
- Standard lifecycle model (aging → savings → lower r*) supported by Japan's 27-year deflation and Germany's persistent current account surplus. Goodhart-Pradhan (aging → dissaving → higher r*) supported by recent emerging evidence (ULC > productivity 12/16 quarters, Japan's 5%+ Shunto wages). N<5 clean episodes. quant_researcher_01 and challenger_01 argue ~50/50; rates_strategist_01 leans 55-60% inflationary. The confounds (post-COVID supply chains, Ukraine energy shock, fiscal stimulus) are enormous and cannot be separated from demographic effects in the current data. Resolution requires watching: Japan dissaving trajectory, Germany savings rate evolution, and whether inflation persistence outlasts the commodity/supply chain normalization. **Flag for continuous monitoring.**

**D2. How much do institutions and adaptation attenuate demographic headwinds? (Magnitude debate)**
- challenger_01 argues the iter_0025 consensus overweights demographic determinism by 30-50% in magnitude and 5-10 years in timing, citing historical pattern of adaptation (Green Revolution, South Korea's growth during TFR collapse, Japan's current outperformance). The consensus view (most other agents) argues binding constraints are real. pair_3 resolved: "Agent A's directional claims are mostly right. Agent B's magnitude objections are mostly right." Effective dependency gap of 4-5pp supports the attenuation thesis. The assumption audit shows the joint probability of 3+ consensus assumptions failing simultaneously is 20-30% — a non-trivial tail risk of consensus unraveling. **Resolution requires empirical measurement of effective vs. headline dependency gap across OECD.**

**D3. Is OECD immigration restriction structural or cyclical?**
- risk_analyst_02 (7/10 structural): national security framing is "sticky," simultaneity is unprecedented, reversal requires simultaneous sentiment collapse across multiple countries. challenger_01 (5/10): labor scarcity is the most reliable predictor of liberalization, Japan 10x'd foreign workers quietly, Canada carved out exceptions within 12 months. pair_3 resolved: "restriction is real but leakier than headline." Both converge toward 5-6/10. The debate centers on whether skilled/sector-specific carve-outs are sufficient to alter dependency ratio trajectories. **Resolution requires tracking sector-specific immigration flows separately from aggregate restriction.**

**D4. Can AI offset demographic labor headwinds within investable horizons?**
- Every agent acknowledges this as the critical wildcard. Base rate of technology-driven productivity accelerations lasting >10 years: ~2 episodes in 150 years (electrification, IT revolution). Both required 15-25 years of infrastructure buildout. challenger_01 assigns 40-50% probability AI substitutes at meaningful scale. If AI delivers, most demographic claims need substantial revision; if not, constraints bind harder. **Genuinely unresolvable with current information. The speed of AI deployment in labor-constrained sectors (healthcare, agriculture, manufacturing) is the leading indicator to monitor.**

**D5. What does China's demographic cliff mean for asset prices? (Arithmetic vs. translation gap)**
- 9/10 on demographics, 4-5/10 on financial translation. commodities_analyst_02 focuses on soybean/agricultural demand. credit_analyst_02 traces contagion through commodity-to-CLO channel. generalist_01 maps copper vs. oil divergence. risk_analyst_02 frames "use it or lose it" strategic calculus. quant_researcher_01 notes N=0 for comparable episodes. The range of financial outcomes is enormous because there is no precedent for a 1.4B-person country aging before reaching high income. **The commodity-specific mapping (oil demand plateau, soybean decline) is the most tractable sub-question; the strategic calculus is the least resolvable.**
