**Claim 1 (Pension-Fiscal Collision):** The $55-60T pension asset figure is well-documented (OECD Pension Markets in Focus, Willis Towers Watson Global Pension Assets Study). The key regime change is that from 2000-2020, both pension demand AND fiscal dynamics supported lower long-end yields — pension demand absorbed issuance, while fiscal deficits were moderate. Post-2020, Treasury issuance is $2T+/year and rising while pension plans are maturing (shifting from accumulation to payout), creating a structural demand-supply mismatch at the exact maturities (10Y-30Y) where pension mandates concentrate. The UK LDI crisis of September 2022 is N=1 for what happens when this structural bid fractures under stress — gilt yields rose 150bp+ in days. The 10Y-30Y butterfly spread is where this collision will be most visible.

**Claim 2 (Term Premium + 40-80bp):** The 14 OECD episodes of 35% dependency crossing → 2-4pp fiscal deterioration is the strongest empirical foundation in the KB (confidence 9). **Claim 3 (5y5y Bimodality):** The aging-inflation-deflation debate is genuinely unresolved at ~50/50 odds. Japan's 27 years of deflation during aging is the strongest single datapoint, but it coincided with unique conditions (NIIP +70% GDP, current account surplus, >90% domestic debt ownership). For the 5y5y forward — currently pricing approximately 3.5% nominal (implying ~1.5% real + ~2% inflation expectations) — the true distribution under demographic uncertainty is two humps: one centered around 2.5-3.0% (Japan/disinflationary path) and one around 4.0-4.5% (Goodhart-Pradhan/inflationary path). Standard term structure models assume unimodal distributions and therefore misprice the tails.

**Claim 4 (Global Aging Eliminates Arbitrage):** Japan + Europe + China + US = ~50-55% of global GDP aging simultaneously. Historically, DM aging was offset by capital flows to younger economies (EM reserve accumulation recycled into Treasuries, suppressing term premium by an estimated 50-100bp during 2000-2015). This channel is structurally impaired: petrodollar recycling is declining, BoJ normalization reduces Japanese UST demand (each 25bp BoJ hike narrows the carry incentive), and China is both aging itself and reducing UST holdings for geopolitical reasons. **Claim 5 (Immigration as Curve Driver):** US ±2M/year immigration swings exceed natural increase (~1M) by 2x. For curve positioning, immigration operates primarily through the expectations channel (front-end pricing of Fed rate path) rather than the term premium channel (long-end supply). This creates a curve steepening bias from immigration restriction: front end reprices higher on stickier inflation, while the long end is already driven by fiscal supply. Conversely, immigration liberalization would flatten the curve.

**Claim 6 (Correlation Regime):** The positive correlation base rate of ~64% over 1950-2025 is established empirical fact. The 1998-2021 negative regime coincided with working-age growth, globalization, and credible inflation targeting. Demographics contributes to the correlation shift through three channels: (a) supply-shock dominance from labor scarcity replacing demand-shock dominance, (b) fiscal expansion driving simultaneous equity selloff + bond selloff dynamics, (c) central bank reaction function distortion as aging political economy erodes independence. The feedback to term premium is critical: if bonds no longer hedge equities, investors demand more compensation for holding duration — this is a term premium amplifier.

**Claim 7 (Term Premium Floor):** Current ACM estimate is 50-80bp; adjusted for model limitations (QT distortion, foreign official demand changes, basis trade positioning), the true term premium is likely 80-130bp. Even in the deflationary aging scenario, healthcare and pension spending rise mechanically — the fiscal deterioration channel operates regardless of inflation outcome. The disinflationary scenario compresses inflation risk premium but cannot eliminate the fiscal supply premium. This sets a floor around 60-100bp, compared to the -50bp to 0bp range that prevailed 2015-2020.

**Claim 8 (Effective Dependency Ratio Buffer):** The 65-74 LFPR increase from 17% to 27% is genuine but has limits — it represents mostly part-time, lower-productivity work. The fiscal contribution per older worker is materially below the average working-age adult (lower income → lower tax revenue; often still receiving partial benefits). The 4-5pp effective dependency gap delays the 35% threshold crossing by perhaps 3-5 years but does not prevent it. For curve positioning, this is a "trade timing" issue, not a "trade direction" issue.
