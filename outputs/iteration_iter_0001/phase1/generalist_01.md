

# Demographics — Cross-Asset Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The global demographic dividend is reversing into a demographic drag, and this is the single most underpriced secular force across asset classes.** Working-age population growth is decelerating or negative in every major developed economy and China, structurally reducing potential GDP growth, equilibrium real rates, and trend earnings growth.

2. **Demographics are structurally inflationary at the margin over the next decade, contradicting the 2010s "secular stagnation" consensus.** The shift from a global labor surplus (driven by China/Eastern Europe integration and baby boomer peak productivity) to a global labor scarcity regime raises the floor on wage growth and reduces the disinflationary impulse that suppressed rates for 30 years.

3. **The stock-bond correlation regime is influenced by demographic structure, and aging populations bias toward a positive stock-bond price correlation (both falling together in stress), degrading the core portfolio diversification assumption.** In an inflationary demographic regime, bonds lose their hedging properties for equity drawdowns — this is the single most dangerous implication for multi-asset portfolios.

4. **Japan is the leading indicator, not an anomaly.** Japan's demographic trajectory (population peaked ~2008, working-age peaked ~1995) previews dynamics now arriving in China, South Korea, and parts of Europe. The asset market consequences — compressed equity risk premia, ultra-low rates followed by rate normalization difficulty, persistent currency weakness when policy diverges — are instructive for positioning.

5. **Demographic divergence across regions creates durable relative value opportunities in FX, equity, and rates markets.** The US (via immigration) and India maintain relatively favorable demographics vs. China, Germany, South Korea, and Japan. This divergence supports structural USD resilience, US equity outperformance, and higher US neutral rates relative to demographically challenged peers.

6. **Dependency ratio inflection points trigger fiscal deterioration that reprices sovereign credit risk and term premia.** As old-age dependency ratios cross critical thresholds, entitlement spending accelerates non-linearly, widening structural deficits and increasing sovereign bond supply — this adds a fiscal premium to long-duration government bonds independent of monetary policy.

7. **The housing market is a primary transmission channel from demographics to asset prices, and the US is entering a period of millennial-driven demand plateau while other markets (China, Japan, Korea) face outright demographic demand collapse.** Residential real estate is the largest asset class globally, and demographic shifts in household formation rates are the strongest predictor of long-run real house prices.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Demographic drag is underpriced:**
The UN projects global working-age (15-64) population growth to decelerate from ~1.2% annually in the 2000s to ~0.5% by the 2030s. China's working-age population has been shrinking since ~2015 (by ~3-5 million per year). Europe's has been declining since ~2010. The standard Solow growth decomposition (GDP growth ≈ labor force growth + productivity growth + capital deepening) implies a ~50-70bp reduction in potential GDP growth from demographics alone. Yet consensus long-run earnings growth assumptions for global equities remain anchored near historical averages that embedded much higher labor force growth. The Shiller CAPE and forward P/E ratios for most developed markets do not fully reflect a permanently lower growth trajectory.

**Claim 2 — Structurally inflationary:**
The Goodhart-Pradhan thesis (articulated in *The Great Demographic Reversal*, 2020) argues that the 1980-2020 disinflationary era was substantially driven by the one-time integration of ~1 billion low-cost workers into the global trading system, coinciding with baby boomer peak earning years (high savings, high productivity). That demographic tailwind is now reversing: the global labor force participation rate has peaked, China's surplus rural labor is exhausted (Lewis turning point), and aging populations shift the consumption/production balance toward excess demand. This raises equilibrium inflation by an estimated 50-150bp, which flows directly into neutral rate estimates and thus bond fair value. Cross-asset implication: if markets are still pricing the old disinflationary demographic regime, real rates are too low and breakeven inflation is too narrow.

**Claim 3 — Stock-bond correlation regime:**
Empirical work (Campbell, Sunderam, Viceira 2017; Ilmanen 2022) shows that the stock-bond return correlation is regime-dependent and heavily influenced by whether the dominant macro risk is demand-driven (negative correlation: bonds hedge equities) or supply-driven/inflationary (positive correlation: both assets sell off together). Demographic-driven labor scarcity is a supply-side shock. In the 1970s-early 1980s (last inflationary regime), stock-bond correlations were persistently positive. If demographics push us back toward a supply-constrained economy, the negative stock-bond correlation that has anchored 60/40 portfolio construction since ~2000 is at risk. Data since 2022 already shows episodes of positive stock-bond correlation returning. A 60/40 portfolio that assumes -0.2 stock-bond correlation but realizes +0.3 experiences roughly 15-25% higher portfolio volatility than expected — a material risk budgeting error.

**Claim 4 — Japan as leading indicator:**
Japan's Nikkei 225 peaked in 1989, roughly coinciding with the peak rate of working-age population growth. Equity market total returns from 1990-2012 were negative in real terms. JGB yields declined for 30 years as the BoJ fought demographic deflation. When Abenomics attempted reflation, the yen weakened ~40% (2012-2015). China's equity market has been flat-to-down since 2021 as its demographic reality became consensus — the CSI 300 has underperformed EM ex-China dramatically. Korea's KOSPI has similarly derated. The sequencing — growth slowdown → rate suppression → currency pressure → fiscal expansion → eventual repricing — appears generalizable.

**Claim 5 — Regional divergence as RV opportunity:**
US working-age population growth, supported by immigration running ~1M+ per year (net), remains positive at ~0.3-0.5% annually vs. negative in Japan (-0.5%), China (-0.3%), Germany (-0.2%), Korea (-0.5%). India's working-age population continues growing at ~1.0% annually through the 2040s. This divergence maps directly to relative potential GDP growth differentials, which are a primary driver of equilibrium rate differentials and long-run currency trends. The US neutral rate (r*) should structurally exceed the Eurozone, Japan, and China neutral rates by 50-100bp on demographics alone, supporting USD carry. Indian and select ASEAN equity markets benefit from demographic tailwinds that echo China's 2000-2010 period.

**Claim 6 — Fiscal and term premia:**
Old-age dependency ratios (65+/working-age) are projected to roughly double in most OECD countries by 2050. Each percentage-point increase in the dependency ratio is associated with ~0.5-1.0% of GDP increase in age-related public spending (pensions, healthcare). At current dependency ratio trajectories, the US faces ~3-5% of GDP in additional structural spending by 2040, the Eurozone ~4-6%, Japan ~3-4% (already elevated). This spending is politically non-discretionary. Without offsetting revenue increases, this implies persistent large deficits and growing debt/GDP ratios, which empirical work (Laubach 2009, Greenlaw et al. 2013) associates with 2-5bp of term premium per percentage point of debt/GDP. Cross-asset implication: long-end government bonds face structural cheapening pressure from supply, adding a demographic fiscal premium to term premia estimates.

**Claim 7 — Housing transmission:**
Mankiw-Weil (1989) demonstrated the demographic-housing price link, though with imperfect timing. Household formation rates, driven by the 25-40 age cohort, are the primary demand driver for housing. In the US, millennials (born ~1981-1996) are currently in peak household formation, but this cohort plateaus by ~2028-2030. China's 25-40 cohort is already declining sharply (down ~25% from peak by 2035). Japan and Korea face even steeper declines. Given that residential real estate represents ~$250-300 trillion globally (larger than equity and bond markets combined), demographic-driven housing demand shifts have enormous wealth effects that propagate through consumer spending, bank balance sheets, and local government revenue.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Demographic drag underpriced | 7/10 | Demographics are slow-moving and well-documented, but "underpriced" is harder to verify — markets may partially embed lower growth via lower rate expectations. |
| 2. Structurally inflationary | 6/10 | The Goodhart-Pradhan framework is compelling but contested. AI/automation could offset labor scarcity. Immigration policy is a wild card. The inflationary impulse from demographics may be partially or fully neutralized by technology. |
| 3. Stock-bond correlation shift | 8/10 | Strong empirical and theoretical support. The correlation regime has already begun shifting (2022-present). This is the highest-conviction portfolio construction implication. |
| 4. Japan as leading indicator | 7/10 | Pattern recognition across Japan → China/Korea is suggestive but not deterministic. Policy responses differ. Cultural and institutional factors matter. |
| 5. Regional divergence as RV | 7/10 | Clear demographic differentials, but FX and equity markets reflect many factors beyond demographics. Immigration policy is a key risk to the US thesis — restrictive policy shifts could narrow the advantage. |
| 6. Fiscal/term premia | 8/10 | Demographic spending pressures are nearly mechanical given existing entitlement structures. The term premium implication is well-supported empirically. |
| 7. Housing transmission | 6/10 | Demographic-housing link is well-established but housing markets are heavily influenced by supply constraints, regulation, and rates in ways that can overwhelm demographic demand shifts. |

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary Policy & Rates:** Demographics are a primary input to r* (neutral rate) estimation. If demographic drag lowers r*, the rates market should reflect a structurally lower terminal rate — but if demographics are also inflationary, r* may not fall as much as pure growth models suggest. This creates a tension that rates strategists must resolve.

- **Fiscal Policy & Sovereign Debt:** Aging populations mechanically increase entitlement spending, making fiscal sustainability analysis inseparable from demographic projection. Debt/GDP trajectories in Japan, Italy, and eventually the US are fundamentally demographic stories.

- **Equity Valuation & Earnings:** Lower labor force growth → lower potential GDP → lower trend earnings growth, which should compress justified P/E ratios by ~2-4 turns at the index level. Sector implications are also significant: healthcare and senior services benefit; residential construction, education, and youth-oriented consumer face headwinds.

- **Inflation & Commodities:** If demographics are inflationary (labor scarcity), commodities — especially energy and agricultural products — face a higher demand floor relative to supply, supporting real asset allocations. Gold benefits from both the inflation hedge demand and the fiscal deterioration channel.

- **Geopolitics & Trade:** Demographic decline in China increases the risk of aggressive foreign policy (the "closing window" thesis) and accelerates the shift toward autarky/reshoring, which is inflationary. US-China strategic competition is partly a race between US immigration-driven demographic resilience and China's demographic decline.

- **Technology & AI:** The critical counterargument to demographic pessimism. If AI/automation can substitute for labor at scale, the inflationary and growth-drag implications of demographics are substantially mitigated. This is the key uncertainty — the cross-asset strategist must assign a probability to the "AI offsets demographics" thesis and size positions accordingly.

- **Currency Markets:** Demographic growth differentials are among the most reliable long-run drivers of real exchange rates. Countries with faster working-age population growth tend to see real currency appreciation via the Balassa-Samuelson effect.

## Open Questions

1. **How quickly can AI/automation offset demographic labor scarcity?** This is the single largest uncertainty. If AI achieves broad labor substitution by 2030-2035, the inflationary and growth implications of demographics are substantially reduced. If it takes until 2040+, the demographic drag dominates the next decade.

2. **Will US immigration policy remain supportive enough to maintain the demographic advantage?** Recent political shifts suggest immigration could be curtailed, which would erode the US demographic premium and narrow the growth differential vs. Europe/Japan.

3. **At what dependency ratio does fiscal stress become non-linear?** Linear extrapolation of spending pressures may understate the risk. Political dynamics around entitlement reform create discontinuities — does the US face a sudden fiscal adjustment, or a gradual grinding pressure?

4. **Is the stock-bond correlation shift durable or episodic?** The post-2022 positive correlation episodes could be transitory (inflation-shock-driven) rather than a permanent regime change. If central banks re-anchor inflation expectations successfully, the negative correlation could reassert.

5. **How do we properly price the demographic divergence between India/frontier markets and DM/China?** Indian equities trade at ~22x forward P/E — is the demographic premium already embedded, or does the market still underweight the long-run growth implication?

6. **What is the interaction between demographics and wealth inequality?** Aging societies tend to concentrate wealth among older cohorts, potentially depressing aggregate demand even as aggregate "wealth" appears high. This has implications for consumption-driven sectors and the savings-investment balance.

7. **How do demographic forces interact with the green energy transition?** Aging, shrinking workforces in key manufacturing countries (China, Germany, Korea) could constrain the pace of energy transition capital expenditure, creating supply bottlenecks in clean energy infrastructure.
