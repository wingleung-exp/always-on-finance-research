

# Risk Appetite Regimes — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite is not an exogenous psychological variable but an endogenous product of the financial structure's leverage and liquidity conditions.** What mainstream finance labels "risk appetite" or "risk sentiment" is better understood through Minsky's framework as the behavioral manifestation of where the economy sits in the financial instability cycle. Rising risk appetite is itself the mechanism by which stability breeds instability.

2. **The current risk appetite regime (as of early 2026) reflects a late-stage speculative/Ponzi transition in key sectors, particularly U.S. corporate credit and AI-adjacent equity, masked by strong nominal GDP growth and fiscal deficits that sustain private sector cash flows.** The U.S. government's persistent fiscal deficit (approximately 6-7% of GDP) is arithmetically providing the income flows that validate leveraged positions — a Kalecki profit equation effect — but this is being misread as fundamental strength rather than fragility held in suspension.

3. **Risk appetite regime shifts are best predicted not by VIX levels or credit spreads in isolation, but by monitoring the sectoral financial balances and their rate of change, particularly the private sector's net lending/borrowing position.** When the domestic private sector swings from net saver to net borrower, this signals an unsustainable risk appetite regime that will eventually reverse violently.

4. **The Minsky cycle produces three distinct risk appetite regimes: hedge (risk appetite disciplined by income coverage), speculative (risk appetite driven by asset price appreciation and refinancing expectations), and Ponzi (risk appetite sustained only by rising asset prices covering both principal and interest).** Regime transitions are nonlinear and path-dependent — the shift from speculative to Ponzi is often invisible in conventional risk metrics until it is too late.

5. **Credit impulse — the second derivative of private credit — is the single most important leading indicator for risk appetite regime shifts.** A decelerating credit impulse, even while credit levels remain high, signals the exhaustion phase of a risk appetite regime and precedes the "Minsky moment" by 6-18 months.

6. **Global risk appetite regimes are now structurally coupled through dollar-denominated debt networks, meaning that U.S. monetary tightening or dollar strength acts as a global risk appetite constraint regardless of local conditions.** The $13+ trillion in offshore dollar-denominated debt creates a feedback loop where dollar appreciation tightens financial conditions for non-U.S. borrowers, forcing deleveraging that further strengthens the dollar — a destabilizing dynamic that mainstream portfolio theory entirely ignores.

7. **The standard risk-on/risk-off binary used by market practitioners is a dangerously simplistic reduction of what are actually at least four distinct regimes: (a) credit-expansion-driven risk-on, (b) fiscal-transfer-sustained risk-on, (c) orderly deleveraging risk-off, and (d) disorderly Minsky moment risk-off.** Policy responses appropriate for one regime are catastrophically wrong for another.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Endogeneity of Risk Appetite:**
Minsky's core insight (1986, *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*) is that prolonged periods of economic stability change the financial structure itself. Banks extend lending criteria, borrowers take on more leverage, and margins of safety erode — all rationally, given recent experience. This is not "animal spirits" in some vague Keynesian sense; it is a structural transformation of balance sheets. The evidence is overwhelming: every major financial crisis since the 1960s was preceded by a period of declining risk perception coinciding with rising actual financial fragility. The 2004-2007 period is the canonical example — VIX was at historic lows while household debt-to-income ratios and bank leverage ratios were at historic highs. Risk appetite was high *because* leverage was high and validating itself through asset price inflation, not despite it.

**Claim 2 — Current Regime Assessment:**
The sectoral balances as of late 2025/early 2026 show a striking pattern. The U.S. federal deficit running at roughly 6-7% of GDP is, by the sectoral balances identity (Government Deficit = Private Surplus + Current Account Deficit), providing substantial income flows to the private sector. With the current account deficit around 3-3.5% of GDP, the domestic private sector is receiving a net financial surplus of approximately 3-4% of GDP from the government sector. This is sustaining corporate profits (per the Kalecki-Levy profit equation: Profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports - Household Saving + Dividends) even as underlying productive investment growth remains moderate relative to financial asset accumulation. The danger is that market participants attribute their returns to skill and fundamental value rather than to the fiscal arithmetic — creating moral hazard and the basis for the next leverage ratchet.

**Claim 3 — Sectoral Balances as Risk Appetite Predictor:**
Godley's (1999) prescient warning about the unsustainability of the late-1990s U.S. expansion was based precisely on the observation that the private sector had swung into deficit (net borrowing) to sustain growth while the government was running surpluses. This was the signature of an unsustainable risk appetite regime. The private sector cannot run persistent deficits indefinitely — it lacks the currency-issuing capacity of the sovereign. When the private sector deficit reached approximately 5% of GDP in 2000 and again in 2006-2007, the subsequent collapses were severe. By contrast, the current situation where the private sector is in modest surplus (sustained by fiscal deficits) creates a more resilient, if still fragile, risk appetite regime.

**Claim 4 — Three Minsky Regimes:**
The hedge-speculative-Ponzi taxonomy is not merely descriptive but analytically predictive. In the hedge regime, borrowers can meet all debt obligations (principal + interest) from current cash flows. In the speculative regime, borrowers can service interest but must refinance principal — making them dependent on functioning credit markets. In the Ponzi regime, borrowers cannot even cover interest from cash flows and depend on asset appreciation. The key insight is that the *composition* of borrower types in the economy shifts endogenously over a boom. Currently, several indicators suggest growing speculative-to-Ponzi migration: (a) the share of corporate debt issued by firms with interest coverage ratios below 1x has risen, (b) private equity and leveraged buyout structures increasingly depend on multiple expansion rather than cash flow improvement, and (c) household debt service ratios, while lower than 2007 peaks, are rising in the context of elevated interest rates for new borrowers.

**Claim 5 — Credit Impulse:**
The credit impulse (change in the change of credit as a share of GDP) captures the marginal contribution of credit to demand growth. Work by Biggs, Mayer, and Pick (2010) demonstrated that it is the *flow* of new credit, not the *stock*, that drives GDP growth at the margin. This means that even if credit levels remain high, a deceleration in credit growth withdraws demand stimulus. Empirically, the credit impulse turned negative before the 2001 and 2008 recessions and before several emerging market crises. The mechanism is straightforward in Post-Keynesian terms: in an endogenous money system, the creation of new bank loans simultaneously creates deposits (purchasing power). When the rate of new loan creation slows, the flow of new purchasing power into the economy declines, undermining the income flows that were validating existing debt — a classic Minskyan feedback loop.

**Claim 6 — Dollar Denomination and Global Coupling:**
Shin and collaborators at the BIS have extensively documented the global dollar credit cycle. When risk appetite is high, dollar credit expands offshore; when it contracts, the resulting dollar demand for debt repayment strengthens the dollar, tightening conditions further. This is not a portfolio rebalancing story — it is a balance sheet story, where gross positions (not net positions as in mainstream models) matter enormously. The 2013 taper tantrum, 2015 China-dollar scare, and 2018 EM selloff all followed this pattern. The current (2026) dynamic is complicated by persistent Fed policy ambiguity and geopolitical fragmentation, but the underlying structure of dollar-denominated debt makes this coupling inescapable.

**Claim 7 — Four Risk Regimes, Not Two:**
The risk-on/risk-off (RORO) framework popularized after 2008 collapses critically different dynamics:

- **(a) Credit-expansion risk-on:** Private sector leverage rising, asset prices rising, credit spreads compressing. This is the classic Minsky upswing. Policy risk: premature tightening kills the expansion; delayed tightening builds fragility.
- **(b) Fiscal-transfer risk-on:** Government deficit spending sustains private sector incomes and cash flows, validating existing asset prices even without new private leverage. This is closer to the current 2025-2026 regime. Policy risk: fiscal consolidation removes the income floor, exposing leveraged positions.
- **(c) Orderly risk-off / deleveraging:** Private sector retrenches, paying down debt, building savings buffers. Growth slows but balance sheets repair. This is the "balance sheet recession" of Koo (2009). Appropriate policy: sustained fiscal deficits to replace withdrawn private demand.
- **(d) Disorderly risk-off / Minsky moment:** Forced asset liquidation, margin calls cascading, credit freezes. Debt-deflation dynamics per Fisher (1933). Appropriate policy: lender-of-last-resort intervention plus massive fiscal stimulus. The difference between (c) and (d) is the difference between Japan 1990-2010 (orderly, with fiscal support) and the U.S. 1929-1933 (disorderly, with fiscal austerity).

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary Policy & Central Banking:** The endogenous money perspective fundamentally challenges the mainstream transmission mechanism. Central banks do not control the money supply; they set the price of reserves, and bank lending responds to perceived creditworthiness and demand, not reserve availability. This means risk appetite regimes are shaped by, but not mechanistically controlled by, central bank policy. The rate-hiking cycle constrains but does not determine the Minsky cycle.

- **Fiscal Policy & Government Debt:** The sectoral balances identity means government fiscal positions cannot be evaluated in isolation. A government surplus *necessarily* implies a combined private sector + foreign sector deficit. Calls for fiscal consolidation during a period of fragile private balance sheets are, in this framework, calls for a Minsky moment. The current large U.S. deficit is, paradoxically, *supporting* financial stability by sustaining the income flows that validate private debt positions.

- **Asset Pricing & Market Microstructure:** Conventional asset pricing models (CAPM, Fama-French) assume exogenous risk premia. The Minsky framework implies risk premia are endogenously determined by the leverage cycle — collapsing during booms (as everyone levers up, driving spreads down) and spiking during crises. This creates fundamental non-stationarity that invalidates standard mean-variance optimization.

- **Currency & Exchange Rate Dynamics:** The dollar-denominated debt overhang creates a "dollar smile" dynamic where the dollar strengthens both in risk-on (capital inflows to U.S. assets) and severe risk-off (dollar funding scramble), with weakness only in moderate risk-off or orderly rebalancing. This is inexplicable in purchasing power parity or interest rate differential frameworks but follows directly from the balance sheet perspective.

- **Private Debt & Credit Cycles:** This is the core of the analysis. Risk appetite regimes *are* credit cycle regimes viewed from the demand side. The household debt cycle, corporate debt cycle, and financial sector leverage cycle each have their own dynamics but interact through the income-expenditure feedback loops emphasized by Kalecki and Godley.

- **Financial Regulation & Systemic Risk:** The Minsky framework implies that deregulation does not merely *allow* greater risk-taking — it *endogenously generates* it by removing the institutional constraints that slow the hedge-to-speculative-to-Ponzi migration. Post-2008 macroprudential regulation (countercyclical capital buffers, stress testing) represents a partial recognition of this, but implementation remains anchored to backward-looking models that underestimate endogenous fragility buildup.

- **Inequality & Distributional Effects:** Rising asset prices during risk-on regimes disproportionately benefit asset holders, exacerbating wealth inequality. Meanwhile, the Ponzi finance dynamics that eventually produce crises impose the greatest costs on workers and those with the least financial resilience. The risk appetite cycle is therefore also a distributional cycle, with political economy implications.

## Open Questions

1. **How much fiscal runway remains?** The U.S. government deficit is currently providing the income floor that sustains risk appetite. But with debt-to-GDP above 120% and political pressure for consolidation, how long can this continue? The MMT-informed answer is "indefinitely for a currency issuer," but political constraints are real. What triggers a premature fiscal consolidation that removes the income floor?

2. **Is the AI investment cycle a genuine techno-economic transformation or a speculative bubble?** Enormous capital expenditure is flowing into AI infrastructure. From a Minsky perspective, the key question is whether these investments will generate sufficient cash flows to service the associated debt. If they do, the investment validates itself (hedge finance). If they do not, and returns depend on continued capital inflows and rising valuations, this is speculative or Ponzi finance on an enormous scale.

3. **What is the actual state of Chinese private sector balance sheets?** China's property sector crisis represents a potential Minsky moment of global significance. The sectoral balances of the Chinese household sector (which massively leveraged into property) and the corporate sector (particularly developers) are critical data gaps. How far through the deleveraging process is China, and what is the contagion pathway to global risk appetite?

4. **How should we measure credit impulse in an era of non-bank financial intermediation?** The traditional credit impulse relies on bank lending data. But private credit funds, shadow banking structures, and crypto-adjacent lending have grown enormously. Are we systematically undermeasuring credit creation and therefore underestimating the fragility of the current risk appetite regime?

5. **Does the Minsky framework adequately account for central bank backstops?** The implicit (and increasingly explicit) central bank put — the expectation that monetary authorities will intervene to prevent systemic liquidation — may fundamentally alter the Minsky cycle by truncating the downside. This could mean longer, more extended speculative phases (as agents price in the backstop) but potentially more catastrophic eventual unwinding when the backstop proves insufficient.

6. **What role does geopolitical fragmentation play in risk appetite regime determination?** The Minsky framework was developed in the context of a single, relatively integrated financial system. The emerging bifurcation of global finance along geopolitical lines (U.S./allied vs. China/BRICS) may create partially decoupled risk appetite regimes with novel interaction dynamics that existing Post-Keynesian models have not addressed.

7. **Can we develop real-time sectoral balance indicators?** The greatest practical limitation of the Godley-Minsky approach is data latency. Flow-of-funds data arrives with substantial lags. Developing higher-frequency proxies for sectoral financial positions — perhaps using banking data, tax receipts, and trade data — would significantly enhance the framework's predictive utility.
