# Demographics — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Aging populations in advanced economies are structurally lowering the natural rate of interest (r-star) by 100-150 basis points relative to 1980s levels**, driven by higher desired saving rates among older cohorts and slower labor force growth reducing investment demand.

2. **Demographic headwinds are reducing potential output growth in the G7 by approximately 0.5-1.0 percentage points per year** relative to the 1990-2010 trend, primarily through shrinking working-age population contributions to the Solow growth decomposition.

3. **The Phillips Curve relationship is being flattened — not eliminated — by demographic shifts**, as aging workforces exhibit lower wage bargaining power and labor market transitions become less frequent, reducing the sensitivity of inflation to output gaps.

4. **Fiscal multipliers in aging societies are structurally lower under normal monetary conditions** because a larger share of government transfers flow to retirees with lower marginal propensities to consume out of transitory income, and Ricardian households represent a growing share of wealth holders.

5. **Dependency ratio inflection points (when old-age dependency exceeds ~40%) create nonlinear fiscal pressure** that constrains the fiscal policy space available for countercyclical stabilization, effectively tightening the policy mix even without explicit austerity.

6. **Immigration-driven labor supply shocks in the US (2022-2025) temporarily boosted potential output by 0.3-0.5pp**, relaxing the Phillips Curve constraint and enabling disinflation without a corresponding rise in unemployment — a demographic dividend that is now reversing under restrictive policy.

7. **China's demographic trajectory implies a structural demand deficiency regime emerging by the late 2020s**, as the working-age population decline of ~5 million per year overwhelms productivity gains and shifts the economy toward chronic excess saving — analogous to Japan post-1995.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — r-star depression.** The Laubach-Williams and Holston-Laubach-Williams models estimate r-star for the US has fallen from ~3.5% in the 1980s to roughly 0.5-1.0% pre-pandemic. Eggertsson, Mehrotra & Robbins (2019, "A Model of Secular Stagnation") formally demonstrate that demographic aging — via higher saving by those approaching retirement and reduced investment demand from slower labor force growth — accounts for 1.0-1.5pp of this decline. The life-cycle hypothesis (Modigliani) predicts a hump-shaped saving profile; as the population median age rises, aggregate desired saving increases relative to investment, depressing the equilibrium real rate. Carvalho, Ferrero & Nechio (2016, "Demographics and Real Interest Rates") corroborate this using an OLG-New Keynesian hybrid, estimating demographics alone explain ~1.5pp of the r-star decline across advanced economies. This matters enormously for monetary policy: if r-star is low, the effective lower bound binds more frequently, requiring unconventional tools and shifting stabilization burden to fiscal policy.

**Claim 2 — Potential output drag.** In the Solow-Swan framework, output growth decomposes into labor input growth, capital deepening, and TFP. Working-age population growth in the G7 has decelerated from ~0.8% annually (1990-2005) to roughly 0.0-0.2% (2020-2030), with Japan and Germany already negative. Holding capital-output ratios and TFP trends constant, this mechanically reduces potential growth. The CBO's long-run projections for US potential GDP growth have been revised down from 2.5% (2005 vintage) to ~1.8% (2024 vintage), with labor force demographics accounting for approximately half the revision. The European Commission's Ageing Report (2024) projects EU potential growth falling to 1.1% by 2040, down from 1.5% in 2023, with demographics the primary driver.

**Claim 3 — Phillips Curve flattening.** Blanchard (2016) and others have documented a progressive flattening of the Phillips Curve slope in advanced economies since the 1990s. While anchored expectations are the primary explanation, demographic composition matters at the margin. Aaronson & Sullivan (2023, Fed working paper) show that older workers exhibit lower job-to-job transition rates and weaker wage Phillips Curve sensitivity. As the labor force ages, the aggregate wage-inflation nexus weakens. Importantly, this is a flattening, not a disappearance — the curve still exists but requires larger output gaps to generate equivalent inflationary pressure, complicating monetary policy calibration.

**Claim 4 — Fiscal multiplier attenuation.** Standard New Keynesian models (Christiano, Eichenbaum & Rebelo, 2011) show fiscal multipliers are state-dependent — large at the ZLB, smaller when monetary policy is active. Demographics add a structural dimension: Basso & Rachedi (2021, ECB Working Paper) show that in aging economies, a larger share of fiscal spending takes the form of pensions and healthcare transfers to older cohorts whose MPC out of transitory income is lower (estimated 0.2-0.3 vs. 0.5-0.7 for working-age liquidity-constrained households). The Ricardian share of the population — wealthier, older households who smooth consumption intertemporally — grows, further dampening multipliers outside recession conditions.

**Claim 5 — Fiscal space nonlinearity.** When old-age dependency ratios cross ~40% (Japan crossed this ~2015, Germany and Italy approaching), mandatory spending on pensions and healthcare begins to crowd out discretionary fiscal capacity. The IMF's Fiscal Monitor (2024) estimates that age-related spending will rise by 3-5% of GDP in most advanced economies by 2050 under current policies. This creates a structural fiscal tightening bias: governments face rising automatic spending commitments that limit their ability to deploy countercyclical fiscal stimulus, effectively reducing the policy toolkit precisely when the ZLB constraint makes fiscal policy most needed.

**Claim 6 — US immigration labor supply shock.** CBO estimates net immigration to the US surged to ~3.3 million in 2023, well above the pre-pandemic trend of ~1.0 million. In a standard New Keynesian framework, a positive labor supply shock shifts the short-run aggregate supply curve outward, allowing higher output without proportionate inflationary pressure. This is consistent with the 2023-2024 experience where unemployment remained below 4% while inflation fell from 6.5% to ~2.5% — the "immaculate disinflation" was partly a demographics story. Peri (2024) estimates the immigration surge added 0.3-0.5pp to potential GDP growth. Current restrictive immigration policies are reversing this, implying a negative supply shock that will either raise inflationary pressure or constrain output growth going forward.

**Claim 7 — China's demographic demand deficiency.** China's working-age population (15-64) peaked around 2015 and is declining by ~5 million annually, accelerating to ~8 million by 2030 (UN World Population Prospects, 2024 revision). In the Solow model, this mechanically lowers potential growth. More critically, from a Keynesian demand perspective, a shrinking labor force combined with high household saving rates (~30% of disposable income) and falling property values creates a chronic excess-saving condition. This mirrors Japan's experience post-1995: the IS curve shifts left persistently, the natural rate falls below zero, and conventional monetary policy becomes ineffective. China's current deflationary pressures (CPI near 0%, PPI negative for extended periods in 2023-2024) are consistent with this diagnosis.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence (1-10) | Justification |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| 1. r-star depression | **8/10** | Strong theoretical foundation (OLG + NK models), multiple independent empirical estimates converge, but r-star is inherently unobservable and other factors (global saving glut, safe asset shortage) contribute. |
| 2. Potential output drag | **9/10** | Near-mechanical accounting identity in Solow decomposition; demographic projections 10-15 years out are highly reliable (these people are already born). Uncertainty is in TFP offsets. |
| 3. Phillips Curve flattening via demographics | **5/10** | Plausible mechanism with some empirical support, but hard to disentangle from expectation anchoring, globalization, and measurement issues. Demographics are likely a secondary factor. |
| 4. Lower fiscal multipliers | **6/10** | Theoretically sound but empirically difficult to isolate. State-dependence of multipliers is well-established; the demographic channel is an incremental addition with limited direct empirical testing. |
| 5. Fiscal space nonlinearity | **7/10** | Spending projections are well-documented; the political economy constraint is real. But governments can reform entitlements, raise taxes, or monetize — the binding nature of the constraint depends on political will. |
| 6. US immigration supply shock | **8/10** | CBO data is solid, the macro implications follow standard NK logic, and the timing aligns well with the disinflation episode. Magnitude estimates have some uncertainty. |
| 7. China demographic demand deficiency | **7/10** | Demographic data is unambiguous; the Japan parallel is instructive but not deterministic. China could offset via productivity gains, policy reform, or capital reallocation — but current policy trajectory makes the base case likely. |

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary Policy & Interest Rates:** Demographics are the single most important structural determinant of r-star, which in turn determines how much conventional monetary policy space exists. Any analysis of central bank reaction functions (Taylor Rule) must account for the demographic-driven decline in neutral rates.

- **Fiscal Policy & Government Debt:** The sustainability of sovereign debt depends critically on the growth-rate-minus-interest-rate differential (g - r). Demographics push down both g (lower potential growth) and r (lower neutral rate), with the net effect ambiguous but likely negative for debt dynamics in most advanced economies as dependency ratios rise.

- **Trade & Globalization:** Aging societies tend toward current account surpluses (lifecycle saving exceeds domestic investment), creating persistent global imbalance pressures. Germany and Japan are canonical examples. China's demographic shift implies it will transition from investment-driven trade surpluses to saving-driven ones — a different composition with different macro implications.

- **Labor Markets & Inequality:** Demographic tightening of labor supply creates wage pressure at the bottom of the distribution (immigration-sensitive sectors), potentially reducing inequality. But aging also concentrates wealth among older cohorts, widening wealth inequality. The net effect on the NAIRU is ambiguous.

- **Asset Prices & Financial Stability:** The "asset meltdown" hypothesis (Poterba, 2001) suggests aging populations eventually sell accumulated assets, depressing returns. More nuanced models (Abel, 2003) show this depends on bequest motives and international capital flows. Lower r-star supports higher equity valuations (lower discount rates) but implies lower expected returns going forward.

- **Technology & Productivity:** Demographics create both a problem (lower labor input growth) and an incentive (automation to offset labor scarcity). Acemoglu's directed technical change framework suggests labor scarcity can accelerate labor-augmenting innovation, partially offsetting the Solow drag — but the empirical magnitude is uncertain.

## Open Questions

1. **Will AI-driven productivity gains offset demographic headwinds to potential growth?** If TFP growth accelerates from ~0.5% to ~1.5% (a historically unusual but not unprecedented shift), it could neutralize the demographic drag on potential output. Current evidence is insufficient to assign high probability to this scenario.

2. **How does demographic aging interact with the transmission mechanism of monetary policy?** Older households are more sensitive to interest income (saving channel) and less sensitive to borrowing costs (mortgage channel). Does this alter the IS curve slope and hence the effectiveness of rate changes? Auclert (2019) provides a framework but empirical calibration is limited.

3. **What is the equilibrium immigration policy response in advanced economies?** The economic case for immigration as a demographic offset is strong, but political economy constraints are binding. Will labor scarcity eventually override nativist politics, or will aging electorates (who bear fewer costs from labor scarcity) entrench restrictive policies?

4. **Is China's demographic trajectory priced into global asset markets?** If China transitions to a Japan-style secular stagnation, the implications for commodity demand, global interest rates, and emerging market growth are profound — but may be unfolding too slowly for markets to price efficiently.

5. **How should the Taylor Rule be modified for demographic regimes?** Standard Taylor Rule specifications assume a stable r-star. If demographics are driving a persistent decline, should central banks adopt explicitly time-varying neutral rate estimates, and how does parameter uncertainty affect optimal policy? The literature (Orphanides & Williams, 2007) suggests robust rules perform well, but hasn't fully integrated demographic dynamics.

6. **What is the true NAIRU in a demographically shifting labor market?** If older workers have different separation and finding rates, the aggregate NAIRU is a moving target. Misestimation of NAIRU in either direction leads to policy errors — too tight (sacrificing output unnecessarily) or too loose (allowing inflation to build).
