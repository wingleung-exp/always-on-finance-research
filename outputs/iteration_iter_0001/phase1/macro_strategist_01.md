# Risk Appetite Regime Shifts & Contagion — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are fundamentally driven by the position of the economy relative to the output gap and the stance of monetary policy relative to the Taylor Rule prescription.** Risk-on environments predominate when policy rates sit below the Taylor-implied rate (accommodative stance) and the output gap is closing or positive; risk-off regimes emerge when policy tightens above neutral or when negative demand shocks widen the output gap.

2. **The "risk-on/risk-off" (RORO) binary is best understood as a reduced-form expression of the covariance structure between risky assets and safe assets, which itself is endogenous to the macro regime.** In New Keynesian models with financial frictions (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist 1999 financial accelerator), aggregate demand shocks produce positive cross-asset correlations (stocks and credit move together, inversely to Treasuries), while aggregate supply shocks produce ambiguous correlations that break the clean RORO pattern.

3. **Contagion velocity has increased structurally due to the interaction of three factors: (a) the compression of information lags via algorithmic trading, (b) the procyclicality of Value-at-Risk-based risk management, and (c) the reduced dealer balance sheet capacity for market-making since the post-GFC regulatory regime.** However, the *fundamental* propagation mechanism remains the financial accelerator channel — asset price declines erode net worth, tighten borrowing constraints, and reduce investment spending, amplifying the initial shock.

4. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades represent a coordination failure that New Keynesian models capture through strategic complementarities in liquidity provision.** When individual market makers withdraw simultaneously — each rationally responding to increased adverse selection risk — the resulting illiquidity is a Nash equilibrium that is Pareto-dominated. This is formally analogous to the bank-run coordination problem (Diamond-Dybvig 1983) extended to securities markets.

5. **The natural rate of interest (r-star) serves as the critical threshold variable separating risk appetite regimes.** When the policy rate is below r-star, the intertemporal substitution channel encourages portfolio rebalancing toward risk assets (risk-on). When the policy rate exceeds r-star, the reverse occurs. The difficulty of observing r-star in real time (Laubach-Williams 2003, Holston-Laubach-Williams 2017) means that regime transitions are often identified only with a lag, creating overshooting dynamics.

6. **Risk appetite regime shifts exhibit asymmetric dynamics: transitions from risk-on to risk-off occur roughly 3-5 times faster than the reverse.** This asymmetry is consistent with the loss aversion and uncertainty aversion in the NK model with heterogeneous agents (Kaplan-Moll-Violante 2018 HANK framework), where wealth-poor agents face binding constraints in downturns, amplifying aggregate demand contractions.

7. **Forward guidance and quantitative easing (QE) have altered the transmission mechanism of risk appetite regimes by compressing term premia and directly targeting the portfolio rebalancing channel.** This means that the *withdrawal* of these unconventional tools can itself trigger regime shifts — as demonstrated by the 2013 "taper tantrum" and the 2022 quantitative tightening cycle — independent of changes in the short-term policy rate.

8. **Cross-country contagion in risk appetite operates primarily through the Mundell-Fleming financial channel: capital flow reversals from emerging markets during global risk-off episodes reflect the unwinding of carry trades that were themselves a product of interest rate differentials under the uncovered interest parity (UIP) framework.** The well-documented failure of UIP (the "forward premium puzzle") means carry trade positions accumulate excessively during risk-on regimes, making the subsequent unwind more violent.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Output gap and Taylor Rule as regime determinants:**
The empirical correlation between accommodative monetary policy and risk asset performance is well-documented. Adrian and Shin (2010) demonstrated that the Federal Reserve's policy rate relative to the Taylor Rule benchmark explains a substantial share of the variation in broker-dealer leverage, which proxies for financial sector risk appetite. When the fed funds rate sits 100-200 basis points below the Taylor-prescribed rate, financial intermediaries expand balance sheets aggressively, increasing the supply of credit and compressing risk premia. The IS curve in the NK model makes this explicit: output above potential requires real rates below the natural rate, and the associated wealth effects and income effects drive risk-seeking behavior. Conversely, when the Fed tightens above the Taylor Rule (as in 2006-2007 and arguably in 2023), the IS curve predicts output contraction, which materializes as widening credit spreads and equity drawdowns — the hallmarks of risk-off.

**Claim 2 — RORO as covariance structure endogenous to shock type:**
The key distinction in the New Keynesian framework is between demand shocks (which move output and inflation in the same direction) and supply shocks (which move them in opposite directions). Under a demand shock — e.g., a fall in consumer confidence — the NK Phillips Curve predicts lower inflation and lower output simultaneously. The optimal monetary policy response (Taylor Rule) is to cut rates, which supports both bonds and equities, producing the canonical risk-on/risk-off covariance structure. Under a supply shock — e.g., an oil price spike — inflation rises while output falls (stagflation). The Taylor Rule gives conflicting signals (raise rates for inflation, cut for output), and the resulting cross-asset correlations are ambiguous. This is why periods dominated by supply shocks (1970s, 2022) exhibit breakdown of traditional risk-on/risk-off correlations: the stock-bond correlation turns positive, as both assets sell off simultaneously. Empirically, Baele, Bekaert, and Inghelbrecht (2010) confirmed that the stock-bond correlation is regime-dependent and driven by the nature of the prevailing macroeconomic shock.

**Claim 3 — Contagion velocity and structural factors:**
The financial accelerator (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist 1999) provides the micro-founded propagation mechanism. Asset prices serve as collateral; when they fall, firms' external finance premium rises, reducing investment and output, which further depresses asset prices. This feedback loop is the fundamental contagion channel. The structural acceleration comes from three overlapping developments: (i) high-frequency trading reduces the time for price discovery from minutes to microseconds, meaning margin calls and stop-losses trigger faster; (ii) VaR-based risk management creates procyclicality — as volatility rises, VaR limits force portfolio deleveraging, which increases volatility further (Danielsson, Shin, and Zigrand 2004); (iii) the Volcker Rule and Basel III leverage ratio constraints reduced dealer inventories, meaning less capacity to absorb selling pressure during stress episodes (Duffie 2012). The net effect is that the financial accelerator loop runs faster, but the *amplitude* of the adjustment is still governed by the same balance sheet dynamics.

**Claim 4 — Liquidity cascades as coordination failure:**
In the NK framework with heterogeneous agents, market liquidity is a public good subject to strategic complementarities. Brunnermeier and Pedersen (2009) formalized the "liquidity spiral" in which market liquidity (the ease of trading) and funding liquidity (the ease of borrowing) are mutually reinforcing. A negative shock to either triggers a cascade: margin increases force deleveraging, which reduces market liquidity, which increases margins further. This is a coordination failure because each individual agent's decision to withdraw liquidity is privately optimal but collectively destructive. The Diamond-Dybvig (1983) analogy is precise: just as bank depositors run because they expect other depositors to run, market makers withdraw because they expect other market makers to withdraw. The NK model captures this through the financial friction of collateral constraints that bind endogenously.

**Claim 5 — r-star as the regime threshold:**
Laubach and Williams (2003, updated through the NY Fed's HLW estimates) estimate r-star as the real interest rate consistent with output at potential and stable inflation. When the actual real rate (federal funds rate minus expected inflation) is below r-star, monetary policy is accommodative, stimulating credit creation and risk-taking through the portfolio rebalancing channel. When above r-star, policy is restrictive. The challenge is that r-star is unobservable and estimated with substantial uncertainty — the 95% confidence interval spans roughly 300 basis points. This estimation uncertainty means market participants can disagree about whether policy is accommodative or restrictive, leading to delayed regime recognition and overshooting. The 2021-2022 episode illustrates this: with r-star estimates near 0.5% and the real fed funds rate deeply negative (approximately -6% at the peak), the risk-on regime was sustained well past the point where inflation signaled it should have ended, precisely because the magnitude of the gap was underestimated in real time.

**Claim 6 — Asymmetric transition speeds:**
The asymmetry between risk-on and risk-off transitions is one of the most robust empirical findings in financial economics. The VIX, as a proxy for aggregate risk appetite, exhibits sharp spikes (risk-off) and gradual mean-reversion (risk-on). In the HANK framework (Kaplan, Moll, and Violante 2018), this asymmetry arises from the distribution of marginal propensities to consume (MPCs): wealth-constrained ("hand-to-mouth") agents have high MPCs and are disproportionately affected by negative income shocks, amplifying demand contractions. In contrast, during recoveries, wealth accumulation is gradual, and precautionary savings motives remain elevated even as fundamentals improve (the "scarring" effect documented by Malmendier and Nagel 2011). Quantitatively, equity bear markets develop 2-4 times faster than bull markets of comparable magnitude — the median S&P 500 bear market lasts approximately 12 months, while the median bull market of similar return magnitude lasts 3-5 years.

**Claim 7 — QE and forward guidance as regime modifiers:**
Unconventional monetary policy tools operate primarily through the term premium channel and the portfolio rebalancing channel (Vayanos and Vila 2021). QE purchases of long-duration safe assets compress term premia, effectively forcing investors into riskier assets. This is distinct from the conventional rate channel and creates a direct link between central bank balance sheet policy and risk appetite. The "taper tantrum" of May-June 2013 demonstrated the regime fragility this creates: a mere suggestion of reduced QE pace produced a 100+ basis point spike in 10-year Treasury yields and a violent EM selloff, without any change in the policy rate. The 2022 QT cycle similarly triggered a repricing of risk premia across asset classes. The NK model with QE (Gertler and Karadi 2011, 2013) formalizes this through the central bank acting as a financial intermediary: when it withdraws from this role, private intermediary balance sheet constraints become binding, and the risk-on regime unravels.

**Claim 8 — Cross-country contagion and Mundell-Fleming:**
The Mundell-Fleming framework predicts that under flexible exchange rates with capital mobility, monetary policy in the center country (the US) transmits globally through capital flows and exchange rates. Rey (2015) argued for a "global financial cycle" driven by US monetary policy that reduces the trilemma to a dilemma: capital flow management or financial instability. During risk-on regimes, low US rates incentivize carry trades into higher-yielding EM currencies, compressing EM risk premia and appreciating EM currencies (the "search for yield" documented by Rajan 2005). During risk-off, these flows reverse violently. The UIP failure (the "forward premium puzzle" — Fama 1984) means that carry trade positions accumulate beyond what rational expectations models would predict, because expected excess returns are positive on average. This over-accumulation during risk-on makes the risk-off unwind disproportionately severe, explaining why EM crises cluster during US tightening cycles (Calvo, Leiderman, and Reinhart 1996 on capital flow bonanzas and their reversals).

## Connections to Other Topics

**Volatility Regimes:** Risk appetite regimes and volatility regimes are two sides of the same coin. In the NK framework, volatility is endogenous to the macro regime — it rises when the output gap widens or when policy uncertainty increases. The VIX term structure (contango vs. backwardation) serves as a proxy for the risk appetite regime. Sustained contango = risk-on; backwardation = risk-off. The connection is mediated by the financial accelerator: higher volatility tightens borrowing constraints, reducing risk appetite, which reduces asset prices, increasing volatility further.

**Credit Cycles:** Risk appetite is the demand-side driver of the credit cycle. When risk appetite is elevated (risk-on), credit spreads compress, lending standards loosen, and credit growth accelerates — the boom phase of the Minsky cycle. The NK framework with financial frictions (Christiano-Motto-Rostagno 2014) models this as the external finance premium declining during expansions due to rising collateral values. The connection is that risk appetite regime shifts *cause* inflection points in the credit cycle, not the reverse — credit conditions are a lagging reflection of risk appetite, though credit deterioration can then feed back to further reduce risk appetite.

**Equity Cycles:** Equity valuations embed risk appetite through the equity risk premium (ERP). In risk-on regimes, the ERP compresses (higher P/E multiples for given earnings); in risk-off, it expands. The Campbell-Shiller decomposition separates equity returns into cash flow news and discount rate news — risk appetite regime shifts operate primarily through the discount rate channel. The connection to the NK model is through the consumption Euler equation: when expected consumption growth is high (output gap closing), the stochastic discount factor declines, compressing the ERP.

**FX Regimes:** Risk appetite regimes determine the direction of cross-border capital flows and hence exchange rate dynamics. The "dollar smile" framework (where the USD strengthens in both deep risk-off and robust US growth, but weakens in moderate risk-on when carry flows favor EM) is a reduced-form expression of Mundell-Fleming capital flow dynamics under varying risk appetite states. EM currency crises cluster during risk-off regime shifts because of the carry trade unwind mechanism described in Claim 8.

**Monetary Policy Transmission:** Risk appetite is itself the key transmission variable for monetary policy in NK models with financial frictions. The central bank's ability to influence the real economy depends on its ability to alter risk appetite — through the interest rate channel (conventional), the portfolio rebalancing channel (QE), or the expectations channel (forward guidance). When financial frictions are severe (zero lower bound, impaired intermediation), the transmission from policy to risk appetite breaks down, creating the "liquidity trap" of the IS-LM framework.

## Open Questions

1. **Is there a reliable real-time indicator of risk appetite regime transitions?** The standard candidates (VIX, credit spreads, the TED spread, financial conditions indices) all have significant false-positive rates. A Markov-switching model combining multiple indicators could improve identification, but optimal specification is unresolved.

2. **Has the post-GFC regulatory regime permanently altered the dynamics of risk appetite regime shifts?** If dealer balance sheets are permanently constrained, the liquidity provision function in risk-off regimes may be structurally impaired, leading to more frequent and more severe flash episodes. Alternatively, central bank backstops (Fed standing repo facility, discount window stigma reduction) may have replaced dealer capacity, creating moral hazard that extends risk-on regimes.

3. **How should the NK framework incorporate the rise of passive/index investing and the growth of private credit markets in its account of risk appetite transmission?** Passive flows are mechanically insensitive to risk appetite (they follow fund flows, not valuations), potentially dampening regime transitions in equity markets but concentrating liquidity risk in index rebalancing events. Private credit markets lack secondary market liquidity entirely, meaning risk appetite shifts may produce latent losses that materialize only at refinancing.

4. **What is the interaction between fiscal policy stance and risk appetite regimes?** The NK model's treatment of fiscal policy through Ricardian equivalence provides limited guidance. In practice, large fiscal deficits during risk-on regimes (as in the US 2023-2025) may be supporting risk appetite by maintaining aggregate demand, but at the cost of crowding out that becomes binding when the regime shifts. The fiscal multiplier is regime-dependent (Auerbach and Gorodnichenko 2012), but the reverse channel — from risk appetite to fiscal sustainability — is undertheorized.

5. **Can central banks engineer "soft landings" from risk-on regimes, or is the asymmetric transition speed (Claim 6) a structural feature that makes gradual regime transitions inherently unstable?** The 1994-1995 tightening cycle is the canonical soft landing example, but it occurred with much lower financial leverage and without the modern microstructure amplification mechanisms. The 2022-2023 cycle may represent another example, but EM stress was substantial and the lagged effects of QT may not yet be fully transmitted.

6. **What is the role of fiscal dominance in shaping future risk appetite regimes?** If sovereign debt levels constrain central bank policy rates (the fiscal theory of the price level — Cochrane 2023), then the traditional Taylor Rule framework may become inadequate, and risk appetite regimes may become driven by fiscal sustainability assessments rather than monetary policy stance. This would represent a genuine structural break in the regime identification framework.
