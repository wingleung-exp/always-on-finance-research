# Risk Appetite Regime Shifts & Contagion — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The yield curve is the primary encoding mechanism for risk appetite regimes, and regime shifts are most reliably identified through term premium decomposition rather than through raw yield levels or slope alone.** When risk appetite collapses, the curve steepens or flattens depending on whether the driver is flight-to-quality (bull flattening) or fiscal/inflation fear (bear steepening), and only term premium decomposition reveals which regime is operative.

2. **Risk-on/risk-off (RORO) regimes can be classified into four distinct states using the yield curve: (a) risk-on with rising term premium (reflationary), (b) risk-on with compressed term premium (QE-suppressed), (c) risk-off with collapsing term premium (flight-to-quality), and (d) risk-off with rising term premium (fiscal crisis / sovereign stress).** These four states have radically different implications for cross-asset allocation.

3. **The 2s10s slope is a noisy but structurally important regime indicator: sustained inversion (negative 2s10s) signals late-cycle risk appetite that historically precedes regime breaks by 6-18 months, while rapid re-steepening after inversion marks the actual transition from risk-on to risk-off as the economy deteriorates.**

4. **The 5y5y forward rate acts as an anchor for long-run risk appetite: when 5y5y forwards break above or below their 12-month range, it signals a shift in the structural regime — not a cyclical fluctuation — because it reflects repricing of the neutral rate (r*) or long-run inflation expectations.**

5. **Contagion velocity in modern markets is amplified by the basis trade (leveraged Treasury cash-futures arbitrage by hedge funds), which creates a feedback loop: risk-off events that widen swap spreads and repo rates force basis trade unwinds, which increase Treasury supply to the market, widening term premium and accelerating the sell-off in a pro-cyclical cascade.**

6. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades originate in the repo market and propagate through the yield curve before reaching equities and credit.** The sequence is: repo rate spikes → dealer balance sheet constraints → reduced Treasury market-making capacity → bid-ask widening in off-the-run Treasuries → term premium spike → credit spread widening → equity vol spike. The yield curve is thus a leading indicator of broader risk-off contagion, not a lagging one.

7. **Central bank balance sheet policy (QE/QT) is the single most powerful determinant of the term premium component of risk appetite regimes since 2008.** QE compressed term premium by 100-150bp at the 10y point (estimates from ACM and Kim-Wright models), artificially sustaining risk-on regimes. QT reversal adds roughly 3-5bp of term premium per $100bn of balance sheet runoff, creating a structural headwind to risk appetite that operates independently of the policy rate.

8. **Foreign official demand for Treasuries functions as a regime stabilizer whose withdrawal amplifies risk-off dynamics.** The decline in foreign official holdings from ~40% of outstanding marketable Treasuries in 2014 to ~25% by 2025 has structurally increased the sensitivity of term premium to risk appetite shifts, because marginal demand now comes from price-sensitive hedge funds and mutual funds rather than price-insensitive central bank reserve managers.

9. **Treasury auction performance (measured by tail, bid-to-cover, and dealer takedown) provides a real-time signal of risk appetite regime transitions.** Repeated auction tails (pricing above the when-issued yield) cluster at regime turning points and serve as a coincident indicator of risk appetite deterioration that precedes moves in equities and credit.

10. **The current (2025-2026) environment represents a historically unusual regime: risk-on in equities coexisting with elevated and rising term premium, driven by the combination of large fiscal deficits, Treasury supply concerns, QT, and reduced foreign demand.** This divergence between equity risk appetite and rates risk appetite is unstable and historically resolves with either a correction in equities or a policy intervention that compresses term premium.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Term Premium Decomposition as Regime Identifier
Raw yield curve movements are ambiguous. A 50bp decline in the 10y yield could reflect: (a) lower expected short rates (dovish Fed repricing — risk-off), (b) lower term premium (flight-to-quality — risk-off), or (c) lower inflation expectations (deflationary — risk-off). Conversely, a 50bp rise could be (a) higher expected rates (hawkish — risk appetite unclear), (b) higher term premium (fiscal supply — risk-off), or (c) higher growth expectations (risk-on). The Adrian-Crump-Moench (ACM) model estimated by the NY Fed decomposes the 10y yield into these components. During the March 2020 COVID crash, the 10y yield fell 150bp, of which ACM attributed roughly 70bp to term premium collapse (flight-to-quality) and 80bp to rate expectations. During the 2022 bear market, the 10y rose ~250bp, with ACM attributing ~120bp to term premium expansion (fiscal/supply-driven) and ~130bp to rate expectations (Fed hiking cycle). These decompositions tell you fundamentally different stories about risk appetite than raw yield movements.

### Claim 2: Four-State RORO Framework
- **Risk-on + rising term premium (Reflationary)**: 2016-2017 post-election, 2021 H1. Curve steepens, equities rally, credit tightens, commodities rise. Term premium rises because growth expectations lift duration risk, but the mood is constructive. Typically early-to-mid cycle.
- **Risk-on + compressed term premium (QE-suppressed)**: 2013-2015, 2020 H2. Curve flat despite risk-on, because central bank purchases absorb duration supply. This is the "everything rally" regime where both bonds and equities perform. Unstable — depends on continued CB intervention.
- **Risk-off + collapsing term premium (Flight-to-quality)**: March 2020, August 2011, 2008 Q4. Curve bull-flattens aggressively, 10y term premium goes negative, equities sell off, credit widens, USD strengthens. Classic safe-haven flow.
- **Risk-off + rising term premium (Fiscal crisis)**: UK gilt crisis September 2022, Euro sovereign crisis 2011-2012. Bonds and equities sell off simultaneously. This is the most dangerous regime because the traditional portfolio hedge (long bonds) fails. Term premium spikes due to loss of sovereign credibility, forced selling, or LDI-style margin cascades.

### Claim 3: 2s10s as Regime Transition Indicator
The 2s10s curve has inverted before every US recession since 1970 (7 for 7, with one false positive in 1998 that was quickly reversed). The median lead time from first inversion to recession onset is 14 months. But the inversion itself is not the risk-off trigger — it is the *re-steepening* after inversion that coincides with the actual regime break. The steepening occurs when the Fed pivots to cutting (front-end rallies) or when recession fears cause long-end term premium to spike, or both. The 2s10s inverted in July 2022 and remained inverted for a historically long period (~26 months). The subsequent re-steepening through 2024-2025 accompanied by equity volatility and credit stress fits the historical pattern.

### Claim 4: 5y5y Forward as Structural Regime Signal
The 5y5y forward rate strips out the cyclical policy path (first 5 years) to reveal the market's view on the "destination" rate. When 5y5y forwards repriced from ~2.5% to ~4.0% between 2021 and 2023, this signaled a structural regime shift: the market was pricing a permanently higher neutral rate (r*), not just a cyclical hiking episode. This repricing affected risk appetite by raising the discount rate applied to all long-duration assets. The 5y5y forward has remained elevated at 3.8-4.2% through 2025, confirming that the structural regime shift is persistent rather than cyclical.

### Claim 5: Basis Trade as Contagion Accelerator
The basis trade (short Treasury futures, long cash Treasuries, financed in repo) has grown to an estimated $800bn-$1tn in notional as of late 2024, according to OFR and Fed staff estimates. This trade is typically leveraged 50-100x. When risk-off events cause: (a) futures margin calls from price moves, (b) repo haircut increases from dealer risk reduction, or (c) swap spread widening that undermines the hedge, levered players must unwind rapidly. The March 2020 Treasury market dislocation was substantially driven by basis trade unwinds, which caused paradoxical Treasury selling during a risk-off event. The feedback loop is: initial risk-off shock → repo stress → basis trade unwind → Treasury selling → term premium spike → further risk-off across assets. This is a structural fragility embedded in the plumbing of the Treasury market.

### Claim 6: Repo-Originating Liquidity Cascades
The September 2019 repo spike (overnight repo rates hit 10% briefly) demonstrated that liquidity stress in the repo market precedes and causes stress in other markets. The transmission channel: (a) banks have finite balance sheet capacity for repo, (b) when reserves become scarce relative to the LCR buffer, banks withdraw from repo, (c) hedge funds and dealers that finance Treasury positions in repo face higher costs, (d) they reduce positions, (e) reduced market-making increases Treasury bid-ask spreads and price volatility, (f) this volatility feeds into option-implied volatility (MOVE index), (g) which triggers risk limits and VaR reduction across all rates-linked positions, (h) cascading to credit and equities. The Fed's Standing Repo Facility (SRF), established in 2021, was designed specifically to interrupt this cascade, but its effectiveness in a genuine stress scenario remains untested.

### Claim 7: QE/QT as Term Premium Driver
Studies from the Fed, ECB, and BOE staff estimate that QE1 through QE3 collectively reduced the 10y term premium by 100-150bp (Gagnon et al. 2011, Li & Wei 2013, Bonis et al. 2017). The mechanism is portfolio balance: by removing duration from the market, the central bank reduces the price of duration risk. QT operates in reverse. The Fed's balance sheet peaked at ~$8.9tn in mid-2022 and has been reduced to ~$6.8tn by early 2026 (~$2.1tn of runoff). At the estimated 3-5bp per $100bn, this implies 60-100bp of term premium added back since QT began. This is broadly consistent with ACM estimates showing 10y term premium rising from roughly 0bp in 2022 to 50-80bp by late 2025. The key insight: this term premium increase is a structural shift in the risk appetite backdrop that operates independently of the Fed funds rate and cannot be reversed by rate cuts alone.

### Claim 8: Foreign Demand Withdrawal
Foreign official holdings of Treasuries have declined from $4.1tn (40% of outstanding) in 2014 to approximately $3.6tn (25% of a much larger outstanding stock) by 2025. China's holdings alone fell from $1.3tn to approximately $700bn. Meanwhile, outstanding marketable Treasuries have grown from $12tn to $27tn. The marginal buyer has shifted from price-insensitive reserve managers to price-sensitive domestic buyers (hedge funds, mutual funds, insurance companies, banks). Price-sensitive buyers demand higher term premium and are more likely to sell in risk-off episodes, amplifying volatility. This structural shift means that risk appetite regime transitions now produce larger moves in term premium than they did in the 2010s.

### Claim 9: Auction Performance as Risk Appetite Barometer
Treasury auction "tails" (the difference between the auction stop-out yield and the pre-auction when-issued yield) cluster during periods of risk appetite deterioration. A tail of 1bp or more at a 10y or 30y auction is statistically unusual and signals marginal demand weakness. In Q4 2023, several auctions tailed significantly (including a 30y auction that tailed 5.3bp in November 2023), coinciding with term premium spikes and equity weakness. Conversely, "through" auctions (pricing below when-issued) signal strong demand and risk appetite. Dealer takedown above 20% signals primary dealer absorption of supply that end investors wouldn't take — a sign of weak end-user demand. Bid-to-cover ratios below the 12-month average similarly flag demand deterioration.

### Claim 10: Current Regime Divergence
As of early 2026, the S&P 500 trades near all-time highs (risk-on in equities), while 10y term premium (ACM estimate) stands at approximately 50-80bp — the highest level since 2014. This divergence has historical precedent in only a few episodes: 1999-2000 (equities rich, bonds cheap ahead of the tech crash), and briefly in 2018 (equities sold off to close the gap). The drivers of elevated term premium — fiscal deficits projected at 6-7% of GDP, $2tn+ annual Treasury issuance, ongoing QT, reduced foreign demand — are structural and unlikely to reverse quickly. Either equities must reprice to reflect higher discount rates (risk-off), or a policy shock (renewed QE, fiscal consolidation, exogenous demand) must compress term premium. The unstable equilibrium makes the current environment particularly vulnerable to risk appetite regime shifts.

## Confidence Assessment

1. **Term premium decomposition as regime identifier**: 9/10. Well-established in academic literature and practitioner frameworks. The main caveat is model dependence — ACM and Kim-Wright can disagree by 50bp+ at the 10y point.

2. **Four-state RORO framework**: 7/10. Conceptually robust and supported by historical episodes, but the boundaries between states are fuzzy and real-time identification is difficult. The "risk-off + rising term premium" state (Claim 2d) is the most novel and least empirically documented outside of the UK gilt crisis.

3. **2s10s as regime transition indicator**: 8/10. Strong empirical record but small sample size (7 recessions since 1970). The prolonged 2022-2024 inversion and the uncertain economic outcome create some uncertainty about whether the pattern holds in the current cycle.

4. **5y5y forward as structural signal**: 7/10. Theoretically sound but the 5y5y is also influenced by convexity hedging flows and pension demand, which can distort the signal. The recent repricing is directionally clear but the precise decomposition into r* vs. inflation expectations vs. technicals is uncertain.

5. **Basis trade as contagion amplifier**: 9/10. Extensively documented by the OFR, Fed staff, and market participants. March 2020 provided a clear proof-of-concept. The only uncertainty is the exact current size and leverage, which are estimated rather than precisely known.

6. **Repo-originating liquidity cascades**: 8/10. September 2019 and March 2020 provide strong evidence. The SRF introduces genuine uncertainty about whether the full cascade would play out in a future stress event.

7. **QE/QT as term premium driver**: 8/10. Strong empirical support from multiple central bank studies, but estimates of the effect size vary by 50% across methodologies. The QT reversal effect is less well-studied than QE because the QT sample is smaller and confounded by other factors.

8. **Foreign demand withdrawal**: 8/10. The data on holdings is clear. The marginal impact on term premium sensitivity is theoretically well-motivated but harder to isolate empirically because it coincides with other structural changes (higher deficits, QT, changed inflation regime).

9. **Auction performance as regime barometer**: 7/10. Useful real-time indicator but noisy — individual auctions can tail for idiosyncratic reasons (positioning, day-of-week effects, concurrent supply). The signal is clearest when multiple consecutive auctions show weakness.

10. **Current regime divergence**: 7/10. The factual description is high-confidence, but the claim that it "must resolve" is a forecast. The divergence could persist longer than expected if AI-driven productivity gains validate high equity valuations while term premium remains elevated for fiscal reasons. The prediction of instability is a probabilistic judgment, not a certainty.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Volatility Regimes (volatility_regimes)
Term premium is intimately linked to realized and implied interest rate volatility. The MOVE index (rates vol) typically leads the VIX (equity vol) at regime turning points because rates markets are the first to price duration and policy uncertainty. A rates vol regime shift (MOVE rising above 120-130) is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a broader risk-off regime transition. Volatility regime analysis and term premium decomposition are two lenses on the same underlying phenomenon.

### Credit Cycles (credit_cycles)
Credit spreads and term premium are both compensation for risk, but they respond to different drivers. In early risk-off, term premium may compress (flight-to-quality) while credit spreads widen — these move in opposite directions. In a fiscal crisis, both widen simultaneously. The stage of the credit cycle determines which of the four risk appetite states is most likely: late-cycle credit with tight spreads and high leverage is the precondition for the basis-trade-driven contagion described in Claim 5.

### Equity Cycles (equity_cycles)
The equity risk premium and the term premium together define the total compensation for risky duration. When term premium rises, the equity risk premium must also rise (equities cheapen) to maintain the spread between the two, unless earnings growth expectations offset the discount rate increase. The current divergence between equity risk appetite and rates risk appetite (Claim 10) is the central tension in the current equity cycle.

### FX Regimes (fx_regimes)
Rate differentials (driven by both policy rate expectations and term premium) are the primary driver of FX in G10. Risk-off regimes that collapse US term premium (flight-to-quality) strengthen USD via safe-haven flows and capital repatriation. Risk-off regimes that *raise* US term premium (fiscal crisis) could weaken USD — a distinction that only term premium decomposition reveals. Japan's term premium dynamics (BOJ yield curve control and its unwinding) create a global contagion channel through the yen carry trade.

### Monetary Policy (monetary_policy)
The yield curve encodes policy expectations, but term premium adds noise that can mislead policymakers. The Fed's own uncertainty about neutral rate (r*) means they partially rely on market signals, creating a feedback loop: the curve prices the Fed's reaction function, and the Fed reads the curve to calibrate policy. Risk appetite regime shifts that are term-premium-driven (rather than expectations-driven) can cause the Fed to misread the macro signal embedded in yields.

### Fiscal Policy (fiscal_policy)
Fiscal deficits directly drive Treasury supply, which is the primary determinant of term premium in the current regime. The fiscal policy stance is thus a *structural* driver of risk appetite regimes in a way that was less true before 2020, when deficits were smaller and the Fed absorbed supply via QE. Fiscal dominance — the regime in which monetary policy is constrained by fiscal sustainability considerations — would represent a permanent shift to the "risk-off + rising term premium" quadrant.

### Central Bank Balance Sheets (central_bank_balance_sheets)
QE/QT is the mechanism through which central bank balance sheet policy transmits into term premium and thus into risk appetite regimes. The transition from QE to QT represents a structural regime shift in the term premium landscape that is distinct from rate policy.

## Open Questions

1. **How precisely can we estimate term premium in real time?** The ACM and Kim-Wright models can diverge by 50bp+. If our regime identification depends on term premium estimates, model uncertainty directly limits regime identification accuracy. Is there a model-free or market-implied approach (using TIPS breakevens, swaptions, or survey-based measures) that provides a more robust signal?

2. **What is the critical mass of basis trade unwinds that would overwhelm the Fed's Standing Repo Facility?** The SRF has a $500bn capacity and is operationally untested in a genuine stress event. If the basis trade is $800bn-$1tn in notional and leveraged 50-100x, do the Fed's backstops prevent the cascade described in Claim 5, or merely delay it?

3. **Is the current equity-rates divergence (Claim 10) sustainable if driven by AI productivity gains?** If AI genuinely raises trend growth by 50-100bp, this could validate both high equity valuations and higher term premium (because growth raises the neutral rate). Would this be a new "structural risk-on" regime that is consistent with elevated term premium, breaking the historical pattern?

4. **How do we separate term premium driven by inflation uncertainty from term premium driven by fiscal supply?** Both raise term premium, but they have different implications for risk appetite: inflation uncertainty is destabilizing, while fiscal supply premium is a repricing that can stabilize at a new equilibrium. TIPS breakevens partially help but are themselves distorted by liquidity premia.

5. **What is the role of passive/systematic flows in accelerating or dampening risk appetite regime transitions?** The growth of risk-parity, CTA, and vol-targeting strategies means that volatility itself triggers mechanical selling/buying that can amplify regime transitions. How much of the contagion velocity described in Claim 6 is attributable to these systematic flows versus fundamental rebalancing?

6. **Does the BOJ's exit from yield curve control represent a global term premium shock?** Japanese investors hold approximately $1.1tn in foreign bonds. If domestic yields rise enough to attract capital home, the repatriation flow could raise term premium globally and trigger a risk-off regime shift originating from Japan — a contagion channel that operates through the term structure rather than through credit or equity markets.

7. **Can Treasury buybacks meaningfully offset the term premium impact of large issuance?** The Treasury's buyback program (restarted in 2024) is designed to improve liquidity and manage the maturity profile. If buybacks concentrate in off-the-run issues, they could reduce liquidity premia without affecting aggregate term premium. The net effect on risk appetite regimes is ambiguous.
