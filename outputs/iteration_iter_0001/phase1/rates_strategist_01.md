# Demographics — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Aging populations in developed markets are a structural downward force on the neutral real rate (r\*), compressing the expected short-rate component of long-term yields.** As dependency ratios rise and labor force growth slows, potential GDP growth declines, pulling down the equilibrium rate that balances saving and investment.

2. **The retirement of baby boomers is simultaneously a term premium suppressant (via demand for duration) and a term premium amplifier (via fiscal deterioration).** These forces partially offset, but the fiscal channel is increasingly dominant as entitlement spending accelerates.

3. **Demographic-driven fiscal expansion — Social Security, Medicare, and healthcare spending — is the primary structural driver of rising term premium at the long end of the U.S. curve, explaining much of the post-2022 steepening bias in 10y+ maturities.**

4. **Japan's demographic trajectory (20+ years ahead of the U.S./Europe) provides a template for how aging affects the yield curve: persistent flattening, compressed term premium, and eventual yield curve control as the central bank becomes the marginal buyer of last resort.** However, the U.S. is unlikely to follow the same path due to different fiscal and reserve-currency dynamics.

5. **Immigration policy is an underappreciated short-cycle swing factor for the curve.** Shifts in immigration flows alter labor supply expectations, wage growth trajectories, and therefore the Fed's expected rate path — directly moving the expectations component of 2y-5y yields.

6. **The 5y5y forward real rate embeds a demographic discount.** Cross-country variation in 5y5y forward OIS rates correlates with projected working-age population growth rates, suggesting markets partially price demographic trajectories into long-run neutral rate expectations.

7. **Pension fund and insurance company duration demand — driven mechanically by aging liabilities — creates a structural bid for 20y-30y Treasuries that suppresses the ultra-long term premium relative to the 10y sector, contributing to persistent belly cheapness in 7y-10y butterfly spreads.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — r\* compression:**
The Laubach-Williams and Holston-Laubach-Williams estimates of r\* for the U.S. declined from ~3.5% in the 1990s to ~0.5-1.0% pre-COVID, coinciding with the demographic transition as boomer saving peaked and labor force growth decelerated from ~1.5% to ~0.5% annually. Cross-country evidence reinforces: Japan (r\* near zero, oldest population), Germany (low r\*, aging rapidly), vs. India/Indonesia (higher r\*, younger demographics). The mechanism runs through both lower potential growth (fewer workers, slower productivity growth as workforce ages) and higher desired saving (life-cycle hypothesis: aging populations save more in aggregate until the dependency ratio tips decisively). The post-COVID bounce in r\* estimates to ~1.0-1.5% may partly reflect temporary fiscal stimulus and immigration-driven labor force expansion rather than a reversal of the demographic trend.

**Claim 2 — Offsetting forces on term premium:**
*Demand channel (suppresses TP):* As populations age, pension funds and insurers face growing duration liabilities. ALM-driven demand for long-duration bonds increases structurally. The UK gilt crisis of 2022 was partly a manifestation of this — LDI strategies had created enormous leveraged demand for long gilts driven by pension demographics. *Fiscal channel (boosts TP):* The CBO projects U.S. federal debt/GDP rising from ~100% to ~150%+ over the next 20 years, driven primarily by Social Security and Medicare spending as boomers retire. Net Treasury issuance must rise to fund these deficits, expanding the supply of duration the market must absorb. ACM term premium estimates for the 10y turned positive in late 2023 for the first time since 2015 and have remained elevated, coinciding with the Treasury's upward revision of borrowing estimates.

**Claim 3 — Fiscal-demographic nexus as TP driver:**
The 2023-2024 term premium repricing was catalyzed by a sequence of events with demographic fiscal roots: larger-than-expected quarterly refunding announcements, CBO long-run deficit projections revised upward on entitlement spending, and Fitch's U.S. downgrade citing "fiscal deterioration." The Kim-Wright 10y term premium rose ~100bp from mid-2023 to late-2023. The ACM model showed similar magnitude. The 10y-30y curve steepened as the market demanded more compensation for absorbing supply at the long end. This is fundamentally a demographic story: the spending driving the deficits is entitlements for an aging population.

**Claim 4 — Japan as template and anti-template:**
Japan's 10y yield was pinned near zero for a decade under YCC, with the BoJ owning >50% of JGBs — the endgame of a demographic spiral where private-sector saving demand overwhelmed issuance. The JGB curve was chronically flat with near-zero or negative term premium. But the U.S. differs critically: (a) USD reserve currency status means foreign demand is structurally higher but also more volatile; (b) U.S. fiscal deficits are running at 6%+ of GDP even at full employment, unlike Japan's which were partly offset by massive private saving; (c) U.S. immigration provides a demographic safety valve Japan lacks. The U.S. is more likely to see rising term premium than Japan-style flattening, because the fiscal response to aging is more aggressive and less savings-funded.

**Claim 5 — Immigration as curve swing factor:**
The 2022-2024 immigration surge added an estimated 3-4 million workers to the U.S. labor force, meaningfully boosting potential GDP growth estimates and contributing to the "soft landing" narrative. This supported higher expected short rates (the Fed could stay higher for longer without causing recession), steepening the front end. Conversely, immigration restrictions would reduce labor supply growth, lower potential GDP, and pull down the expected rate path — a flattener via the expectations component. The 2y yield is particularly sensitive to this channel because it directly prices Fed expectations over the policy-relevant horizon.

**Claim 6 — 5y5y forward as demographic barometer:**
The 5y5y forward OIS rate strips out near-term cyclical expectations and approximates the market's long-run neutral rate view. Cross-sectionally: U.S. 5y5y real forward ~1.5-2.0%, Eurozone ~0.5-1.0%, Japan ~near zero. These rank-order consistently with projected working-age population growth over the next decade. The correlation isn't perfect (fiscal policy, productivity assumptions also matter), but demographics explain a meaningful share of the cross-country dispersion.

**Claim 7 — Pension demand and the ultra-long bid:**
U.S. pension funds remain collectively underfunded and have been extending duration as rates rose in 2023-2024 ("derisking" by matching assets to liabilities). This creates a structural bid for 20y-30y paper. The 20y bond has chronically traded cheap relative to an interpolated 10y-30y line, partly because of lower liquidity but also because pension demand concentrates at specific duration targets. The 7y-10y sector, by contrast, is less favored by pensions and more exposed to supply dynamics (the 7y and 10y are the largest coupon issuance tenors), resulting in persistent belly cheapness in the 5s-10s-30s butterfly.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Aging compresses r\* | **8/10** | Strong cross-country and time-series evidence. Post-COVID r\* bounce introduces some uncertainty about magnitude. |
| 2. Offsetting TP forces | **8/10** | Both channels are well-documented. The net effect and timing of dominance shifts are harder to pin down. |
| 3. Fiscal-demographic TP driver | **7/10** | Direction is clear; attribution is harder because TP models disagree on levels by 50-100bp. Supply matters, but quantifying its contribution precisely is model-dependent. |
| 4. Japan as partial template | **6/10** | The comparison is illustrative but structural differences (reserve currency, immigration, fiscal stance) limit direct applicability. |
| 5. Immigration as swing factor | **7/10** | Mechanism is sound and supported by 2022-2024 episode. Magnitude is uncertain because immigration data has large lags and revisions. |
| 6. 5y5y encodes demographics | **6/10** | Correlation exists but causation is confounded by fiscal, productivity, and monetary regime differences. Sample of countries is small. |
| 7. Pension demand shapes ultra-long | **7/10** | Consistent with observed curve shape and flow data, but hard to isolate from other factors (liquidity premium, primary dealer hedging). |

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Fiscal policy / government debt:** Demographics are the *engine* of the long-run fiscal trajectory. Entitlement spending projections are demographic projections with dollar signs. Any analysis of Treasury supply and its curve impact must start with demographics.
- **Monetary policy:** Demographics feed directly into r\* estimates, which anchor the terminal rate the market prices. A lower demographic-implied r\* means the Fed's "neutral" rate is lower, compressing the entire expected rate path and flattening the curve, all else equal.
- **Inflation:** Aging populations may be disinflationary (lower demand growth, Japan precedent) or inflationary (labor scarcity, rising healthcare costs, fiscal dominance). This ambiguity is itself a source of term premium — inflation uncertainty driven by demographic uncertainty.
- **Housing / real assets:** Demographic cohort size drives housing demand cycles (millennials entering peak home-buying years), which feeds back to shelter inflation and therefore the Fed's rate path.
- **Geopolitics / deglobalization:** Aging workforces in China and Europe may accelerate reshoring/friend-shoring trends, affecting trade flows, FX reserve accumulation, and therefore foreign demand for Treasuries (a key term premium driver).
- **Technology / AI / productivity:** If AI boosts productivity growth, it could partially offset the demographic drag on potential GDP and r\*, raising the expected rate path and steepening the curve. This is the key upside risk to the "demographics compress yields" thesis.

## Open Questions

1. **How much of the post-2022 r\* increase is demographic (immigration) vs. fiscal (deficit spending) vs. structural (AI/productivity optimism)?** This decomposition matters enormously for curve positioning because the channels have different durability.

2. **At what pace will pension derisking flows materialize as funded status improves?** If pensions aggressively extend duration, the ultra-long bid could intensify, further compressing the 20y-30y term premium even as 10y TP rises — a significant relative value opportunity.

3. **What is the elasticity of term premium to projected deficit/GDP?** We observe the correlation, but the functional form matters: is there a threshold (e.g., 120% debt/GDP) where term premium rises nonlinearly?

4. **How will demographic divergence across EM and DM affect global capital flows and the U.S. curve?** Young, growing EM populations may absorb more domestic saving, reducing capital outflows to U.S. Treasuries and raising TP — but this depends on EM institutional development and capital account openness.

5. **Will the U.S. experience "Japanification" or "Italianification"?** Japan aged into deflation and curve flattening; Italy aged into fiscal stress and spread widening. The U.S. path depends on whether the demographic fiscal costs are monetized (Japan path → flat curve, low TP) or funded by market issuance (Italy path → steep curve, high TP). Current trajectory points toward the latter, but a future crisis could trigger the former.

6. **What discount does the market currently apply to long-run entitlement reform?** If markets assign meaningful probability to benefit cuts or revenue increases that reduce projected deficits, the demographic fiscal premium in term premium is lower than the CBO baseline implies. There is no clean way to extract this from prices.
