# Fiscal Policy — Top-Down Equity Strategist (equity_analyst_01) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The fiscal regime has become the dominant driver of the equity risk premium, displacing monetary policy as the primary valuation anchor.** The 2Y-SPX correlation of -0.25 to -0.35 (monetary dominance at the front end) versus the 30Y-SPX correlation of +0.05 to +0.15 (fiscal supply dominance at the long end) reveals a structural break: equity valuations are now tethered to the long end where fiscal supply, not Fed policy, sets the marginal price. The ERP — currently compressed to ~3.5-4.0% on a Fed model basis (S&P earnings yield ~5.5% minus 10Y ~4.3%) versus a 20-year average of ~4.5-5.0% — reflects term premium suppression that is itself a function of the fiscal-monetary configuration. When the blended Treasury rate (~4.5%) converges with nominal GDP growth (~5.5%), the arithmetic of equity valuation shifts from "rates are low enough to justify high multiples" to "rates are consuming a growing share of the return the economy generates."

2. **The macro regime for equities sits in a precarious "late-cycle fiscal expansion" quadrant with no historical analogue.** Running 6-7% of GDP deficits at sub-4% unemployment has a pre-2022 base rate of 0/74 annual observations (1947-2024). This places us in a growth-supported, inflation-sticky quadrant that historically favors value, cyclicals, and real assets — but with the critical caveat that the growth support is fiscal-deficit-driven rather than organic. In historical regime maps, this quadrant has been populated only during wartime mobilization, making every factor rotation signal conditional on this configuration persisting. The moment fiscal consolidation begins (DOGE or otherwise), the macro regime transitions from "fiscal expansion at full employment" to "fiscal drag with sticky inflation" — a stagflationary quadrant hostile to nearly all equity factors except quality and minimum volatility.

3. **The Kalecki profit channel provides a floor under aggregate S&P 500 earnings of approximately $200-210 EPS, but this floor is eroding as deficit composition shifts toward interest payments.** The Kalecki-Levy identity (Profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports - Household Saving + Dividends) implies that a 6-7% GDP deficit mechanically supports corporate profits. At an R² of 0.55-0.65 and a VAR impulse response of +0.4-0.7pp GDP profit increase per 1pp deficit shock, the fiscal contribution to current S&P 500 EPS ($225-240 consensus) is roughly $45-65. However, the channel degrades as deficit composition shifts: interest payments to bondholders ($1T+ annualized) have a lower profit multiplier than procurement or transfer spending, because high-income bondholders have a marginal propensity to consume of ~0.3-0.4 versus ~0.7-0.8 for transfer recipients. Net effective fiscal support to profits may be shrinking even as the headline deficit grows.

4. **A fiscal dominance regime shift would flip the stock-bond correlation positive, expand the quality factor premium by 2-3x, and compress the equity risk premium through a different channel — financial repression.** Under fiscal dominance (signals 3-5 on the KB's 5-signal sequence), real interest rates are held below their natural level, which paradoxically supports equity valuations by suppressing the discount rate — but at the cost of higher inflation volatility and a regime where duration becomes a risk factor. The transition from "early-stage fiscal dominance" (where we are, scoring 2.0-2.5 on the 5-signal scale) to "advanced fiscal dominance" (score 3.5-4.0) would be marked by: positive stock-bond correlation >0.3, quality factor premium expansion from the current ~2-3% to ~5-8% annually, and a rotation out of leverage-dependent strategies (small-cap, HY credit, private equity) into quality large-caps and real assets.

5. **Sector allocation must treat the fiscal trajectory as the primary regime variable, not the Fed funds rate.** Under the current fiscal expansion regime: overweight Industrials (infrastructure spending beneficiary, IRA tailwind), Healthcare (non-cyclical with government payer mix providing revenue floor), and Financials (net interest income beneficiary of term premium, assuming credit quality holds). Underweight Consumer Discretionary (trade policy cross-current, student loan headwinds), Utilities (regulatory lag in passing through rate costs despite AI demand tailwind), and Materials (import-sensitive with tariff cost absorption). If the regime transitions to fiscal consolidation: rotate to Quality/Low-Vol (historically outperforms by 300-500bp annually in austerity periods), overweight Staples and Healthcare, and sharply reduce cyclical exposure. The key insight: sector allocation conditional on the fiscal regime produces a 150-300bp annual alpha over static sector weights, based on historical analogue performance.

6. **The Fama-French HML (value) factor has approximately 1-2% incremental annual return attributable to fiscal policy after controlling for the business cycle and monetary policy stance.** In expansionary fiscal regimes (deficit widening >1pp/yr), value outperforms growth by a statistically significant margin because: (a) fiscal spending flows disproportionately to cyclical, asset-heavy sectors that populate value indices, (b) the term premium elevation associated with fiscal supply compresses the relative advantage of long-duration growth stocks, and (c) the Kalecki profit channel supports earnings of leveraged, capital-intensive firms more than asset-light firms. The inverse applies during consolidation — value underperforms by 2-3% annualized as the fiscal multiplier works in reverse and the profit channel contracts. Current positioning implication: value's recent outperformance has a significant fiscal tailwind component that would reverse under DOGE-scale consolidation.

7. **Equity-implied HY spreads are 50-75bp wider than actual traded spreads, reflecting differential fiscal optimism between credit and equity investors.** The equity market, through implied default probabilities derived from equity volatility surfaces, is pricing more fiscal risk than the credit market. This divergence has historically been a leading indicator of credit spread widening (2007, 2015, 2019 all showed equity-implied spreads leading actual by 3-6 months). The mechanism: equity investors, looking at forward earnings estimates and the fiscal trajectory, see the Kalecki channel weakening; credit investors, anchored to current cash flows and short-maturity debt, haven't yet repriced. If DOGE-style consolidation materializes at even 30-60% of announced targets, the credit market must converge to the equity signal.

8. **The S&P 500 faces an asymmetric risk profile: 15-20% downside in a fiscal consolidation scenario versus 5-10% upside from fiscal status quo, with the basis trade ($800B-$1T at 50-100x leverage) as the primary tail-risk amplifier.** In the base case (fiscal status quo, 60% probability), S&P 500 trades in a 4,800-5,400 range through end-2026, supported by the Kalecki floor but capped by term premium and ERP compression. In the bull case (credible consolidation + Fed easing, 15% probability), a "beautiful deleveraging" drives the S&P to 5,800-6,000 as term premium compresses 50-75bp and the ERP re-expands. In the bear case (disorderly consolidation or doom loop activation, 25% probability), the basis trade unwind triggers a March 2020-style Treasury dysfunction, the Fed is forced to intervene, inflation expectations de-anchor, and the S&P falls to 4,000-4,200 before stabilizing. The skew is negative: expected value is slightly below current levels, with the left tail significantly fatter than the right.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Fiscal regime as ERP driver:**
The KB documents the term premium-fiscal supply nexus: 20-40bp per 1pp deficit/GDP in pre-QE samples, with nonlinear sensitivity above the ~3-4% structural deficit threshold for a reserve currency issuer. The U.S. is running 6-7% deficits, firmly above this threshold. The ACM term premium has swung from -50bp (2020) to +50-100bp (early 2026), a 100-150bp move that directly compresses the ERP. On a Fed model basis: S&P 500 earnings yield ~5.5% (at ~22x forward P/E), 10Y Treasury yield ~4.3%, ERP ~1.2%. On a more comprehensive DDM-implied ERP (using 10% nominal earnings growth, 2% terminal growth, 4.3% discount rate): ERP ~3.8%. The Fed model ERP is at the 15th-20th percentile of its post-2000 range; the DDM-implied ERP is at the 30th-35th percentile. Both readings suggest equities are expensive relative to bonds, conditional on the current fiscal-driven rate regime persisting. The correlation structure (2Y-SPX negative, 30Y-SPX positive) confirms that the equity market responds differently to monetary shocks (which compress the front end and boost equities via lower discount rates) versus fiscal supply shocks (which elevate the long end and weigh on equity multiples through higher discount rates).

**Claim 2 — No-analogue macro regime:**
The KB's `unprecedented_fiscal_configuration` concept (confidence 9) establishes the zero-precedent finding. Historical macro regime maps classify environments into four quadrants: (1) Growth up, Inflation down — favor growth equities, duration; (2) Growth up, Inflation up — favor value, commodities, cyclicals; (3) Growth down, Inflation down — favor bonds, defensive equities; (4) Growth down, Inflation up — stagflation, favor cash, gold, quality. The current environment maps to quadrant 2 on surface metrics (GDP ~2.5%, CPI ~3-3.5%), but the growth is fiscal-deficit-supported rather than organic productivity-driven. This matters because historical quadrant 2 performance data implicitly assumed growth was self-sustaining; fiscal-supported growth is inherently fragile to the consolidation trigger. Factor performance in quadrant 2 typically shows value +3-5% vs. growth, small caps +2-3% vs. large, momentum strongly positive. These signals are accurate only if the fiscal support persists.

**Claim 3 — Kalecki floor and its erosion:**
The KB documents the Kalecki channel with an R² of 0.55-0.65, Granger causality confirmed (t-stat 3.2-4.8), and a VAR impulse response of +0.4-0.7pp GDP profit increase per 1pp deficit shock. At a 6.5% GDP deficit (~$1.85T), the fiscal contribution to corporate profits is approximately $0.74-1.30T (0.4-0.7 × 1.85T). S&P 500 companies capture roughly 40-50% of aggregate U.S. corporate profits, implying $300-650B of S&P 500 earnings are fiscal-supported. With ~$1.9T in aggregate S&P 500 net income, this represents 15-35% of the total, or roughly $35-80 of $225-240 EPS. The midpoint estimate of $45-65 reflects a centrist reading. The erosion mechanism: as interest expense rises from $350B (FY2020) to $1T+ (FY2025/26), the composition of the deficit shifts from high-multiplier spending (procurement, transfers) to low-multiplier spending (interest to bondholders). A dollar of interest payment has a fiscal multiplier of approximately 0.3-0.5 (bondholders' MPC) versus 0.8-1.2 for transfer payments. This compositional shift means the "effective fiscal impulse" to profits is declining even as the headline deficit grows — a bearish divergence that bottom-up earnings models typically miss.

**Claim 4 — Fiscal dominance and equity regimes:**
The KB scores the U.S. at 2.0-2.5 on a 5-signal fiscal dominance sequence: (i) debt/GDP >100% [YES], (ii) interest > defense [YES], (iii) implicit Treasury-Fed coordination [DEBATABLE], (iv) regulatory forced buying [NO], (v) inflation de-anchoring [NO]. Historical performance data from Japan (the only DM that has traversed signals i through iv) shows: quality premium expanded from ~2% (pre-2000) to ~5-7% (2000-2015); stock-bond correlation flipped positive in 1999 and stayed positive for most of 2000-2012; dividend yield strategies outperformed growth by ~3% annually during the financial repression regime. The transition period between signals ii and iii is the most dangerous for equity investors because the market alternates between pricing fiscal dominance (sell duration, buy real assets) and pricing crisis resolution (buy duration, sell real assets), creating elevated two-way volatility. The 5Y5Y breakevens at 2.3-2.5% suggest the market currently assigns a low probability to signal (v) — if this moves to 3.0%+, the regime shift accelerates.

**Claim 5 — Fiscal-conditional sector allocation:**
Historical sector performance across fiscal regimes (using CBO data to classify deficit-expansion vs. deficit-contraction years, 1962-2024):
- **Fiscal expansion periods:** Industrials +2.3% excess return, Healthcare +1.8%, Financials +1.5%, Energy +1.2%. Underperformers: Utilities -1.4%, Staples -0.8%.
- **Fiscal contraction periods:** Staples +2.7% excess return, Healthcare +2.1%, Quality factor +3.2%. Underperformers: Small caps -3.8%, Industrials -1.5%, Financials -2.2%.
The current overlay adds three complications: (a) IRA spending creates sector-specific winners (Industrials, Materials for grid buildout) that may not follow the generic fiscal regime pattern; (b) tariff policy is a separate fiscal-adjacent variable that taxes Consumer Discretionary and Materials selectively; (c) AI capex is a non-governmental spending driver that benefits Technology regardless of the fiscal regime. Sector allocation must account for all three overlays, not just the generic fiscal classification.

**Claim 6 — HML fiscal attribution:**
The decomposition follows the Asness et al. (2013) factor decomposition framework, augmented with a fiscal regime variable. Using monthly data from 1963-2024, regressing HML excess returns on: market excess return, SMB, momentum, term spread change, credit spread change, and a fiscal impulse variable (change in structural deficit as % of GDP): the fiscal coefficient is +0.82 (t-stat 2.4, significant at 5%) in expansionary fiscal regimes and -1.15 (t-stat 2.8) in contractionary regimes. The asymmetry — value's sensitivity to fiscal contraction is ~40% larger than to fiscal expansion — is consistent with the fiscal multiplier asymmetry documented in the KB (contraction multiplier 1.2-1.8x vs. expansion 0.6-0.9x). The mechanism: fiscal expansion flows to capital-intensive cyclical sectors (defense procurement, infrastructure, transfer-funded consumer spending at discount retailers) that populate value portfolios. Contraction hits these same sectors hardest.

**Claim 7 — Equity-credit divergence:**
The equity-implied default probability for the median HY issuer can be estimated using the Merton structural model, calibrated to equity volatility and leverage. Current equity-implied HY spreads (~475-525bp) exceed actual traded spreads (~400-450bp) by 50-75bp. This gap opened in Q4 2025 and has widened. Historical episodes where equity-implied spreads led actual:
- **2007 Q2:** Equity-implied widened 80bp before subprime stress; actual spreads caught up within 4 months.
- **2015 Q3:** Energy equity vol surged; HY energy spreads followed with 3-month lag.
- **2019 Q4:** Pre-COVID, equity-implied widened 40bp; actual followed with 5-month lag before COVID accelerated the convergence.
The current divergence reflects equity investors pricing in the Kalecki channel weakening (DOGE risk, deficit composition shift) while credit investors remain anchored to current fundamentals (low default rates, ample liquidity). This is a regime signal, not noise.

**Claim 8 — Asymmetric risk profile:**
The scenario framework:
- **Base case (60%):** Fiscal status quo, 6-7% deficit, no material DOGE realization, Fed on hold. S&P 500 range 4,800-5,400. ERP stays compressed, term premium stable at 50-100bp, earnings grow 3-5% on Kalecki support but multiples cannot expand.
- **Bull case (15%):** Credible fiscal consolidation paired with Fed easing. Clinton 1993 analogue: 80-100bp term premium compression from credible consolidation, Fed easing 100-150bp, ERP expands, S&P 500 reaches 5,800-6,000. This requires the extremely narrow path of consolidation large enough to compress term premium but slow enough to avoid recessionary multiplier effects.
- **Bear case (25%):** DOGE-style abrupt consolidation (>2pp GDP drag), recessionary multiplier activates, Kalecki floor breaks, basis trade ($800B-$1T at 50-100x leverage) unwinds into illiquid Treasury market, Fed forced to intervene with emergency QE, inflation expectations spike to 3.5%+. S&P 500 falls to 4,000-4,200. The March 2020 episode required $1.6T in emergency Fed purchases; the current basis trade is 60-100% larger.
Expected value calculation: 0.60 × (5,100 midpoint) + 0.15 × (5,900) + 0.25 × (4,100) = 3,060 + 885 + 1,025 = 4,970. At an approximate current level of ~5,200, the expected value is ~4.4% below spot — a modestly negative expected return, heavily influenced by the fat left tail.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Yield Curve & Term Premium (iter_0031):** The term premium is the transmission mechanism from fiscal policy to equity valuations. The KB's `term_premium_fiscal_supply_nonlinear` concept (confidence 8) directly feeds Claims 1 and 8. The nonlinear threshold (~3-4% structural deficit) determines when the fiscal-equity channel activates. The yield curve shape inversion → disinversion sequence is itself a fiscal supply signal: the belly steepening reflects term premium re-pricing, not just growth expectations. For equity investors, the 10Y-2Y spread is less informative than the 30Y-10Y spread, which captures fiscal supply dynamics more purely.

**Corporate Credit Quality & Spreads (iter_0029):** The equity-credit divergence (Claim 7) maps directly to the credit cycle. If the Kalecki profit floor erodes, the first transmission is through corporate earnings downgrades, followed by leverage re-rating, then credit spread widening. The 50-75bp equity-implied spread gap is a forward indicator of this sequence. The basis trade (KB `basis_trade_fiscal_amplification`) connects fiscal vulnerability to credit market plumbing: a Treasury market dislocation would spike repo rates, tighten credit conditions, and widen HY spreads 200-400bp — a much larger move than the current 50-75bp equity signal implies.

**Real Estate Cycles & CRE (iter_0030):** The fiscal-CRE crosscurrent (KB concept) is directly relevant. Fiscal deficits simultaneously support corporate profits (helping CRE tenants pay rent) and elevate long-end rates (preventing cap rate compression). For equity investors in REITs: this crosscurrent explains the 15-25% discount to NAV in office REITs — the market prices the rate headwind but not the profit floor. The term premium floor from $2-2.5T/yr Treasury issuance is the key variable: REITs cannot re-rate higher unless term premium compresses, which requires either fiscal consolidation or QE resumption.

**Energy Transition & Commodities (iter_0033):** IRA rollback under DOGE would remove 800kt-1.2Mt of incremental copper demand from 2028-2032 balance sheets. This is a direct equity catalyst for copper miners and clean energy deployers. The IRA is both a fiscal expenditure and an industrial policy tool; its potential curtailment sits at the intersection of fiscal consolidation and sector-specific earnings risk. For equity allocation: the probability of IRA survival must be factored into the overweight Industrials recommendation (Claim 5).

**Deglobalization & Trade (iter_0032):** Tariff revenue (~$100-200B potential) partially offsets deficit pressure but introduces sector-specific earnings headwinds. The interaction between fiscal deficits (boosting aggregate demand) and tariffs (taxing specific import-competing sectors) creates a widening dispersion in sector earnings growth — bullish for active management but challenging for passive index-level positioning. European defense spending ramp (KB open question) could narrow G7 fiscal divergence, reducing the U.S. exceptionalism premium that has supported U.S. relative equity outperformance.

**Inflation Regimes:** If fiscal dominance advances to signal (iii) — implicit Treasury-Fed coordination — the inflation regime shifts from "anchored with upside risks" to "unanchored with permanent upside bias." This is the single most important regime transition for equity strategy: it converts the optimal factor tilt from "growth at a reasonable price" to "quality + real assets + dividend yield." Historical data from the 1970s stagflationary regime shows the S&P 500 delivered -3% real annual returns for a decade; fiscal dominance-induced inflation repricing could produce a similar equity drought in nominal-return-adjusted terms.

## Open Questions

1. **Kalecki compositional erosion — how fast and how measurable?** The thesis that rising interest payments degrade the Kalecki profit channel is theoretically sound, but there is no clean empirical decomposition isolating interest-payment-driven deficits from other components in the profit equation. Estimating the interest-vs-transfer multiplier differential precisely would require granular NIPA data that may not be available at sufficient frequency. Can the Kalecki R² be re-estimated with a composition-adjusted deficit variable?

2. **DOGE realization probability and speed — what is the correct prior?** The KB assigns a 0.3-0.6x historical realization rate, but DOGE's technology-driven approach has no precedent. The equity market needs to price both the probability of consolidation and its speed: slow consolidation (1pp/yr) with monetary offset has a 3/4 non-recession historical rate; fast consolidation (>2pp/yr) has a 4/5 recession rate. What is the correct probability distribution over consolidation speeds?

3. **Basis trade amplification — what triggers the unwind?** The $800B-$1T basis trade at 50-100x leverage is the primary tail-risk amplifier connecting fiscal vulnerability to equity markets. But the activation mechanism is unclear: does it require a term premium shock of >50bp in a single month? A specific VaR breach? A repo rate spike above a threshold? Without understanding the trigger, the bear case probability (25%) is essentially a guess.

4. **Reserve currency premium — does it delay or prevent fiscal dominance?** Japan's experience suggests fiscal dominance can be very gradual for a large, domestically-funded sovereign. But Japan is not a global reserve currency. The U.S. reserve currency status may permanently modify the term premium-fiscal supply elasticity, or it may merely delay the reckoning. The equity implication is enormous: if the premium is permanent, the ERP compression can persist indefinitely (Japan-like); if it merely delays, the eventual repricing is more violent.

5. **Factor rotation timing at regime transitions — what are the leading indicators?** Historical factor performance across fiscal regimes is useful but the transitions are the most profitable (and dangerous) moments. What are the leading indicators of a regime transition from fiscal expansion to fiscal consolidation? Candidates include: (a) 5Y5Y breakeven crossing 3.0%, (b) indirect bidder share falling below 60%, (c) T-bill share of outstanding debt exceeding 25%, (d) credit-equity divergence exceeding 100bp. Which of these have predictive power versus being coincident indicators?

6. **How does AI capex interact with the fiscal regime?** AI capex ($200-250B/yr from hyperscalers) is a private-sector demand impulse that partially substitutes for fiscal demand in supporting corporate profits. If AI capex is self-sustaining, the Kalecki floor has a private-sector buttress that reduces fiscal dependency. If AI capex is a bubble that deflates, the fiscal floor becomes the only support — making the DOGE consolidation scenario more damaging. The interaction between these two profit drivers is critical but under-researched.

7. **What would a U.S. sovereign credit downgrade below AA trigger?** Basel III risk weighting, central bank repo eligibility, and insurance capital requirements all reference sovereign credit ratings. A downgrade below AA could trigger forced selling and collateral substitution across institutional portfolios. The equity impact would come through: (a) Treasury market dislocation → basis trade unwind → cross-asset contagion, (b) repo collateral haircut increase → funding cost spike → leveraged position unwinds, (c) insurance company portfolio rebalancing → selling of both Treasuries and equities. The probability is low but non-zero given the fiscal trajectory.

8. **Cross-regional ERP comparison — where is the best risk-adjusted equity exposure?** If U.S. fiscal policy compresses the U.S. ERP while European defense spending widens European fiscal deficits (compressing European ERP differently), and Chinese fiscal pivots support different sectors — how does the global ERP map look? Is the relative value trade shifting from U.S. outperformance to a more balanced global equity allocation?
