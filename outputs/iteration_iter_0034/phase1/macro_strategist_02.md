# Fiscal Policy Divergence & Debt Sustainability — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The U.S. sectoral financial balances reveal a meta-stable but ultimately unsustainable configuration: a private sector surplus of ~3% of GDP is entirely dependent on a government deficit of ~6.5-7% of GDP, and any attempt to reduce that deficit without accommodating private sector desire to save will mechanically force private balance sheet deterioration or current account collapse.** This is the Godley identity: Private Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance ≡ 0. It is not a theory — it is accounting arithmetic. The current configuration (private surplus ~+3%, government deficit ~-6.5%, foreign surplus ~+3.5%) is meta-stable because private sector financial positions are healthy. But the configuration is *policy-dependent*: if fiscal consolidation reduces the government deficit to ~3.5% of GDP (as some DOGE scenarios propose), the private sector must move toward deficit by the same amount, or net exports must improve by an equivalent magnitude. Neither adjustment occurs voluntarily or benignly.

2. **The Minsky Financial Instability Hypothesis classifies the current U.S. borrower population into a dangerously bifurcated structure: hedge-financing dominance in the household sector (locked-in low mortgage rates, positive net worth gains) coexists with accelerating speculative and Ponzi financing in corporate credit, private credit, and leveraged strategies — aggregate statistics mask the fragility concentrated in the latter.** The ~95% of outstanding mortgage debt locked below 5% means the household sector is structurally insulated from rate tightening in a manner historically unprecedented among DM economies. This is genuine hedge financing — cash flows cover debt service with margin to spare, and balance sheets are sound ($30T in net worth gains 2020-2023). But corporate credit tells a different story: 40%+ of Russell 2000 firms are unprofitable (speculative/Ponzi units), covenant-lite issuance exceeds 90% of leveraged loans, PIK toggle usage in private credit is proliferating, and the basis trade ($800B-$1T at 50-100x leverage) represents the most extreme Ponzi financing in the financial system — where returns depend entirely on continued market stability and rollover access.

3. **Private debt dynamics, not public debt levels, are the binding constraint on financial stability — the KB's focus on public debt/GDP ratios and deficit sustainability reflects the mainstream confusion between a currency issuer and a currency user, while the real fragility accumulates in private credit channels that lack a sovereign backstop.** Japan at 250%+ public debt/GDP without crisis (as the KB correctly notes) is not an "anomaly" — it is the *expected outcome* for a monetary sovereign issuing debt in its own currency with a central bank that can and does backstop the sovereign bond market. The U.S. is in an identical structural position. The binding constraint is not the federal debt stock but the *private* debt-to-GDP ratio (~155% of GDP for household + corporate, down from ~170% pre-GFC but rising since 2021) and the *debt service ratio* (~15% of household disposable income, below the ~18% pre-GFC peak but rising as ARM resets and new borrowing occurs at higher rates). The Minsky moment, when it comes, will originate in private balance sheets — not the Treasury market.

4. **The Kalecki-Levy profit equation, properly understood as a flow-of-funds identity rather than a regression relationship, establishes that the 6-7% of GDP federal deficit is providing a structural floor under corporate profits of approximately $1.3-1.5T annually — withdrawing this floor via fiscal consolidation would produce a profit recession before any credit event occurs.** The KB reports R² of 0.55-0.65 and Granger causality confirmation. From the Post-Keynesian perspective, the "unexplained" 35-45% is not a deficiency of the Kalecki channel but reflects the *other terms* in the identity: private investment spending, net exports, household saving behavior, and capitalist consumption. The fiscal term is currently the dominant driver because the other terms are either stable (household saving rate ~4-5%), negative (net exports ~-3.5% GDP), or decelerating (private investment, ex-AI capex). Remove or substantially reduce the fiscal term, and the profit identity requires either a surge in private investment (counter-cyclical in a consolidation) or a collapse in household saving (which means household deficit spending — the pre-GFC fragility pattern). Neither is plausible as a voluntary adjustment.

5. **The deficit-term premium "doom loop" identified in the KB is real but fundamentally misdiagnosed: it is not a solvency problem for a monetary sovereign but a *political economy* problem — the loop activates not because the U.S. "can't afford" its debt but because institutional arrangements (the Fed's inflation mandate, Congressional debt ceiling, rating agency frameworks) create artificial constraints that mimic the constraints of a currency user, producing self-fulfilling instability.** The UK gilt crisis of September 2022 is the paradigm case: the UK government was never at risk of involuntary default (it issues debt in sterling with the Bank of England as backstop), but institutional arrangements (LDI leverage, BoE's inflation-fighting credibility concerns, market expectations of fiscal orthodoxy) produced a self-reinforcing sell-off that forced a policy reversal within days. The U.S. doom loop operates through an analogous mechanism: rising deficits → term premium expansion → higher debt service → Congressional deficit panic → threatened consolidation → economic slowdown → falling revenues → higher deficits. The loop is *political*, not *arithmetic*.

6. **The "unprecedented fiscal configuration" (6-7% deficit at sub-4% unemployment) is not unprecedented when analyzed through the sectoral balance lens — it is the predictable consequence of persistently large private sector saving desire combined with a structural current account deficit, requiring the government to run deficits of equivalent magnitude to prevent a demand shortfall.** The mainstream is shocked by this configuration because it views the deficit as a policy choice rather than an endogenous outcome. Godley's stock-flow consistent framework explains it: if the private sector desires a financial surplus of ~3% of GDP (driven by post-GFC deleveraging, wealth inequality concentrating income among high-savers, corporate cash hoarding) and the current account deficit is ~3.5% of GDP (driven by the dollar's reserve currency role and U.S. consumption patterns), then the government *must* run a deficit of ~6.5% of GDP by accounting identity — unless it is willing to force the private sector into deficit, which is the definition of a Minskyan fragility-building policy.

7. **The asymmetry between fiscal contraction and expansion multipliers (the KB identifies 1.2-1.8x for contraction vs. 0.6-0.9x for expansion) is explained by Kaleckian effective demand theory and Minsky's debt deflation dynamics: fiscal contraction at high private debt levels triggers a paradox of thrift cascade where reduced government spending → reduced private income → increased private deleveraging → reduced private spending → further income decline, with the debt overhang amplifying each round.** This is not a symmetry-breaking anomaly to be explained — it is the baseline prediction of the Post-Keynesian framework. Keynes's paradox of thrift operates through the Kalecki profit equation: reduced government deficit directly reduces aggregate profits, which reduces investment, which further reduces profits (the multiplier-accelerator mechanism). In a high-private-debt environment, the contraction is amplified because falling incomes impair debt service, triggering distressed asset sales, deflating collateral values, and tightening credit conditions — Fisher's debt deflation spiral, which Minsky incorporated into his Financial Instability Hypothesis.

8. **The stock-bond correlation regime shift under fiscal dominance — from negative to positive — is the market's recognition that the Keynesian transmission mechanism has reversed: previously, recessions triggered Fed easing (bond rally) while hurting equities (divergent moves = negative correlation); under fiscal dominance, inflation pressures prevent Fed easing during slowdowns, and the fiscal expansion required to support growth simultaneously increases bond supply — both assets sell off together.** This is a structural regime change with first-order portfolio construction implications. The 2Y-SPX correlation at -0.25 to -0.35 versus 30Y-SPX at +0.05 to +0.15 (KB knowledge gap) is diagnostic: the front end still reflects monetary dominance (the Fed *can* cut), while the long end reflects fiscal supply dominance (Treasury *must* issue). The bifurcation confirms that the U.S. is in the early stages of fiscal dominance — the long end of the curve is already pricing the sovereign supply constraint, while the front end retains monetary policy optionality. The Post-Keynesian prediction: as fiscal dominance deepens, the positive correlation extends to the front end, and the entire duration complex loses its hedging property.

9. **A U.S. sovereign credit downgrade below AA would have mechanistically severe but economically absurd consequences — forced selling through Basel III risk weighting, central bank repo eligibility, and insurance capital requirements represents a pure institutional fragility: the creditworthiness of a monetary sovereign is not meaningfully captured by rating agency metrics designed for currency users.** The transmission chain is real: downgrade → higher risk weights → banks forced to hold more capital against Treasuries or sell → insurance companies face higher capital charges → repo haircuts increase → collateral values decline → forced selling cascade. But the underlying logic is incoherent: the U.S. government, which creates the dollars in which its debt is denominated, cannot involuntarily default. Rating agency frameworks, designed for entities that *borrow* money they cannot create, are categorically misapplied to monetary sovereigns. Yet the institutional arrangements — Basel III, insurance regulation, repo market conventions — treat the rating as meaningful, creating a self-fulfilling fragility that has nothing to do with actual default risk. This is the quintessential Minsky institutional fragility: the system's own rules create the instability.

10. **The "crowding-out" debate is resolved by endogenous money theory: government deficits do not "borrow" from a fixed pool of private savings but create the income (and therefore the savings) that funds the government's debt ex post — the disappearance of the crowding-out correlation post-2008 (KB: partial correlation dropped from -0.38 to -0.12) is not an anomaly of QE but the permanent empirical refutation of the loanable funds framework.** The crowding-out thesis depends on the loanable funds model: a fixed supply of savings from which both private investment and government borrowing must draw. Endogenous money theory (Lavoie 2014, Keen 2011) demonstrates that bank credit creation generates deposits, and government spending generates private sector income that is saved (partially) as government bonds. The correlation was never causal — it reflected periods when tight monetary policy coincided with fiscal expansion (the Volcker era), creating the *appearance* of crowding-out through the interest rate channel. Post-2008, with rates at the zero lower bound and excess reserves flooding the system, the mechanism could not even produce the spurious correlation. The KB asks whether QT will "re-activate" crowding-out — the Post-Keynesian answer is no, because the mechanism never existed as described. What *does* exist is the fiscal supply channel on the *term premium*, which is a different animal entirely: not savings scarcity but duration supply.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Sectoral Balance Arithmetic

The sectoral financial balances identity is:

**(S – I) + (T – G) + (M – X) ≡ 0**

Where (S-I) is the private sector balance, (T-G) is the government balance, and (M-X) is the foreign balance (current account). This is not a behavioral equation but a national accounting identity derived from GDP ≡ C + I + G + NX.

Current U.S. sectoral balances (approximate, 2025 Q4 - 2026 Q1):
- **Private sector**: +3.0-3.5% of GDP (net financial surplus — the private sector is accumulating financial assets faster than financial liabilities)
- **Government sector**: -6.5 to -7.0% of GDP (combined federal + state/local deficit)
- **Foreign sector**: +3.0-3.5% of GDP (the current account deficit, which is the foreign sector's surplus)

The identity holds by construction: +3.0 + (-6.5) + (+3.5) ≈ 0.

Wynne Godley used this framework at the Levy Institute to forecast the unsustainability of the Clinton-era surpluses (published 1999): if the government was running a surplus of ~2% of GDP and the current account deficit was ~3% of GDP, the private sector *had* to be running a deficit of ~5% of GDP — which meant households and businesses were collectively borrowing more than they earned, an unsustainable trajectory that unwound in the 2001 recession and ultimately the 2008 crisis.

The current configuration is the mirror image: the private sector surplus is healthy precisely *because* the government deficit is large. The G7 divergence (KB: g7_fiscal_divergence_extreme, confidence 8) shows the U.S. at -6.5 to -7.0% versus Germany at -2% — but Germany's private sector surplus is correspondingly smaller and its current account surplus provides the external demand that the U.S. private sector gets from fiscal flows. The divergence is not an anomaly but the reflection of different private sector saving desires and trade positions.

**Sustainability assessment**: The current configuration is sustainable as long as: (a) financial markets continue absorbing Treasury issuance without destabilizing term premium spikes; (b) the private sector's financial surplus is maintained by continued government transfers; and (c) foreign central banks and investors continue recycling current account surpluses into U.S. financial assets. Condition (a) is the operational constraint — not fiscal "affordability" but market absorption capacity. The KB's deficit-term premium doom loop (confidence 6) correctly identifies this as the binding constraint, though it misidentifies the mechanism as solvency rather than liquidity/absorption.

### Claim 2: Minsky Financial Fragility — Bifurcated Classification

The Minsky taxonomy classifies borrowers by their cash flow position relative to debt service:
- **Hedge units**: Cash flows cover both interest and principal — can survive indefinitely without refinancing.
- **Speculative units**: Cash flows cover interest but not principal — must roll over debt at maturity.
- **Ponzi units**: Cash flows do not cover even interest — must borrow more to service existing debt, or depend on asset appreciation.

**Household sector — overwhelmingly hedge financing:**
- ~95% of outstanding mortgage debt locked below 5% (KB: single_factor_dominance_bias)
- Household debt service ratio: ~9.7% of disposable income (Fed DSR), well below the 13.2% peak in Q3 2007
- Household net worth: ~$160T+, with ~$30T in gains 2020-2023
- New mortgages at 6.5-7.0% represent a small flow relative to the stock — the median homeowner's mortgage is at ~3.5-4.0%

This is the most robust household hedge-financing position in at least 40 years. The Minsky fragility cycle *has not advanced* in the household sector because the rate lock-in prevents the transmission of tighter monetary policy to existing borrowers. This is a critical distinction from pre-GFC: in 2005-2007, the ARM share was ~35-40% of originations, meaning rate hikes transmitted rapidly into household debt service.

**Corporate sector — stratified between hedge and speculative/Ponzi:**
- Investment-grade corporates: mostly hedge financing (interest coverage >4x, fixed-rate debt, long maturities)
- Mid-market and leveraged borrowers: increasingly speculative (floating-rate leveraged loans at SOFR+300-500bp, interest coverage compressing from 3.5x to ~2.0x)
- Russell 2000 unprofitable companies (~40%+): Ponzi financing by definition
- Private credit (~$1.8T): PIK toggle proliferation means a growing share are Ponzi units disguised as performing loans

**Financial sector — extreme Ponzi concentration in leveraged strategies:**
- Treasury basis trade: $800B-$1T at 50-100x leverage (KB: basis_trade_fiscal_amplification, confidence 7). This is the textbook Ponzi unit: returns depend entirely on the convergence of a narrow basis, funded by overnight repo that must be rolled continuously. Any disruption to repo availability or widening of the basis produces losses that exceed equity capital, forcing liquidation. March 2020 required $1.6T in emergency Fed purchases to prevent cascading failure.
- Private credit leverage: fund-level leverage of 1.5-2.5x on portfolios of illiquid, opaque corporate loans, many with PIK features that defer cash interest in favor of compounding notional — the debt grows even when the borrower cannot service it.

**Aggregate Minsky classification (estimated):**
- Hedge units: ~55-60% of total private credit outstanding (down from ~70% in 2019)
- Speculative units: ~25-30% (up from ~20%)
- Ponzi units: ~10-15% (up from ~5-7%)

The direction of travel is toward fragility, even though the *level* remains below 2007 peaks. This is the critical Minsky insight: it is the *direction* that matters, not the level, because stability breeds the very risk-taking that produces instability.

### Claim 3: Private Debt as the Binding Constraint

The distinction between a currency issuer and a currency user is foundational to Post-Keynesian monetary theory (Wray 1998, Kelton 2020). A currency-issuing government with a floating exchange rate and debt denominated in its own currency cannot be forced into involuntary default — it can always instruct its central bank to credit accounts. This does not mean deficits are costless (they can cause inflation if they push demand beyond productive capacity), but it does mean the "solvency" framing is categorically wrong.

Japan is the definitive case study, not the "anomaly" the mainstream treats it as. At 250%+ debt/GDP (KB: debt_gdp_threshold_debunked, confidence 8), Japan has experienced *deflation*, not the inflation or default that debt-threshold models predict. This is because Japan's private sector has been in persistent surplus (a balance sheet recession following the 1989 asset bubble), requiring the government to run continuous deficits to prevent output collapse. Richard Koo's balance sheet recession framework, which is analytically compatible with the Post-Keynesian approach, demonstrates that when the private sector is collectively deleveraging, government deficits are not inflationary but *necessary to prevent deflation*.

The actual constraint on the U.S. is private debt dynamics:
- **Household debt/GDP**: ~77% (down from ~100% in 2008, up from ~73% in 2020)
- **Non-financial corporate debt/GDP**: ~78% (up from ~73% in 2019)
- **Total private debt/GDP**: ~155% (the relevant Keen/Minsky metric)
- **Private debt service ratio (aggregate)**: rising as new borrowing occurs at higher rates, though partially insulated by the mortgage lock-in
- **Credit impulse**: decelerating — bank lending growth slowing, non-bank (private credit) still expanding but at declining rates

Steve Keen's empirical work (Keen 2011, 2017) demonstrates that it is the *change in private debt* (the credit impulse) that drives demand, and the *level* of private debt/GDP that determines fragility. The U.S. is in a middle zone: the private debt level is elevated but not extreme, the credit impulse is positive but decelerating, and the household sector's lock-in provides an unusual buffer. The Minsky moment risk is concentrated in corporate credit, private credit, and leveraged financial strategies — not in the Treasury market.

### Claim 4: Kalecki Profit Equation — The Fiscal Floor

The Kalecki-Levy profit identity:

**Profits ≡ Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports – Household Saving + Capitalist Consumption**

This is derived from the national accounts and holds by construction. The KB (kalecki_channel_empirical_partial, confidence 7) reports an R² of 0.55-0.65 between deficit changes and profit changes, with Granger causality confirmed. The VAR impulse response of +0.4-0.7pp GDP profit increase per 1pp GDP deficit shock quantifies the marginal contribution.

Current decomposition (approximate annual flows, 2025-2026):
- Government deficit: ~$1.8-2.0T (~6.5-7% of GDP) — the dominant positive term
- Private investment: ~$4.2T gross, ~$1.0T net — positive but stable/decelerating
- Net exports: ~-$0.9T — negative contribution (draining profits to foreign sector)
- Household saving: ~$0.8-1.0T — negative contribution (savings leak from profit circuit)
- Capitalist consumption: ~$0.5-0.7T — stable positive contribution

The deficit accounts for roughly 40-50% of the *flow* supporting profits, which is consistent with the KB's R² estimate. The critical question is what happens when the deficit shrinks: the Kalecki identity predicts a proportionate reduction in profits unless another term expands. The KB's knowledge gap on "fiscal contraction asymmetric multiplier (1.2-1.8x vs 0.6-0.9x)" is explained by this mechanism: contraction removes the profit floor, triggering investment decline (the accelerator), which amplifies the profit decline beyond the initial fiscal withdrawal.

The deficit composition issue (KB open question: "Can the channel persist when deficits are dominated by interest payments rather than productive spending?") is important: interest payments flow to bondholders, who have a lower marginal propensity to consume than transfer recipients or government employees. The Kalecki channel still operates (interest payments are income to the private sector), but with a lower multiplier per dollar — estimated at 0.3-0.5x for interest payments versus 1.0-1.5x for direct government purchases. As interest expense rises from ~$1T to a projected ~$1.5T by 2030, the "effective" fiscal impulse (multiplier-weighted) declines even if the headline deficit remains constant.

### Claim 5: The Doom Loop as Political Economy, Not Arithmetic

The deficit-term premium doom loop (KB: confidence 6) is mechanically real: larger deficits → more issuance → higher term premium → higher debt service → larger deficits. Net interest from $350B (FY2020) to ~$1T (FY2025/26) is quantitatively significant.

But the *interpretation* matters enormously. The mainstream reads this as a solvency spiral approaching a fiscal limit. The Post-Keynesian reading is different:

The U.S. government, as the issuer of the dollar, faces no *financial* constraint on debt service — the Fed can always ensure Treasury auctions are successful, either through direct purchases or by supplying reserves to the banking system that are recycled into Treasuries. This is not theoretical: it is what happened in 2020-2021 ($5T+ in Fed purchases) and, more subtly, what happens continuously through the Fed's standing repo facility and bank reserve management.

The constraint is *institutional* and *political*:
- **Institutional**: The Fed's dual mandate (price stability + maximum employment) creates a tension with fiscal accommodation. If supporting fiscal flows requires balance sheet expansion that is perceived as inflationary, the Fed faces a credibility crisis. This is the fiscal dominance dilemma (KB: fiscal_dominance_early_stage, confidence 6) — not "can the government pay?" but "will institutional arrangements permit the central bank to support the government without destabilizing inflation expectations?"
- **Political**: The debt ceiling, CBO scoring conventions, and Congressional Budget Act create artificial constraints that can produce genuine crises (2011 downgrade, 2023 near-default) despite zero risk of involuntary default. These are *manufactured* crises that have real market consequences — the doom loop operates through political dysfunction, not fiscal arithmetic.

The UK gilt crisis (September 2022) is instructive: Truss's mini-budget was rejected not because the UK couldn't afford it but because it violated the institutional expectations of "sound finance" in a way that triggered LDI leverage unwind. The BoE was forced to intervene with £65B in emergency gilt purchases — demonstrating simultaneously that the central bank *could* backstop the market (no solvency problem) and that institutional arrangements *could* produce a genuine financial crisis (the leverage was real even if the solvency concern was not).

### Claim 6: The "Unprecedented" Configuration as Endogenous Outcome

The KB (unprecedented_fiscal_configuration, confidence 9) correctly identifies that 6-7% deficits at sub-4% unemployment have zero pre-2022 precedent. But "unprecedented" does not mean "inexplicable."

The Godley sectoral balance framework provides the explanation:
1. **Private sector saving desire has structurally increased** post-GFC: household saving rates have not returned to the 0-2% range of 2005-2007, corporate cash hoarding has increased (Apple alone holds $160B+), and wealth inequality concentrates income among high-savers.
2. **The current account deficit is structurally persistent** at 3-3.5% of GDP, driven by the dollar's reserve currency role (Triffin dilemma) and U.S. consumption patterns.
3. **Given these two structural features, the government deficit is *endogenous***: it *must* equal the private surplus + foreign surplus by identity. If the private sector wants to save 3% of GDP and the rest of the world wants to save 3.5% of GDP (by running current account surpluses with the U.S.), the government *must* run a 6.5% deficit — or output will contract until the desired saving is realized at a lower income level (i.e., a recession).

The "unprecedented" nature reflects the *combination* of high private saving desire (post-GFC structural shift) and low unemployment (strong labor market). In prior cycles, high unemployment was the release valve: when the private sector desired to save more than the government was willing to dissave, unemployment rose until income fell enough to reduce desired saving. The current cycle, with massive pandemic fiscal intervention and subsequent structural deficits, bypassed this adjustment — maintaining high employment *and* allowing high private saving by running the correspondingly large deficits.

This is not profligacy. It is the arithmetic consequence of the private sector's financial preferences and the trade position. Viewing it as "stimulus" implies a counterfactual of lower deficits with the same employment outcome — but the sectoral balance identity shows that lower deficits at the same trade position require either lower private saving (i.e., private sector deficit spending, which *is* the fragility-building pattern) or lower output (recession).

### Claim 7: Asymmetric Multipliers and Debt Deflation

The KB knowledge gap on "fiscal contraction has an asymmetrically higher multiplier (1.2-1.8x) than fiscal expansion (0.6-0.9x)" aligns with both the empirical literature (Auerbach and Gorodnichenko 2012, regime-switching SVAR) and Post-Keynesian theory.

The theoretical mechanism:
- **Expansion multiplier is attenuated** by: leakage to imports (~20-25% of marginal demand), leakage to saving (especially when income accrues to high-savers), and potential monetary offset (Fed raises rates to offset fiscal expansion, reducing the net demand impulse). At current high employment, some expansion leaks into prices rather than output, further attenuating the real multiplier.
- **Contraction multiplier is amplified** by: the paradox of thrift (reduced income → increased desire to save → reduced spending → further income decline), debt deflation dynamics (reduced income impairs debt service → asset sales → collateral deflation → credit tightening → further income decline), and the absence of automatic monetary offset at the zero lower bound or when the Fed is constrained by inflation concerns.

The critical regime-dependence: the asymmetry is largest when private debt/GDP is high and monetary policy is constrained. Both conditions apply to the current environment — private debt/GDP at ~155% (elevated, though not extreme) and the Fed constrained by above-target inflation (unable to offset fiscal contraction with rate cuts without losing credibility). This is the worst possible starting point for fiscal consolidation: the multiplier is high, the monetary offset is unavailable, and the private sector is carrying enough debt that income shocks feed directly into financial distress.

The 2013 sequester counterexample (KB: consolidation_recession_conditional_probability, confidence 5) requires careful interpretation: (a) the consolidation was ~1.5-2pp of GDP, below the 2pp threshold associated with elevated recession probability; (b) the Fed was in the middle of QE3, providing massive monetary accommodation; (c) household balance sheets were in the early stages of post-GFC recovery with substantial pent-up demand; (d) the consolidation was concentrated in defense and discretionary spending with relatively low multipliers. None of these conditions apply in 2026: any meaningful consolidation would exceed 2pp, the Fed is not easing, household balance sheets are mature rather than recovering, and the consolidation targets likely include transfer spending with high multipliers.

### Claim 8: Stock-Bond Correlation Under Fiscal Dominance

The KB knowledge gap on the 2Y-SPX correlation (-0.25 to -0.35) versus 30Y-SPX correlation (+0.05 to +0.15) provides direct empirical confirmation of the fiscal dominance transition.

The Post-Keynesian explanation for the maturity bifurcation:
- **Front end (2Y)**: still governed by monetary policy expectations. When equities sell off on growth fears, the market prices Fed cuts, pushing 2Y yields down — maintaining the traditional negative correlation. The Fed retains policy autonomy at the short end.
- **Long end (30Y)**: increasingly governed by fiscal supply. Treasury issuance at $2-2.5T annually means the long end must clear enormous supply regardless of the growth or inflation outlook. When equities sell off on growth fears, investors *should* buy Treasuries as a safe haven — but the sheer volume of supply prevents yield compression. Additionally, growth fears imply larger deficits (automatic stabilizers + discretionary response), which means *more* supply, pushing 30Y yields *up* even as growth weakens.

This is the definition of fiscal dominance: the government's financing needs dominate the price formation of long-duration government bonds, overriding the safe-haven demand that historically compressed yields during risk-off episodes.

The portfolio construction implications are severe: (a) long-duration Treasuries lose their hedging property for equity portfolios; (b) risk parity strategies (which depend on negative equity-bond correlation to justify high leverage) face structural impairment; (c) pension fund asset-liability matching becomes more difficult (liabilities are long-duration, but long-duration bonds no longer diversify equity risk). The UK gilt crisis revealed what happens when levered strategies built on the old correlation regime encounter the new one.

### Claim 9: Sovereign Downgrade as Institutional Fragility

The KB knowledge gap on sovereign credit downgrade mechanics identifies a genuine transmission channel:
- **Basel III risk weighting**: Treasuries currently carry 0% risk weight under the standardized approach. A downgrade to below AA- would require a 20% risk weight, forcing banks to hold additional capital against ~$4T in bank-held Treasuries — estimated capital requirement increase of ~$80-160B.
- **Central bank repo eligibility**: no immediate impact (the Fed sets its own eligibility criteria), but foreign central banks may apply haircut adjustments, reducing the effective reserve value of Treasury holdings.
- **Insurance capital requirements**: state-level NAIC risk-based capital would require higher capital charges on Treasury holdings, potentially forcing portfolio rebalancing.

The mechanism is real but the logic is absurd from the Post-Keynesian perspective. The U.S. government cannot default involuntarily on dollar-denominated debt — it is the monopoly issuer of the dollar. Rating agency methodology, designed for entities that borrow in a currency they do not create, is categorically misapplied. Yet the institutional architecture — Basel III, NAIC, repo market conventions — treats the rating as meaningful, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy: the downgrade itself (through forced selling, higher capital requirements, and collateral haircuts) can produce the financial instability that the downgrade ostensibly warns about.

This is Minsky institutional fragility in its purest form: the system's own regulatory and risk management rules create procyclical dynamics that amplify rather than dampen financial stress. The rules were designed to prevent a crisis but become the *mechanism* of crisis transmission.

### Claim 10: Endogenous Money and the Death of Crowding-Out

The crowding-out thesis rests on the loanable funds model: there is a fixed supply of savings, and government borrowing absorbs savings that would otherwise fund private investment. The interest rate rises to "crowd out" private investment.

Endogenous money theory (Moore 1988, Lavoie 2014, McLeay, Radia, and Thomas 2014 — BoE Quarterly Bulletin) demonstrates that this model is empirically false:
1. **Banks create deposits when they lend** — loans create deposits, not the reverse. The "money multiplier" textbook model is rejected by every major central bank (BoE 2014, Bundesbank 2017, Fed staff research).
2. **Government spending creates private sector income** — when Treasury spends, it credits bank reserves and recipient accounts simultaneously. The "money" to buy government bonds is created by the government spending itself.
3. **The interest rate is a policy variable**, not a market-clearing price for savings. The Fed sets the overnight rate; the term structure is determined by expected future policy rates plus a term premium.

The KB's finding (crowding_out_post2008_attenuation, confidence 5) that the partial correlation dropped from -0.38 to -0.12 post-2008 is explained by the Post-Keynesian framework: the pre-2008 correlation was spurious, driven by Volcker-era monetary policy that raised rates in response to fiscal expansion (not savings scarcity but deliberate policy tightening). Post-2008, with rates at the ZLB and the Fed explicitly targeting accommodation, the spurious correlation disappeared.

The KB asks whether QT will "re-activate" the channel. The answer is: what will re-emerge is not crowding-out (savings scarcity) but the **fiscal supply channel on term premium** (duration supply). These are different: crowding-out operates through a *quantity* of savings being exhausted (false); the term premium channel operates through the *price* of absorbing additional duration risk (real). The distinction matters because the policy prescription differs: crowding-out implies government should borrow less to "free up" savings for private investment; the term premium channel implies government should manage the *maturity profile* of its debt (shifting to bills from bonds) to reduce duration supply without reducing the deficit. Treasury has been doing exactly this — the KB notes T-bill share rising from 15% to 22% — which is the correct response to a term premium problem but would be incoherent as a response to a loanable funds problem.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Sovereign Debt Sustainability
The sectoral balance framework reframes sovereign debt sustainability from a debt/GDP ratio question (meaningless for a monetary sovereign) to a *private sector* sustainability question: is the private sector balance consistent with financial stability? A government deficit that produces a private sector surplus is *stabilizing*, not destabilizing. The debt_gdp_threshold_debunked concept (confidence 8) confirms: no robust threshold exists, and the linear negative relationship shrinks 40% for monetary-sovereign issuers. Japan at 250%+ is the permanent counterexample.

### Fiscal Dominance & Monetary Policy Constraints
The fiscal dominance early-stage diagnosis (2.0-2.5 on the 5-signal sequence) maps directly onto the Post-Keynesian framework: fiscal dominance is not a pathology but a regime where fiscal policy takes primacy over monetary policy in determining macro outcomes — the normal condition throughout most of economic history (pre-1979 Volcker shock). The stock-bond correlation shift and maturity bifurcation (Claim 8) are the market manifestations of this transition. The term premium fiscal supply channel (KB: nonlinear, 20-40bp per 1pp deficit) is the transmission mechanism.

### Inflation Regimes
The Post-Keynesian inflation view differs fundamentally from the mainstream: inflation is a *distributional conflict* (workers vs. capital vs. government vs. foreign sector) mediated through administered prices, market power, and supply-chain structure — not an "excess demand" phenomenon captured by output gaps or NAIRU. The current elevated deficit at low unemployment is *not* inflationary if it is financing investment, servicing debt, or compensating for a trade deficit. The KB's concern about "unprecedented fiscal configuration → inflation risk" overstates the linkage by assuming a Phillips curve relationship that the Post-Keynesian tradition rejects as theoretically incoherent and empirically unreliable.

### Growth Models & Cycle Positioning
The credit impulse framework (Claim 7 from the equity analysis) provides the leading indicator: when the total credit impulse (bank + non-bank + fiscal) turns negative, a growth deceleration follows with a 2-3 quarter lag. The current read — bank credit impulse decelerating, non-bank still positive, fiscal impulse stable — is consistent with late-cycle positioning but not imminent contraction. The Minsky cycle is in the speculative-to-Ponzi transition phase: stability is endogenously generating the leverage and risk-taking that will eventually produce instability.

### Real Estate Cycles (CRE)
The fiscal_policy_cre_crosscurrent concept (KB, confidence 7) correctly identifies the tension: Kalecki channel supports corporate profits (positive for CRE earnings) while fiscal supply keeps term premium elevated (negative for CRE valuations). The Post-Keynesian addition: this cross-current explains the Japan 1990s analogue (KB: japan_1990s_cre_analogue, confidence 8) — Japan's persistent fiscal deficits prevented a total CRE earnings collapse (no systemic defaults) while the Bank of Japan's rate suppression was insufficient to compress cap rates (because the private sector was deleveraging, not investing). The U.S. CRE cycle will follow a similar prolonged-adjustment path rather than a V-shaped recovery, precisely because the fiscal architecture supports earnings while preventing the rate collapse needed for valuation recovery.

### Credit Markets & Corporate Quality
The Minsky bifurcation in corporate credit (Claim 2) connects directly to the corporate credit quality and spread analysis (KB: recent iteration iter_0029). The Kalecki profit floor suppresses default rates, compressing credit spreads, encouraging leveraged carry strategies sized to the low-vol environment (KB relationship: kalecki → basis_trade_fiscal_amplification, confidence 7). This is the Minsky dynamic operating through credit markets: fiscal-supported stability reduces perceived risk, incentivizing the very leverage that creates fragility. The equity-implied HY spread divergence (KB knowledge gap: equity-implied spreads 50-75bp wider than actual) suggests equity markets are pricing the Minsky fragility that credit markets are not.

### Currency & Dollar Dynamics
The G7 fiscal divergence (KB: confidence 8) maps onto the Mundell-Fleming framework filtered through the Post-Keynesian lens: the U.S. fiscal expansion at this scale requires either (a) continued foreign financing (maintaining the current account deficit and dollar strength) or (b) dollar depreciation to shift the current account balance, reducing the fiscal burden on domestic demand. The historical mapping (2001-04 dollar-weak pattern most analogous) suggests gradual dollar weakening — which would improve the trade balance and allow a smaller deficit to sustain the same private sector surplus.

### Commodity Markets
The OPEC fiscal breakeven constraint (KB: confidence 8) connects through the Godley framework: oil-exporting nations are fundamentally dependent on oil revenues to finance government spending. Their fiscal breakevens ($78-85/bbl) set a floor under oil prices *because* their sectoral balance requires it — the government deficit must be financed by oil revenues, and below-breakeven prices force either fiscal contraction (recessionary) or reserve drawdown (temporary). China's commodity demand mix shift (KB: confidence 7) alters the composition of global commodity demand in ways that the fiscal architecture of producing nations has not adjusted to.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the credit impulse doing** *right now*, across all channels (bank, non-bank, fiscal, shadow)? The aggregate credit impulse is the single most important leading indicator in the Post-Keynesian framework, and I lack real-time granularity on non-bank credit (private credit, CLOs, direct lending) which may be more important than bank lending in the current cycle.

2. **How large is the "effective" fiscal impulse** when adjusted for the rising share of interest payments? If net interest reaches $1.5T by 2030 (with a multiplier of ~0.3-0.5x) while direct government purchases and transfers grow more slowly (multiplier ~1.0-1.5x), the headline deficit may overstate the actual demand support. Is there a point where interest payments dominate the deficit, and the Kalecki channel effectively shuts down despite a large headline deficit?

3. **Can the household mortgage lock-in persist as a Minsky circuit breaker?** The ~95% of outstanding mortgage debt below 5% is historically unprecedented and fundamentally alters the transmission of monetary policy. But: (a) new household formation must borrow at 6.5-7%, creating a generational divide; (b) HELOC and second-lien borrowing at floating rates could gradually erode the lock-in; (c) job loss transmits to debt distress regardless of the mortgage rate. How durable is this buffer?

4. **What is the Minsky classification of the private credit complex ($1.8T)?** The opacity of private credit — mark-to-model, PIK toggle prevalence, undefined manager-lender conflicts — makes it impossible to assess from public data whether this is 20% Ponzi or 50% Ponzi. This is the critical unknown for crisis risk assessment: if a significant share is Ponzi financing disguised as performing credit, a marginal tightening of conditions could trigger a cascade that the Kalecki profit floor cannot contain.

5. **Does the fiscal supply channel on term premium operate differently under QT than under QE?** The KB identifies a structural break around QE regimes (confidence 8). The current QT regime is too short to establish the new coefficients empirically. If the term premium sensitivity to fiscal supply is *higher* under QT (because the marginal buyer is now a price-sensitive private investor rather than a price-insensitive central bank), the doom loop dynamics may be more intense than historical estimates suggest.

6. **How would a Minsky moment propagate given the bifurcated fragility structure?** The household sector is robust (hedge financing), corporate credit is mixed, and leveraged financial strategies are extreme Ponzi. A crisis originating in the basis trade or private credit might not transmit to the household sector at all — producing a *financial* crisis without a *real economy* recession. This would be unprecedented and suggests the standard recession playbook (Fed cuts → housing recovery → consumer spending recovery) may not apply because the housing sector was never the problem.

7. **What would Godley's sectoral balance model predict for a DOGE-scale fiscal consolidation of 2-3pp of GDP?** The identity requires the private sector surplus to shrink by the same amount (if the current account is unchanged). Does this manifest as: (a) higher household borrowing (a return to the pre-GFC fragility pattern); (b) lower corporate investment (profit squeeze → investment pullback); (c) rising unemployment (income decline → lower saving by reducing income rather than increasing borrowing)? The composition of the adjustment determines whether the outcome is a Minsky crisis (a), a Kaleckian profit recession (b), or a Keynesian demand deficiency recession (c). All are contractionary, but with different financial stability implications.

8. **Is the 2Y/30Y correlation bifurcation with equities a leading or coincident indicator of fiscal dominance?** If the long end is *already* in the fiscal dominance regime while the front end retains monetary autonomy, is this a stable split or a transitional state? The Post-Keynesian prediction is that fiscal dominance deepens — but the timeline (Japan: decades; UK: days) is radically uncertain and depends on institutional factors (Fed credibility, Congressional behavior, reserve currency status) that are not amenable to quantitative modeling.

9. **What is the interaction between AI investment and the Kalecki profit equation?** Hyperscaler capex of $200B+ annually is a major positive term in the "Investment" component of the Kalecki identity. If AI capex decelerates (return disappointment, hyperscaler discipline), this removes a profit support that is independent of the fiscal channel. Conversely, if AI capex accelerates, it partially substitutes for fiscal demand support, potentially allowing deficit reduction without a proportionate profit decline. The direction and magnitude of AI capex may be the most important *non-fiscal* variable in the profit equation.

10. **How should the IRA rollback scenario be modeled through sectoral balances?** The KB knowledge gap on "IRA rollback under DOGE would remove 800kt-1.2Mt of incremental copper demand from 2028-2032" represents a real fiscal impulse withdrawal with specific sectoral and commodity-market consequences. Through the Kalecki lens, IRA spending is high-multiplier public investment (~1.2-1.8x) — its removal would produce a larger demand contraction per dollar than equivalent cuts to transfer spending or interest payments. The copper demand effect is a second-order consequence of the first-order Kaleckian demand withdrawal.
