# Fiscal Policy — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Fiscal supply has displaced monetary policy as the marginal price-setter for duration beyond 5 years.** The variance decomposition of 10Y+ yields has shifted: term premium now accounts for ~60-70% of yield moves at the long end, up from ~20-30% during the QE era (2010-2021). The $2-2.5T annual gross Treasury issuance schedule is the single largest structural force on the term structure.

2. **The yield curve is encoding a fiscal-monetary regime conflict through a structural bear-steepening bias.** The front end (2Y) reflects Fed policy expectations (~4.0-4.25%), while the long end (30Y) incorporates a fiscal supply premium that prevents the rally a traditional easing cycle would deliver. The 2s10s slope (~+30-60bp) and especially the 10s30s (~+30-40bp) are steeper than pure expectations models justify, with the excess attributable to fiscal term premium.

3. **The 5y5y forward rate (~4.0-4.5%) is contaminated by fiscal term premium and overstates the market's neutral rate estimate by 50-75bp.** Adjusting for the ACM term premium component embedded in 5y5y forwards, the market-implied neutral short rate is closer to 3.25-3.75%, not the headline 4.0-4.5%. Policymakers and strategists using raw 5y5y as a neutral rate proxy are systematically overestimating where the market thinks rates settle.

4. **Treasury bill share at ~22% of outstanding debt is a term premium suppression mechanism operating on borrowed time.** The 2023-24 maturity shortening compressed term premium an estimated 30-50bp by shifting supply away from duration-sensitive investors. This creates rollover concentration risk: ~$5-6T in bills reprices within 12 months, and any disruption (debt ceiling, auction failure, money fund outflows) forces rapid coupon issuance that reverses the compression.

5. **Basis trade positioning ($800B-$1T notional) creates synthetic demand that masks true private-sector term premium by 15-30bp.** Leveraged basis trades buy cash Treasuries and sell futures, absorbing duration that would otherwise require a higher term premium to clear. The "true" term premium — net of leveraged arbitrage demand — is materially higher than ACM or Kim-Wright models report.

6. **ACM and Kim-Wright term premium models are diverging by 30-50bp at the 10Y point, and the divergence itself signals fiscal regime uncertainty.** ACM shows term premium of ~50-75bp; Kim-Wright reads lower at ~20-50bp. The spread between models widens when the yield curve driver mix shifts — this divergence is characteristic of periods where the relative weight of fiscal supply versus expectations is in flux.

7. **A 100bp term premium shock — plausible in a fiscal accident scenario — would trigger a cascading unwind through basis trades → repo market → cross-asset contagion, requiring Fed intervention of $1.5-2.5T.** This is larger than the $1.6T March 2020 intervention because the basis trade is 60-100% larger and leverage ratios are higher. The paradox: Fed intervention to contain the unwind would re-expand the balance sheet, partially validating fiscal dominance fears that triggered the shock.

8. **The curve is pricing neither full DOGE consolidation nor continued fiscal expansion, creating a fat-tailed bimodal distribution around current levels.** Options-implied volatility on 10Y Treasuries remains elevated (~120-140bp annualized rate vol) relative to realized, reflecting the market's inability to resolve whether the fiscal trajectory bends toward consolidation (bull flattener) or continued expansion (bear steepener). Breakeven straddles on the 10Y are attractively priced relative to the range of fiscal outcomes.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Fiscal supply as marginal price-setter:**
The ACM term premium has swung from -50bp (2020) to +50-100bp (early 2026), a 100-150bp move. Over the same period, expected short-rate paths have moved less dramatically. The KB documents the non-linear fiscal supply channel: 20-40bp per 1pp deficit/GDP in pre-QE samples. At 6-7% deficit/GDP, well above the ~3-4% threshold for reserve currency sensitivity, the supply channel is operating in its steep portion. The declining indirect bidder share (72% → 65%) is the auction-level confirmation: the marginal buyer of duration is demanding more compensation. QT removing ~$60B/month in demand compounds the supply pressure. The regime break from the QE era — where the Fed was the marginal buyer and term premium was structurally suppressed — is now complete.

**Claim 2 — Bear-steepening bias from fiscal-monetary conflict:**
Traditional yield curve frameworks decompose steepening into either (a) expectations of rate cuts (bull steepener) or (b) rising inflation/growth expectations (bear steepener). The current steepening fits neither cleanly. The front end is anchored by a Fed on hold at ~4.25-4.50%, with markets pricing modest cuts. The long end is elevated not by inflation expectations (5Y5Y breakevens at ~2.3-2.5%, within range) but by duration supply that requires term premium compensation. This is a *fiscal bear steepener* — a category that barely existed pre-2022 because QE suppressed it. The 10s30s slope is the clearest expression: it captures the supply premium on the longest duration without front-end policy noise.

**Claim 3 — 5y5y contamination:**
The 5y5y forward is calculated from the 5Y and 10Y zero-coupon yields. Both contain term premium. If ACM term premium at the 10Y point is 50-75bp and at the 5Y point is 25-40bp, the term premium embedded in the 5y5y forward is approximately 75-110bp minus 25-40bp = 35-70bp. Subtracting this from the raw 5y5y forward (4.0-4.5%) yields an expectations-only neutral rate estimate of 3.3-3.8%. This matters because the 5y5y is the most-watched proxy for r* in rates markets. The fiscal contamination means the market is NOT signaling that neutral rates have permanently risen as much as the headline suggests.

**Claim 4 — Bill share as deferred term premium:**
The KB documents the 2023-24 Treasury maturity shift and its 30-50bp term premium compression. The mechanism is straightforward: bills are absorbed by money market funds (~$6T AUM) and bank reserve substitution, bypassing the duration-sensitive investor base that prices term premium. But T-bills at 22% of outstanding (up from ~15%) concentrate refinancing risk. At current short rates (~4.3-4.5%), the interest cost on bills is higher than coupons locked in at lower rates in prior years. The maturity shortening saves term premium today but accelerates the effective rate convergence toward marginal rates — the very dynamic the r-g transition concept describes. It's a temporal transfer, not a solution.

**Claim 5 — Basis trade term premium masking:**
Basis traders buy cash Treasuries (absorbing duration supply) and sell futures (transferring the duration to asset managers with different risk preferences). This intermediation is economically equivalent to additional demand at auction, suppressing yields by an estimated 15-30bp at current notional (~$800B-$1T). But this demand is fragile — dependent on financing conditions (repo rates stable, haircuts low) and low vol (VIX <20). A margin increase or repo rate spike forces position reduction, converting synthetic demand into selling pressure. The ACM model doesn't fully capture this because it uses Treasury yield data that already reflects basis trade absorption.

**Claim 6 — Model divergence:**
ACM is a 5-factor affine term structure model estimated on yield curve data plus macroeconomic variables. Kim-Wright uses a 3-factor model with survey expectations as an anchor. When fiscal supply dominates yield moves, ACM's yield-curve factors capture it as term premium, while Kim-Wright's survey anchor — which reflects economists' more optimistic view of fiscal trajectories — partially attributes the same yield level to higher expected short rates. The 30-50bp spread between models is itself diagnostic: it's largest when the curve is being driven by supply/technicals rather than macro fundamentals, which is exactly the current regime.

**Claim 7 — Cascading unwind arithmetic:**
The KB documents the March 2020 precedent: $1.6T in emergency Fed purchases to stabilize a basis trade unwind from a smaller (~$500B) starting position. The current position ($800B-$1T) at higher leverage (50-100x) implies a larger unwind. A 100bp term premium shock is within the distribution of outcomes: the UK gilt crisis delivered ~100bp in 4 days from a fiscal policy shock. If the U.S. experienced an analogous (albeit different) catalyst — a failed auction, a ratings downgrade, a debt ceiling crisis — the transmission would run: term premium spike → basis widens beyond no-arbitrage bounds → forced selling of cash Treasuries → repo market stress (higher haircuts, reduced capacity) → broader deleveraging. The Fed's intervention toolkit (SRF, standing repo, emergency QE) would need to be deployed at scale. The question from the KB — whether the Fed can intervene without reigniting inflation concerns — is the binding constraint on the intervention size.

**Claim 8 — Bimodal distribution:**
DOGE-style consolidation (the KB estimates 0.3-0.6x realization of announced savings) would reduce issuance, compress term premium, and flatten the curve — a bull flattener of potentially 50-100bp at the long end if credible. Continued fiscal expansion under the current trajectory would steepen further and push term premium higher. The market, unable to assign high probability to either, is pricing the mean — which is close to current levels — but with elevated variance. Swaption implied vols on 1Y10Y and 1Y30Y tails remain wide relative to realized, reflecting this irreducible uncertainty.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Fiscal supply as marginal price-setter | 8/10 | Multiple convergent indicators (term premium level, auction metrics, QT pace). The regime shift from QE-dominated to supply-dominated is well-established. Slight uncertainty on exact variance decomposition shares. |
| 2. Bear-steepening bias | 7/10 | Curve shape is observable and the fiscal attribution is well-supported. Reduced confidence because alternative drivers (global growth expectations, foreign demand shifts) also affect the long end. |
| 3. 5y5y contamination | 6/10 | The direction is almost certainly correct, but the magnitude (50-75bp) depends heavily on which term premium model you use. The Kim-Wright reading would give a smaller contamination estimate. This is model-dependent analysis. |
| 4. Bill share as deferred premium | 8/10 | The mechanism is mechanical and the arithmetic is clear. The KB evidence of 30-50bp compression from the maturity shift is well-documented. Uncertainty is in timing of any normalization, not in the mechanism. |
| 5. Basis trade masking | 5/10 | The direction is correct, but the 15-30bp estimate is back-of-envelope. Precise estimation requires knowledge of the marginal price elasticity of duration demand, which is itself uncertain. The OFR notional estimates carry measurement uncertainty. |
| 6. Model divergence as signal | 6/10 | This is an interpretation of model mechanics rather than an empirical finding. The divergence is real and measurable, but attributing it specifically to fiscal supply versus other drivers requires judgment. |
| 7. Cascading unwind | 5/10 | Conditional on a 100bp term premium shock, the transmission mechanism is well-validated (March 2020). But the probability of the trigger event is hard to assess, and post-2020 regulatory changes may alter the dynamics. The $1.5-2.5T intervention estimate is calibrated from one episode. Applying the KB's own confidence-calibration-regime-novelty principle: this is a novel-regime extrapolation that deserves reduced confidence. |
| 8. Bimodal distribution | 7/10 | Options-implied vol is directly observable. The interpretation that it reflects fiscal policy uncertainty specifically (rather than general macro uncertainty) is supported but not uniquely identified. |

**Aggregate confidence on fiscal-curve interaction framework: 7/10.** The core mechanism — fiscal supply → term premium → curve shape — is robust across models and time periods. Individual quantitative estimates (basis points of contamination, intervention sizes) carry wider error bars.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Yield Curve → Corporate Credit:**
The term premium level determines the all-in borrowing cost for long-dated corporate issuers. If term premium is structurally 50-100bp higher than the QE era, investment-grade corporate spreads that look "tight" on an OAS basis may be appropriate because the underlying risk-free rate already embeds fiscal risk. The spread analysis from the corporate credit KB concepts should be adjusted for the term premium regime shift. Tightness in IG credit spreads may partially reflect the Kalecki profit channel (fiscal deficits supporting corporate earnings), creating a paradoxical but internally consistent regime: fiscal policy simultaneously raises the risk-free rate and supports the creditworthiness of the borrower.

**Yield Curve → Real Estate Cycles:**
The KB concept `term_premium_cre_valuation_driver` is the most direct connection. My analysis reinforces and extends it: if the bill-share mechanism is deferring rather than eliminating term premium, and if the basis trade is masking 15-30bp of "true" term premium, then the effective term premium facing CRE valuations is even higher than models report. The CRE cap rate decompression documented in the KB (~4.5% → 6.0-6.5%) is consistent with a term premium regime shift, and the remaining adjustment (to the 6.5-7.5% equilibrium implied by the 200-300bp spread over 10Y) reflects the market's expectation that the current term premium level persists.

**Yield Curve → Monetary Policy Transmission:**
The bear-steepening bias creates an unusual monetary policy transmission problem. If the Fed cuts rates to support growth but the long end doesn't rally (because fiscal supply keeps term premium elevated), then easing doesn't reduce mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, or CRE discount rates as effectively as in prior cycles. This is the yield curve signature of early-stage fiscal dominance: monetary policy loses traction at the long end. The 5y5y contamination finding compounds this — if the Fed is interpreting raw 5y5y as a neutral rate signal, it may be calibrating policy to a neutral rate that is 50-75bp higher than the true expectations component.

**Yield Curve → Deglobalization & Trade:**
European defense spending ramps (mentioned in `g7_fiscal_divergence_extreme`) would increase sovereign supply in Bunds and OATs, potentially narrowing the US-Europe term premium differential. This has cross-currency basis swap implications: narrower yield differentials reduce the incentive for European and Japanese investors to buy hedged USTs, removing a demand source and further elevating US term premium. The deglobalization of sovereign bond markets is a slow-moving structural force on the curve.

**Yield Curve → Dollar & FX:**
The G7 fiscal divergence concept maps directly to curve differentials. The US running 6-7% deficits while Europe runs 2-3% creates a structurally wider US term premium, which historically supports the dollar through carry. But the KB notes the current mix (Fed on hold, wide deficits) maps closer to the 2001-04 dollar-weak pattern. From a curve perspective, the key distinction is whether the fiscal premium attracts capital (dollar-positive, as in 1981-85 when Volcker tightening complemented Reagan deficits) or repels it (dollar-negative, when markets begin pricing fiscal sustainability risk). The current regime is ambiguous, which is why the dollar is range-bound.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the "true" term premium net of basis trade synthetic demand?** The 15-30bp estimate is a rough calibration. A precise answer requires microstructure data on basis trade flows and their price elasticity that is not publicly available. The OFR and Fed are the only entities with the data to answer this definitively.

2. **At what term premium level does the doom loop become self-reinforcing?** The KB identifies the mechanism but not the threshold for the U.S. specifically. The UK gilt crisis activated at ~100bp of term premium shock on ~100% debt/GDP. The U.S. at ~124% debt/GDP with reserve currency status may have a higher threshold, but there is no empirical basis for estimating it precisely. The range could be 150-300bp — far too wide for risk management.

3. **How would the Fed respond to a term premium shock that threatens financial stability but is driven by fiscal (not monetary) factors?** Emergency QE would address the symptom (Treasury market dysfunction) but worsen the cause (fiscal dominance perception). A standing repo facility could provide liquidity without expanding the balance sheet, but may be insufficient if the shock is fundamentally about solvency/sustainability concerns. This is the central policy dilemma and it is unresolved.

4. **Is the 5y5y term premium contamination stable or growing?** If fiscal deficits remain at 6-7% GDP and Treasury issuance stays at $2-2.5T/year, the fiscal supply component of term premium should remain elevated or grow. But if the DOGE consolidation achieves even 30-60% of its targets, the contamination could shrink. The 5y5y forward would then decline, and the market would re-read this as falling neutral rate expectations — when it's actually falling term premium. Misattribution risk runs in both directions.

5. **Does the maturity composition of new issuance matter more than the total?** The KB treats total issuance as the key variable, but from a curve perspective, $2T in bills has radically different term premium implications than $2T in 10Y+ coupons. The Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee's quarterly refunding decisions may be a more important curve driver than the aggregate deficit number. This suggests monitoring TBAC recommendations and QRA announcements as leading indicators for term premium, not just CBO deficit projections.

6. **Can the basis trade grow large enough to permanently suppress term premium, or is there a natural limit?** If basis trade arbitrage is suppressing 15-30bp of term premium, it's performing a quasi-public function (lowering government borrowing costs). But the leverage and fragility make it a poor substitute for genuine demand. Is there a size at which regulatory constraints (supplementary leverage ratio, initial margin) cap growth? The answer determines whether the current term premium level is a "managed" equilibrium or a genuinely market-clearing price.

7. **What is the cross-country term premium elasticity of substitution?** If European fiscal expansion (defense spending) raises Bund term premium, does this mechanically raise or lower UST term premium? Standard portfolio theory suggests substitution effects (capital flowing to higher-returning Bunds) would raise UST term premium, but complementarity effects (global rate rise reducing aggregate demand for duration) could lower it. The direction matters for the G7 fiscal divergence trade.
