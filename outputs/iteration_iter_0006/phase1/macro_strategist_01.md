# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are endogenous to the monetary policy cycle, not exogenous shocks.** The primary driver of regime shifts between risk-on and risk-off is the stance of monetary policy relative to the natural rate of interest (r*). When real policy rates sit persistently below r*, accommodative financial conditions compress risk premia and push portfolio allocations toward higher-beta assets, generating a structurally risk-on regime. The reverse produces risk-off.

2. **The "risk-taking channel" of monetary policy (Borio & Zhu, 2012) is the transmission mechanism linking policy rates to risk appetite regimes.** Low rates affect risk appetite through three sub-channels: (a) search-for-yield behavior as fixed-income returns fall below liability-driven targets, (b) reduced volatility of asset prices under accommodative conditions which mechanically lowers measured risk (VaR constraints loosen), and (c) improved borrower balance sheets via asset price appreciation, which relaxes collateral constraints and enables further leverage.

3. **Risk appetite regimes exhibit asymmetric transition dynamics: the shift from risk-on to risk-off is faster and more violent than the reverse.** This asymmetry is consistent with New Keynesian models incorporating occasionally binding financial constraints (Brunnermeier & Sannikov, 2014). During risk-on phases, leverage builds gradually as agents optimize against a perceived low-volatility environment. The transition to risk-off occurs when a shock—often a policy tightening that crosses a threshold—triggers margin calls and forced deleveraging, producing nonlinear amplification.

4. **The output gap is a necessary but insufficient predictor of risk appetite regime shifts; the interaction between the output gap and the policy rate gap (r minus r*) is what identifies transition points.** A closing output gap alone does not trigger risk-off; it is the central bank's reaction—tightening toward or above r*—that withdraws the monetary accommodation fueling risk appetite. This is why late-cycle environments with patient central banks (e.g., 2017-2018) can sustain risk-on regimes even as slack diminishes.

5. **Current macro conditions (Q1 2026) suggest an intermediate risk appetite regime—neither full risk-on nor risk-off—consistent with a mid-cycle transition where policy rates are near but modestly above estimated r*, output gaps are small, and inflation is converging toward but not yet at target in several major economies.** Financial conditions indices are roughly neutral, neither stimulative nor restrictive.

6. **Fiscal policy stance conditions the durability of risk appetite regimes by altering the IS curve and hence the equilibrium interest rate.** Expansionary fiscal policy in a near-full-employment economy raises r*, requiring higher policy rates to maintain the same degree of restriction. This fiscal-monetary interaction can either sustain risk-on (if fiscal expansion outpaces monetary tightening) or accelerate risk-off (if central banks respond aggressively to fiscal-driven inflation pressure).

7. **Cross-country dispersion in risk appetite regimes has increased since the pandemic, reflecting desynchronized business cycles and divergent central bank reaction functions.** The Mundell-Fleming framework predicts that under floating exchange rates, monetary policy independence allows distinct risk appetite regimes across economies—capital flows and exchange rate adjustments serve as the equilibrating mechanism.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 — Monetary Policy as the Primary Driver

The empirical regularity is well-documented: the Goldman Sachs Financial Conditions Index, the Chicago Fed's National Financial Conditions Index (NFCI), and the ECB's Composite Indicator of Systemic Stress all move in close tandem with the policy rate gap. Adrian & Shin (2010) demonstrate that broker-dealer leverage—a direct proxy for risk appetite—expands when short rates decline and contracts when they rise. Miranda-Agrippino & Rey (2020) identify a "Global Financial Cycle" in capital flows, asset prices, and credit growth that is primarily driven by US monetary policy, reinforcing that risk appetite is not randomly distributed but systematically generated by the policy stance of the dominant central bank. The New Keynesian IS curve, where output depends on the gap between the real rate and the natural rate, provides the structural link: accommodative policy raises output above potential, improving corporate earnings and credit quality, which compresses spreads and raises equity valuations—the observable manifestations of risk-on.

### Claim 2 — The Risk-Taking Channel

Borio & Zhu's (2012) framework, subsequently formalized in DSGE models by Angeloni, Faia & Lo Duca (2015), shows that the standard interest rate channel and credit channel are insufficient to explain the amplitude of financial cycles. The risk-taking channel operates through: (i) institutional investors with nominal return targets (pension funds, insurance companies) who must move down the credit quality spectrum when safe yields fall—this is mechanically observable in flow-of-funds data; (ii) VaR-based risk management where low realized volatility (an endogenous consequence of stable policy) widens permissible position sizes; (iii) the financial accelerator mechanism (Bernanke, Gertler & Gilchrist, 1999) where rising collateral values relax borrowing constraints and enable further risk-taking. Each sub-channel has been independently validated empirically.

### Claim 3 — Asymmetric Transitions

The skewness of equity returns, the behavior of the VIX term structure (contango in risk-on, backwardation in risk-off), and the speed of credit spread widening versus tightening all confirm asymmetry. From a modeling perspective, Brunnermeier & Sannikov (2014) show that in a continuous-time macro-finance model, the economy spends most of its time in a "tranquil" region (risk-on) but occasionally visits an "crisis" region (risk-off) where amplification mechanisms—fire sales, margin spirals, liquidity hoarding—produce rapid, self-reinforcing deterioration. The New Keynesian framework's standard log-linearization around steady state misses this nonlinearity, which is why risk appetite regime analysis requires models with occasionally binding constraints or regime-switching specifications (Hamilton, 1989).

### Claim 4 — Output Gap × Policy Rate Gap Interaction

Consider the counterexample: in 2017, the US output gap was closing and unemployment was near NAIRU estimates (~4.4%), yet risk appetite remained robust—S&P 500 realized volatility hit multi-decade lows. The explanation: the Fed was tightening gradually, but the real federal funds rate remained below most r* estimates (Laubach-Williams r* was ~0.5%, the real FFR was approximately 0.0-0.4%). The policy rate gap was therefore still negative-to-neutral, maintaining accommodative financial conditions. Contrast this with late 2018, when cumulative tightening pushed the real FFR above several r* estimates, triggering an abrupt risk-off episode (Q4 2018 selloff). The output gap had not materially changed—what changed was the policy rate gap crossing into restrictive territory.

### Claim 5 — Current Regime Assessment (Q1 2026)

As of early 2026, the Fed has likely eased modestly from peak rates, with the policy rate corridor in the 3.75-4.25% range, against core PCE running in the mid-to-upper 2% range, implying a real rate of roughly 1.5-1.75%. Laubach-Williams and related r* estimates have drifted upward modestly (toward 1.0-1.5%) reflecting structurally higher fiscal deficits and possibly higher trend productivity from AI-adjacent capital deepening. This places the real rate near or modestly above r*—a neutral-to-slightly-restrictive stance. Financial conditions reflect this: neither the extreme compression of risk premia seen in 2021 nor the acute stress of 2022. Credit spreads are moderate, equity volatility is neither suppressed nor elevated, and cross-asset correlations are not at regime extremes. This is a transitional regime where directionality depends on incoming data—particularly whether inflation continues to converge and whether the labor market softens enough to justify further easing.

### Claim 6 — Fiscal-Monetary Interaction

The IS-LM framework makes this transparent: fiscal expansion shifts the IS curve rightward, raising the equilibrium interest rate for any given LM stance. If the central bank accommodates (holds rates fixed), output rises and financial conditions ease—risk-on is sustained or intensified. If the central bank follows a Taylor Rule and raises rates to offset the inflationary impulse, the LM curve shifts left, partially or fully crowding out the fiscal stimulus and tightening financial conditions. The current environment of elevated fiscal deficits in the US (~6% of GDP structurally) with a Fed that has signaled data-dependent policy creates an unstable equilibrium: risk appetite is sustained as long as the market believes the Fed will accommodate, but any shift toward a more hawkish reaction function would reintroduce the crowding-out channel.

### Claim 7 — Cross-Country Divergence

Post-pandemic, the timing of inflation peaks, the aggressiveness of tightening cycles, and the pace of subsequent easing have diverged significantly across the US, Eurozone, Japan, and EM economies. Japan's exit from YCC and negative rates in 2024-2025 occurred while the Fed was beginning to ease—a rare desynchronization. Under Mundell-Fleming with floating rates, this produces yen appreciation pressure and capital repatriation, altering risk appetite dynamics within the Japanese financial system independently of global conditions. Similarly, divergent EM monetary cycles (Brazil tightening while others ease) create dispersion in local risk appetite regimes, visible in local-currency bond spreads and equity beta.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary policy transmission**: Risk appetite regimes are arguably the most market-relevant dimension of monetary transmission—more direct than the textbook interest rate channel for asset allocation.
- **Credit cycles and financial stability**: Risk-on regimes generate credit expansion and leverage buildup; the transition to risk-off triggers the deleveraging dynamics that create systemic risk. Connects directly to macroprudential policy design.
- **Currency regimes and capital flows**: Under Mundell-Fleming, risk appetite regimes propagate internationally through capital account flows; the "global financial cycle" thesis (Rey, 2013) challenges the trilemma by arguing that risk appetite transmission occurs regardless of exchange rate regime.
- **Inflation dynamics**: The Phillips Curve mechanism operates partially through risk appetite—tight financial conditions reduce aggregate demand and thus dis-inflationary pressure, while loose conditions stimulate demand and cost-push via commodity price appreciation.
- **Secular stagnation / r-star debates**: If r* has structurally declined (Summers, 2014; Rachel & Summers, 2019), then achieving a neutral-to-accommodative policy stance requires lower nominal rates, which means the risk-taking channel operates more intensely for any given level of policy accommodation—potentially explaining the perceived increase in financial cycle amplitude since the GFC.
- **Equity factor premia**: Risk appetite regimes are a primary driver of factor rotation—value and momentum factors have regime-dependent payoffs (momentum crashes cluster at risk-off transitions, per Daniel & Moskowitz, 2016).

## Open Questions

1. **Has r* shifted upward sufficiently to change the risk appetite regime map?** If structurally higher deficits and AI-driven productivity growth have raised r* by 50-100bp, the current policy rate may be less restrictive than traditional estimates suggest, implying a more risk-on neutral than surface readings indicate. Verification requires several more years of data.

2. **Does QT (quantitative tightening) operate on risk appetite through the same channels as rate policy, or is it a distinct mechanism?** The portfolio balance channel suggests QT should drain duration from private portfolios and raise term premia, tightening financial conditions. But the empirical evidence on QT's impact on risk appetite is limited and contested—the 2017-2019 QT episode is a single, confounded observation.

3. **How should we model the interaction between AI/technology-driven productivity shocks and risk appetite?** A genuine positive supply shock raises potential output and r*, but also compresses equity risk premia through higher expected earnings growth. The net effect on risk appetite is ambiguous within the standard framework.

4. **What is the role of passive investment flows and options market structure in altering regime transition dynamics?** The growth of systematic volatility-selling strategies and zero-day-to-expiry options may have altered the microstructure of risk appetite regime transitions in ways that the macro-financial models have not yet incorporated.

5. **Can central bank communication (forward guidance) sustain a risk-on regime even when the policy rate gap has turned restrictive?** The 2023-2024 experience—where equities rallied despite restrictive real rates, partially on the expectation of a "Fed pivot"—challenges the simple policy-rate-gap framework and suggests that expected future policy paths may dominate current policy levels in determining risk appetite.
