### Claim 5: Multi-state taxonomy as analytical trap

HC-4 asserts that binary RORO is "empirically inadequate" and a minimum of three regimes is required. HC-8 further differentiates with Minsky phases. Combined, the framework posits:

- 3 macro regimes (risk-on, deflationary risk-off, stagflationary) × 4 Minsky phases (hedge, speculative, Ponzi, crisis) = 12 possible states
- 4 credit-specific phases (compression, complacency, repricing, distress) as an additional overlay

The analytical question is not whether this taxonomy is *correct* (it likely is) but whether it is *useful*. Usefulness requires:

1. **Identifiability:** Can you determine which of the 12 states you are in, in real time? The panel itself admits timing confidence of only 4/10 even for the simpler directional assessment.
2. **Predictive power:** Does classifying the current state improve your forecast of future states? No evidence has been presented that multi-state classification outperforms simple momentum or mean-reversion heuristics on out-of-sample data.
3. **Actionability:** Does knowing you're in "stagflationary-speculative" vs. "risk-on-complacency" change your portfolio construction? The panel's own HC-9 suggests no single indicator predicts transitions well, meaning the multi-state classification doesn't help you time the shift.

The danger: the framework gives analysts the *feeling* of understanding without the *reality* of prediction. This is worse than admitting ignorance, because it creates false confidence in positioning.
