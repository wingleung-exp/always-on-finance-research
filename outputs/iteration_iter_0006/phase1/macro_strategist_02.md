# Risk Appetite Regimes — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are not exogenous psychological states but endogenous products of credit dynamics.** What market practitioners label "risk-on" and "risk-off" are emergent properties of the Minsky cycle — periods of expanding leverage and compressing risk premia (hedge → speculative → Ponzi financing) versus disorderly deleveraging when the structure inverts.

2. **The sectoral financial balances identity governs the sustainability of any risk appetite regime.** A prolonged risk-on regime requires *someone* to run a deficit that funds *someone else's* surplus. When the private sector as a whole is running deficits to finance speculative asset acquisition, the regime is inherently fragile regardless of what sentiment surveys report.

3. **Credit impulse — the change in the change of credit — is the leading causal driver of risk appetite regime transitions, not volatility itself.** Volatility is a symptom. The turn in credit impulse (from positive to negative second derivative) typically leads the shift from risk-on to risk-off by 2–4 quarters, as Minsky's framework would predict: the reversal of credit growth exposes Ponzi units first.

4. **Contagion velocity in modern market structure has been amplified by the shift from bank-intermediated to market-based credit.** The "originate-to-distribute" model means credit risk is dispersed but *liquidity risk* is concentrated in dealer balance sheets and open-ended fund structures. Risk-off transitions are faster than in bank-dominated eras because redemption cascades force mark-to-market losses in ways that bank loan books historically absorbed.

5. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades are a balance-sheet phenomenon, not a behavioral one.** The standard finance explanation that panic causes illiquidity reverses causation. In the Minskyan framework, the *balance sheet structure* (maturity mismatch, leverage ratios, margin/collateral haircut pro-cyclicality) mechanically forces asset sales when funding conditions tighten. The "flight to quality" is a portfolio rebalancing forced by collateral constraints, not a psychological flight.

6. **Government sector balance positions act as the stabilizing (or destabilizing) anchor for risk appetite regimes.** Per Godley's sectoral balances, a government running surpluses necessarily drains net financial assets from the non-government sector, increasing private sector fragility and shortening the Minsky cycle. Conversely, sustained fiscal deficits provide the income flows that validate private debt structures and extend risk-on regimes — until inflation constraints bind.

7. **The current (early 2026) risk appetite regime is in late-stage speculative territory in the U.S. private sector, with corporate leverage ratios elevated and household wealth-to-income ratios at historically extreme levels.** The sectoral balance configuration — narrowing government deficit, persistent current account deficit — is compressing the private sector's capacity to sustain net saving, which is inconsistent with the continued risk-on posture in asset markets.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Endogeneity of risk appetite:**
Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis (1986, *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*) demonstrates that stability itself is destabilizing. During prolonged expansions, success emboldens borrowers and lenders alike: cash-flow margins of safety are eroded, hedge units become speculative, speculative become Ponzi. What appears as "rising risk appetite" in market-speak is the mechanical consequence of validated expectations breeding additional leverage. The causality runs from credit structure to sentiment, not the reverse. The evidence is the robust correlation between credit growth acceleration and risk premia compression (e.g., high-yield spreads tighten as leveraged loan issuance accelerates, not independently of it).

**Claim 2 — Sectoral balances constraint:**
The accounting identity (Private Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance ≡ 0) is not a theory — it is a tautology that must hold. Godley and Lavoie (2007, *Monetary Economics*) built stock-flow consistent models demonstrating that any configuration where the private sector runs persistent deficits necessarily implies rising private debt-to-income ratios, which are unsustainable. The "Great Moderation" risk-on regime of 2003–2007 was sustained by household deficit spending (mortgage credit expansion) that was only possible because the government was running modest deficits and the current account was extracting demand. The Kalecki profit equation further shows that corporate profits depend on investment, government deficits, and net exports — risk appetite in equity markets is thus directly downstream of fiscal flows.

**Claim 3 — Credit impulse as leading indicator:**
The credit impulse (defined as change in new credit as % of GDP) has empirically led turning points in GDP and risk asset performance. Biggs, Mayer, and Pick (2010) formalized this. From the Post-Keynesian perspective, this is because in an endogenous money system, bank lending *creates* deposits and purchasing power — the impulse of new money creation directly funds demand. When credit impulse turns negative, spending slows before income does, creating the cash-flow gaps that expose fragile balance sheets. This precedes VIX spikes and credit spread widening, not the reverse.

**Claim 4 — Market-based credit and contagion velocity:**
The shift from bank-held loans to tradable securities (CLOs, corporate bond ETFs, money market funds holding CP) means that credit conditions can tighten via mark-to-market channels faster than through traditional bank credit rationing. The March 2020 episode showed this: corporate bond market dysfunction transmitted to the real economy within days via a spike in commercial paper rates and a freeze in primary issuance. Dealer balance sheet constraints (post-Volcker Rule/Basel III leverage ratios) mean that market-making capacity evaporates precisely when it is most needed, pro-cyclically amplifying the regime shift.

**Claim 5 — Balance sheet mechanics of liquidity withdrawal:**
Brunnermeier and Pedersen (2009) document the "margin spiral" and "loss spiral" — mechanisms where declining asset prices raise margin requirements, force sales, further depress prices. Adrian and Shin (2010) show that financial intermediary leverage is pro-cyclical: intermediaries *expand* balance sheets in upswings and *contract* them in downswings, amplifying both risk-on and risk-off. These are structural, not behavioral, dynamics. The collateral frameworks of central clearing and repo markets mechanically increase haircuts when volatility rises, forcing de-risking regardless of any individual agent's "appetite."

**Claim 6 — Fiscal balance as anchor:**
Kalecki's profit equation (Profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports − Household Saving) shows that fiscal deficits directly support corporate cash flows and therefore the capacity to service debt. The Clinton-era surplus (late 1990s) coincided with the U.S. private sector moving into unprecedented deficit — the dot-com bust was the Minsky moment that followed. Similarly, Euro-area austerity (2010–2014) forced the private sector into surplus via income deflation, producing a balance sheet recession. The lesson: government surpluses during speculative booms *shorten* the cycle by draining the net financial assets that private balance sheets require.

**Claim 7 — Current regime assessment:**
U.S. nonfinancial business debt-to-GDP remains near post-WWII highs (~78%). Household net worth-to-disposable income sits above 800%, driven by equity and real estate valuations rather than income growth — a classic Ponzi-adjacent configuration where wealth depends on asset price appreciation rather than income flows. The federal deficit, while still substantial (~6% of GDP), has been framed politically as needing reduction, introducing fiscal consolidation risk. The current account deficit (~3.5% of GDP) continuously drains domestic demand. The sectoral balance arithmetic: if the government deficit narrows and the current account deficit persists, the private sector *must* reduce its surplus or go into deficit. If this is achieved via credit expansion, the Minsky clock accelerates. If via income contraction, risk-off arrives through the demand channel.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Credit Cycles (credit_cycles, confidence: 6.0):** Risk appetite regimes are *the cross-asset expression* of the credit cycle. The Minsky classification (hedge/speculative/Ponzi) is the credit cycle theory. This topic should be treated as the transmitter that connects credit_cycles to asset prices. The credit impulse indicator bridges directly.

- **Volatility Regimes (volatility_regimes, confidence: 3.0):** Volatility is the *output* of risk appetite regime transitions, not the input. Low-vol regimes correspond to the hedge-to-speculative phase; vol spikes correspond to the Ponzi-to-deleveraging phase. The VIX is a thermometer, not a thermostat. Analysis of volatility regimes that doesn't ground in credit dynamics will misidentify causation.

- **Equity Cycles (equity_cycles, confidence: 5.0):** Equity valuations are downstream of the Kalecki profit equation and thus of fiscal and credit dynamics. Risk appetite regimes in equities reflect the sustainability of the profit sources — primarily government deficits and corporate investment. When risk appetite shifts, it is because the income flows validating equity valuations are being withdrawn.

- **FX Regimes (fx_regimes, confidence: 4.0):** The "foreign balance" leg of the sectoral identity is the current account, which is the FX channel. Dollar risk appetite (carry trades, EM flows) is a direct extension of the global Minsky cycle. Dollar strength during risk-off episodes reflects the unwinding of dollar-denominated liabilities globally (as per Shin's "global dollar" framework — essentially a Minskyan insight applied to the international monetary system).

- **Fiscal Policy / Fiscal Dominance (structural theme):** The most critical connection. Whether the government sector acts as automatic stabilizer or pro-cyclical destabilizer (austerity during downturns) determines whether the Minsky cycle produces a recession or a depression. MMT-adjacent analysis (Wray, Kelton) emphasizes that a currency-issuing government faces no solvency constraint, only inflation constraint — meaning fiscal space to arrest risk-off cascades is always available if politically chosen.

- **Central Bank Balance Sheets:** The post-GFC innovation of central bank backstops (LSAP, standing facilities, swap lines) has created a *Minsky super-cycle* by truncating the deleveraging phase. Each intervention prevents full balance sheet repair, meaning the next cycle begins from a higher leverage base. This is "stability breeding instability" at the institutional level.
