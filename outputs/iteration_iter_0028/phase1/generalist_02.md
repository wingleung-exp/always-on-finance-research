### Claim 5 — CLO Reinvestment Expiry as SIV/ABCP Analogue

**ANALOGUE DECOMPOSITION: SIV/ABCP Crisis (2007)**

SIVs held ~$400B in structured credit, funded by short-term ABCP. When ABCP investors refused to roll (August 2007), SIVs became forced sellers. The key mechanism: SIVs were price-insensitive *buyers* during the accumulation phase (absorbing ~55% of structured credit supply), then became forced *sellers* when funding failed. This buyer-to-seller flip created discontinuous spread widening.

**CLO parallel:** CLOs in their reinvestment period are contractually obligated buyers — they must reinvest principal proceeds. CLOs post-reinvestment are contractually constrained to passive run-off. The $250-350B in 2020-2022 vintage CLOs entering run-off in 2025-2027 represents a structural flip from buyer to non-participant. Unlike SIVs, CLOs don't become forced *sellers* (no rollover risk), but they remove the marginal bid for 60-70% of institutional leveraged loan demand.

**Key difference that limits the analogue:** SIV failure was driven by *funding* risk (ABCP rollover). CLO run-off is driven by *contractual* terms (reinvestment period expiry). The CLO dynamic is slower, more predictable, and doesn't create fire-sale dynamics the way SIV liquidation did. However, the *volume effect* — removing the buyer for 60-70% of marginal demand — is arguably comparable in magnitude to the SIV funding freeze.

**What the analogue predicts:** Not a sudden crisis but a gradual, accelerating widening in leveraged loan spreads as primary issuance finds fewer buyers. The historical SIV analogue suggests the transition from "less demand" to "no bid" can happen quickly once it begins — in 2007, ABCP markets went from stressed to frozen in ~6 weeks. The CLO version would be slower but could accelerate if coincident with maturity wall refinancing demand.

**How this alters analogue outcomes:**
