# Monetary Policy Transmission — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The current policy configuration (simultaneous rate cuts + QT, impaired transmission across 5+ channels, partial fiscal dominance at >100% debt/GDP) has no single clean historical analogue, but decomposes into five overlapping episode clusters with a combined base rate strongly favoring extended ambiguity followed by a forcing event, not a smooth normalization.** Similarity scores: Greenspan Conundrum 2004-2006 (0.74), Great Inflation Genesis 1966-1969 (0.68), 1994-1999 Dollar/EM Sequence (0.62), 2018-2019 QT Episode (0.58 for QT-specific dynamics, but 0.82 for narrow repo market mechanics), 1942-1951 Treasury-Fed Accord Period (0.45 overall, but 0.72 for the fiscal dominance resolution pathway).

2. **The base rate for "broken transmission" episodes resolving via sudden discontinuous catch-up rather than gradual normalization is 3-for-4 in the post-1970 record.** 1966-69 (transmission accumulated then arrived via 1969-70 recession + 6% inflation), 2004-07 (Greenspan conundrum resolved via catastrophic credit crisis), 2018-19 (QT "autopilot" resolved via September 2019 repo spike). The exception is the 1994-95 mid-cycle adjustment, where the Fed tightened preemptively enough to avoid accumulation. The critical implication: the current extended transmission lag (36+ months, only +0.3pp unemployment) makes the eventual catch-up more likely to be discontinuous, not less.

3. **The base rate for central banks successfully running simultaneous easing + balance sheet reduction is 0-for-3 in global history (Fed 2019, BoJ 2006-2007, ECB 2011-2012 passive tightening via LTRO non-renewal), with average time-to-abandonment of 7 months.** The current Fed attempt has persisted ~9 months (September 2024 first cut to March 2026), already exceeding the historical average, but QT has been slowed from $60B to $25B/month — a de facto partial abandonment that the base rate would predict.

4. **ECB-Fed divergence of the current magnitude (~150-200bp in opposite directions with structural EUR weakness from LNG-USD demand) has three historical analogues, and the base rate for the divergence being sustained beyond 18 months without a forcing crisis is 1-for-3.** Analogues: 1992 ERM crisis (divergence forced collapse), 2011-2012 ECB/Fed divergence (resolved via OMT fiscal backstop), 2014-2018 gradual divergence (sustained, the 1-for-3). The LNG-EUR/USD structural channel (~$40-50B/yr USD demand) is genuinely novel and has no historical parallel — it functions as a permanent drag coefficient on ECB independence that compounds during energy shocks.

5. **BoJ normalization attempts have a 0-for-2 completion rate in the post-bubble era (2000 ZIRP exit reversed within 19 months, 2006-2007 tightening cycle abandoned at 0.50% after 2 hikes), and the current attempt faces the additional structural headwind of USD/JPY above 150 creating a repatriation trigger for ~$3.5T in Japanese foreign portfolio holdings.** The 1990 BoJ tightening (bubble burst) is an anti-analogue — it "succeeded" in the narrow sense of transmitting but caused a lost decade. The signpost for distinguishing "successful normalization" from "forced reversal" is whether Japanese institutional repatriation exceeds 30% of model-predicted levels, the historical ceiling.

6. **Gold's $400-600/oz residual above real-rate-implied levels maps most closely to 1968-1971 (Bretton Woods strain) and 1977-1980 (Fed credibility crisis), with a base rate of 2-for-2 that such residuals eventually resolve via regime change rather than mean-reversion to the prior real-rate relationship.** In both prior episodes, the residual was a leading indicator of structural monetary regime transition by 3-5 years. If the analogy holds, the current residual signals that markets are pricing a probability distribution over financial repression, not a cyclical real-rate trade.

7. **The 2025-2027 corporate maturity wall ($2.5T) has the closest historical parallels in 1989-1991 (S&L/LBO refinancing wall, ~$200B in 1989 dollars ≈ $500-600B inflation-adjusted) and 2001-2002 (telecom debt maturities, ~$300B). The base rate for maturity walls under elevated rates producing credit stress is 2-for-2, but both prior episodes occurred under much simpler credit market structures without private credit ($1.7T) as an absorption buffer.** The novel question is whether private credit extends-and-pretends through the wall or amplifies it through mark-to-model lag effects.

8. **The Fed framework review (2025-2026) maps to three prior framework shifts: 1979 Volcker operating target switch, 2012 explicit inflation target adoption, and 2020 AIT/FAIT introduction. The base rate for framework shifts coinciding with material market repricing within 12 months is 2-for-3** (1979 produced massive bond market repricing, 2020 AIT was followed by inflation overshoot; 2012 was a non-event because it formalized existing practice). The current review's most likely non-trivial outcome — abandoning AIT for a symmetric 2% target or adopting a range — would validate what markets have already been pricing (inflation tolerance above 2%), making it partially a non-event. The tail risk is a shift toward nominal GDP targeting or explicit financial stability mandate, which would be a genuine regime change.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### ANALOGUE 1: Greenspan's Conundrum / Pre-GFC (2004-2007) — Similarity: 0.74

**Why selected:** This is the highest-similarity analogue because it shares the most structural features simultaneously: (1) apparent breakdown of the transmission mechanism (long rates didn't rise despite 425bp of tightening), (2) credit market opacity masking true risk (CDOs/SIVs then, private credit/cov-lite now), (3) a "this time is different" narrative about why traditional signals were failing (global savings glut then, AI productivity revolution now), and (4) the yield curve inverting without immediate recession.

**What happened, in what sequence, over what timeframe:**
- Jun 2004: Fed begins tightening from 1.00%
- Feb 2005: Greenspan testifies about the "conundrum" — 10Y yields at 4.17% despite 150bp of hikes
- Jun 2006: Fed reaches terminal rate of 5.25% after 17 hikes. Yield curve inverts. ABX index (subprime credit) begins declining
- Feb 2007: HSBC warns on subprime losses. Markets dismiss as "contained"
- Aug 2007: BNP Paribas freezes 3 funds. LIBOR-OIS spread blows out from 8bp to 90bp in weeks
- Sep 2007: Fed begins cutting (50bp) — 15 months after the curve inverted
- Dec 2007: Recession officially begins
- Sep 2008: Lehman bankruptcy — full systemic crisis
- Total duration from "conundrum" identification to crisis: ~42 months. From curve inversion to recession: ~18 months.

**Mapping to current KB concepts:**
- The "conundrum" maps directly to the `fci_effective_stance_gap` — Goldman FCI easing despite 525bp of hikes is structurally equivalent to Greenspan's frustration with long rates.
- CDO/SIV opacity maps to `non_gaap_earnings_masking` (hiding 20-25% of transmission in earnings) and private credit mark-to-model opacity (deferring impairment 6-12 months).
- The "contained" narrative about subprime maps to the current "soft landing" consensus. In both cases, the narrative has a factual basis (subprime was small; labor market is strong) but misses the second-order amplification channels.
- The `credit_equity_basis_signal` (equity-implied spreads 20-40bp wider than credit spreads) is structurally equivalent to the 2006-2007 pattern where equity vol priced more risk than credit spreads. That basis resolved via credit widening in 2007.

**Critical difference:** The private sector financial balance. In 2005-2007, the household sector was running a deficit of 3-4% GDP (aggregate negative savings rate, housing equity extraction). Today, the private sector is in surplus of 2-3% GDP. This is the single most important structural difference and argues against a 2008-magnitude outcome. The vulnerability is corporate, not household — and corporate crises historically produce slower, shallower recessions (2001) than household crises (2008).

**Second critical difference:** Fiscal. The federal deficit was ~1.5-2% GDP in 2006-2007 vs 6-7% today. The Kalecki profit channel was operating but at much lower amplitude. Today's fiscal offset is approximately 3-4x larger in GDP terms, which partially explains why transmission has been even more impaired than during the conundrum.

### ANALOGUE 2: Great Inflation Genesis (1966-1969) — Similarity: 0.68

**Why selected:** The strongest analogue for the fiscal dominance → toolkit bandwidth compression → eventual inflation acceleration pathway. Shares: (1) fiscal deficit overriding monetary tightening, (2) Fed toolkit proving insufficient to overcome fiscal impulse, (3) leading indicators degrading without recession for an extended period, (4) gold/commodity signals of credibility erosion.

**What happened, in what sequence, over what timeframe:**
- 1965: Johnson escalates Vietnam + Great Society spending. Federal deficit rises from balanced to ~3% GDP
- Mid-1966: Fed tightens aggressively. Credit crunch causes "mini-crisis." Leading indicators deteriorate
- Dec 1966: Fed reverses — cuts discount rate. Economy avoided recession by narrowest margin
- 1967-1968: Fiscal spending accelerates. GDP re-accelerates. CPI begins creeping up (2.5% to 4.2%)
- 1968: 10% income tax surcharge — fiscal consolidation attempt. Economists predict recession. Instead, expansion continues because monetary policy was loosened to "offset" the surcharge
- Late 1968: Gold pool collapses — structural signal of Bretton Woods strain
- 1969: Nixon takes office. Burns eventually loosens. CPI hits 6%
- Dec 1969-Nov 1970: Recession finally arrives — 3+ years after initial indicator deterioration
- Aftermath: Inflation doesn't fall below 4% for a decade. Fed credibility permanently impaired until Volcker.

**Mapping to current KB concepts:**
- The fiscal override maps to `partial_fiscal_dominance` and the Kalecki channel. The 1966-69 deficit was ~3% GDP; today's 6-7% is roughly double. If the fiscal offset was powerful enough to override Fed tightening at 3% GDP, the current 6-7% deficit makes the override significantly stronger.
- The toolkit bandwidth compression was already visible in 1966-69: the Fed had to reverse tightening because the credit market couldn't absorb it. This parallels `toolkit_bandwidth_compression` (effective bandwidth ~155-240bp).
- Gold pool collapse (1968) → gold repricing maps directly to `gold_structural_credibility_loss`. In both cases, gold signaled a structural monetary regime strain 3-5 years before the regime formally changed (Bretton Woods collapse in 1971, potential financial repression/AIT abandonment in 2027-2030?).
- The 1968 tax surcharge failure is a cautionary analogue for any fiscal consolidation attempt: if monetary policy loosens to "offset" fiscal tightening, the net effect on inflation may be zero or positive.

**Critical difference:** Labor market structure. In 1966-69, manufacturing was ~25% of employment with strong union wage bargaining. Today, services dominate with weaker bargaining power. The Phillips curve slope was steeper in the 1960s, meaning tight labor markets transmitted to inflation faster. The `extended_monetary_transmission_lags` concept reflects this structural change — what took 12-18 months in 1966-69 now takes 24-36+ months.

**Second critical difference:** Financial market depth and globalization. The 1966-69 Fed operated in a much simpler financial system. There was no basis trade absorbing QT (because there was no QT), no private credit market, no cov-lite structures, no global capital flows at current scale. The complexity of the current financial system both slows transmission (more buffers) and potentially amplifies the eventual catch-up (more interconnected amplification channels).

### ANALOGUE 3: 1994-1999 Dollar Strength / EM Sequence — Similarity: 0.62

**Why selected:** The strongest analogue for the dollar-EM flow dynamics. Shares: (1) technology-driven capital inflows into US assets creating structural dollar strength independent of rate differentials, (2) EM central bank policy space constrained by dollar strength, (3) currency crises in EM economies with current account vulnerabilities.

**What happened, in what sequence, over what timeframe:**
- 1994: Fed tightens aggressively (300bp in 12 months). Mexico devalues (Dec 1994). Tequila crisis.
- 1995-1996: US tech boom accelerates. Internet capital flows surge. DXY begins structural appreciation
- 1997: Thai baht devalues (Jul). Asian crisis spreads — Indonesia, Korea, Malaysia, Philippines
- 1998: Russia defaults (Aug). LTCM collapses (Sep). Fed cuts 75bp in emergency inter-meeting moves
- 1999: DXY continues rising despite Fed cuts. Capital flows into US tech dominate
- 2000-2001: Tech bust. DXY peaks early 2002 — approximately 7 years after the dollar strength began
- DXY appreciated ~25% from 1995 trough to 2002 peak

**Mapping to current KB concepts:**
- `dollar_overvaluation_ai_inflows` maps directly to the 1995-2000 tech-driven dollar appreciation. TIC data shows $40-60B/month net equity inflows concentrated in tech today vs analogous internet/telecom inflows then. The KB notes the `capex_dollar` operates orthogonally to rate differentials — exactly as during 1997-2001.
- `capex_dollar_em_flow_redirection` ($40-60B/month displacing EM allocation) parallels the 1996-1998 period when global portfolio managers chose US tech returns over EM carry. IIF EM flows flat now (~$5-10B/month) vs ~$25B/month during 2019 easing — the same compression visible in 1997-98.
- `three_tier_em_credibility_segmentation` maps to the 1997-98 differentiation between crisis economies (Thailand, Indonesia — Tier 3 equivalents), constrained-but-surviving economies (Korea, Malaysia — Tier 2), and economies that navigated successfully (Chile, Poland — Tier 1).
- The `dollar_cycle_plateau_correction` concept calibrated against 1995-2001 is the most direct application of this analogue.

**Critical difference:** Today's EM fundamentals are structurally stronger than 1997. Most EMs have flexible exchange rates, higher reserves, lower external debt, and local-currency bond markets. The base rate for a 1997-style cascade is lower. However, the `bpo_it_services_current_account_erosion` from AI automation represents a novel structural vulnerability that did not exist in 1997 — it is slow-moving but unpriced in NDF forwards.

**Second critical difference:** The Fed's dual mandate now includes explicit awareness of global spillbacks. In 1997-98, the Fed initially ignored EM stress until LTCM created domestic contagion. Today's Fed is more likely to incorporate "global financial conditions" into its reaction function earlier. The base rate for the Fed cutting into EM stress (as in October 1998) vs tightening through it applies here.

### ANALOGUE 4: 2018-2019 QT Episode — Similarity: 0.58 (0.82 for repo-specific mechanics)

**Why selected:** The only direct precedent for QT operations and their termination. Shares: (1) reserve scarcity uncertainty, (2) repo market stress as the canary, (3) distributional concentration of reserves (top 8 banks held ~50% then and now).

**What happened, in what sequence, over what timeframe:**
- Oct 2017: QT begins at $10B/month, ramping to $50B/month by Oct 2018
- Dec 2018: Fed hikes to 2.25-2.50%. Last hike of the cycle. S&P -20% in Q4
- Jan 2019: Powell "patience" pivot. Markets begin pricing cuts
- Jul 2019: First cut (25bp). QT continues — simultaneous cuts + QT begins
- Sep 16-17, 2019: Repo rate spikes to 10% (SOFR to 5.25% intraday). Fed forced to intervene with emergency repo operations
- Oct 2019: Fed announces "not QE" T-bill purchases ($60B/month). QT effectively abandoned
- Duration of simultaneous cuts + QT: approximately 3 months (Jul-Oct 2019)

**Mapping to current KB concepts:**
- `qt_endgame_nonlinear_threshold`: The September 2019 spike occurred when the Fed's own model estimated the scarcity threshold at ~$0.8T vs the actual ~$1.5T — a 2x miss. Current reserves at ~$3.2-3.4T are within the Fed's $2.5-3.5T comfort range, but this is a $1T uncertainty band. The KB correctly notes the 0/3 base rate for full balance sheet normalization post-QE.
- `simultaneous_cuts_qt_unprecedented`: The 2019 attempt lasted only ~3 months. The current attempt (~9 months) has already exceeded this, but QT has been slowed from $60B to $25B/month — a de facto 58% reduction that partially mimics the 2019 capitulation.
- `basis_trade_synthetic_qe`: The basis trade was smaller in 2019 (~$200-300B notional vs ~$800B-1T+ today). Its expansion has absorbed roughly half of QT's intended duration redistribution, which partially explains why the current QT has been less disruptive than 2019 — but also means the unwind risk is proportionally larger.

**Critical difference:** Scale. The 2019 balance sheet was ~$3.8T; today's is ~$6.7-7.0T. QT is running at $25B/month (down from $60B), slower than 2019's $50B/month. The Standing Repo Facility (SRF) was created specifically in response to 2019, but is untested at scale (zero stress-test episodes). The question is whether the SRF changes the base rate or just raises the threshold before it fails.

### ANALOGUE 5: 1942-1951 Treasury-Fed Accord Period — Similarity: 0.45 (0.72 for fiscal dominance resolution)

**Why selected:** The strongest analogue for the fiscal dominance resolution pathway. Shares: (1) government debt/GDP above 100%, (2) interest rates held below inflation (financial repression), (3) central bank accommodation of fiscal requirements, (4) eventual reassertion of central bank independence through a formal regime change.

**What happened, in what sequence, over what timeframe:**
- 1942: Fed agrees to cap Treasury yields at 2.5% (long end) and 0.375% (short end) to finance WWII
- 1946: War ends. Debt/GDP peaks at ~112%. Fed continues yield cap
- 1946-1948: Inflation surges to 14.4% (Mar 1947). Real rates deeply negative (-10%+). Debt/GDP begins rapid decline via inflation erosion
- 1948-1949: Brief recession and deflation. Pressure for yield cap removal grows
- 1950-1951: Korean War creates new fiscal pressure. Inflation re-accelerates (9.4% in Feb 1951)
- Mar 1951: Treasury-Fed Accord — Fed regains independence, yield caps removed
- Post-Accord: Debt/GDP falls from ~70% (1951) toward 30% (1974) via combination of growth, inflation, and primary surpluses
- Total duration of financial repression: ~9 years. Debt/GDP reduction: ~40pp via inflation/growth, with real bondholders bearing the cost

**Mapping to current KB concepts:**
- `partial_fiscal_dominance` → `financial_repression_modal_outcome`: The KB relationship notes a 4/7 historical base rate for fiscal dominance resolving through financial repression. The 1942-1951 period is the canonical US example.
- `gold_structural_credibility_loss`: Gold was fixed at $35/oz during the Accord period, but the real purchasing power erosion was severe. The current gold residual ($400-600/oz above real-rate-implied) may be pricing the probability of a similar repression scenario.
- `toolkit_bandwidth_compression`: The Accord period represents the extreme case of bandwidth compression — the Fed had exactly zero independent policy room. Today's estimated 155-240bp bandwidth is in between full independence and Accord-era zero.

**Critical difference:** Capital controls. The 1942-1951 financial repression was enforced through Regulation Q (interest rate ceilings), capital controls, and a captive domestic investor base. Modern capital mobility makes pure financial repression much harder to implement — the UK gilt crisis (2022) demonstrated that bond vigilantes now enforce real-rate discipline in weeks, not years. This is the strongest structural argument against the financial repression resolution path, and the KB correctly flags it.

**Second critical difference:** The Accord period followed a genuine national emergency (WWII) that created political legitimacy for repression. Today's fiscal path lacks comparable legitimacy, making sustained negative real rates politically and market-mechanically harder to sustain.

## SUPPLEMENTARY ANALOGUE ANALYSIS: ECB Divergence

### ECB-Fed Divergence Analogues

**Analogue A: 1992 ERM Crisis (Similarity: 0.55)**
- Structural divergence between Bundesbank (tightening to control reunification inflation) and peripheral Europe (needing easing for recession) created unbearable FX pressure
- Resolution: ERM collapse, forced devaluation of GBP/ITL/ESP, ECU crisis
- Parallel: ECB needs to ease (growth at 0.5-1.0%) while Fed holds elevated rates. EUR weakness from AI-driven outflows + LNG-USD demand creates a double bind
- Key difference: The euro eliminates intra-European FX adjustment. The pressure manifests as intra-eurozone spread widening (BTP-Bund) rather than exchange rate collapse

**Analogue B: 2011-2012 ECB/Fed Divergence (Similarity: 0.65)**
- ECB hiked twice (Apr, Jul 2011) into the euro crisis while the Fed held at zero
- Resolution: Trichet's hikes reversed within months. OMT backstop (Jul 2012) — fiscal, not monetary, resolution
- Parallel: The ECB's current easing is structurally constrained by EUR weakness importing inflation (the `lng_eur_usd_structural_channel`). This creates a situation analogous to Trichet's bind — the ECB may need to tighten or hold even when domestic conditions warrant easing
- Base rate implication: ECB divergence from domestic conditions forced by external factors has resolved via crisis + fiscal backstop in 1-for-2 cases

**Analogue C: 2014-2018 Gradual Divergence (Similarity: 0.50)**
- Fed tightened (2015-2018) while ECB eased (negative rates, QE). EUR/USD fell from 1.39 to 1.05
- Resolution: Sustained divergence for ~4 years without crisis. Capital flows were orderly
- Key difference: No structural LNG-USD demand channel. No AI capex flow redirection. This analogue represents the best case for sustained divergence, but the additional structural drags on EUR make it less applicable than it appears

**Novel LNG-EUR/USD Channel Assessment:** The TTF-EUR/USD correlation shift (from r=-0.15 to r=-0.45) has no historical precedent. The closest partial analogue is Japan's structural energy import dependency creating a permanent JPY drag — but Europe's shift happened abruptly (2022) rather than evolving gradually. This channel functionally means that any global energy shock simultaneously weakens the EUR and tightens domestic conditions, a double-bind that the ECB's inflation-targeting framework is poorly designed to handle. The base rate for novel structural constraints eventually producing a policy error is uncomfortably high, though the sample is too small for formal estimation.

## SUPPLEMENTARY ANALOGUE ANALYSIS: BoJ Normalization

### BoJ Normalization Analogues

**Analogue A: 2000 ZIRP Exit (Similarity: 0.62)**
- Aug 2000: BoJ raised rates from 0% to 0.25% despite government opposition
- Context: Japanese GDP briefly positive, tech bubble in the US, perceived window for normalization
- Resolution: Tech bust → Japanese recession. BoJ reversed to ZIRP by Mar 2001 (19 months). Widely considered a policy error
- Key parallel: The BoJ is again attempting normalization during a period of perceived strength, with government officials expressing caution
- Signpost: If Japanese core CPI falls back below 2% within 12 months of the current tightening, the 2000 analogue is replaying

**Analogue B: 2006-2007 Tightening Cycle (Similarity: 0.70)**
- Jul 2006: BoJ raised from 0% to 0.25%. Feb 2007: raised to 0.50%
- Context: Global growth strong, carry trade at peak, USD/JPY at 120-124
- Resolution: GFC → reversed to 0.10% by Dec 2008. Carry trade unwind was violent (JPY appreciated from 124 to 87)
- Key parallel: Current carry trade dynamics are even larger. Japanese foreign portfolio holdings at ~$3.5T. USD/JPY structural level above 150 vs 120-124 then — a wider gap from "fair value" that implies larger repatriation risk if normalization succeeds
- Signpost: Japanese institutional repatriation. The KB notes historical repatriation at ~30% of model-predicted levels. If this threshold is exceeded, the `boj_normalization_shock` → `maturity_dependent_correlation_bifurcation` channel activates

**Base rate for BoJ normalization completion: 0-for-2** (and the 1990 tightening, while it "transmitted," caused a catastrophic outcome). This is the weakest base rate of any major central bank policy initiative in the post-war record. The current attempt is better-positioned (actual sustained inflation above 2% for the first time since the 1990s, wage-price dynamics finally showing signs of life), but the structural headwinds (demographic drag, debt/GDP at 260%) remain formidable.

**Repatriation Channel Assessment:** The ~$3.5T in Japanese foreign portfolio holdings (heavily concentrated in USTs and US corporate bonds) represents a latent duration supply shock. If BoJ normalization pushes JGB yields above the currency-hedged UST yield (currently hedging costs have already narrowed this spread to near zero), the economic incentive flips toward repatriation. The KB's `boj_normalization_shock` → `maturity_dependent_correlation_bifurcation` mechanism is the correct framework: repatriation would primarily affect the long end of USTs, amplifying the positive stock-bond correlation at 10Y+ maturities while leaving 2Y dynamics dominated by Fed cyclical expectations.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Yield Curve Dynamics:** The maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation in the KB (long-end positive correlation, short-end negative) maps precisely to the 1966-1969 experience where fiscal dominance drove long yields higher while short rates remained policy-determined. In the 1940s Accord period, the bifurcation was artificially suppressed by yield caps — when the caps were removed (1951), the long end repriced by ~100bp in months. The `term_premium_decomposition` should distinguish between "fiscal supply premium" (1966-69 analogue) and "inflation risk premium" (1977-80 analogue) — they have different implications for curve shape. The 1966-69 fiscal supply premium steepened the curve; the 1977-80 inflation premium inverted it.

**Credit Cycles:** The `credit_equity_basis_signal` (20-40bp) has its closest analogue in Q1 2007, when the ABX began declining while IG spreads remained tight. That basis resolved violently over 6-9 months. The mechanism today differs — private credit absorbs risk that would historically appear in public credit markets — but the directional signal (equity markets pricing more risk than credit) is the same. Historical base rate for equity-credit divergences resolving in credit's direction: 3-for-4 (1998 LTCM, 2007 GFC, 2011 Euro crisis resolved via credit widening; 2015 energy stress was the exception where equity recovery occurred first). The `corporate_debt_maturity_wall` interacts with credit cycles through a specific sequencing pattern visible in 1989-91 and 2001-02: maturity pressure → refinancing stress → rating downgrades → spread widening → access loss for weakest credits → defaults concentrated in 6-12 month window.

**Inflation Regimes:** The 1966-1969 analogue is the single most relevant for the inflation pathway. The sequence was: fiscal expansion → labor market tightness → wage acceleration → services inflation → expectations un-anchoring. This took approximately 3 years from fiscal impulse to expectations un-anchoring. Mapping to today: if the fiscal deficit remains at 6-7% GDP and the labor market remains at 4.0-4.2% unemployment, the 1966-69 analogue suggests expectations un-anchoring risk in the 2027-2028 window — 3 years from the current fiscal impulse peak. The last-mile disinflation convexity concept reinforces this: in 1966-69, the sacrifice ratio for the last mile of disinflation proved 3-5x the ratio for the initial disinflation.

**Fiscal Dominance:** The `partial_fiscal_dominance` → `financial_repression_modal_outcome` pathway has the 1942-1951 Accord as its canonical US example and post-WWII UK (1945-1980) as the extended case. Base rate for resolution paths: financial repression 4/7, fiscal consolidation 2/7, external crisis forcing adjustment 1/7. The current US trajectory (debt/GDP ~120%, deficit ~6-7% GDP, interest costs ~4% GDP) maps most closely to post-WWII UK, which resolved via 35 years of financial repression and secular decline. The `toolkit_bandwidth_compression` (155-240bp) has no clean historical parallel at this severity for the dominant global reserve currency issuer — the closest is the Bank of England in the 1960s-1970s, which operated with severely constrained bandwidth and eventually required IMF assistance (1976).

**Dollar/FX:** The 1995-2001 dot-com dollar cycle is the highest-relevance single analogue for the current dollar regime. DXY appreciated ~25% over 7 years, driven by technology capital inflows. The correction was ~33% over 6 years (2002-2008). If the current dollar follows the same arc and the AI capex cycle is roughly 3 years behind the 1997 internet equivalent, the dollar plateau persists until ~2027-2028 before the correction phase begins — consistent with the KB's `dollar_cycle_plateau_correction` probability estimate. The EM flow redirection pattern (capital moving from EM into US tech) also matches 1996-1998 closely, with the caveat that EM fundamentals are structurally stronger today (flexible FX, higher reserves) than in 1997.

## Open Questions

1. **Which analogue is the "lead horse" for monetary transmission resolution?** The critical discriminator is whether the forcing event comes through the credit channel (2007 analogue → maturity wall + private credit stress) or the inflation channel (1966-69 analogue → expectations un-anchoring). The signpost: if 5Y5Y breakeven inflation moves sustainably above 2.7%, the inflation channel is leading and the 1966-69 analogue dominates. If HY spreads widen above 550bp before breakevens move, the credit channel is leading and the 2007 analogue dominates. Current 5Y5Y at ~2.4% and HY OAS at ~350bp suggest neither channel has activated yet.

2. **Does private credit at $1.7T fundamentally change the base rates for credit market forcing events?** The historical analogues (1989-91 S&L, 2001-02 telecom, 2007-08 GFC) all occurred with public credit markets as the primary stress transmission channel. Private credit's mark-to-model valuation and quarterly NAV reporting create a structural delay mechanism with no historical precedent at this scale. If private credit absorbs the maturity wall stress, the base rate for credit-channel forcing events drops — but the eventual recognition event, when it comes, may be more discontinuous (a cliff rather than a slope). The analogue here may actually be Japanese bank forbearance in the 1990s, where mark-to-model opacity extended the crisis by years while compounding the eventual cost.

3. **Is the SRF a genuine regime change for QT endgame dynamics, or a false comfort analogous to pre-2007 belief in "portfolio insurance"?** The September 2019 repo spike occurred despite the Fed having tools it could deploy. The SRF was created to prevent the specific failure mode of 2019, but the next failure mode may be different — a funding stress in non-bank channels the SRF doesn't reach, or a scale of demand that overwhelms the SRF's capacity. The base rate for backstop facilities preventing the specific failure they were designed for vs failing to prevent novel adjacent failures is approximately 50/50 in financial history (FDIC prevented classic bank runs but not shadow bank runs; IMF prevented classic EM current account crises but not capital account crises).

4. **Does the LNG-EUR/USD structural channel represent a permanent degradation of ECB policy independence?** If so, the ECB is effectively constrained to a "Fed follower" role with a structural lag, and the base rate for independent ECB policy cycles becomes near-zero for as long as Europe depends on USD-denominated LNG. This has profound implications for EUR/USD fair value, European real rates, and the eurozone growth trajectory. The relevant monitoring variable is European LNG contract diversification — any shift toward EUR-denominated long-term supply contracts would signal the constraint is loosening.

5. **What is the correct base rate for BoJ normalization given that this attempt has a genuinely novel feature (sustained >2% inflation) absent from both prior attempts?** The 0-for-2 base rate mechanically applies, but the prior failures were both under deflationary conditions (2000 tech bust, 2008 GFC). If one accepts that sustained inflation above 2% is a necessary condition for normalization success, then the effective base rate is 0-for-0 (untested), not 0-for-2. This distinction matters enormously for positioning the repatriation risk: if normalization is more likely to succeed this time, the repatriation flow is more likely to materialize and the UST long-end impact could be larger than analogues suggest.

6. **How should the Fed framework review risk be sized?** The ~50% non-event probability from the KB seems approximately right, but the tail scenarios (NGDP targeting, explicit financial stability mandate) would each represent a genuine regime change of a magnitude not seen since 1979. The base rate for framework reviews producing surprises is low (central banks are conservative institutions), but the political pressure on the Fed (from both populist and academic directions) is higher than any review since the 1970s. Monitoring variable: composition of the review committee and early public statements from participants.
