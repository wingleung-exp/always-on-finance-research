# Fiscal Policy — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## MACRO REGIME ASSESSMENT

The U.S. economy operates in a configuration that the New Keynesian framework identifies as internally contradictory and therefore unstable. The KB's finding of zero pre-2022 precedents for 6-7% deficits at sub-4% unemployment (concept: `unprecedented_fiscal_configuration`, confidence 9) is not merely a statistical curiosity — it represents a violation of the standard business cycle taxonomy. In the NK framework, large deficits arise endogenously during recessions via automatic stabilizers and countercyclical discretionary action, not during late-cycle expansion. The current configuration implies either (a) a massive positive output gap being masked by measurement error, (b) a structural shift in potential output that has not been recognized, or (c) a discretionary fiscal stance wildly misaligned with the cycle phase. The evidence favors (c), with implications I develop below.

**Output gap estimate:** +0.5% to +1.5% of potential GDP. The sub-4% unemployment rate sits below most NAIRU estimates (4.0-4.5% per CBO, 4.0-4.3% per Fed staff models). Core PCE at ~2.6-2.8% running persistently above the 2% target is consistent with a modestly positive output gap in the expectations-augmented Phillips Curve framework. However, the gap is smaller than the fiscal impulse would suggest under standard multiplier assumptions — evidence that the fiscal multiplier at current conditions is substantially below 1.0, consistent with the KB's state-contingent multiplier range of 0.0-0.5x during expansion with monetary offset (`fiscal_multiplier_deep_uncertainty`).

**Inflation dynamics:** The New Keynesian Phillips Curve (NKPC) at current parameterization implies inflation persistence. With the output gap positive and inflation expectations modestly above target (5Y5Y breakevens at 2.3-2.5%), the NKPC predicts inflation will remain sticky above 2% absent either demand destruction or a favorable supply shock. The KB's `shelter_oer_mission_accomplished_trap` concept (referenced in cross-asset inconsistency) reinforces this — base effects in shelter will create an illusion of progress that reverses mechanically.

## Key Claims

**1. The Taylor Rule prescribes a federal funds rate 75-150bp above the current stance, implying the Fed is already in a quasi-accommodative posture relative to the fiscal impulse.**

With core PCE at ~2.7%, the output gap at +0.5-1.5%, r-star at 1.0-1.5% (my estimate, below the KB's 2.0-2.5% range discussed in `cross_asset_inconsistency`), and an inflation target of 2.0%, a standard Taylor (1993) rule prescribes:

> r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*) = 1.25 + 2.7 + 0.5(0.7) + 0.5(1.0) = 4.8%

A Taylor (1999) rule with a coefficient of 1.0 on the output gap gives 5.3%. The current federal funds rate of 4.25-4.50% is thus 50-100bp below Taylor (1993) and 75-150bp below Taylor (1999). The Fed is running policy looser than rules-based prescriptions would dictate, which means the monetary stance is not providing the offset to fiscal expansion that the standard NK framework assumes. This is a critical finding: the fiscal multiplier should be compressed toward zero by monetary offset, but the offset is incomplete.

**2. The fiscal-monetary policy mix replicates the Reagan 1981-85 configuration in direction but exceeds it in magnitude, with Mundell-Fleming implications for the dollar and external balance.**

The IS-LM/Mundell-Fleming framework makes a sharp prediction: expansionary fiscal policy combined with neutral-to-tight monetary policy under flexible exchange rates produces currency appreciation, current account deterioration, and partial crowding out of net exports. The KB documents G7 fiscal divergence at post-WWII extremes (`g7_fiscal_divergence_extreme`, confidence 8) with the U.S. running -6.5 to -7.0% versus Euro Area -3%. The 1981-85 episode saw the dollar appreciate 50% under Volcker tightening plus Reagan deficits. However, the KB notes that the current monetary stance maps closer to the 2001-04 dollar-weak pattern — I partially disagree. The Mundell-Fleming prediction depends on the *relative* monetary-fiscal mix, and the U.S. is running both looser fiscal AND comparable monetary to peers. The equilibrium outcome is ambiguous, which I assign:

- Dollar gradual weakening (DXY -5-10% over 18 months): 45% probability
- Dollar range-bound (DXY ±3%): 35% probability  
- Dollar strength on safe-haven/rate differential: 20% probability

**3. The r-g convergence creates a zone of fiscal instability that the NK framework identifies as a necessary (but not sufficient) condition for a debt sustainability regime shift.**

The KB documents r≈g convergence (`r_g_transition_sustainability`, confidence 7) with the effective rate at ~3.3% rising toward marginal rates of 4.5-5.0%. In the standard Solow-Swan/neoclassical growth framework, debt sustainability requires the primary balance condition:

> d(debt/GDP)/dt = (r - g)(debt/GDP) - primary_surplus/GDP

At 124% debt/GDP, each 100bp of r>g adds 1.24pp to the required primary surplus. With the structural primary deficit at approximately 3-4% of GDP (after stripping interest), achieving sustainability requires either (a) r<g by at least 2.5-3.2pp, (b) primary surpluses that have not been achieved since 1998-2001, or (c) nominal GDP growth acceleration. Option (a) was the 2010-2021 regime. Option (c) requires either productivity acceleration or above-target inflation — both possible but neither assured.

I assess the probability of a disorderly fiscal adjustment (basis trade unwind, failed auction, forced austerity) at 8-12% over 5 years, lower than the KB's IMF cross-country base rate of 35-40% because reserve currency status (`reserve_currency_exceptionalism`) is genuinely not captured in panel regressions. But I assess the probability of a gradual fiscal dominance regime (financial repression, above-target inflation tolerated to erode debt/GDP) at 35-45% over 10 years.

**4. DOGE consolidation falls squarely into the fiscal uncanny valley unless accompanied by 200bp+ of monetary easing — which the Fed cannot deliver with inflation above target.**

The KB's `fiscal_uncanny_valley` concept (confidence 6) identifies the worst-case outcome: consolidation large enough to contract GDP but insufficient to stabilize the debt trajectory. Applying the NK framework rigorously: fiscal contraction multipliers of 1.2-1.8x (`fiscal_contraction_multiplier_asymmetry`) at current conditions mean that $150B in genuine spending cuts would reduce GDP by approximately 0.5-0.9% — enough to raise unemployment toward NAIRU but insufficient to generate the 3-4pp primary balance swing needed for sustainability.

The standard NK escape route is monetary accommodation: the central bank cuts rates to offset the fiscal drag, maintaining aggregate demand while the fiscal position improves. This is the Clinton-1993 / Canada-1994-97 playbook. But the 2026 constraint set is binding: inflation at 2.6-2.8% above target means the Taylor Rule already prescribes tighter, not looser, policy. The Fed cannot credibly ease 200bp+ without de-anchoring inflation expectations. This is the monetary policy trap (`monetary_policy_trap`, confidence 7) identified in the KB, and it is the single most important constraint on fiscal adjustment.

**5. The Kalecki profit channel is real but the NK framework reveals it as a partial equilibrium result that ignores the general equilibrium offset through the interest rate channel.**

The KB documents the Kalecki profit identity with R² of 0.55-0.65 (`kalecki_channel_empirical_partial`, confidence 7). From the NK perspective, this is an accounting identity dressed up as a causal mechanism. The identity holds by construction, but the *behavioral* claim — that larger deficits support profits — requires specifying the general equilibrium transmission. In the NK IS-LM framework, deficit-financed spending raises income (IS shift right) but also raises interest rates (movement along LM), partially crowding out private investment and net exports. The net effect on profits depends on the multiplier, which at current conditions is compressed.

The KB's own evidence supports this: crowding-out has attenuated post-2008 (`crowding_out_post2008_attenuation`, confidence 5) but the question of whether QT reactivates the channel is critical and unresolved. If the traditional IS-LM crowding-out channel re-emerges — which I assess at 40-50% probability as QT progresses and global savings surplus narrows — the Kalecki profit floor weakens substantially.

**6. The cross-asset inconsistency will resolve through credit widening and term premium repricing, not through equity repricing — over a 6-18 month horizon.**

The KB identifies the cross-asset inconsistency (`cross_asset_inconsistency`, confidence 7.33) as the central market puzzle. Applying the NK macro regime framework: the inconsistency arises because different asset classes are pricing different probability-weighted scenarios. Equities price the "soft landing + productivity boom" scenario (35-40% probability). Credit prices the "benign carry" scenario (similar probability). Rates price the "fiscal supply + sticky inflation" scenario (higher probability at the long end).

The NK framework predicts that the resolution pathway depends on which macro shock dominates. A demand shock (recession) resolves via equity and credit repricing simultaneously. A supply shock (tariff-driven stagflation) resolves via rates repricing higher AND equity repricing lower, with credit intermediate. A fiscal shock (term premium spike) resolves via rates first, then credit, then equity — with a 2-6 month transmission lag.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Taylor Rule):** The calculation above uses standard Taylor Rule parameters (α=0.5 on both gaps for 1993 version). The key uncertainty is r-star: the KB's `r_star_estimation_uncertainty` is referenced across multiple concepts. If r-star is genuinely 2.0-2.5% (as the 5Y5Y forward rate suggests), the Taylor prescription rises to 5.3-5.8%, making the current stance 100-150bp too loose. The Laubach-Williams model's 1.0-1.5% estimate would narrow the gap. I use 1.25% as my point estimate, acknowledging ±75bp uncertainty.

**Claim 2 (Mundell-Fleming):** Historical episodes (KB: 1981-85, 2001-04, 2017-19) confirm the framework's predictions directionally but with substantial noise. The critical variable is the monetary-fiscal policy mix *differential* versus trading partners. European rearmament (`european_rearmament_fiscal_shift`, confidence 7) is narrowing the fiscal divergence at the margin, which weakens the dollar-strengthening prediction.

**Claim 3 (r-g):** Blanchard (2019) AEA presidential address established the r<g condition formally. The KB's evidence on blended rates rising at ~50-70bp/year toward marginal rates is mechanically sound. My lower crisis probability than the IMF base rate reflects the reserve currency adjustment documented in the KB — but I note this is a subjective judgment with wide confidence intervals.

**Claim 4 (DOGE uncanny valley):** The four historical failure cases (Eurozone 2010-13, Japan 1997, UK 2010-13, Argentina 2018) all lacked adequate monetary offset. The 2013 sequester succeeded partly because QE3 was providing offset — unavailable in 2026. The success conditions (200bp+ easing, 10%+ depreciation, external demand boom) are documented in the KB with appropriate caveats about small-N inference.

**Claim 5 (Kalecki GE offset):** This is the core NK critique of the KB's partial-equilibrium Kalecki analysis. The `crowding_out_post2008_attenuation` concept (partial correlation dropping from -0.38 to -0.12) is the key evidence — but the attenuation coincides precisely with QE, and QT is unwinding that regime. Reinhart-Sbrancia (2015) on financial repression provides the historical parallel.

**Claim 6 (Cross-asset resolution):** The equity-implied vs. actual HY spread divergence of 50-75bp (`equity_implied_hy_spread_divergence`) has a 75% historical base rate of resolving via credit widening (3/4 episodes). The NK framework adds the mechanism: as the output gap closes and the fiscal impulse fades, the default cycle will normalize from abnormally low levels, widening credit spreads from their carry-compressed state.

## POLICY STANCE EVALUATION

**Monetary policy:** The Fed is running policy 75-150bp below Taylor Rule prescriptions, creating an implicit accommodation of fiscal expansion. This is not deliberate easing — it reflects the Fed's perception that inflation is on a glide path toward target, permitting patience. If that perception proves correct (my base case probability: 40%), the current stance is defensible. If inflation reaccelerates (probability: 30%), the Fed faces a credibility problem. If a recession materializes (probability: 25%), the Fed has limited room to cut before reaching the effective lower bound — approximately 400-450bp of conventional space.

**Fiscal policy:** Running the largest peacetime structural deficit in history at full employment. The primary deficit (excluding interest) is approximately 3-4% of GDP. Interest expense is approaching 4% of GDP. The combined deficit of 6.5-7.0% of GDP is stimulative at the margin, but with a compressed multiplier (0.0-0.5x at current conditions), the incremental GDP boost is perhaps 0.0-0.35pp — well below what naive multiplier application would suggest.

**Policy coordination failure:** The NK framework identifies this as a classic Stackelberg game between the fiscal authority (leader) and the central bank (follower). The fiscal authority's expansion forces the central bank to maintain tighter policy than otherwise warranted, raising the policy rate, increasing debt service costs, and worsening the fiscal trajectory. This is the deficit-term-premium doom loop (`deficit_term_premium_doom_loop`) operating through the monetary transmission mechanism rather than directly through term premia.

## GROWTH AND INFLATION OUTLOOK

### Base Case: Stagnation at Altitude (45% probability)
- GDP growth: 1.5-2.0% (below trend, above recession)
- Core PCE: 2.5-2.8% (sticky above target)
- Unemployment: 4.0-4.3% (gradual NAIRU convergence)
- Fed: On hold through 2026, one cut in Q1 2027
- Fiscal deficit: 6.0-6.5% (modest DOGE savings offset by rising interest expense)

This is the muddle-through scenario. The economy grows below potential as fiscal impulse fades at the margin and monetary policy remains restrictive. Inflation declines slowly but does not reach 2%, keeping the Fed on hold. DOGE delivers $50-100B in genuine savings — enough to matter politically but insufficient to alter the debt trajectory.

### Upside: Productivity Acceleration (20% probability)
- GDP growth: 2.5-3.0%
- Core PCE: 2.0-2.3% (favorable supply response)
- Unemployment: 3.5-3.8%
- Fed: Able to cut 75-100bp as inflation falls
- Fiscal: Denominator growth improves debt/GDP dynamics

This requires AI-driven productivity gains to materialize in measured TFP within 12-18 months — historically fast for a GPT (general purpose technology) but not impossible given the deployment speed of LLMs. The Solow-Swan framework suggests TFP acceleration of 0.5-1.0pp would raise potential output growth sufficiently to maintain r<g even at current fiscal settings.

### Downside A: Recession via Fiscal Cliff (25% probability)
- GDP growth: -0.5 to -1.5%
- Core PCE: 2.0-2.5% (initially sticky, then disinflationary)
- Unemployment: 5.0-5.5%
- Fed: Cuts 200-300bp over 12 months
- Fiscal: Automatic stabilizers widen deficit to 8-9%

Triggered by: DOGE consolidation landing in the uncanny valley, tariff escalation producing a supply shock that forces the Fed to tighten further, or a term premium spike triggering basis trade unwind. The recession would likely be moderate (not GFC-like) because household balance sheets remain strong (`bifurcated_minsky_fragility`), but corporate/financial sector stress could be significant.

### Downside B: Stagflation (10% probability)
- GDP growth: 0.0-0.5%
- Core PCE: 3.5-4.5%
- Unemployment: 4.5-5.0%
- Fed: Paralyzed (monetary policy trap)
- Fiscal: Worsening on both primary and interest expense

Triggered by: tariff-driven supply shock + fiscal impulse preventing demand contraction. This is the worst macro outcome and the one where the cross-asset inconsistency resolves most violently. The NKPC with backward-looking expectations component (hybrid Phillips Curve) predicts inflation persistence in this scenario.

## MARKET IMPLICATIONS

**Rates:** The term premium is structurally supported at 50-100bp by fiscal supply (`fiscal_supply_duration_pricing`, confidence 8). Base case: 10Y range 4.0-4.8%, with the distribution skewed to the upside by fiscal risk. Long-duration bonds are poor risk-reward: limited upside in the base case, significant downside in stagflation. The 2s10s curve should steepen as the front end eventually prices cuts while the long end holds on fiscal supply. Recommended positioning: underweight duration beyond 5Y, overweight the belly (3-5Y) for carry with less fiscal supply exposure.

**Equities:** The Kalecki profit floor provides support but is eroding. S&P EPS estimates of ~$260-270 for 2026 embed the fiscal contribution. A 1pp reduction in the deficit from DOGE would reduce the Kalecki-attributed profit floor by approximately $5-10 in EPS, or 2-4%. More concerning is the multiple: P/E of 21-22x on non-GAAP earnings is consistent with the soft landing / productivity acceleration scenario (combined probability: ~65%) but vulnerable to the recession / stagflation scenarios (combined probability: ~35%). Expected return over 12 months: 2-6% in the base case, reflecting below-average risk premium at elevated valuations. Sector implications: defense and infrastructure benefit from the geopolitical spending floor; small-cap Russell 2000 (40% unprofitable) is most vulnerable to multiplier contraction.

**Credit:** The 50-75bp equity-implied vs. actual HY spread divergence signals underpriced risk. IG spreads at 90-110bp are compressed below where the fiscal regime would suggest. I expect 25-50bp of widening over 6-12 months as the business cycle matures and the covenant-lite/PIK structures face their first serious test. The timing is uncertain but the direction is high conviction.

**FX:** Dollar direction is genuinely uncertain (see Claim 2). The Mundell-Fleming framework gives ambiguous predictions given the current policy mix. I lean toward gradual depreciation (45% probability) on the basis that the fiscal trajectory, once priced, is dollar-negative — analogous to the 2001-04 twin deficit episode. European rearmament fiscal expansion narrows the rate differential at the margin.

**Gold:** The $400-600 residual above model fair value (`gold_fiscal_dominance_pricing`) is partially justified by the fiscal dominance probability I assign (35-45% over 10 years). Gold is the cleanest expression of the tail risk that the fiscal trajectory resolves via financial repression rather than consolidation. Central bank buying at 1,000+ tonnes/year is the revealed-preference confirmation.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary policy transmission mechanism:** The fiscal supply channel has fundamentally altered how monetary policy transmits to the real economy. Rate cuts that would normally lower long-term rates may be partially offset by term premium from fiscal supply, reducing the potency of conventional easing. This connects to the `monetary_policy_trap` — the Fed's tools are less effective in both directions.

**Real estate cycles:** The `fiscal_policy_cre_crosscurrent` concept is a direct application of the NK framework. Fiscal deficits simultaneously support CRE earnings via the Kalecki channel while keeping long-end rates elevated via Treasury supply, preventing cap rate compression. The NK model predicts this cross-current resolves only when either (a) fiscal consolidation removes both forces simultaneously, or (b) recession triggers rate cuts large enough to overcome the term premium floor.

**Commodities:** China's fiscal pivot (`china_commodity_demand_mix_shift`) interacts with G7 fiscal divergence to reshape commodity demand patterns. The NK framework adds: fiscal demand for commodities at full employment is more inflationary than at recession, amplifying the supply-demand imbalance and making the inflation persistence problem worse.

**Financial stability:** The basis trade (`treasury_basis_trade_amplification`) is the transmission mechanism between fiscal vulnerability and systemic crisis. In NK terms, this is a financial accelerator mechanism (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist 1999) where leveraged positions in the Treasury market create a non-linear amplification of fiscal shocks.

**Emerging markets:** The agricultural commodity → EM fiscal stress transmission chain (`ag_shock_em_fiscal_leading_indicator`) is a Mundell-Fleming open-economy spillover: G7 fiscal expansion → commodity demand → price spike → EM terms-of-trade shock → forced EM tightening → EM fiscal stress.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the true r-star?** The entire policy stance evaluation pivots on this parameter. The range of 1.0-2.5% across models propagates into ±150bp of Taylor Rule uncertainty. The Fed's own Summary of Economic Projections has converged on ~3.0% nominal (≈1.0% real), but market-implied r-star via 5Y5Y real forwards suggests 1.5-2.0%. This is not a question that can be resolved with current data — it requires observing how the economy responds to the current rate level over the next 12-18 months.

2. **Has the fiscal multiplier at full employment genuinely collapsed to near-zero, or is the output gap mismeasured?** If the output gap is larger than estimated (i.e., potential output is higher due to immigration and AI), then the multiplier is higher than the NK framework at full employment would predict, and the fiscal impulse is contributing more to growth than I have credited.

3. **Does the deficit composition shift toward interest payments (low MPC recipients) reduce the effective fiscal impulse even at constant deficit levels?** This is the Kalecki erosion question. If interest payments have an MPC of 0.3-0.5 versus transfers at 0.7-1.2, then the same nominal deficit delivers a declining real impulse — a stealth fiscal consolidation that would show up as slower growth without any policy change.

4. **Can the Fed implement targeted intervention (SRF expansion, standing repo) in a basis trade crisis without reigniting inflation expectations?** This is the operational question underlying the `monetary_policy_trap`. If the answer is yes — intervention is perceived as financial plumbing, not monetary easing — the tail risk from the basis trade is substantially reduced. If no, the fiscal-financial stability nexus is tighter than current pricing suggests.

5. **What is the behavioral response of private investment to a credible fiscal consolidation announcement?** The Clinton 1993 experience (Rubin's "bond market vigilante" thesis) suggests credible consolidation can lower long rates enough to stimulate private investment, partially or fully offsetting the direct fiscal drag. This is the Ricardian equivalence channel operating in practice. But "credibility" is binary and subjective — and the current political environment may not support credible long-term commitments.

6. **Is the post-2008 attenuation of crowding-out structural or an artifact of the QE/excess-reserves regime?** If structural (driven by global savings glut, demographic surplus, intangible-capital-intensive growth requiring less physical investment), the IS-LM crowding-out channel remains suppressed even as QT progresses. If QE-driven, crowding-out re-emerges as reserves drain, and the NK framework's prediction of fiscal expansion → higher rates → private investment crowding-out becomes operative again. The sample since QT began is too short to distinguish these hypotheses.
