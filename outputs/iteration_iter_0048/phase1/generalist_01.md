# Fiscal Policy — Cross-Asset Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The cross-asset inconsistency has entered a meta-stable equilibrium sustained by three self-reinforcing mechanisms — and resolution will be non-linear, not gradual.** Equity multiples (21-22x), tight credit (IG 90-110bp), and elevated term premium (50-100bp ACM) are not merely "disagreeing" — they are each reinforcing the others' stability through the Kalecki profit floor (fiscal deficits → profits → low defaults → tight spreads → easy financial conditions → equity multiples sustained). This equilibrium is meta-stable: it persists until a discrete catalyst breaks one link in the chain, at which point the repricing cascades across all three simultaneously rather than sequentially.

2. **Credit is the most mispriced asset class on a risk-adjusted basis, and the resolution sequencing will likely follow: rates (already repricing) → credit (next, 3-9 months) → equities (last, with Kalecki floor providing 6-12 month delay).** The equity-implied HY spread divergence of 50-75bp has a 75% historical base rate of resolving via credit widening. The current credit market is pricing a benign scenario (~2% default rate) while rates are pricing fiscal risk (50-100bp term premium) and equities are pricing a goldilocks path. Credit is the "swing vote" — when it reprices, it removes the financial conditions anchor supporting equities.

3. **The global fiscal supply surge (U.S. $2-2.5T + European rearmament ~$100-150B/yr + Japan refinancing) creates a structural duration shortage globally for the first time since the pre-QE era, fundamentally altering the stock-bond correlation regime.** The stock-bond correlation is bifurcating by maturity: negative at the front end (monetary policy driven), positive at the long end (fiscal supply driven). This bifurcation means the traditional 60/40 portfolio's diversification benefit is concentrated in short-duration bonds, while long-duration bonds have become a correlated risk factor rather than a hedge. Portfolio construction using full-curve duration as a hedge is systematically overestimating diversification.

4. **The fiscal-tariff interaction is an underpriced cross-asset signal: tariffs function as a regressive consumption tax (~0.5-1.5% effective rate increase) that partially offsets DOGE fiscal consolidation while simultaneously raising inflation, creating a stagflationary fiscal impulse that no single asset class is pricing correctly.** Tariffs raise government revenue ($200-400B at discussed rates), support nominal GDP (denominator effect), but compress real growth and raise inflation expectations. This is bullish for gold and TIPS breakevens, bearish for credit and small-cap equities, and ambiguous for nominal Treasuries — a combination no standard macro regime cleanly captures.

5. **The correlation regime is transitioning from "disinflationary" (negative stock-bond correlation, post-2000 default) toward a "fiscal dominance" regime (positive stock-bond correlation at longer maturities), with the transition ~40-60% complete.** The KB's correlation_regime_taxonomy identifies two prior split episodes (1965-69, 2003-06) with divergent resolutions. The current fiscal backdrop (6-7% deficit at sub-4% unemployment, r≈g transition, fiscal supply dominance beyond 5Y) maps more closely to the 1965-69 resolution path (fiscal/inflation dominance → sustained positive correlation). Probability: ~60% fiscal dominance resolution, ~40% monetary dominance resolution (requiring credible consolidation or deflationary shock).

6. **The best risk-adjusted cross-asset trade is long gold / short long-duration IG credit — it expresses the fiscal dominance probability asymmetry while being hedged against both the "muddle through" and "acute crisis" scenarios.** Gold has positive convexity to fiscal dominance (both through real rate suppression and central bank buying) while IG credit has negative convexity (tight spreads = asymmetric downside). The trade profits in 3 of 4 macro scenarios and loses only in "credible fiscal consolidation + disinflation" — the lowest probability path.

7. **The dollar's trajectory is the key cross-asset arbiter: a sustained move below DXY 100 would validate the "fiscal excess → external adjustment" thesis and trigger a cascade of cross-asset repricing (EM outperformance, commodity rally, European asset rotation).** G7 fiscal divergence maps closest to the 2001-04 pattern (wide deficits + neutral-to-easy Fed = dollar weakness), and the twin deficit (fiscal + current account) backdrop is the widest since that episode. However, rate differentials and safe-haven demand are providing a floor. The dollar is the asset class where the fiscal signal faces the strongest counter-signal.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Meta-stable equilibrium):**
The chain of self-reinforcement: fiscal deficit ($6.5-7% GDP) → Kalecki profit floor ($45-65 S&P EPS, R² 0.55-0.65) → suppressed default rates → tight credit spreads (IG 90-110bp) → easy financial conditions → equity multiples sustained (21-22x) → low equity vol → leveraged carry trade sizing ($800B-$1T basis trade) → compressed term premium volatility → reinforces tight credit. Each link in this chain depends on the prior link's stability. The KB documents this through multiple concepts (cross_asset_inconsistency, kalecki_profit_channel_erosion, basis_trade_fiscal_amplification, bifurcated_minsky_fragility) but hasn't explicitly mapped the self-reinforcing loop as a single mechanism. Historical meta-stable equilibria (pre-2007 credit, pre-2000 dot-com) persisted longer than fundamental analysis suggested but resolved in weeks, not quarters.

**Claim 2 (Credit reprices next):**
The equity-implied vs. actual HY spread divergence (50-75bp, 3 of 4 episodes resolved via credit widening) is the concrete signal. Credit is structurally slow to reprice: covenant-lite >90% of leveraged loans means defaults are delayed (extend and pretend), and carry income incentivizes holding. But when credit reprices, the mechanism is non-linear — dealer balance sheet constraints create a cliff effect. The 40%+ of Russell 2000 unprofitable (Ponzi financing by Minsky definition) represents the weakest link. Rate markets have already moved (ACM term premium from -50bp to +50-100bp); credit has not followed.

**Claim 3 (Global duration supply):**
U.S. gross issuance $2-2.5T/yr + European rearmament ($100-150B/yr incremental, with Germany's EUR 500B fund and France's €413B LPM) + Japan's refunding needs at 250%+ debt/GDP = unprecedented global sovereign duration supply. QT ($60B/month = $720B/yr) compounds this. The KB documents fiscal_supply_duration_pricing (confidence 8) and european_rearmament_fiscal_shift (confidence 7) separately but hasn't aggregated the global supply impact. Pre-QE, global sovereign duration supply was a binding constraint on yields; we are returning to that regime, but with structurally higher government leverage.

**Claim 4 (Fiscal-tariff interaction):**
Tariffs create fiscal revenue ($200-400B at discussed 10-25% rates on major trading partners) while simultaneously raising consumer prices (0.5-1.5% CPI impact per Peterson Institute estimates) and compressing real growth via supply chain disruption and retaliation. This is arithmetically a regressive fiscal tightening (revenue up) combined with an inflationary supply shock — a combination that partially offsets DOGE spending cuts on the revenue side while worsening the inflation constraint on Fed easing. No standard "risk-on" or "risk-off" regime captures this; it requires stagflationary positioning.

**Claim 5 (Correlation regime transition):**
The KB's fiscal_supply_duration_pricing → stock_bond_correlation_bifurcation relationship (confidence 8) documents the mechanism. The 1965-69 analogue: large fiscal deficits (Vietnam + Great Society) at full employment → positive stock-bond correlation → resolved into fiscal/inflation dominance lasting until 1982. The 2003-06 analogue: large deficits (Iraq + tax cuts) → split correlation → resolved via monetary dominance (Fed tightening restored discipline). The current fiscal backdrop is structurally more 1965-69: deficit is larger (6-7% vs. ~3-4%), the political constraint on consolidation is tighter (mandatory spending + geopolitical floor), and the monetary offset is more limited (above-target inflation constrains the Fed).

**Claim 6 (Gold/IG credit trade):**
Scenario matrix:
- *Fiscal dominance accelerates* (25% probability): Gold +15-25%, IG credit -5-8% (spread widening). Trade profits significantly.
- *Muddle through / slow burn* (45% probability): Gold +5-10% (central bank buying continues), IG credit flat to -2%. Trade profits modestly.
- *Acute fiscal crisis / basis trade unwind* (15% probability): Gold +20-30% (flight to real assets), IG credit -8-15%. Trade profits significantly.
- *Credible consolidation + disinflation* (15% probability): Gold -10-15%, IG credit +2-3%. Trade loses modestly.
Expected value is clearly positive, with convexity in the trade's favor.

**Claim 7 (Dollar as arbiter):**
G7 fiscal divergence (U.S. -6.5 to -7%, Euro Area -3%, Canada -1.5 to -2%) at the widest since post-WWII. Historical pattern: 1981-85 (tight money + wide deficit = dollar up 50%); 2001-04 (easy money + wide deficit = dollar down 35%); 2017-19 (mixed = initial strength then fading). Current mix (Fed on hold, wide deficits) maps to 2001-04. But European fiscal expansion (rearmament) narrows the divergence on the other side, complicating the signal. The dollar is where the fiscal narrative meets the strongest structural resistance (reserve currency demand, rate differentials).

## Connections to Other Topics

**To real estate cycles:** The global duration supply surge (Claim 3) directly reinforces term_premium_cre_valuation_driver — if term premium has a structural fiscal floor, CRE cap rate compression requires either recession or QE, neither of which the fiscal landscape makes easy. The fiscal_policy_cre_crosscurrent remains the binding framework: deficits support CRE earnings (Kalecki) but prevent valuation recovery (term premium). Cross-asset implication: CRE is a *negative carry, positive convexity* position — it loses modestly in the muddle-through but profits significantly if fiscal consolidation triggers enough recession to collapse term premium.

**To monetary policy:** The monetary_policy_trap is the binding constraint on portfolio construction. If the Fed cannot deploy the March 2020 playbook (massive QE) during a fiscal-supply-driven crisis because inflation is above target, the traditional "buy duration into a crisis" trade is impaired. This breaks the most basic cross-asset hedge in institutional portfolios.

**To EM sovereign credit:** The ag_shock_em_fiscal_leading_indicator (agricultural prices lead EM CDS by 2-4 months) connects to the tariff-fiscal interaction: tariffs on agricultural inputs could create a food price transmission channel to EM fiscal stress, compounding the G7 fiscal divergence spillover.

**To commodity markets:** The china_commodity_demand_mix_shift (copper up, iron ore down) interacts with the global fiscal supply surge: if European rearmament and U.S. IRA spending both drive copper-intensive investment while Chinese property declines reduce iron ore demand, the copper/iron ore ratio becomes a fiscal regime indicator.

**To geopolitical risk:** The geopolitical_spending_floor (1.5-2.0% GDP) and us_china_fiscal_accelerator create a one-way ratchet on fiscal expenditure that constrains all consolidation scenarios. Cross-asset implication: defense sector equities have an implicit fiscal put that strengthens as geopolitical tensions persist.

## Open Questions

1. **Which link in the self-reinforcing chain (Claim 1) breaks first, and what is the catalyst?** The KB identifies multiple potential triggers (basis trade unwind, sovereign downgrade, DOGE over-delivery) but no framework for assessing which is most likely to be the proximate cause versus merely an amplifier.

2. **What is the cross-country elasticity of substitution between U.S. Treasuries and European sovereign bonds?** The european_rearmament_fiscal_shift creates Bund supply, but whether this raises or lowers UST term premium depends on whether reserve managers treat them as substitutes (UST loses demand) or complements (global duration risk premium rises together). This is the single most important unresolved parameter for global fixed income allocation.

3. **Can the fiscal-tariff interaction be decomposed quantitatively?** The tariff revenue ($200-400B), CPI impact (0.5-1.5%), and growth drag need to be netted against DOGE savings ($50-200B) and existing deficit dynamics. No cross-asset model currently integrates these three fiscal vectors simultaneously.

4. **What is the correct duration hedge for a portfolio when stock-bond correlation is maturity-dependent?** If the front end maintains negative correlation (monetary-driven) while the long end shifts positive (fiscal-driven), the optimal hedge is a barbell of short-duration bonds (hedge) and long-duration TIPS (inflation protection). But this creates convexity risk that standard risk models don't capture.

5. **Is private credit ($1.8T, largely unobservable) the hidden variable that makes the credit repricing (Claim 2) more severe than expected?** Covenant-lite leveraged loans and PIK toggles may be masking the true stress in corporate balance sheets. If private credit is the "shadow" credit market, the equity-implied spread divergence may understate the actual credit risk by 25-50bp.

6. **What is the terminal state of the correlation regime transition?** The KB documents that both prior split episodes resolved within 2-4 years, but the resolution direction was opposite. We are approximately 2 years into the current split. If resolution means sustained positive stock-bond correlation (1965-69 path), the implications for pension funding ratios, risk parity strategies, and insurance company ALM are profound — these are the portfolio construction frameworks built on the assumption of negative correlation.

7. **Does the Kalecki channel work differently when deficit composition is dominated by interest payments AND tariff revenue simultaneously?** Interest payments (MPC ~0.3-0.5) are lower-multiplier than transfers (MPC ~0.7-1.2), and tariff revenue is extracted from consumers (negative MPC). The net fiscal impulse of a deficit funded increasingly by interest payments and tariff revenue may be substantially weaker than the headline deficit number suggests — meaning the Kalecki profit floor is already thinner than the R² of 0.55-0.65 implies for the current regime.
