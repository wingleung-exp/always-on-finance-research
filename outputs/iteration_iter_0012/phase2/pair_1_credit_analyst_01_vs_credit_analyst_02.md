## REFUTED_CLAIMS

**1. Agent B, Claim 4: "The maturity wall is the single most reliable predictor of when a risk appetite regime shift becomes self-reinforcing" — overstated to the point of being misleading.**

The maturity wall is a *conditional* amplifier, not a *predictor*. "Predictor" implies it tells you *when* the regime will shift. It does not. The 2020-2021 wall was neutralized by Fed intervention. The 2015-2016 energy wall produced a sector-specific dislocation, not a broad regime shift. The 2012-2013 wall was absorbed by the post-GFC recovery. Maturity walls have existed in every credit cycle; they amplify transitions that occur for other reasons but do not independently cause them. Agent A's iter_0008-corrected framing ("binary converter") is more defensible. Agent B's 8/10 confidence should be closer to 6/10 for the *predictive* aspect, though the refinancing arithmetic supporting the *amplification* aspect is solid.

**2. Agent A, Claim 7: The demographic adjustment raising the distressed ratio threshold to 12-14% — insufficiently supported.**

Agent A rates this at 5/10, which is generous. The claim rests on a knowledge base concept rated at confidence 6 and a single data point (March 2020) that is irreparably confounded by the Fed's $750B corporate credit backstop (SMCCF). Removing the Fed intervention, there is no evidence that pension rebalancing alone would have prevented spreads from widening further. The Japanese life insurer bid is real but concentrated in IG, not HY, where the distressed ratio is measured. The threshold adjustment from 10% to 12-14% implies a precision (±2 percentage points) that the evidence cannot support. This is a plausible hypothesis that has been dressed up as a calibrated result.

**3. Agent B, Claim 5: "EBITDA addbacks of 25-40% mean leverage multiples are 1-2x higher than headline figures" — presented as a market-wide fact when it is a feature of specific deal segments.**

Moody's and S&P research on EBITDA addbacks documents the problem in *sponsored leveraged buyouts*, particularly in healthcare, software, and business services. Extending this to the entire private credit universe (~$1.7-1.8T) without qualification is misleading. Many private credit portfolios include asset-based lending, specialty finance, and real estate credit where addbacks are minimal or structurally different. The directional point (reported leverage understates true leverage in parts of the portfolio) is valid; the quantification (25-40% overstatement, 1-2x leverage gap) is cherry-picked from the worst segments and applied universally.

**4. Agent A, Claim 3: The specific magnitude of the non-default residual (35-45bp) — false precision.**

Agent A acknowledges this (6/10 confidence), but even that may be too high for the specific number. The decomposition requires three model-dependent inputs: expected default losses (varies by methodology and horizon), liquidity premium (which Agent A estimates at 15-25bp with no cited source for IG specifically), and the "pure risk appetite residual" (derived as a residual of residuals). Each layer of estimation compounds uncertainty. The *direction* (thinner cushion than 2019) is plausible at 7-8/10; the specific range of 35-45bp is at best 4/10. Agent A should have led with the directional claim and presented the magnitude as illustrative rather than estimated.

**5. Agent A's claim that the rates-credit correlation represents a "new structural equilibrium" (Claim 6) — premature.**

A correlation that "oscillates between -0.1 and +0.3" is not an equilibrium — it's a range wide enough to include the old regime (-0.1 end) and a meaningfully different regime (+0.3 end). Calling this an equilibrium after ~3 years of observation, with no Fed cutting cycle during this period to test the lower bound, is premature. Agent A's own open question 8 ("What happens to this correlation if the Fed begins sustained cutting?") implicitly concedes the point. This is better described as an *unresolved regime* than a *new equilibrium*.
