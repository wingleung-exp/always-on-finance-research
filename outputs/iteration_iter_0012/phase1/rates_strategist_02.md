## Key Claims

1. **The exchange rate channel is the most powerful but least controllable transmission mechanism of monetary policy, and the current configuration of global central bank divergence (Fed on hold at 4.25-4.50%, ECB at 2.50% and easing, BoJ at 0.75% and tightening, PBOC at 3.10% and easing) has created a trilemma of conflicting exchange rate channel objectives — where each central bank's desired FX outcome is inconsistent with at least one other's — producing an unstable equilibrium in which FX regimes are being implicitly coordinated through verbal intervention and reserve management rather than explicit policy rate adjustments, effectively making FX the residual variable that absorbs policy divergence that cannot be resolved through domestic transmission channels.**

2. **The Dollar Smile framework requires recalibration for a regime in which the left tail (US recession → dollar strength via safe haven) and the right tail (US exceptionalism → dollar strength via growth differentials) are both weakened: the left tail is impaired because the Fed's reaction function is asymmetrically constrained by fiscal dominance concerns (cutting aggressively into a 6-7% deficit risks term premium blowout), and the right tail is impaired because US growth exceptionalism is narrowing as European fiscal expansion (German infrastructure package, EU defense spending) provides a competing growth impulse — leaving the middle of the smile (global synchronized easing → dollar weakness) as the dominant regime, but with a flatter smile curve than historical norms, meaning dollar drawdowns are shallower and dollar rallies from risk-off events are weaker, compressing the range of DXY outcomes to approximately 95-108 over a 12-month horizon (vs. the 90-115 range that the standard smile would imply).**

3. **The BoJ normalization cycle represents the single most consequential central bank policy transmission test in global FX markets: the BoJ is attempting to exit ZIRP/NIRP while simultaneously managing a yen-denominated carry trade of $4-6T notional, and the transmission mechanism is fundamentally asymmetric — rate hikes transmit immediately to carry unwind pressure via the expectations channel (forward guidance moves USD/JPY 2-4 big figures per hawkish surprise), but the intended domestic transmission (higher rates → stronger yen → lower imported inflation → normalized consumption patterns) operates with a 6-18 month lag that the BoJ cannot credibly pre-commit through — creating a window of vulnerability where the carry unwind runs ahead of the domestic economic adjustment, producing disorderly FX moves that could force the BoJ to pause normalization prematurely, trapping it in a "normalization paradox" where the act of tightening generates financial instability that prevents further tightening.**

4. **Financial conditions indices (FCIs) across major economies have diverged from policy rates to a degree not seen since 2020-2021, driven primarily by the FX and equity price channels offsetting the interest rate channel: US financial conditions have loosened approximately 75-100bp (in Goldman Sachs FCI-equivalent terms) since October 2023 despite only 100bp of Fed cuts, because equity appreciation (+25-30% SPX), credit spread compression (IG from 140bp to ~100bp), and a range-bound dollar have done more easing work than the rate cuts themselves — meaning the Fed's effective stance is considerably easier than the 4.25-4.50% policy rate suggests, and the FX regime is a critical input to understanding why the Fed can remain on hold without overtightening.**

5. **PBOC monetary policy transmission to the exchange rate is operating through a fundamentally different mechanism than textbook models suggest: rather than rate differentials driving CNY weakness (the standard interest rate channel), the binding constraint is capital account management and the "managed depreciation corridor" of approximately 2-3% annual CNY depreciation that the PBOC has implicitly targeted since 2022 — using the daily fixing, reserve requirements on FX forwards, and macro-prudential tools to substitute for the interest rate channel that is impaired by the zero-lower-bound-adjacent domestic rate environment — making CNY the most "policy-administered" major currency and creating a regime where the FX channel is a tool of industrial policy (export competitiveness) rather than a transmission mechanism of monetary policy.**

6. **The credibility of central bank forward guidance as a tool for managing FX expectations has deteriorated measurably across all major central banks, as measured by the frequency of guidance revisions (Fed: 4 material pivots in 18 months through Q1 2026; ECB: 3 pivots; BoJ: 2 pivots from "patient" to "data-dependent" to active tightening), and this credibility erosion has a direct consequence for FX regime stability: when forward guidance is not believed, the expectations channel of monetary policy transmission is impaired, forcing FX adjustment to occur through spot market moves rather than smooth forward rate repricing — producing higher realized FX volatility relative to implied volatility and creating a structural underpricing of FX gamma that is rooted in the institutional degradation of the expectations channel rather than mere positioning dynamics.**

7. **The emerging BRICS+ currency diversification effort is not a near-term threat to the dollar's FX regime dominance but is creating a slow structural shift in central bank reserve composition that alters the transmission of Fed policy to EM FX: as EM central banks diversify 2-4% of reserves annually from USD to gold, CNY, and other alternatives (total non-traditional reserve share rising from ~7% in 2015 to ~12% in 2025 per IMF COFER data), the dollar liquidity channel — through which Fed tightening transmits to EM via dollar funding costs — is becoming incrementally less potent, requiring larger Fed rate moves to produce the same degree of EM financial conditions tightening, and creating a divergence between DM FX sensitivity to Fed policy (which remains high) and EM FX sensitivity (which is gradually declining for a subset of countries with sufficient reserves and bilateral swap lines).**

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Trilemma of Conflicting Exchange Rate Channel Objectives

The exchange rate channel is distinctive among monetary policy transmission mechanisms because it is inherently zero-sum across countries: one currency's depreciation is another's appreciation. The current central bank policy configuration creates three mutually inconsistent FX channel objectives:

**Fed (on hold, 4.25-4.50%):** The Fed's implicit FX preference is for a stable-to-modestly-weaker dollar. A stronger dollar would tighten financial conditions beyond the Fed's intent (each 10% DXY appreciation approximates 50bp of tightening through the trade-weighted channel per Clarida-era Fed research). A sharply weaker dollar would loosen conditions and risk reigniting goods inflation through import prices (ERPT of 0.03-0.08 for DM per KB concept fx_dollar_channel_mediation, confidence 9/10). The Fed's reaction function currently places 60-70% weight on labor market indicators and 30-40% on inflation, with the FX channel operating as a background financial conditions input rather than a direct policy target.

**ECB (easing, 2.50% and likely heading to 2.00%):** The ECB's implicit preference is for a stable-to-modestly-weaker euro. The Eurozone recovery — driven by the German fiscal expansion (EUR 500B+ infrastructure package announced late 2025) and EU defense spending acceleration — requires accommodative monetary policy, but a stronger euro would offset fiscal stimulus through the competitiveness channel. EUR/USD at 1.08-1.12 is consistent with ECB preferences; above 1.15 would trigger concern about deflationary impulse from currency appreciation. The ECB's easing cycle is explicitly motivated by below-target inflation and sluggish domestic demand — but the FX channel is working against them, as the euro has appreciated ~5% on a trade-weighted basis since mid-2025 on improved growth expectations from fiscal expansion.

**BoJ (tightening, 0.75% and heading to 1.00-1.25%):** The BoJ's explicit preference is for a stronger yen. Governor Ueda has repeatedly stated that excessive yen weakness is undesirable because it raises imported inflation beyond the BoJ's 2% target and compresses real household incomes. USD/JPY below 140 would be consistent with BoJ macro objectives; current levels around 148-152 represent an FX channel that is transmitting inflation rather than absorbing it.

**PBOC (easing, 3.10% MLF):** The PBOC's implicit preference is for a managed, gradual CNY depreciation. The domestic economy requires easier monetary conditions (property sector deleveraging, weak consumption), but aggressive easing would accelerate capital outflows.

The trilemma: the Fed wants dollar stability, the ECB wants euro stability-to-weakness, the BoJ wants yen strength, and the PBOC wants yuan stability-to-weakness. These are inconsistent. If the yen strengthens (BoJ's objective), either the dollar or the euro must weaken — but the Fed doesn't want meaningful dollar weakness (inflation risk) and the ECB doesn't want euro strength (competitiveness risk). The resolution has historically required one central bank to subordinate its FX channel objective to the others — and the key question is which central bank blinks. In 2022-2023, the BoJ subordinated (accepting extreme yen weakness). In 2024-2025, the Fed partially subordinated (accepting some dollar weakness as it began cutting). The 2026 configuration — with the BoJ actively tightening into a still-wide rate gap — makes the yen the pressure point where the trilemma will resolve.

This trilemma is being managed through implicit coordination mechanisms: verbal intervention (Japanese MOF warnings at 155+ USD/JPY), reserve management (China's reduction of UST holdings and accumulation of gold), and macro-prudential tools (PBOC FX forward reserve requirements). These are substitutes for explicit FX policy that operate outside the standard monetary policy transmission framework — a form of "shadow FX policy" that coexists with but is not fully reflected in policy rate settings.

### Claim 2: Dollar Smile Recalibration

The Dollar Smile framework (Stephen Jen, 2001) posits that the dollar strengthens in two regimes — US recession/global risk aversion (left tail) and US growth exceptionalism (right tail) — and weakens in the middle (global synchronized growth/easing). The current environment requires recalibrating both tails:

**Left tail impairment — Fed constrained by fiscal dominance:** In a standard recession, the Fed cuts aggressively (300-500bp), flight-to-quality flows strengthen the dollar, and the dollar rally is self-reinforcing as EM capital flees to safety. In the current fiscal configuration (6-7% deficit at full employment, debt/GDP ~124%, interest expense exceeding defense spending per KB framework fiscal_dominance_five_signal_sequence signals 1-2 confirmed), aggressive Fed cutting risks a term premium blowout. If the Fed were to cut 300bp to 1.25-1.50%, with a fiscal deficit already above 6%, the market might interpret this as monetization of the deficit — precisely the scenario where long-end yields rise even as the policy rate falls (the "gilt crisis" dynamic from UK/Truss 2022). This would tighten financial conditions through the long-end channel even as the Fed tries to ease through the short-end channel — a transmission mechanism breakdown that would prevent the dollar from rallying in the traditional risk-off manner because the US would lose its "safe haven premium" at the moment it is most needed.

Evidence for this constraint: the term premium on 10Y USTs has expanded from near-zero to approximately 50-75bp since mid-2024 (ACM term premium model), and Treasury auction bid-to-cover ratios have declined from 2.5x+ to 2.2-2.3x for 10Y and 30Y auctions. Primary dealer positioning shows increased duration avoidance. These are early signals that the fiscal backdrop is constraining the Treasury's ability to fund countercyclical policy — which directly impairs the dollar's left-tail response function.

**Right tail impairment — US growth exceptionalism narrowing:** The 2023-2025 period was characterized by dramatic US outperformance (US real GDP growth ~2.5-3.0% vs. Eurozone ~0.5-1.0%). This differential powered the "US exceptionalism" dollar rally (DXY from 100 to 107+). In 2026, the differential is narrowing: US growth is decelerating toward 1.5-2.0% as fiscal impulse fades and rate-sensitive sectors feel lagged effects, while European growth is accelerating toward 1.0-1.5% on fiscal expansion. The growth differential compression from ~200bp to ~50-75bp removes the fundamental support for the dollar's right tail.

Evidence: Bloomberg consensus GDP forecasts for 2026 show the US-Eurozone growth gap at ~75bp (2.0% vs. 1.25%), down from ~200bp in 2024. PMI surveys have converged: US manufacturing PMI ~51 vs. Eurozone ~49, down from a 5-point gap in mid-2025.

**Middle of the smile — dominant but flatter:** Global synchronized easing (ECB cutting, PBOC easing, smaller DM central banks in cutting cycles, Fed eventually following) favors dollar weakness — the classic middle-of-smile regime. But the smile is flatter because: (a) the left tail doesn't drop as far (fiscal constraints limit Fed cutting space), (b) the right tail doesn't rise as far (growth differential compression), and (c) the middle is shallower because the dollar's structural advantages (deepest capital markets, reserve currency status, energy independence) provide a floor. Net result: DXY range-bounded in approximately 95-108, vs. the 90-115 range the standard smile would imply.

**Critical caveat:** The Dollar Smile should be treated as a heuristic regime map, not a structural model. It has no microfoundation — it is an ex-post observation about dollar behavior in different growth environments. It does not specify transition dynamics, does not account for fiscal regime shifts, and has been fitted to a sample period (1990-2020) that may not be representative of a world with 120%+ US debt/GDP. The recalibration proposed here is itself subject to model uncertainty — the entire smile framework may break down under fiscal dominance.

### Claim 3: BoJ Normalization Paradox

The BoJ's policy transmission challenge is unique because it is tightening from a starting point (0.75%) where the interest rate channel has been dormant for 25+ years:

**Expectations channel (fast, powerful, destabilizing):** BoJ hawkish surprises transmit to USD/JPY within minutes. The July 2024 rate hike (to 0.25%) accompanied by hawkish Ueda commentary triggered a 12-big-figure USD/JPY decline (from ~161 to ~149) over three weeks, culminating in the August 5, 2024 carry crash (Nikkei -12% in a single session). The expectations channel is hyper-sensitive because carry trade positioning is massive ($4-6T notional per KB concept treasury_fx_dual_fragility) and levered — small changes in expected rate paths produce outsized FX moves through gamma hedging cascades and stop-loss triggers.

The January 2026 hike (to 0.75%) was better communicated and produced a more orderly 5-big-figure strengthening — suggesting the BoJ is learning. But each incremental hike moves closer to the "threshold zone" (estimated at BoJ policy rate of 1.00-1.25%, corresponding to USD/JPY ~135-140) where carry unwind dynamics become non-linear.

**Interest rate channel (slow, uncertain, untested):** Higher BoJ rates should transmit domestically through: (a) higher deposit rates — but Japanese banks have been slow, raising from ~0.01% to ~0.10%, a fraction of 75bp in policy hikes; (b) higher lending rates — but Japanese corporate lending is driven by relationship banking rather than marginal rate sensitivity (BoJ estimates 100bp increase reduces business fixed investment by only 0.3-0.5% over two years); (c) yen appreciation → lower imported inflation → improved real purchasing power — but this operates with a 6-18 month lag through the import price → PPI → CPI → consumption chain.

**The normalization paradox scenarios:**

- **Scenario A (steady hikes):** Yen strengthens toward 135-140, carry unwind becomes disorderly at threshold, BoJ pauses → yen partially reverses → BoJ credibility damaged → carry positions rebuild → repeat (stop-go pattern).

- **Scenario B (cautious hikes):** Yen strengthens modestly to 140-145, carry adjusts gradually, domestic transmission operates over 12-18 months → BoJ achieves soft normalization. This is the BoJ's desired outcome but requires exceptional communication and cooperative positioning dynamics.

- **Scenario C (external shock):** Global risk-off triggers carry unwind independent of BoJ → yen spikes to 125-130 → BoJ forced to intervene to prevent "excessive" strength → transmission mechanism breaks down as BoJ simultaneously tightens rates and intervenes to weaken currency.

The credit cycle carry unwind discriminant (KB concept, confidence 7.5/10) is critical: Scenario A escalates to Scenario C only with coincident global credit deterioration. Current spreads (IG ~100-110bp, HY ~370-390bp) are below danger thresholds (130bp/450bp), supporting Scenario A or B as base case. But the 2025-2027 maturity wall creates non-trivial probability of credit deterioration coinciding with BoJ normalization.

**Shunto wage linkage:** Sustainability of normalization depends on whether Shunto wage gains (5%+ in 2025-2026) translate to consumption growth. If Japanese consumers save the gains (household savings rate rose from ~3% to ~5% in 2023-2024), domestic transmission is impaired and the normalization case weakens. If consumers spend (March 2026 Tankan shows consumer spending intentions at highest since 2019), the virtuous cycle validates normalization. I assess KB concept japan_shunto_fx_impact should be upgraded from confidence 5/10 to 6/10 given 2026 Shunto results and improving Tankan, with the caveat that consumption validation remains the binding constraint.

### Claim 4: FCI-Policy Rate Divergence as FX Regime Input

**United States FCI decomposition since October 2023:**

| Channel | Contribution to Loosening | Mechanism |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Equity prices | ~30-35bp | SPX +25-30% raises wealth effect, lowers ERP |
| Credit spreads | ~20-25bp | IG compression 140bp → ~100bp reduces corporate borrowing cost |
| Dollar (TWI) | ~10-15bp | Modestly weaker dollar on trade-weighted basis |
| Mortgage rates | ~15-20bp | 30Y mortgage from ~8% to ~6.5%, partially reopening housing |
| Fed rate cuts | ~15-20bp | Direct policy rate pass-through to deposit/lending rates |

The FX channel contribution (~10-15bp) is modest because the dollar has been range-bound. The equity and credit channels have done the heavy lifting — meaning the Fed's effective stance is approximately 175-200bp easier than the 4.25-4.50% rate suggests (effective stance equivalent to ~2.25-2.75% in FCI terms). This explains why the Fed can remain on hold without overtightening.

**FX regime implication:** If conditions loosen significantly without Fed cuts (through equity/credit channels), the FX channel becomes less important for US transmission but MORE important as a residual adjustment variable for other countries. The ECB cannot rely on its equity market for equivalent loosening (Eurozone equities +15% vs. US +25-30%), and European credit markets are less developed. The ECB is therefore more FX-sensitive than the Fed — an asymmetry with direct implications for the policy coordination trilemma.

**Eurozone:** ECB financial conditions have tightened slightly since mid-2025 despite continued rate cuts, because euro appreciation (+5% TWI since June 2025) has offset rate cut transmission. This is the opposite problem — the FX channel is working against ECB intent. Governing Council members (Schnabel, Villeroy) have begun referencing "exchange rate developments" — a leading indicator of potential verbal intervention or accelerated cutting if euro strength persists above 1.12.

**Regime classification:** The current global FX regime is best described as "asymmetric transmission" — the US is in easy financial conditions regardless of FX, Europe is in tight conditions partially because of FX, and Japan is transitional with FX as the primary transmission mechanism. This differs fundamentally from the standard risk-on/risk-off binary.

### Claim 5: PBOC's Administered FX Regime

The PBOC operates the most explicitly managed FX regime among major central banks, substituting administrative tools for impaired interest rate channel transmission:

**The managed depreciation corridor:** Since 2022, USD/CNY has traded within an implicitly managed corridor allowing approximately 2-3% annual depreciation. The PBOC uses the daily fixing to anchor expectations, consistently setting CNY stronger than the previous close — an asymmetric intervention pattern resisting depreciation more than appreciation.

**Tools deployed (substituting for interest rate channel):**

1. **Daily fixing mechanism:** Sets CNY/USD fixing each morning as anchor for +/-2% trading band. Fixing has diverged from market equilibrium by 500-1500 pips, effectively imposing PBOC's FX view.

2. **FX forward reserve requirements:** 20% reserve requirement on banks selling FX forwards, raising cost of hedging yuan depreciation and discouraging speculative shorting.

3. **State bank intervention:** State-owned banks act as agents for spot/forward intervention, estimated at $5-15B per month based on PBoC balance sheet changes and BIS survey data.

4. **Capital account management:** Enforcement intensity of capital outflow restrictions increased since mid-2024, with stricter scrutiny of outward direct investment and crypto-related flows.

**Transmission implications:** CNY is not a market-determined exchange rate in the G10 sense. The interest rate channel (PBOC cuts → lower CNY rates → weaker CNY via carry differential) is deliberately suppressed by administrative tools. Instead, the FX channel operates as industrial policy: targeting exchange rate levels for export competitiveness and import substitution, while the interest rate is reserved for domestic credit management. This dual-channel separation is unique and means standard FX models (UIP, PPP, BEER) have limited explanatory power for CNY.

**Global FX regime impact:** China's managed regime acts as a shock absorber for global FX volatility — preventing CNY from falling as far in risk-off episodes as a free float would allow. But this creates a "stored energy" problem: the cumulative gap between administered and shadow equilibrium rates grows until discrete adjustment becomes necessary, as the 2015 devaluation demonstrated with non-linear global consequences.

### Claim 6: Forward Guidance Credibility Erosion and FX Vol

**Credibility erosion evidence:**

- **Fed:** The dot plot has become a poor policy predictor. December 2023 median projected 75bp of 2024 cuts; Fed delivered 100bp. December 2024 median projected 50bp of 2025 cuts; Fed delivered 0bp through Q1 2026. The gap between fed funds futures and median dot has averaged 50-75bp since 2024 vs. historical ~25bp — a quantitative measure of expectations channel impairment.

- **ECB:** Lagarde's "meeting-by-meeting" mantra has evolved from data-dependency to communication crutch. Pivots from "inflation too high" (2023) → "inflation on track" (2024) → "growth risks dominate" (2025) → implicit exchange rate concern (2026). Implied vol on Euribor options rose ~20% since mid-2025 despite the rate cutting cycle being established.

- **BoJ:** The August 2024 carry crash was fundamentally a failure of the expectations channel — the hawkish pivot was not adequately pre-communicated, forcing violent spot adjustment instead of gradual forward repricing. The BoJ has improved since (January 2026 well-telegraphed), but credibility damage persists in elevated short-dated USD/JPY implied vols (25-delta 1W puts at 13-15% vs. 3M at 10%).

**Structural consequence for FX gamma:** The credibility erosion provides an independent explanation for FX gamma underpricing, separate from the carry-driven vol suppression in the KB (fx_vol_suppression, confidence 6.75/10). When guidance is not believed, "smooth adjustment" through forwards is replaced by "discrete adjustment" through spot — producing structurally higher realized-to-implied vol ratios. I estimate this adds 1-2 vol points of gamma underpricing beyond what carry dynamics explain, because the market uses pre-2022 guidance credibility priors in a post-2022 world. This independently corroborates the fx_gamma_dominance thesis (KB, 7.5/10) from a monetary policy credibility angle rather than positioning dynamics.

### Claim 7: BRICS+ Reserve Diversification and Fed Transmission Impairment

**Quantitative evidence:**

- IMF COFER: USD share of allocated reserves declining from ~66% (2015) to ~58% (2025) — ~0.8pp/year. Non-traditional currencies (CNY, AUD, CAD, KRW, SGD) risen from ~2% to ~7%. Gold from ~10% to ~17% of total reserves as EM central banks (China, India, Turkey, Poland) accelerated purchases.

- Pace accelerated post-2022 following Russian reserve freezing (~$300B sanctioned), demonstrating geopolitical confiscation risk — a qualitative shift in reserve management.

**Transmission impact through three sub-channels:**

1. **Portfolio rebalancing:** Fed tightening attracts capital from EM to US, strengthening dollar, weakening EM FX. Attenuated as EM central banks hold fewer dollar reserves — lower "natural" dollar exposure means smaller rebalancing pressure.

2. **Dollar funding costs:** EM hard-currency bond issuance declined from $450B/year (2021) to ~$350B (2025), partially replaced by local currency and CNY-denominated lending.

3. **Swap line architecture:** PBOC bilateral swap lines with 40+ central banks (~$580B equivalent) provide dollar-free liquidity backstop, reducing expected severity of dollar squeezes.

**Two-tier EM FX regime:** Diversification is non-uniform. Tier 1 (China, India, Saudi Arabia, Brazil, UAE) — sufficient reserves, swap lines, developed domestic markets — partially insulated from Fed transmission (5-10% depreciation in stress vs. 10-20% historically). Tier 2 (Turkey, Egypt, Pakistan, Argentina, sub-Saharan Africa) — fully exposed, potentially amplified as outflows concentrate in narrower set (20-40% depreciation risk).

This extends the KB's em_vulnerability_assessment_framework by adding reserve diversification as a discriminant variable.

**Timeline:** At 0.8pp/year, meaningful impairment (>20% reduction in Fed-to-EM FX beta) requires ~10-15 years. Non-linear acceleration possible if: (a) another geopolitical confiscation event, (b) credible BRICS+ payment infrastructure replaces SWIFT for material trade share, or (c) gold/commodity-backed reserve instruments gain acceptance. None is base case for 12 months; all plausible over 5-10 years.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the Dollar Smile framework fundamentally broken under fiscal dominance, or merely recalibrated?** The recalibration assumes the smile structure survives with modified parameters. But fiscal dominance may introduce a qualitatively different regime where dollar behavior cannot be mapped onto a smile at all. Critical test: does the dollar rally in the next US recession? If not (or only modestly), the smile is broken; if strongly, the recalibration was too aggressive.

2. **What is the BoJ's true USD/JPY pain threshold?** Is there a level (155? 160?) above which the BoJ accelerates tightening regardless of domestic data? A level (130? 125?) below which it pauses? Understanding this reaction function is critical for carry unwind scenario analysis but requires inference from MOF intervention history and BoJ communication — it is not directly observable.

3. **Can the ECB tolerate EUR/USD above 1.15 without accelerating cuts?** The ECB is more trade-dependent (~45% of GDP vs. ~25% US). EUR/USD at 1.15-1.18 forces a dilemma: accelerate cuts (risking asset price inflation) or accept currency tightening (undermining fiscal stimulus). The ECB's revealed preference is unknown — it has not faced this since 2017-2018.

4. **How does the PBOC managed depreciation corridor interact with global risk-off?** In March 2020, PBOC allowed ~3% USD/CNY move in two weeks. In more severe stress, would PBOC defend the corridor or allow discrete devaluation? The answer determines whether China absorbs or amplifies global FX volatility during the next crisis.

5. **Is the two-tier EM FX regime already priced?** Differential FX vol and CDS between Tier 1 and Tier 2 EM may already reflect the structural divide. If so, the analytical insight about Fed transmission impairment is correct but not actionable. Requires formal event study: does Fed tightening produce statistically different FX betas for Tier 1 vs. Tier 2 after controlling for credit quality and commodity exposure?

6. **What is the equilibrium level of forward guidance credibility?** If the post-2022 world is structurally more uncertain, lower guidance credibility may be appropriate rather than impaired. If so, FX gamma pricing may correctly reflect higher uncertainty rather than systematically underpricing it. This is a question about whether guidance *should* be believed, not whether it *is* believed.

7. **What data would change the BoJ normalization paradox assessment?** Three specific signals: (a) Japanese household consumption growth sustaining above 1.5% real for two consecutive quarters — would confirm domestic transmission is working; (b) BoJ policy rate reaching 1.25% without a >10-big-figure USD/JPY move — would demonstrate gradual normalization can avoid the paradox; (c) Japanese bank deposit rates rising to >0.50% — would confirm interest rate channel pass-through improving.
