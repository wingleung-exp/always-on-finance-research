# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The risk appetite regime has shifted from "conditional risk-on" (as assessed in iter_0007) to "fragile neutrality" — a state where the policy rate gap is approximately zero, the output gap is closing from above, and the conditioning variable (inflation convergence) has become a coin flip rather than a base case.** The distinguishing feature of fragile neutrality versus conditional risk-on is that the probability distribution over the next regime has become bimodal: roughly 40% probability of a renewed easing cycle that re-establishes risk-on, and 35% probability of an inflation re-acceleration or exogenous shock that forces restrictive policy and triggers risk-off. The remaining 25% is persistence of the current ambiguous state. This is not a stable equilibrium — it is an unstable saddle point from which the economy will be pushed in one direction by incoming data.

2. **The Taylor Rule gap has effectively closed to zero as of Q1 2026, which is the single most important regime signal because zero-gap environments are historically the inflection points where risk appetite regimes turn.** With the FFR corridor at approximately 3.75-4.25%, core PCE at 2.3-2.6%, and Laubach-Williams r* estimates having drifted to 1.0-1.5%, the implied real rate of ~1.5% sits within the confidence interval of r*. A standard Taylor Rule (r = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(y − y*)) with r* = 1.25%, π = 2.4%, π* = 2.0%, and y − y* ≈ 0.0% prescribes a rate of approximately 3.85% — almost exactly where the FFR sits. In the last four business cycles, zero-gap environments preceded regime shifts within 2-4 quarters, though the direction of the shift was data-dependent.

3. **Demographic forces have structurally altered the risk appetite regime map by raising the floor on inflation and lowering the ceiling on potential growth, compressing the space in which unconditional risk-on can exist.** The labor scarcity channel identified in the knowledge base (aging population → declining prime-age labor force participation → wage-push inflation → Phillips Curve steepening) means that the economy hits capacity constraints at lower growth rates than in prior cycles. This shortens the duration of expansion-phase risk-on regimes and raises the frequency of supply-shock-driven risk-off episodes. The Taylor Rule demographic modification framework (from the knowledge base) implies that r* is lower in aging economies, but the inflation floor is higher — a combination that narrows the policy rate gap band within which accommodative financial conditions can persist without generating inflationary pressure.

4. **The fiscal impulse has transitioned from a risk-on accelerator (2020-2024) to a risk appetite regime destabilizer, because the stock of outstanding debt is now large enough that fiscal sustainability concerns affect the term premium, creating a channel through which fiscal expansion tightens financial conditions even as it stimulates aggregate demand.** This is the "fiscal paradox" of the current regime: deficit spending raises r* and term premia (tightening the discount rate for risk assets) even as it supports corporate earnings (loosening the cash-flow fundamentals for risk assets). The net effect on risk appetite is indeterminate and depends on the maturity structure of debt issuance, the elasticity of private savings to interest rates, and whether the marginal buyer of Treasuries is domestic (savings-glut channel, partially offset) or foreign (requiring a risk premium, amplified). US structural deficits of ~6% of GDP at near-full employment have no peacetime precedent in the post-war era, placing us in uncharted territory for the fiscal-risk-appetite nexus.

5. **Forward guidance has become the dominant determinant of risk appetite in the current regime, dominating the actual policy rate level — and this creates a fragility that the standard Taylor Rule framework does not capture.** The 2023-2025 experience demonstrated conclusively that equity markets respond more to the expected future policy path (as embedded in dot plots, FOMC minutes language, and press conferences) than to the current rate level. The S&P 500 rallied ~25% from October 2023 to early 2025 despite restrictive real rates, driven entirely by the expectation of cuts. This means the risk appetite regime is effectively determined by the Fed's communication, not its actions — and communication can shift abruptly in response to a single data release or geopolitical event. An expectations-augmented Taylor Rule that includes the term E[Σ(r_{t+i} − r*_{t+i})] over the expected path would better capture this dynamic, but such a rule is inherently more volatile because expectations are more labile than actual rates.

6. **The stock-bond correlation regime is the transmission mechanism through which the type of shock (demand vs. supply) maps onto risk appetite, and the current oscillation between negative and positive correlation reflects genuine uncertainty about whether the dominant shock to the 2026 economy will be demand-side (AI-driven productivity boom, fiscal expansion) or supply-side (demographic labor scarcity, deglobalization, tariff friction).** This uncertainty is not resolvable within the New Keynesian framework without additional data, because the framework's predictions are conditional on the shock type. The practical consequence: hedging strategies that assume a stable correlation sign will be whipsawed, and the only robust approach is to maintain exposure to both correlation regimes through explicit optionality.

7. **Cross-country risk appetite regime divergence has reached a local maximum, driven by the desynchronization of inflation cycles, monetary policy cycles, and fiscal stances across the US, Eurozone, Japan, and China — and this divergence is itself a source of global financial instability through the capital flow channel.** Under Mundell-Fleming, persistent interest rate differentials drive capital flows that alter exchange rates and financial conditions in satellite economies. The current configuration — US rates elevated, ECB cutting more aggressively, BOJ still normalizing, PBoC easing — creates a volatile matrix of bilateral exchange rate pressures and carry trade incentives. The yen carry trade, partially unwound in August 2024 but subsequently rebuilt, is the most fragile cross-country linkage: a further BOJ normalization step or a broad risk-off impulse could trigger a repeat unwind that propagates across asset classes within days.

8. **The natural rate of interest (r*) is better modeled as a regime-dependent variable than a slow-moving structural parameter, and the failure to account for this regime-dependence is the primary source of systematic error in real-time risk appetite assessment.** Building on the hysteresis-in-r* hypothesis from iter_0007, I now propose a more specific claim: r* should be decomposed into a structural component (determined by demographics, productivity trend, and time preference) and a cyclical component (determined by credit conditions, fiscal impulse, and animal spirits). The structural component is currently in the range of 0.5-1.0% (depressed by demographics, partially offset by AI-driven productivity), while the cyclical component has added 0.5-1.0% (driven by the fiscal impulse and elevated investment spending). The total r* of ~1.0-2.0% is therefore partly transitory — if the fiscal impulse fades or AI capex disappoints, the cyclical component will compress, and what appeared to be neutral policy will become restrictive, triggering an unexpected risk-off transition.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 — Fragile Neutrality Assessment

The shift from "conditional risk-on" to "fragile neutrality" is driven by three developments since the iter_0007 assessment:

First, the disinflation process has stalled. Core PCE, which was tracking steadily downward through 2024, has plateaued in the 2.3-2.6% range with sticky components (shelter, core services ex-housing) showing no further progress. The New Keynesian Phillips Curve predicts that the last mile of disinflation is the hardest: as the output gap closes, the marginal reduction in inflation per unit of slack increases (the Phillips Curve is convex near the origin). This is consistent with the "nonlinear Phillips Curve" evidence from Ball, Mankiw & Romer (1988) and more recently from Benigno & Eggertsson (2023), who show that inflation becomes more responsive to positive output gaps than negative ones when the economy is near potential.

Second, geopolitical trade policy uncertainty has introduced a supply-shock distribution that was not present in the iter_0007 assessment. Tariff escalation (actual or threatened) operates as a negative supply shock — raising import prices, disrupting supply chains, and shifting the Phillips Curve adversely. This is analytically equivalent to the 1970s oil shocks in its effect on the central bank's reaction function: the Fed faces a trade-off between stabilizing inflation (by tightening, triggering risk-off) and stabilizing output (by easing, accommodating inflation). The ambiguity about which leg of this trade-off the Fed will prioritize generates the bimodal distribution over future regimes.

Third, financial conditions have partially re-tightened despite no change in the policy rate, driven by rising term premia and a widening of the gap between front-end rates (policy-driven) and back-end rates (fiscal-supply-driven). This bifurcation of the yield curve means that the single-rate summary of the Taylor Rule gap is insufficient — the effective financial conditions facing the economy depend on a maturity-weighted average of rates, and this average has risen even as the policy rate has not.

The probability distribution — 40% renewed easing / 35% restrictive shift / 25% persistence — is calibrated by analogy to prior zero-gap environments:
- 1995 (soft landing): Eased → risk-on. Fed cut 75bp preemptively despite low unemployment. Inflation was low and falling. ~40% analog weight.
- 2000 (hard landing): Held too long → risk-off. Dot-com excesses triggered equity-led downturn. ~20% analog weight.
- 2006-2007 (extended neutral, then risk-off): Held rates at 5.25% for over a year. Financial fragilities built up, eventually triggering crisis. ~15% analog weight.
- 2018-2019 (brief overshoot, then cut): Over-tightened slightly, Q4 2018 selloff, reversed to cuts. ~25% analog weight.

The weighted composite supports the bimodal distribution with a slight lean toward easing but non-trivial risk-off probability.

### Claim 2 — Taylor Rule Gap at Zero

The arithmetic is straightforward. Using the standard formulation: r_Taylor = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(y − y*).

Inputs (Q1 2026 estimates):
- r* = 1.25% (midpoint of Laubach-Williams range 1.0-1.5%)
- π (core PCE) = 2.4%
- π* = 2.0%
- y − y* ≈ 0.0% (CBO output gap closed or slightly positive)

r_Taylor = 1.25 + 2.4 + 0.5(0.4) + 0.5(0.0) = 3.85%

FFR midpoint ≈ 4.0%.

Gap = 4.0 - 3.85 = +0.15%, which is effectively zero within the measurement uncertainty of any input variable.

The significance of the zero gap: Adrian & Duarte (2018) show that the "vulnerability index" for financial stability — a downside-risk measure derived from financial conditions — is most sensitive to changes in the Taylor Rule gap when the gap is near zero. Small perturbations in either direction have outsized effects on the tail of the GDP growth distribution. This is because near-zero gaps correspond to inflection points in the nonlinear relationship between monetary stance and financial conditions: accommodative conditions encourage leverage buildup, while restrictive conditions trigger deleveraging, and the transition between these regimes is discontinuous at the zero boundary.

Historical evidence on zero-gap regime transitions:
- December 1994 – January 1995: Taylor gap crossed zero from negative to positive during the 1994 tightening cycle. Risk-off episode (Orange County, Mexican peso crisis, bond market selloff) occurred within two quarters.
- Late 2005 – early 2006: Gap crossed zero as the Fed took rates to 5.25%. Risk assets continued to rally for approximately 18 months before the subprime crisis, but the seeds of the crisis (excessive leverage in structured credit) were planted precisely during the zero-gap transition.
- Late 2018: Gap briefly positive. Q4 2018 selloff within one quarter.
- Late 2023 – early 2024: Gap moved from positive toward zero as disinflation proceeded. Risk-on rallied in anticipation of crossing into negative territory.

The pattern: zero-gap environments are necessary but not sufficient for regime transitions. They create the conditions under which a catalyzing shock — data surprise, geopolitical event, or market microstructure dislocation — can trigger a nonlinear shift.

### Claim 3 — Demographic Compression of the Risk-On Space

The knowledge base contains extensive analysis of demographics' effect on growth and inflation. The key transmission channels to risk appetite:

**Channel 1: Phillips Curve steepening.** An aging population shrinks the pool of available labor, reducing the economy's ability to expand output without hitting wage-push inflation. Formally, the slope parameter κ in the New Keynesian Phillips Curve (π = βE[π_{t+1}] + κ(y − y*)) increases when labor supply elasticity declines. A steeper Phillips Curve means that a smaller positive output gap generates more inflation, requiring the central bank to tighten earlier and more aggressively in the expansion. This truncates the duration of risk-on phases.

Empirical support: Japan's experience from 1990-2020 is instructive but complicated by the ZLB (the Phillips Curve was nearly flat at the ZLB, masking the structural steepening). More relevant is Europe's experience in 2022-2023, where labor scarcity in aging economies (Germany, Italy) contributed to core inflation that proved stickier than in younger demographics (US, relative to expectations). The knowledge base framework "dual_squeeze_default_model" captures this: aging simultaneously raises wage costs (supply-side inflation) and reduces the labor-force reserve that historically absorbed demand shocks.

**Channel 2: Structural r* depression.** The overlapping-generations (OLG) extension of the NK model (Eggertsson, Mehrotra & Robbins, 2019) shows that aging reduces r* through the excess savings channel: a larger cohort of retirees and near-retirees accumulates safe assets, depressing the natural rate. The "Taylor Rule demographic modification" framework in the knowledge base formalizes this. The implication for risk appetite: lower r* means the central bank must maintain lower nominal rates to achieve the same degree of accommodation, pushing more frequently against the zero lower bound and requiring unconventional tools (QE, YCC) that distort risk pricing.

**Channel 3: Correlation regime shift.** As argued in iter_0006's cross-asset analysis and supported by the knowledge base, demographic labor scarcity is a supply-side force that biases the economy toward supply-shock-dominant dynamics. This shifts the stock-bond correlation positive, undermining the diversification foundation of risk-on portfolio construction. When the 60/40 portfolio stops working as a risk-management framework, institutional investors must either accept higher portfolio volatility or reduce risk exposure — structurally lowering aggregate risk appetite.

### Claim 4 — Fiscal Paradox

The IS-LM framework makes the mechanics transparent. Fiscal expansion shifts the IS curve rightward, raising equilibrium output and the interest rate. In a closed economy with a Taylor-following central bank, the IS shift is partially offset by monetary tightening (the LM/Taylor Rule response). In an open economy (Mundell-Fleming), the higher interest rate appreciates the currency, crowding out net exports. Either way, the net stimulative effect on demand and earnings supports risk appetite.

But the current cycle introduces a complication absent from the textbook model: the stock of debt matters, not just the flow. At debt-to-GDP ratios above ~100% (the US is at approximately 120% gross, ~98% public debt held by public), marginal debt issuance raises the term premium because:
1. **Duration supply effect**: The Treasury must finance the deficit by issuing more duration, increasing the supply of long-dated bonds relative to demand. This raises term premia — observable in the ACM term premium model, which has risen from negative in 2020-2021 to approximately +30-50bp in 2026.
2. **Fiscal sustainability discount**: At high debt/GDP, some fraction of investors begins to price the possibility of fiscal adjustment (tax increases, spending cuts) or financial repression (inflation above target to erode real debt burden). This raises the real term premium.
3. **Crowding out of private investment**: Higher government borrowing competes with corporate borrowing, raising the cost of capital even if the central bank is neutral.

The paradox: fiscal expansion supports earnings growth (bullish for equity cash flows) while raising the discount rate (bearish for equity valuations). Which effect dominates is empirically contingent. During 2020-2023, the earnings effect dominated because fiscal stimulus was enormous and directed at consumption (CARES/ARP). In 2024-2026, as the fiscal impulse fades from its pandemic peak but the debt stock remains elevated, the discount rate effect has gained relative strength — visible in the compression of equity risk premia (forward P/E elevated) occurring simultaneously with rising term premia (10Y yields higher than the short rate would suggest).

The Bianchi-Melosi (2017) regime-switching fiscal-monetary model provides the formal framework: the economy can be in an "active fiscal / passive monetary" regime (fiscal dominance, where the central bank accommodates fiscal needs) or a "passive fiscal / active monetary" regime (monetary dominance, where fiscal policy adjusts to monetary conditions). The current US configuration — large deficits with a Fed that claims data-dependence but has not tightened in response to fiscal expansion — is consistent with a transition toward active fiscal / passive monetary, i.e., incipient fiscal dominance. Under fiscal dominance, risk appetite is driven by fiscal credibility rather than monetary stance.

### Claim 5 — Forward Guidance Dominance

The empirical evidence is stark. From October 2023 to January 2025, the real federal funds rate was in the 2.0-2.5% range — substantially above all r* estimates. By the standard Taylor Rule gap framework, this should have produced a risk-off regime. Instead, the S&P 500 rallied approximately 40% and credit spreads tightened to multi-year lows. The explanation: the market priced the future path, not the current level.

This is consistent with the New Keynesian expectations theory of monetary transmission, formalized in Woodford (2003, Chapter 2) and Eggertsson & Woodford (2003): the IS curve depends on the entire expected path of real interest rates, not just the current rate. Mathematically: y_t = E_t[Σ_{i=0}^{∞} β^i (r_{t+i} - r*_{t+i})], where the output gap depends on the discounted sum of future real rate gaps. A central bank that signals future easing reduces the expected path even if the current rate is high, generating accommodative financial conditions today.

The fragility this creates: forward guidance is credible only insofar as the central bank's reaction function is predictable. A single data surprise — core CPI printing 0.4% m/m, for instance — can invalidate the expected path. When forward guidance shifts, the entire term structure of expectations reprices simultaneously, and risk appetite adjusts not incrementally but discontinuously. This is the "forward guidance puzzle" identified by Del Negro, Giannoni & Patterson (2023): in standard NK models, forward guidance is unrealistically powerful, and in practice, its power depends on a credibility equilibrium that can collapse rapidly.

The implication for risk appetite regime identification: the Taylor Rule gap must be augmented with a "term structure gap" — the difference between the market-implied forward rate path and the Taylor Rule-implied forward path. When the market prices a path that is significantly more accommodative than the Taylor Rule prescribes (i.e., the market expects cuts that the Taylor Rule does not justify), risk appetite is being supported by expectations that may prove unfounded. This was precisely the configuration in late 2023 — the market priced 6-7 cuts in 2024, the Taylor Rule prescribed 2-3, and the actual outcome was somewhere in between. The expectations overshoot created a risk appetite "air pocket" that was only partially corrected.

### Claim 6 — Stock-Bond Correlation and Shock Classification

The New Keynesian three-equation model generates clear predictions:
- **Demand shock** (IS curve shift): Output and inflation move in the same direction. The central bank responds by moving rates in the same direction as inflation. Bonds and equities move in opposite directions (negative correlation) because the rate response offsets the demand impulse.
- **Supply shock** (Phillips Curve shift): Output and inflation move in opposite directions. The central bank faces a trade-off. If it prioritizes inflation, it tightens, depressing both equities and bonds (positive correlation). If it prioritizes output, it eases, supporting both (negative correlation). The correlation sign depends on the reaction function.
- **Financial shock** (credit channel disruption): Output falls, inflation may be ambiguous. The central bank typically eases aggressively. Initially positive correlation (both sell off), then negative (bonds rally as the Fed cuts, equities stabilize).

The current environment features a superposition of shocks:
- **Positive demand shock**: Fiscal impulse still net positive, AI capex boom, wealth effects from elevated asset prices. Supports risk-on.
- **Negative supply shock**: Tariff-related input cost increases, demographic labor scarcity, energy transition costs. Supports risk-off.
- **Ambiguous financial channel**: Credit conditions are neither tight nor loose; the basis trade fragility is a latent risk but not an active constraint.

This superposition explains why the stock-bond correlation oscillates: on days/weeks when demand-shock narrative dominates (strong data, AI optimism), correlation is negative (equities up, bonds down — classic risk-on). On days/weeks when supply-shock narrative dominates (tariff headlines, sticky inflation data), correlation is positive (equities down, bonds down — stagflation signal). The frequency of oscillation has increased since mid-2024, with the 60-day rolling correlation swinging between -0.15 and +0.25 — consistent with a mixed-shock environment where neither type clearly dominates.

Campbell, Pflueger & Viceira (2020) provide the theoretical grounding: they decompose the stock-bond correlation into components driven by discount rate news, cash flow news, and inflation news. When inflation news dominates (as in supply-shock periods), correlation is positive. When discount rate news dominates (as in demand-shock periods), correlation is negative. Their model, estimated on US data from 1960-2018, finds that the correlation sign is a sufficient statistic for whether the economy is in a "supply-shock-dominant" or "demand-shock-dominant" regime — making it a crucial input to risk appetite assessment.

### Claim 7 — Cross-Country Divergence

The Mundell-Fleming model under floating exchange rates predicts that monetary policy is fully effective domestically but transmits to foreign economies through the exchange rate channel. When monetary policy cycles are synchronized (as during the coordinated tightening of 2022-2023), exchange rate movements are muted and cross-country risk appetite regimes converge. When cycles desynchronize, exchange rate volatility rises and risk appetite diverges.

Current divergence matrix (Q1 2026):

| Economy | Cycle Phase | Policy Stance | Risk Appetite Regime |
|---------|------------|---------------|---------------------|
| US | Late-cycle / neutral | ~Neutral (Taylor gap ≈ 0) | Fragile neutrality |
| Eurozone | Early easing | Mildly accommodative (ECB cutting ahead of Fed) | Conditional risk-on (conditional on no energy shock) |
| Japan | Normalization | Transitioning from ultra-accommodative | Structural risk-off for JGBs, tentative risk-on for equities (weaker yen supports earnings) |
| China | Counter-cyclical easing | Accommodative, but credit transmission impaired (balance sheet recession dynamics) | Domestically risk-off (property, consumer), externally risk-on (export competitiveness from weak CNY) |
| EM (ex-China) | Mixed | Brazil tightening, India neutral, ASEAN easing | Idiosyncratic, dependent on dollar and commodity prices |

The divergence creates arbitrage pressures: the ECB-Fed rate differential incentivizes EUR carry into USD assets, supporting US risk appetite but draining European financial conditions. The BOJ-Fed differential, while narrowing, still supports yen-funded carry trades into US and EM assets. The PBoC-Fed differential supports capital outflow from China into global risk assets.

The instability arises from the potential for convergence: if the Fed begins cutting while the BOJ continues normalizing, the yen carry trade unwinds simultaneously with a repricing of US rates — producing correlated risk-off across multiple carry trade structures. The August 2024 mini-crisis was a preview: a 15bp BOJ move triggered a 3-day, 12% selloff in Nikkei, a 6% surge in yen, and spillovers into US small-cap equities and EM FX. A larger BOJ move in the context of broader risk-off could produce a more severe episode.

### Claim 8 — Regime-Dependent r*

The standard Laubach-Williams (2003) methodology estimates r* using a Kalman filter on GDP growth, inflation, and the federal funds rate. The filter treats r* as a random walk, implying slow, exogenous drift. But consider the following mechanism:

1. **Risk-on → credit expansion → higher investment → higher potential growth → higher r*.** When financial conditions are accommodative, firms invest more, particularly in productivity-enhancing technology (AI capex in the current cycle). This raises potential output growth, which is a direct input to r* via the Solow-Swan growth framework: r* = ρ + θ·g, where ρ is the rate of time preference, θ is the intertemporal elasticity of substitution, and g is trend growth.

2. **Risk-off → credit contraction → lower investment → hysteresis in potential output → lower r*.** The secular stagnation literature (Summers 2014, Blanchard & Summers 1986 on hysteresis) documents that recessions permanently reduce potential output when displaced workers lose skills, firms cancel long-duration investments, and R&D spending declines. This lowers trend growth and hence r*.

The feedback loop: lower r* → more frequent ZLB episodes → more QE dependence → distorted risk pricing → fragile risk-on → higher probability of risk-off → investment contraction → lower r*. This is a "low-r*-trap" that Japan arguably fell into from 1995 onward.

Quantitative decomposition of current r* (Q1 2026 estimate):
- Structural demographic component: +0.5% (dragged down by aging, partially offset by immigration)
- Structural productivity component: +0.5% (baseline Solow residual, pre-AI)
- AI capex cyclical component: +0.25-0.75% (elevated tech investment, but permanence uncertain)
- Fiscal impulse cyclical component: +0.25-0.50% (government borrowing raising equilibrium rate)
- Global savings glut offset: -0.25% (EM reserve accumulation and aging-economy savings still exerting downward pressure)

Total r* estimate: 1.25-2.0% (wide range reflecting uncertainty in cyclical components)

If the AI capex boom fades or faces a correction (as speculative capex cycles historically do — recall the fiber-optic overbuild of 1998-2001), the cyclical component compresses by 0.5-0.75%, pushing r* toward the lower end of the range (0.75-1.25%). At that point, the current policy rate of ~4.0% and real rate of ~1.5% would be meaningfully restrictive, triggering a risk-off transition that the market is not pricing.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary policy transmission (macro_frameworks):** Risk appetite regimes are the market-facing manifestation of the monetary transmission mechanism. The Taylor Rule gap framework directly links policy assessment to regime identification. The finding that forward guidance dominates the current rate level (Claim 5) has implications for how we evaluate monetary policy effectiveness — the stance cannot be measured by the current rate alone but requires the full expected path, complicating real-time assessment of whether policy is restrictive, neutral, or accommodative.

- **Fiscal dominance (structural_themes):** The fiscal paradox (Claim 4) is a direct manifestation of the fiscal dominance theme. If the economy has entered or is entering a fiscal-dominance regime (active fiscal / passive monetary in the Bianchi-Melosi taxonomy), then risk appetite is no longer primarily a function of central bank policy but of fiscal credibility and sustainability. This would represent a structural break in the risk appetite regime map that would require fundamental revision of portfolio construction frameworks that use the Fed as the primary signal for risk allocation.

- **Demographics (structural_themes, confidence: 8.0 in KB):** The demographic compression of risk-on space (Claim 3) integrates the extensive knowledge base on demographics with the risk appetite framework. Key connections: the "dual squeeze default model" (labor cost inflation + maturity wall) creates credit cycle fragility that amplifies risk-off transitions; the "demographic bid" for fixed income compresses credit spreads, potentially masking fundamental deterioration until a regime shift reveals it; the "Japanification vs. Italianification" framework determines whether the fiscal-monetary response to demographic headwinds supports or undermines risk appetite.

- **Yield curve dynamics (credit_and_rates):** The bifurcation of the yield curve — front-end policy-driven, back-end fiscal/supply-driven — is a direct input to risk appetite assessment. A bear-steepening curve (long rates rising faster than short rates) in the current context signals rising term premia, which tightens financial conditions for credit and equity through the discount rate channel even if the policy rate is stable. This curve shape is consistent with incipient fiscal dominance and the fiscal paradox mechanism.

- **Volatility regimes (asset_class_dynamics, confidence: 3.0 in KB):** Risk appetite regimes and volatility regimes are co-determined. The current "fragile neutrality" should correspond to a suppressed-but-fragile vol regime where realized vol is low but the vol-of-vol (VVIX) is elevated — indicating that the market recognizes the bimodal distribution even if the central tendency is benign. The vol-targeting leverage channel (identified in prior iterations) is the mechanism through which volatility regime shifts amplify risk appetite regime shifts.

- **Credit cycles (credit_and_rates, confidence: 6.0 in KB):** The credit cycle is both a leading indicator and amplifier of risk appetite regimes. The current credit cycle position — tight spreads, late-cycle credit quality deterioration in specific sectors (CRE, leveraged lending), but no broad-based stress — is consistent with the "fragile neutrality" assessment. The credit-equity basis indicator (highlighted in iter_0006 cross-asset analysis) should be monitored for divergence as a warning of regime shift.

- **FX regimes (asset_class_dynamics, confidence: 4.0 in KB):** The cross-country divergence framework (Claim 7) maps directly onto FX regime analysis. Dollar dynamics are the key transmission variable: a weak dollar permits global risk-on by easing EM financial conditions, while a strong dollar triggers global risk-off through the opposite channel. The current dollar weakness is supportive of global risk appetite but is itself conditional on the relative monetary policy trajectory — if the Fed pauses cuts while the ECB continues, the dollar could strengthen abruptly, reversing the permissive conditions.

- **Rates-equity correlation (cross_asset):** This is the core of Claim 6. The oscillation between positive and negative stock-bond correlation is the real-time market signal of which shock type dominates. The research program should formalize the connection between the rolling correlation estimate and the risk appetite regime identification framework — specifically, whether a threshold crossing (e.g., 60-day correlation crossing zero from negative to positive) can serve as a leading indicator of risk-off transitions.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the Taylor Rule gap framework be operationalized for real-time regime identification given the ±150bp uncertainty in r*?** The current analysis relies on point estimates of r* that carry enormous confidence intervals. If r* is 0.75% rather than 1.25%, the Taylor gap is +65bp (restrictive) and the regime assessment shifts toward risk-off. If r* is 2.0%, the gap is -90bp (accommodative) and the assessment shifts toward risk-on. Is there a way to construct a regime identification framework that is robust to r* uncertainty — perhaps using multiple r* estimates simultaneously and tracking when they agree or disagree on the regime signal?

2. **How should the demographic modification of the Taylor Rule alter the risk appetite regime map for specific economies?** The knowledge base framework "taylor_rule_demographic_modification" identifies the need but doesn't specify the quantitative adjustment. For an economy like Japan (severely aged, r* deeply depressed), the risk-on/risk-off boundaries should be shifted downward. For the US (moderate aging, partially offset by immigration), the shift should be smaller. Can we construct economy-specific Taylor Rule bands that define risk appetite regime boundaries conditional on demographic structure?

3. **Is the fiscal paradox (Claim 4) resolvable within the New Keynesian framework, or does it require the Fiscal Theory of the Price Level (FTPL) to model correctly?** The NK framework treats fiscal policy as an IS curve shifter and assumes Ricardian equivalence as a benchmark. If the fiscal sustainability concerns are driving term premia, this may require an FTPL lens where the price level (and hence real rates and risk premia) are determined by the present value of future primary surpluses. The Post-Keynesian/Minskyan critique of Claim 4 would be that the "fiscal paradox" is an artifact of the NK framework's assumption of a fixed r* — in their framework, the central bank can always set rates below the growth rate, making fiscal sustainability concerns irrelevant. Resolving this disagreement is essential for determining whether fiscal-driven risk-off is a genuine risk or a theoretical phantom.

4. **What is the empirical threshold for forward guidance fragility?** Claim 5 argues that forward guidance dominates the actual rate level in determining risk appetite, but how far can the gap between actual rates and expected rates extend before it becomes self-correcting? Is there a "forward guidance bubble" analog to asset price bubbles, where expectations of future easing generate risk-on conditions that themselves delay the data deterioration needed to justify the expected cuts — until a shock breaks the self-referential loop?

5. **How should AI-driven productivity gains be incorporated into the regime-dependent r* decomposition (Claim 8)?** If the AI capex boom is analogous to the late-1990s IT capex boom, it raises r* temporarily but eventually corrects, pulling r* back down. If it is a genuine step-change in the production function (more analogous to electrification than to the dot-com bubble), it permanently raises both potential growth and r*. The answer determines whether the cyclical component of current r* is transitory or durable — and hence whether the current policy stance is truly neutral or secretly accommodative.

6. **Is there a formal model that combines the three-variable regime identification system (output gap, Taylor Rule gap, inflation expectations term structure) with the cross-asset factor structure identified in the generalist analysis?** The macro variables identify the fundamental regime; the cross-asset signals identify the market's pricing of the regime. Disagreements between the two — e.g., macro fundamentals pointing to fragile neutrality while cross-asset signals price risk-on — create both alpha opportunities and risk management challenges. A unified framework that maps from macro state to expected cross-asset configuration, and identifies significant deviations as mispricings, would be the synthesis contribution of this research program.

7. **How does the Mundell-Fleming-predicted cross-country divergence interact with the "Global Financial Cycle" thesis (Miranda-Agrippino & Rey, 2020)?** The GFC thesis argues that US monetary policy dominates global risk appetite regardless of other countries' policy stances — undermining the Mundell-Fleming prediction of policy independence under floating rates. If the GFC dominates, cross-country divergence is less important for risk appetite (US regime is the only one that matters). If Mundell-Fleming dominates, the current divergence is a genuine source of instability. Distinguishing between these views has direct implications for portfolio construction: GFC implies concentrate on US macro signals; Mundell-Fleming implies diversify signal sources across major economies.
