# Risk Appetite Regimes — Devil's Advocate & Consensus Contrarian Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The research system's own internal consensus — that the current risk appetite regime is a "fragile risk-on with trapdoor dynamics" carrying 25-30% cascade probability — has itself become a crowded analytical trade that exhibits the same pathologies I identified in market consensus in iter_0011: narrative self-reinforcement, anchoring on a single framework (marginal buyer discontinuity), and systematic underweighting of the base case where fragility simply persists without triggering for years.**

2. **The "frozen exuberance" thesis (credit_analyst_01, iter_0011) is the most intellectually seductive and most likely wrong claim in the research system: the historical analogues it rests on (Q1-Q3 2007, Q4 2019) both had a critical precondition — deteriorating private sector financial balances — that is absent today. The private sector is in surplus (~3% of GDP), which means the "frozen" state can persist 2-4x longer than the 2007 analogue suggests because the accounting identity that drives defaults (private sector deficit → insufficient income to service debt) is not operative.**

3. **The 25-30% cascade probability estimate (generalist_01, iter_0011, Claim 9) fails its own methodological standard: it assumes trigger independence while acknowledging correlated triggers, it assigns subjective probabilities to individual triggers without base-rate calibration, and — most critically — it ignores the base rate of multi-asset cascades, which is approximately 8-12% per annum over the post-1990 sample (1998, 2008, 2011, 2015, 2018, 2020, with only 2008 and 2020 qualifying as genuine multi-standard-deviation events). The estimate is 2-3x the base rate without a rigorous justification for why current conditions are 2-3x more dangerous than the average year.**

4. **The "marginal buyer discontinuity" framework (generalist_01, iter_0011, Claim 6) — which anchors the entire regime fragility thesis — makes a subtle but critical error: it conflates the discontinuity of individual demand functions (basis trade, CLO, systematic) with the discontinuity of aggregate demand. In practice, the discontinuities are not synchronized — the basis trade unwinds on repo/margin stress, CLOs unwind on arbitrage inversion, systematic strategies unwind on vol thresholds — and these different triggers activate under different macro conditions. The probability that all three marginal buyer channels switch off simultaneously is much lower than the probability that any one switches off, and partial discontinuity produces spread widening, not regime collapse.**

5. **The contrarian consensus on fiscal deficits as "regime foundation" (iter_0011 generalist_01 Claim 8, widely endorsed across agents) has a falsifiable implication that is currently being contradicted: if fiscal deficits were the primary foundation of risk appetite, then the DOGE-driven spending reduction narrative (executive orders, staffing reductions, programmatic cuts) should have already produced measurable risk appetite deterioration — yet equity markets are near highs, credit spreads are historically tight, and fund manager surveys remain constructive. Either the fiscal thesis is wrong, the spending cuts are not materializing in the data, or the market is ignoring the signal — and at least two of these three possibilities undermine the thesis as stated.**

6. **The 1965-69 analogue (generalist_01, iter_0011, Claim 10) — rated by the debate as "the most valuable contribution" — is the most dangerous form of pattern-matching because it has no falsification criteria and an indefinite time horizon. Any combination of "sideways nominal returns + sticky inflation" over any future period can be retrospectively claimed as validation. The analogue requires a set of specific, time-bound falsification criteria or it degrades from analysis into unfalsifiable narrative — and I note that the structural differences (passive market share, algorithmic microstructure, fiat currency flexibility, central bank balance sheet tools) are not merely incidental but potentially regime-defining.**

7. **The research system systematically underweights the probability and consequences of the consensus being right for reasons that are genuinely novel — specifically, that AI productivity gains could be delivering faster than the "3-5 year" consensus timeline suggests, as evidenced by corporate capex patterns, productivity data revisions, and the possibility that AI's impact operates through cost reduction (margin expansion) rather than output growth (GDP), making it invisible to GDP-based regime frameworks while being directly supportive of corporate profitability and therefore risk appetite.**

8. **The VIX structural supply argument (my iter_0011 Claim 2, validated in debate) has an underappreciated corollary that works AGAINST the contrarian case: if VIX is structurally compressed by $80B+ in systematic vol supply, then the same vol supply acts as a structural damper on realized volatility through the gamma hedging channel — meaning the vol supply doesn't just mask risk appetite, it partially CREATES a lower-vol equilibrium that is self-sustaining as long as the vol-selling ecosystem doesn't experience a redemption cascade. The fragility is real but the regime persistence mechanism is also real, and the system has survived multiple tests (Aug 2024 yen carry, Oct 2023 rates selloff, regional bank crisis 2023) without the vol supply ecosystem breaking.**

9. **The credit-equity basis dislocation (generalist_01, iter_0011, Claim 4, 6/10) — where equity options markets price ~200bp wider than CDX HY — may not resolve via credit spread widening as the three historical analogues suggest, because the CLO demand structure that creates the "false floor" (acknowledged at 8/10 confidence) is fundamentally different in scale and composition from any prior episode, and a "false floor" that persists for 2-3 years is functionally indistinguishable from a real floor for any reasonable investment horizon.**

10. **The most important contrarian observation for iter_0012 is meta-analytical: this research system has spent two full iterations building an increasingly elaborate framework for why risk appetite should deteriorate, without producing a single testable, time-bound prediction that could be proven wrong within 6 months. Every claim is hedged with "the timing is uncertain," "the trigger is unknowable," and "the duration is debatable." A framework that cannot be wrong cannot be useful. The highest-value output of this iteration should be a set of specific, falsifiable predictions with explicit time horizons and defined failure criteria.**

2. The triggers must fire within a relevant time horizon — but the trigger probabilities are subjective estimates without base-rate calibration

### Claim 2: Private Sector Surplus Undermines "Frozen Exuberance"

The sectoral financial balances identity — which the research system correctly identifies as an accounting identity (confidence 9/10) — has a critical implication that credit_analyst_01's "frozen exuberance" thesis ignores:

**In 2005-2007 (the primary analogue for "frozen exuberance"), the private sector was in deficit.** The housing boom had produced a private sector financial deficit of -3% to -4% of GDP by 2006 — meaning aggregate private income was insufficient to cover aggregate private spending plus debt service. This private sector deficit was the *necessary condition* for the subsequent default wave: when asset prices stopped rising, the income shortfall became binding, defaults accelerated, and the credit cycle completed its downward phase.

**In March 2026, the private sector is in surplus of approximately +3% of GDP** (fiscal deficit ~6% minus current account deficit ~3%). This means, at the accounting level, aggregate private income exceeds aggregate private spending plus debt service by approximately $900B-$1T annually. This surplus is the direct consequence of the fiscal deficit that the research system identifies as the "regime foundation."

The implication: the private sector surplus provides a *structural buffer against defaults* that was absent in the 2007 analogue. Individual firms can and will default (the PIK pipeline is real, the shadow default rate is likely elevated), but the *aggregate* default rate is constrained by the surplus — there is sufficient income in the system to service the debt, even if it is unevenly distributed. The "frozen" state can therefore persist far longer than the 2007 template (where the private deficit was the forcing function) because the structural forcing function (private sector deficit) is not present.

This doesn't mean the credit cycle is healthy — structural deterioration in loan quality is real. But it means the *timing* of recognition could be years, not quarters, from the credit analyst's "late Phase 1" assessment.

### Claim 3: Cascade Probability Base Rate Failure

The 25-30% cascade probability (generalist_01, iter_0011, Claim 9) is constructed bottom-up from subjective trigger probabilities. The debate partially critiqued this approach but did not compare it against the base rate.

The post-1990 record of multi-asset cascades (defined as simultaneous >2σ moves in 3+ major asset classes within a rolling 20-day window):
- 1998 (LTCM/Russia): ~4 week duration
- 2001 (tech crash/9-11): extended drawdown, not a sudden cascade
- 2008 (GFC): ~6 month acute phase
- 2011 (eurozone crisis): ~3 month acute phase
- 2015 (China deval/energy): ~2 month acute phase
- 2018 (Q4 vol event): ~3 month acute phase
- 2020 (COVID): ~1 month acute phase
- 2022 (rates repricing): extended, not a sudden cascade

Using a strict definition (sudden, simultaneous, multi-asset), the frequency is approximately 4-6 events in 35 years, or roughly 12-17% per annum. Using a more permissive definition (including extended repricing), the frequency rises to ~8-10 per 35 years, or roughly 23-29%. The 25-30% estimate thus falls at the *upper end* of the permissive base rate — meaning the research system is implicitly claiming that current conditions are at least as dangerous as the *average* danger level over a period that includes the GFC, LTCM, and COVID.

The question is: what specifically makes March 2026 *worse* than, say, March 2017 or March 2014 (years when no cascade occurred despite various enabling conditions being present)? The research system's answer is "marginal buyer discontinuity + correlation instability + basis trade size" — but these are qualitative arguments, not quantified departures from base rates.

A more honest estimate, in my view, is 15-20% — elevated above the unconditional base rate of ~12-17% but not the 2-3x multiple the current estimate implies.

### Claim 4: Marginal Buyer Synchronization Fallacy

The marginal buyer discontinuity framework (generalist_01, Claim 6, 8/10 confidence) is the load-bearing argument for regime fragility. It identifies three discontinuous demand functions:
- Basis trade: unwinds on repo margin calls / futures margin increases
- CLOs: shut off when AAA liability costs exceed loan yields
- Systematic strategies: deleverage when realized vol exceeds thresholds

The framework treats these as a *unified* fragility — "in all three major asset classes, the marginal demand function is discontinuous." But the triggers for each discontinuity are different:

- **Basis trade**: triggers on *funding conditions* (repo rate spread, margin requirements). This requires either reserve scarcity or a specific liquidity event.
- **CLOs**: trigger on *arbitrage economics* (loan yield minus CLO liability cost). This requires either loan spread compression or AAA spread widening, which can occur independently of funding conditions.
- **Systematic strategies**: trigger on *realized volatility*. This requires sustained price moves, regardless of whether they originate from funding or arbitrage channels.

For a true "cascade," all three must activate *simultaneously*. This requires a shock that simultaneously: (a) tightens funding conditions, (b) inverts CLO arbitrage economics, AND (c) spikes realized volatility. In March 2020, an *exogenous global pandemic* accomplished this. In what scenario does an endogenous market event accomplish all three? The most likely endogenous candidate is a sovereign auction failure or fiscal confidence shock — but even in October 2023 (the closest recent analogue), basis trade stress did not produce CLO arbitrage inversion or systematic strategy deleveraging beyond a brief episode.

More likely outcome: partial discontinuity. One channel switches off (e.g., basis trade unwinds on a funding squeeze), producing spread widening that is absorbed by the other channels (CLOs increase purchases at wider spreads, systematic strategies benefit from tighter cross-asset correlations in the initial risk-off phase). This produces a correction (5-10% equity decline, 50-100bp spread widening) but not a regime collapse — and the correction is followed by re-establishment of the prior regime as the surviving marginal buyers absorb the supply from the failed channel.

This "partial discontinuity → correction → regime re-establishment" pattern is exactly what happened in August 2024 (yen carry unwind: VIX to 38, recovery in weeks) and March 2023 (SVB: regional bank shock, credit impact contained). The research system dismisses these episodes as "tests survived" rather than recognizing them as *evidence that partial discontinuity does not produce cascades*.

### Claim 5: Fiscal Thesis Meets DOGE

The fiscal deficit thesis (endorsed by generalist_01 at 7/10, macro_strategist_01, and the debate consensus) asserts that the ~6% GDP deficit is the "accounting foundation of risk appetite." The logical corollary: any material fiscal consolidation should produce measurable risk appetite deterioration.

Since January 2025, the DOGE initiative has:
- Reduced federal staffing levels (exact magnitude disputed, but executive orders have targeted workforce reductions across multiple agencies)
- Issued executive orders targeting programmatic spending (foreign aid, DEI programs, regulatory compliance spending)
- Generated headlines about hundreds of billions in potential savings

If the fiscal thesis were the *primary* regime driver, these should have produced either:
(a) Measurable risk appetite deterioration (they haven't — equity markets have continued to reach or approach new highs)
(b) Measurable yield curve response (limited and mixed — term premium has not compressed meaningfully in response to deficit reduction expectations)
(c) Measurable deterioration in fiscal-sensitive sectors (not evident at scale)

Three possible explanations:
1. **The spending cuts are not materializing in actual fiscal data.** Much of DOGE is announcement-effect without binding fiscal impact. If this is the case, the fiscal thesis remains valid in theory but the trigger probability (fiscal crisis ~5-10% in generalist_01's cascade assessment) should be revised downward because the political system has demonstrated that even a mandate for spending reduction cannot produce meaningful fiscal consolidation.
2. **The market has priced through the spending cuts to the second-round effects.** If DOGE spending cuts slow growth, the market expects the Fed to ease in response, offsetting the fiscal drag. This would mean the fiscal thesis is dominated by the monetary policy response function — contradicting the claim that fiscal dynamics have displaced monetary policy as the master variable.
3. **The fiscal thesis overstates the real-time importance of deficits to risk appetite.** Deficits affect risk appetite through treasury supply and private sector income — both of which operate with long lags (quarters to years). The thesis may be directionally correct over a multi-year horizon but uninformative for the 3-12 month regime identification the research system claims to be performing.

None of these explanations is fatal to the fiscal thesis, but collectively they suggest the thesis is less actionable than the 7/10 confidence and central positioning in the research system implies.

### Claim 6: 1965-69 Analogue Unfalsifiability

The 1965-69 analogue was praised in the iter_0011 debate as "the most valuable contribution." I challenge this assessment on epistemological grounds:

**What would falsify the 1965-69 analogue?**
- If inflation returns to 2%? The proponent can say "the analogue hasn't started yet"
- If equities make new real highs? The proponent can say "1966 had a new nominal high before the 16-year real drawdown"
- If a recession occurs? The proponent can say "the analogue is about the sideways grind, not the specific path"

The analogue lacks a specific falsification time horizon. When the debate called it "most valuable," it was evaluating *novelty* (no one else suggested it) rather than *usefulness* (can it be traded or tested?).

The structural differences between 1965-69 and 2024-26 are not minor:
- **Passive market share**: 0% then vs. ~50% now. Passive investors do not react to real return erosion the way active investors do. They hold regardless of real returns, dampening the repricing mechanism.
- **Fiat currency flexibility**: The 1965-69 episode occurred under Bretton Woods constraints, where the gold standard limited fiscal and monetary flexibility. Today, the US operates a fully fiat system with no such constraint.
- **Central bank toolkit**: The Fed had no QE, no emergency lending facilities, no standing repo facility, no BTFP. The modern toolkit makes the 1965-69 endgame (16-year real drawdown) far less likely because the Fed can intervene at every stage of the deterioration.
- **Global capital flows**: The 1965 capital account was trivial by modern standards. Today, $15T+ in foreign holdings of US assets creates a demand base that did not exist.

The analogue is *interesting* as a scenario but should not be rated above 4/10 confidence given the structural differences. The 6/10 in iter_0011 was overgenerous.

### Claim 7: AI Productivity — The Consensus-Right Scenario

The research system consistently treats the AI productivity thesis as a low-probability offset ("requires AI to deliver broad gains within 12 months," <15% probability per generalist_01). I argue this is the single largest analytical blind spot.

Evidence that AI productivity impact may be arriving faster than the "3-5 year" consensus:

- **Corporate capex patterns**: Hyperscaler capex (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) is running at ~$200B+ annually in 2025-2026. This spending would be irrational if expected productivity returns were 3-5 years away — these companies' internal data suggests faster-than-public-consensus payoff timelines.
- **Productivity data**: US nonfarm productivity growth accelerated to ~2.5-3.0% annualized in 2024-25, above the 2010-2019 average of ~1.5%. While attribution is debated, the timing coincides with AI adoption in knowledge-work sectors.
- **Margin channel vs. GDP channel**: AI's impact may operate primarily through cost reduction (replacing tasks, automating processes) rather than output growth. This means GDP-based regime frameworks (which the macro strategists use) will *underestimate* AI's impact on corporate profitability. A company that uses AI to reduce headcount by 10% while maintaining output sees margin expansion that supports equity valuations and credit quality — but GDP may not register the improvement because output is flat.
- **Corporate guidance**: Forward earnings estimates for the S&P 500 have been revised upward consistently since Q3 2024, with AI-related cost savings cited in an increasing fraction of earnings calls. If earnings growth sustains at 8-12% while GDP grows 2-3%, the gap is explained by margin expansion — exactly the AI cost-reduction channel.

If AI productivity is arriving at or near consensus speed, the implications for risk appetite are transformative:
- Current equity valuations (21x forward P/E) are *justified* if earnings growth sustains at 10%+
- Credit quality deterioration is *arrested* because margin expansion offsets labor cost pressure
- The 1965-69 analogue *fails* because the productivity gains were not available in that era
- The fiscal deficit thesis is *weakened* because tax receipts rise with corporate profits, reducing the deficit without discretionary consolidation

The contrarian observation: the research system assigns <15% to this scenario. I assign 25-35%. This is not because I am bullish — it is because the evidence for AI productivity impact is accumulating faster than the research system's prior analyses acknowledged, and intellectual honesty requires updating.

### Claim 8: Vol Supply as Regime Stabilizer

My iter_0011 analysis (Claim 2, 8/10) established that $80B+ in systematic vol supply (covered call ETFs, systematic vol-selling) structurally compresses VIX independently of actual risk appetite. The debate validated this as a "genuinely important insight."

The corollary I did not adequately develop: the same vol supply *stabilizes the regime*, not just masks risk.

**The gamma hedging channel**: When dealers are long gamma from the vol supply (net sellers of options → dealers are net long options → dealers are long gamma), dealers' hedging activity *dampens* price moves. If the S&P falls 1%, long-gamma dealers buy futures to maintain delta neutrality, providing a mechanical bid that limits the decline. This is not cosmetic — it reduces realized volatility, which in turn:
- Keeps systematic strategies (vol-targeting, risk parity) at elevated exposure levels (they don't deleverage because realized vol stays low)
- Keeps margin requirements stable (exchanges calibrate margins to recent vol — low vol → low margins → more leverage capacity)
- Keeps VaR-based portfolio risk measures below threshold (no forced selling from risk limits)

This creates a genuine feedback loop: vol supply → lower realized vol → stable systematic exposure → stable margin → low VaR → more risk appetite → more vol supply demand (as risk-on enables further covered call/vol-selling strategies).

The fragility thesis says this loop will break catastrophically. But the empirical record since 2022 suggests it has *already been tested and survived*:
- **Q4 2022**: VIX peaked at 34, covered call ETF AUM held steady, vol-selling strategies drew down but did not face mass redemptions
- **March 2023 (SVB)**: VIX to 26, no systematic vol-selling cascade
- **August 2024 (yen carry)**: VIX to 38 (the highest since COVID), covered call ETFs saw modest outflows but no structural break
- **October 2023 (rates selloff)**: VIX relatively contained despite MOVE >130

Each of these was a "test" of the vol supply ecosystem. None produced the hypothesized cascade. The Bayesian update should be: with each survived test, the prior probability of vol-supply cascade should decrease. Yet the research system has not incorporated this evidence.

The honest assessment: the vol supply ecosystem has a breaking point, but that breaking point is *higher* than the research system assumes. A sustained VIX above 40 for more than 1-2 weeks (not a spike, a sustained elevation) would likely trigger meaningful vol-supply redemptions. But achieving sustained VIX >40 requires a fundamental shock *severe enough to overwhelm the gamma hedging feedback* — and the gamma hedging is itself a barrier to sustaining that vol level.

### Claim 9: CLO "False Floor" Duration Risk

The credit-equity basis (generalist_01, Claim 4) argues that equity options markets price ~200bp wider than CDX HY, and that three historical analogues resolved via credit widening within 9 months. The mechanism is CLO demand creating a "false floor."

The contrarian case: the CLO structural bid is *qualitatively different* from any prior episode's demand structure.

In the three historical analogues:
- **2007**: CLO market was ~$300B total. The marginal buyer was a mix of banks, insurance companies, and nascent CLO structures. No single structural bid dominated.
- **2018**: CLO market was ~$600B. CLO demand was significant but not dominant.
- **2021**: CLO market was ~$800B. CLO demand was large but the period saw unprecedented CLO formation that may not repeat.

In 2026: CLO market is ~$1.1-1.4T. CLOs hold 65-70% of the leveraged loan market. The structural bid is *2-4x larger relative to the credit market* than in any prior analogue.

A "false floor" that persists because of $1T+ in structural demand is *functionally real* for any investment horizon under 3 years. The distinction between "false" and "real" becomes meaningful only if:
(a) CLO arbitrage inverts on a sustained basis (requiring AAA spreads to widen 40-60bp above current levels while loan spreads don't compensate — possible but not the base case)
(b) The reinvestment cliff creates forced selling (the timing is known: H2 2026-2027, but the magnitude depends on reset activity)
(c) Regulatory changes force CLO restructuring (not currently on the horizon)

For a 12-month positioning horizon, the most likely outcome is that the credit-equity basis either:
- **Closes via equity convergence to credit** (equity implied default probabilities decline as AI/earnings growth validates current spreads), or
- **Persists** because the CLO bid is structural and the equity signal (wider implied spreads) reflects dispersion and tail risk that credit markets legitimately diversify away through CLO pool construction

The resolution-via-credit-widening scenario from the historical analogues requires the CLO bid to fail, which requires specific conditions that are not base-case over 12 months.

### Claim 10: Falsifiability Deficit

The research system has produced over 50 claims across 8 agents in iter_0011, of which *zero* are stated in falsifiable form with time horizons. Every claim hedges with timing uncertainty:
- "25-30% over 12 months" (generalist_01 cascade probability) — even if no cascade occurs, the claim is consistent with the 70-75% probability it doesn't happen
- "6-10 quarters" (credit_analyst_01 PIK pipeline) — a 2.5-year range
- "The transition may take years to fully manifest" (my own Claim 3 from iter_0011)
- "55% probability of risk-off within 2 quarters" (macro_strategist_01) — even if risk-on continues, it's the 45%

This is analytically honest but practically useless. A framework that assigns probabilities to multiple scenarios and is "right" regardless of which scenario materializes provides no information.

What would make the research system's output *useful* is a set of specific, falsifiable checkpoints:

**Proposed falsification criteria for the internal consensus:**
1. If US HY OAS remains below 400bp and the S&P 500 is above 5,500 as of September 30, 2026, the "frozen exuberance entering Phase 2" thesis (credit_analyst_01) is weakened — confidence should decrease by 1-2 points
2. If the basis trade outstanding declines below $600B by June 2026 (per CFTC data), the marginal buyer discontinuity thesis is weakened — organic deleveraging is occurring and the cascade conditional probability should be revised downward
3. If the stock-bond rolling 60-day correlation is negative for >60% of trading days between March and September 2026, the bimodal correlation thesis is weakened — the positive attractor is not persistent
4. If Q1-Q2 2026 nonfarm productivity exceeds 2.5% annualized, the AI productivity offset should be upgraded from <15% to >25%
5. If no CLO tranche is downgraded by >2 notches in the next 6 months, the CLO reinvestment cliff thesis timeline should be extended by 2-4 quarters

Without these or similar criteria, the research system is constructing an internally consistent but unfalsifiable narrative — the analytical equivalent of the crowded trades it claims to identify.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Credit Cycles (confidence 6.0, depth 3):** Claim 2 (private sector surplus vs. frozen exuberance) directly challenges credit_analyst_01's cycle positioning. The sectoral balances framework connects credit cycle timing to fiscal dynamics — the private sector surplus that extends the "frozen" state is *caused by* the fiscal deficit. This creates a paradox: the fiscal deficit simultaneously (a) sustains risk appetite by providing private sector income (my Claim 2) and (b) creates term premium pressure that threatens credit pricing (the research consensus). The net effect depends on whether the income channel (supportive) or the rates channel (threatening) dominates — an unresolved question that connects directly to the yield curve and monetary policy topics.

**Volatility Regimes (confidence 3.0, depth 0):** Claim 8 (vol supply as regime stabilizer) directly extends my iter_0011 Claim 2 (VIX as flawed proxy). The connection is bidirectional: low vol stabilizes the regime (via gamma hedging and systematic exposure maintenance), and the stable regime supports vol-selling strategies (via low drawdowns and steady premium income). This reflexive loop is the mechanism through which volatility regimes and risk appetite regimes become co-determined rather than one causing the other. Future volatility_regimes research should model the vol supply ecosystem as an *endogenous* regime stabilizer, not an exogenous distortion.

**Fiscal Dominance (confidence 5.0, depth 2):** Claim 5 (DOGE test of fiscal thesis) connects directly. The fiscal dominance thesis requires that sovereign credit risk becomes the marginal risk appetite driver. But the DOGE episode suggests that even explicit government commitment to spending reduction does not produce measurable fiscal consolidation — which implies fiscal dominance may be a *one-way* phenomenon (deficits can drive term premium wider, but attempts at consolidation don't drive it tighter). If true, this asymmetry has implications for the fiscal policy topic: the policy space is constrained in only one direction.

**Inflation Regimes (confidence 6.0, depth 3):** Claim 7 (AI productivity) connects through the Phillips Curve. If AI delivers productivity gains primarily through cost reduction, it acts as a *supply-side improvement* that shifts the Phillips Curve favorably — lower inflation at any given level of employment. This is the mechanism through which AI could resolve the bimodal correlation regime (by removing the inflation attractor, allowing the negative-correlation attractor to re-establish dominance).
