# Risk Appetite Regimes — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are endogenously generated by credit dynamics, not exogenously imposed by central bank policy.** The Minsky cycle — hedge → speculative → Ponzi — *is* the risk appetite cycle. What mainstream finance calls "risk-on" is simply the upward phase of endogenous credit expansion where increasingly fragile financial structures are validated by rising asset prices and cash flows.

2. **The sectoral financial balances identity (Private + Government + Foreign ≡ 0) imposes a hard accounting constraint on the sustainability of any risk appetite regime.** If the private sector is net borrowing (deficit spending) while the government is consolidating and the current account is in deficit, the arithmetic guarantees that private balance sheets are deteriorating — regardless of what spreads or VIX say. The current US configuration (~6-7% government deficit sustaining private sector near balance) is the load-bearing wall of the present risk-on regime.

3. **The Kalecki profit equation governs the validation mechanism for speculative and Ponzi positions: Profits ≡ Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports + Capitalist Consumption − Worker Saving.** Fiscal deficits of 6-7% of GDP are directly sustaining aggregate corporate profits at levels that validate current leverage ratios. Any fiscal consolidation of meaningful magnitude (>2pp of GDP) would mechanically shrink profits and trigger the Minsky moment in sectors currently classified as speculative.

4. **The current regime (Q1 2026) exhibits a dangerous sectoral divergence: households are in hedge finance, corporates are in late-stage speculative finance trending toward Ponzi, and the government sector is providing the residual financing that prevents the system from unwinding.** This is not a monolithic "risk-on" — it is a structurally fragile configuration where one sector's prudence is enabled by another's profligacy, which in turn is validated by fiscal flows.

5. **Private debt-to-GDP dynamics, not public debt ratios, are the leading structural indicator of risk appetite regime fragility.** US non-financial corporate debt/GDP stands near historical highs (~50%), while private credit's opaque $1.7-2.0T has pushed effective leverage ratios well beyond reported figures. The credit impulse (second derivative of credit growth) — not the policy rate, not the output gap — is the most upstream indicator of regime transition because it captures actual changes in the flow of purchasing power into the economy.

6. **The "risk-off" event, when it comes, will not be a symmetric reversal of "risk-on" but a balance sheet recession in the Koo sense — a regime shift in private sector behavioral objectives from profit maximization to debt minimization.** The asymmetry documented in prior iterations (risk-off 3-5x faster than risk-on) is explained by Minsky: the upward phase requires incremental belief revision, while the collapse requires only the recognition that cash flows cannot service debt at current structures.

7. **Non-bank financial intermediation (NBFI) — CLOs, private credit, semi-liquid vehicles — represents a Minsky blind spot that extends the speculative phase but dramatically amplifies the eventual correction.** These entities operate outside the original Minsky taxonomy of banks/firms/households, with mark-to-model accounting that delays recognition of Ponzi dynamics. The $100B+ in semi-liquid private credit vehicles (non-traded BDCs, interval funds) is a new and untested contagion channel.

8. **The stock-bond correlation regime shift toward positive correlation is a structural consequence of supply-side inflationary pressures and fiscal dominance, invalidating the traditional "bonds as risk-off hedge" assumption.** In a Godley-style framework, when the dominant source of instability shifts from demand (where bonds and equities are negatively correlated) to supply and fiscal (where they are positively correlated), the entire portfolio construction paradigm of the last 25 years breaks down.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Endogenous credit as the driver:**
Minsky's core insight is that stability itself is destabilizing. During periods of tranquility, agents rationally reduce safety margins, increase leverage, and extend maturities — generating the very conditions for fragility. The 2003-2007 episode is devastating for the mainstream view: the Fed hiked from 1% to 5.25%, yet risk appetite *escalated* because endogenous credit creation through securitization, CDOs, and subprime overwhelmed the policy rate signal. Prior iteration research (iter_0003, pair_2 debate) refuted the claim that "policy rate relative to r-star is the single most important macro determinant of risk appetite" on precisely these grounds. Credit creation by banks and shadow banks is the causal mechanism — not the cost of reserves, which is at best a modulating factor. As Schumpeter argued, banks create purchasing power ex nihilo; they don't intermediate existing savings.

**Claim 2 — Sectoral balances as hard constraint:**
The identity Private Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance ≡ 0 is not a theory — it is an accounting tautology. If the US government runs a deficit of ~6-7% of GDP and the current account deficit is ~3% of GDP, then the domestic private sector must be accumulating net financial assets of ~3-4% of GDP. This is what funds the "risk-on" regime. Reverse the fiscal position (even partially — a 3pp consolidation from 7% to 4% deficit) and the private sector must either reduce its surplus or swing to deficit. Wynne Godley used this framework to predict the 2001 and 2007-08 crises by identifying unsustainable private sector deficit spending. The Eurozone 2010-2013 experience — fiscal consolidation triggering a balance sheet recession across the periphery — is the definitive real-world experiment. Prior iteration iter_0003 confirmed this as HIGH_CONFIDENCE finding #6: "fiscal deficits are currently load-bearing for risk appetite, and fiscal consolidation is the most underpriced risk."

**Claim 3 — Kalecki profit equation as validation mechanism:**
The Kalecki-Levy profit equation is another accounting identity: aggregate profits equal investment plus the government deficit plus net exports plus capitalist consumption minus worker saving. With government deficits at 6-7% of GDP, this is the single largest contributor to aggregate profit flows. These profit flows validate the debt-service capacity of corporations currently classified as speculative borrowers (those who can cover interest but must refinance principal). Remove the fiscal support, and the Minsky classification shifts: speculative units become Ponzi units (unable to cover even interest from operations), triggering the cascade. Japan post-1990 and the Eurozone post-2010 demonstrate the "pushing on a string" asymmetry — fiscal expansion supports profits reliably, but fiscal contraction destroys them non-linearly.

**Claim 4 — Sectoral divergence:**
Household balance sheets are in genuine hedge finance territory: mortgage debt service ratios near historical lows (~4.5% of disposable income), low adjustable-rate exposure (post-GFC deleveraging), and substantial equity buffers. Corporate balance sheets tell a different story: HY leverage at 5.5-6.0x (public), estimated 6.5-7.5x (private per credit_analyst_02), covenant-lite share >80%, and EBITDA addbacks potentially inflating reported coverage ratios by 15-40%. This is textbook late-stage speculative finance. The government sector is the residual that makes the system balance. This sectoral heterogeneity means that aggregate indicators (like VIX or composite spread indices) are structurally misleading — a lesson reinforced by 2006, when households were in Ponzi mode while corporates were relatively sound.

**Claim 5 — Private debt dynamics as leading indicator:**
Steve Keen's empirical work demonstrates that the change in private debt (credit impulse) is the strongest leading indicator of aggregate demand changes, outperforming policy rate changes, output gap estimates, and confidence surveys. The mechanism is straightforward: in an endogenous money economy, aggregate demand = GDP + change in debt. When credit growth decelerates (even without turning negative), demand weakens. US non-financial corporate debt/GDP near 50%, with private credit adding an opaque additional layer, means the debt service burden is extremely sensitive to any tightening of financial conditions. The BIS debt service ratio for US non-financial corporates is a more honest signal than spreads, which are distorted by CLO price-insensitive demand.

**Claim 6 — Balance sheet recession dynamics:**
Richard Koo's work on Japan demonstrates that when the private sector collectively shifts from profit maximization to debt minimization, monetary policy becomes impotent (the "liquidity trap" is really a balance sheet recession). The speed asymmetry (risk-off 3-5x faster) arises because the shift in behavioral objective is discontinuous — one day firms are leveraging up to maximize ROE, the next they are paying down debt regardless of interest rates. This is not a smooth transition governed by utility maximization; it is a regime break in the Minskyan sense.

**Claim 7 — NBFI as Minsky blind spot:**
Minsky's original framework was developed for a banking system where regulators could observe balance sheets. The modern NBFI ecosystem — $1.7-2T in private credit with mark-to-model accounting, $100B+ in semi-liquid vehicles with untested redemption mechanisms, CLOs purchasing 60-70% of institutional leveraged loans — extends the speculative phase by delaying price discovery but amplifies the eventual correction by accumulating unrecognized losses. Amendment activity surging to an estimated 30-40% of 2021-2022 vintage in private credit is a classic extend-and-pretend dynamic that Minsky would recognize as the late-stage masking of Ponzi dynamics.

**Claim 8 — Stock-bond correlation regime:**
In a Godley-style stock-flow consistent model, the correlation between equities and bonds depends on the nature of the shock hitting the economy. Demand shocks produce negative correlation (both respond to growth expectations in the same direction, offset by flight-to-quality). Supply shocks and fiscal dominance produce positive correlation (inflation damages both bonds and equity multiples simultaneously). The structural shift toward supply-side inflationary pressures — labor scarcity (demographic, per iter_0001), energy transition costs, deglobalization, and fiscal dominance — biases the correlation regime persistently positive. The 2022 episode (equities -25%, bonds -15%) was not an anomaly but a preview.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Credit Cycle Dynamics & Late-Stage Signals:** The Minsky classification of borrower types (hedge/speculative/Ponzi) is the structural foundation for credit cycle analysis. Covenant deterioration, addback inflation, and CLO arbitrage economics are the microstructural manifestation of the macro Minsky dynamics. The dual_squeeze_default_model from iter_0001 — where maturity wall refinancing costs and demographic labor cost inflation converge — is a sector-specific instance of the speculative → Ponzi transition.

- **Fiscal Policy Divergence & Debt Sustainability:** The sectoral balances framework reframes "fiscal sustainability" entirely. Government debt for a currency issuer is the private sector's financial asset — they are two sides of the same ledger. The question is not whether government debt is "too high" but whether the fiscal stance is consistent with a viable private sector financial balance. Current 6-7% deficits are sustaining the risk-on regime; any meaningful consolidation is a regime change trigger.

- **Yield Curve Shape & Term Premium:** The structural term premium shift (+50bp from deeply negative) is consistent with the Post-Keynesian analysis: increased fiscal supply, QE withdrawal, and widened inflation distribution all reflect the underlying fiscal dominance regime. The 2s10s decomposition (expectations vs. term premium) matters enormously — a bear steepening driven by term premium has entirely different portfolio implications than one driven by rate expectations.

- **Volatility Regime Analysis:** VIX's structural degradation (0DTE supply, vol-targeting reflexivity) creates a dangerous divergence between *measured* volatility and *structural* fragility. This is precisely the Minskyan problem: stability breeds instability, and the instruments designed to measure stability become co-opted by the very dynamics that produce fragility.

- **Stock-Bond Correlation Regime:** The positive correlation regime is the portfolio-construction consequence of the supply-side inflationary bias that the Post-Keynesian framework identifies as structural (labor scarcity, fiscal dominance, deglobalization). The 60/40 portfolio is an artefact of the demand-deficient, low-inflation regime that ended in 2021.

- **Demographics (iter_0001):** The minsky_cycle_demographic_conditioning concept connects directly — demographic booms accelerate the hedge → speculative → Ponzi transition by expanding the pool of borrowers and the collateral base (housing). Demographic busts create prolonged balance sheet recessions (Japan). Current US demographics, partially offset by immigration, place the economy in a more moderate demographic position than Japan 1990 but less favorable than 2000s US.

- **Currency Regimes & FX:** The Bruno-Shin dollar feedback loop ($13T in offshore dollar-denominated debt) is the open-economy extension of the Minsky framework. Dollar appreciation in risk-off tightens global financial conditions endogenously, accelerating the unwinding of speculative positions internationally.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the effective private sector debt-to-GDP ratio when private credit's opaque leverage is properly accounted for?** If EBITDA addbacks truly inflate leverage from reported 6.5x to effective 8-10x across $1.7-2T in private credit, the aggregate fragility assessment changes materially. No systematic data exists.

2. **At what fiscal deficit level does the Kalecki profit mechanism cease to validate current corporate leverage ratios?** The identity tells us the direction; it does not tell us the threshold. A 2pp consolidation? 3pp? The non-linearity is real but unmapped.

3. **Can NBFI entities undergo a Minsky moment independently of the banking system, or do they require a banking sector trigger?** The semi-liquid vehicle contagion channel (non-traded BDCs, interval funds) is theoretically compelling but has never been tested at scale. March 2020 was too brief.

4. **Is the current sectoral balance configuration (government deficit sustaining private near-balance) politically sustainable?** The Post-Keynesian framework identifies fiscal consolidation as the key regime risk, but political economy determines whether consolidation actually occurs. DOGE-style expenditure cuts or revenue increases triggered by political dynamics could impose consolidation regardless of macroeconomic consequences.

5. **How should the Minsky framework be extended to incorporate algorithmic and systematic strategies ($300-500B in vol-targeting/risk-parity)?** These entities do not fit cleanly into hedge/speculative/Ponzi categories — their leverage is mechanically determined by realized volatility, not by cash flow expectations. They compress transitions (~40% per iter_0003) in ways Minsky did not anticipate.

6. **Is the credit impulse signal degraded by private credit opacity?** If an increasing share of credit creation occurs in mark-to-model vehicles that delay recognition, the credit impulse as measured by flow-of-funds data may structurally lag reality.

7. **Does the positive stock-bond correlation regime survive a recession?** If recession triggers demand-side dynamics (disinflation, flight to safety), the correlation could temporarily revert to negative, confusing portfolio positioning. The structural supply-side bias would reassert post-recession, but the transition could be violent.
