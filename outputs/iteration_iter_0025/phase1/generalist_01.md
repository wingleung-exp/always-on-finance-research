# Demographics — Cross-Asset Strategist (Generalist) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Demographics is the single most powerful cross-asset correlation regime driver on secular timescales.** The 1998-2021 negative stock-bond correlation anomaly was underwritten by favorable demographics (peak working-age population, high savings, disinflationary labor supply). The reversal of these forces is a necessary — though not sufficient — condition for a structural positive stock-bond correlation regime.

2. **The developed-world dependency ratio inflection creates a multi-decade negative r-g dynamic that is inconsistent with current sovereign debt pricing.** As dependency ratios rise (US: ~54% now → ~70% by 2050; Japan: already ~70%; EU: ~56% → ~75%), the fiscal arithmetic for debt sustainability worsens non-linearly. Real growth (g) declines via labor supply while real rates (r) face upward pressure from dis-saving. Markets price at most 1-2 standard deviations of this structural shift into term premia.

3. **A "demographic divergence trade" — long EM demographic dividend recipients (India, Indonesia, Philippines, parts of sub-Saharan Africa) vs. short demographic deficit markets (China, Korea, Germany, Italy) — is the highest Sharpe ratio structural relative value opportunity in cross-asset space, but timing is uncertain and the trade has poor carry.** The demographic fundamentals are unambiguous in direction but have historically taken 10-20 years to fully price.

4. **China's demographic cliff is the single largest cross-asset macro risk for 2026-2035, exceeding US fiscal dominance in potential portfolio impact.** Working-age population peaked ~2015 and will decline by ~100M by 2035. This is a structural demand shock for commodities (base metals, energy), a structural supply shock for global manufactured goods (disinflationary for goods, inflationary for services requiring local labor), and the primary driver of China's shift from capital-intensive investment to consumption-oriented growth — which historically fails to generate the same commodity intensity.

5. **Pension duration demand from aging populations creates a structural bid for long-duration fixed income that compresses term premia and flattens yield curves — but this force is now colliding with fiscal supply.** The resolution of this collision (pension demand vs. sovereign supply) is the defining question for long-end rates over the next decade. The two forces approximately offset at current levels, making the 10Y-30Y curve abnormally sensitive to marginal shifts in either force.

6. **The aging-driven shift from accumulation to decumulation structurally changes equity market microstructure: higher dividend demand, lower risk tolerance, increased preference for yield over growth, and reduced capacity to absorb drawdowns.** This "retailization of risk" combined with the secular shift from DB to DC pensions transfers duration risk from institutions with long horizons to individuals with shorter horizons and behavioral biases, increasing tail risk and volatility clustering.

7. **Immigration policy is the highest-variance demographic variable for G7 economies over 5-10 year horizons.** Natural fertility trends are slow-moving and largely irreversible on policy-relevant timescales. Immigration is the only lever that can materially shift dependency ratios, potential GDP growth, and housing demand within a single business cycle. The US absorbed 3-4M immigrants above pre-pandemic trend (per CBO), adding ~0.3-0.4pp to GDP growth — a demographic injection equivalent to roughly a decade of natural labor force growth.

8. **GLP-1 agonists and longevity science represent an underpriced demographic wildcard.** Compressed morbidity (living healthier longer, dying quickly) could reduce late-life healthcare costs by 15-25%, extend productive working lives by 3-5 years, and partially offset dependency ratio deterioration. This is potentially the only force that could structurally alter the demographic trajectory of developed markets within the next 10-15 years, excluding immigration.

**Claim 3 — Demographic divergence trade:**

**Claim 4 — China demographic cliff:**
China's working-age population (15-64) peaked at ~1.01B around 2015 and is projected to decline to ~900M by 2035 and ~700M by 2050 (UN medium variant). Birth rates have collapsed from ~17M/year (2016, post-two-child policy) to ~9M/year (2023), below replacement since ~1992. This is not a forecast — it is a demographic fact for the working-age population through 2040. Cross-asset implications: copper demand growth structurally decelerates (China = 55% of global copper consumption per the KB), base metals supercycle thesis weakens, CNY faces structural depreciation pressure, Chinese government bonds face compression as savings channel shifts. The `japan_non_generalizability` concept (confidence 8/10) warns against using Japan as a benign template — China lacks Japan's net creditor position, current account surplus magnitude, and domestic ownership concentration.

**Claim 5 — Pension demand vs. fiscal supply:**
Global pension assets (~$55-60T) are structurally long duration. As populations age, DB pensions shift toward liability-driven investment (LDI), increasing demand for 20-30Y bonds. Simultaneously, aging populations drive higher healthcare/pension spending, increasing sovereign issuance — particularly at the long end as debt management offices extend maturity. The UK gilt crisis (2022) was a preview of what happens when these forces collide with a fiscal shock: LDI demand created leveraged duration exposure that amplified the sell-off. The structural tension is that pension funds need long bonds to match liabilities, but governments need to issue long bonds to fund the pensions — a reflexive loop that makes the long end simultaneously the most demanded and most supplied part of the curve.

**Claim 6 — Decumulation and equity microstructure:**
The DB-to-DC pension transition shifts duration management from professional ALM to individual behavioral finance. DC participants exhibit: panic selling during drawdowns (March 2020, 2022), home bias, recency bias, and dramatically shorter effective investment horizons. As retirees draw down rather than accumulate, net equity flows from the pension system turn negative. This creates a structural headwind for equity multiples (reduced marginal buyer) and increases volatility clustering (behavioral selling reinforces drawdowns).

**Claim 7 — Immigration as highest-variance lever:**
The KB concept of immigration adding 0.3-0.4pp to US GDP growth (documented at confidence 8/10 in prior iterations, based on CBO's 3.3M net immigration revision for 2023) demonstrates the magnitude. Fertility policy changes take 20-25 years to affect the working-age population. Immigration changes affect it within 1-2 years. Political volatility around immigration policy in the US, EU, UK, and Australia makes this the highest-uncertainty input to demographic models.

**Claim 8 — GLP-1/longevity wildcard:**
Semaglutide/tirzepatide adoption is scaling rapidly, with potential to reduce obesity-related healthcare costs (diabetes, cardiovascular, orthopedic) that represent ~30% of US healthcare spending. If these drugs extend healthy working years by 3-5 years and reduce late-life disability, the effective dependency ratio improves by the equivalent of ~2-4 years of immigration flow. This is a genuine structural break candidate, but adoption rates, long-term efficacy, and insurance coverage are highly uncertain.
