# Central Bank Balance Sheets — Earnings Quality & Valuation Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **QT-driven cost-of-capital repricing has not yet been reflected in reported earnings quality metrics, creating a widening gap between stated profitability and economic returns.** The ~$2.0-2.6T/year in net Treasury duration absorption is structurally repricing the risk-free rate and term premium, yet corporate ROIC calculations using historical WACC inputs systematically overstate value creation.

2. **The basis trade ($800B-$1T) is suppressing the discount rate that should be applied to equity valuations by 20-40bp, making current multiples appear cheaper than economic reality warrants.** Reverse DCF analysis using a term premium adjusted for basis trade fragility implies the market is pricing 200-400bp less terminal rate risk than the structural supply picture demands.

3. **Covenant-lite structures with 25-40% EBITDA addbacks represent the single largest earnings quality distortion in leveraged credit, and this distortion is bleeding into equity valuations of PE-backed and leveraged companies.** True leverage at 5.5-7.5x vs. reported 4.0-4.5x means that any equity valuation built on adjusted EBITDA multiples is working from a fabricated denominator.

4. **The regime shift from RRP-draining QT (painless) to reserve-draining QT (binding) will compress corporate cash conversion ratios and expose accruals-heavy balance sheets within 4-8 quarters.** Tighter funding conditions reduce the tolerance for working capital float, channel-stuffing, and aggressive revenue recognition.

5. **Private credit NAV smoothing (autocorrelation 0.5-0.7) is artificially inflating the reported earnings and book values of BDCs, interval funds, and any public company with material private credit exposure.** True economic volatility is 2-3x reported, meaning P/B and P/E multiples for these vehicles are systematically misleading.

6. **BoJ normalization, even modest, will degrade earnings quality for U.S. and European companies that have benefited from suppressed JPY (competitor currency advantage) and cheap Japanese capital flows into CLO/credit markets.** The $130-200B reallocation is not just a rates event — it reprices the cost of capital for sub-IG borrowers dependent on CLO formation.

7. **The QE/QT asymmetry (100-150bp compression vs. 30-60bp decompression) has permanently embedded a valuation subsidy in equity prices that market participants treat as structural rather than cyclical.** Normalized earnings multiples should be computed using a through-cycle term premium that includes the full QT decompression, not the suppressed QE-era level.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — WACC lag:** The KB documents compound duration absorption requiring private sector to absorb $2.0-2.6T/year vs. $500-800B pre-pandemic. The term premium channel adds 30-60bp to long rates. Yet corporate WACC models typically use trailing equity risk premia and bond yields that still reflect partial QE-era suppression. Any company reporting ROIC > WACC using stale inputs is overstating economic value creation. This is most acute for capital-intensive sectors (utilities, REITs, telecoms) where small WACC changes flip the ROIC-WACC spread from positive to negative.

**Claim 2 — Basis trade discount rate suppression:** The basis trade at $800B-$1T absorbs cash Treasury duration, suppressing term premium by an estimated 20-40bp. This is pro-cyclical — it unwinds during stress. For valuation purposes, this means the "risk-free" rate used in DCF models is artificially low by the suppression amount. A reverse DCF on the S&P 500 using a risk-free rate 30bp higher produces an implied growth rate ~150-200bp above what is achievable at current ROIC levels. The market is either pricing in permanent basis trade suppression (wrong — it's fragile) or is simply mispriced.

**Claim 3 — EBITDA addback distortion:** The KB establishes that >90% of institutional loans are covenant-lite with EBITDA addbacks averaging 25-40%. This means EV/EBITDA multiples used for leveraged buyout valuations, comparable company analysis, and credit assessments are systematically understated. A company trading at "6x EBITDA" on adjusted numbers is actually at 8-9x on economic cash earnings. For equity analysts using comparable transaction multiples, this inflates perceived cheapness across the entire leveraged universe.

**Claim 4 — Cash conversion deterioration:** Phase 2 QT (reserve-draining) tightens bank funding conditions. Historical precedent (September 2019 repo spike at $1.5T reserves; March 2023 stress at $3.0T with distributional concentration) shows that funding stress emerges nonlinearly. When funding tightens, the first corporate metric to deteriorate is the cash conversion cycle — days sales outstanding rise, inventory builds, and operating accruals widen relative to CFO. Companies with persistent positive accruals (earnings > cash flow) are the most vulnerable to this exposure. The RRP near-exhaustion (<$200B) means we are now in the regime where this matters.

**Claim 5 — Private credit NAV fiction:** Autocorrelation of 0.5-0.7 in private credit NAVs implies that roughly half of each period's true return is deferred to future periods. For publicly traded BDCs and interval funds, this means reported EPS and book value are smoothed versions of a much more volatile reality. Any P/E or P/B-based valuation that takes reported figures at face value is working from distorted inputs. The $300B+ in semi-liquid vehicles with structural liquidity mismatch compounds this — eventual gating or forced marking will produce sudden earnings restatement-like events.

**Claim 6 — BoJ transmission to corporate fundamentals:** Japanese investors hold 15-25% of CLO AAA tranches. BoJ normalization triggering even 10-15% reallocation ($130-200B) would widen CLO liability spreads, compress CLO equity IRR below ~12-15% hurdle rates, and impair new formation. The maturity wall ($900B-$1.5T for 2025-2028) requires functional CLO demand. Companies dependent on leveraged loan refinancing face a cost-of-capital step-change that directly compresses operating margins (higher interest expense) and reduces earnings quality (more restructuring charges, more non-GAAP adjustments to exclude "one-time" refinancing costs that are actually recurring).

**Claim 7 — Embedded valuation subsidy:** QE compressed term premia by 100-150bp; QT has decompressed only 30-60bp. The remaining 40-90bp of residual QE suppression is baked into current equity multiples. A properly normalized earnings multiple would use a cost of equity reflecting the full QT decompression. On the S&P 500, this difference implies 5-12% overvaluation relative to a through-cycle valuation framework.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. WACC lag | 7.5/10 | Observable in corporate filings; the lag is mechanical. Uncertainty is in the magnitude of ROIC-WACC spread degradation. |
| 2. Basis trade discount rate | 6.5/10 | The 20-40bp suppression estimate has wide bounds. The directional point is solid; the precision is not. |
| 3. EBITDA addback | 8/10 | KB evidence is strong (>90% cov-lite, 25-40% addbacks). The transmission to equity valuations is direct and measurable. |
| 4. Cash conversion deterioration | 6/10 | The mechanism is sound but the timing is uncertain. Reserve scarcity threshold is unknowable ex ante ($1T uncertainty band per Fed estimates). |
| 5. Private credit NAV | 7.5/10 | Autocorrelation evidence is quantitative and well-documented. The valuation distortion follows mechanically. Uncertainty is in when marking-to-reality occurs. |
| 6. BoJ transmission | 5.5/10 | This is a 2-3 year thematic, contingent on BoJ behavior. The chain of causation is plausible but long and partially hedged. |
| 7. Valuation subsidy | 7/10 | The QE/QT asymmetry is one of the KB's highest-confidence findings (confidence 8). Translation to equity overvaluation requires assumptions about equilibrium multiples. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**To credit markets:** The EBITDA addback distortion (claim 3) and covenant-lite default dynamics are the bridge between balance sheet policy and credit cycle timing. The bimodal default distribution (successful refinancing or liquidity crisis) means earnings quality deterioration in leveraged companies will appear binary — invisible until catastrophic. This connects to **credit spreads**, **default cycle timing**, and **recovery rate** analysis across all sectors with leveraged balance sheets.

**To equity factor investing:** QT's cost-of-capital repricing should mechanically favor high-quality, low-leverage, strong-cash-conversion companies over leveraged, accruals-heavy, non-GAAP-adjusting firms. This connects to **quality factor** and **value factor** rotation, and suggests that "cheap" stocks on adjusted EBITDA may be value traps if the addbacks are illusory.

**To FX and international earnings:** BoJ normalization connects to **JPY appreciation**, which affects earnings quality for Japanese exporters (weaker translation effects) and U.S./European companies competing against Japanese manufacturers (reduced competitor pricing pressure). The carry unwind also connects to **EM capital flows** and **commodity financing**.

**To fiscal policy interaction:** The Fed's deferred asset ($200B+ cumulative) and interest on reserves (~$180B/year) are fiscal costs that connect to **government funding costs**, **deficit trajectory**, and ultimately to the corporate tax and spending environment. The fiscal-monetary interaction is underappreciated in bottom-up earnings models.

**To financial sector earnings:** Bank earnings quality is directly affected by the reserve scarcity dynamic. Net interest margin expansion from rate hikes is partially offset by unrealized securities losses (SVB being the extreme case). The SRF's untested status means bank liquidity risk premia are potentially mispriced, affecting the appropriate valuation multiple for bank equities.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the current aggregate accruals ratio for the S&P 500 relative to CFO, and how does it compare to pre-QE norms?** If operating accruals have widened during the QE era, the reversion under QT could be a leading indicator of earnings disappointments.

2. **How much of the current equity risk premium is attributable to residual QE term premium suppression vs. structural changes in risk appetite?** This is critical for determining whether the valuation subsidy is permanent or cyclical.

3. **What fraction of public company M&A and buyback activity was financed at QE-era rates and faces refinancing at QT-era rates?** Capital allocation decisions made under suppressed cost-of-capital may prove value-destructive when recapitalized at higher rates, directly affecting ROIC trajectories.

4. **Can the SRF's stigma problem be tested empirically before a crisis, or is this inherently an unknown until it matters?** The answer determines whether we should use a lower or higher discount rate for bank equities in a stress scenario.

5. **At what point does the basis trade unwind trigger forced selling in Treasury markets that cascades into equity market repricing?** The March 2020 episode occurred at $200-300B notional; current $800B-$1T is 3-4x larger, suggesting amplification, but there is no model for the threshold.

6. **Are rating agencies adjusting their EBITDA calculations to back out addbacks, or are they accepting sponsor-provided figures?** If the latter, the entire credit rating architecture is calibrated to fictional earnings, and the default wave when it comes will be underestimated by every model that uses ratings as input.

7. **What is the normalized P/E for the S&P 500 using a WACC that reflects full QT decompression (i.e., adding back the 40-90bp of residual QE suppression)?** This is the single most important valuation question for long-term equity allocators in the current regime.
