# Stock-Bond Correlation Regime Analysis — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The sign of stock-bond correlation is fundamentally determined by whether the dominant macroeconomic disturbance is a demand shock or a supply shock, mediated by the central bank reaction function.** Under demand shocks, monetary policy moves pro-cyclically with growth, producing negative stock-bond correlation (bonds hedge equities). Under supply shocks, the Phillips Curve tradeoff forces the central bank to tighten into weakening growth, producing positive correlation (both assets fall together). This is the single most important structural determinant and has been formalized in Campbell, Pflueger, and Viceira (2020) and Goncharov and Sarkissian (2024).

2. **The current macro regime (Q1 2026) is characterized by an ambiguous shock mix — residual supply-side inflation persistence coexisting with demand-side softening — placing us in a transitional correlation regime with 60% probability of reverting to negative correlation and 40% probability of sustained positive correlation.** The output gap has closed from approximately +1.5% in mid-2023 to roughly 0% to -0.5% in early 2026, while core PCE remains 30-50bp above most estimates of the Fed's comfort zone (~2.3-2.5% vs 2.0% target). This "last mile" inflation persistence keeps the shock classification ambiguous.

3. **The maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation documented in the knowledge base (2Y negative, 30Y positive) is best explained within the NK framework as a separation between monetary policy credibility at the front end and fiscal term premium at the back end, and it implies the market is pricing partial fiscal dominance.** In a standard NK model, the short rate is pinned by the Taylor Rule — 2Y rates respond to expected policy rate paths, which remain counter-cyclical. The 30Y rate, however, includes a term premium increasingly driven by fiscal supply expectations (debt/GDP trajectory, issuance calendar) that correlates positively with equity risk premia. This is not a breakdown of the NK framework but rather the activation of its fiscal theory extension (Leeper, 2021; Bianchi and Melosi, 2022).

4. **The neutral real rate (r*) has shifted upward by 50-100bp relative to pre-pandemic estimates (from ~0.5% to ~1.0-1.5%), and this level shift is a necessary condition for the correlation regime transition.** When r* was near zero, monetary policy was persistently at or near the effective lower bound, compressing rate volatility and mechanically pushing stock-bond correlation negative (Kiley, 2024). A higher r* restores two-sided rate risk, allows supply shocks to transmit through the rate channel, and structurally raises the unconditional stock-bond correlation toward zero. The post-2000 negative correlation era was in part an artifact of the low-r* environment, not a permanent structural feature.

5. **The Taylor Rule prescription for the current environment is a federal funds rate of approximately 4.0-4.5%, implying the Fed is roughly at neutral or slightly restrictive, which creates asymmetric correlation dynamics: dovish surprises restore negative correlation, hawkish surprises reinforce positive correlation.** Using a standard Taylor Rule (r* = 1.25%, inflation gap = +0.3-0.5pp, output gap = -0.25 to 0%), the prescribed rate is ~4.0-4.5%. With the effective funds rate at 4.25-4.50% (as of March 2026), policy is approximately at the rule-consistent level. This means the correlation regime is highly sensitive to the next marginal data surprise: a demand slowdown (rising unemployment, falling ISM) would trigger rate cuts that help equities (negative correlation), while an inflation resurgence would trigger rate hikes that hurt equities (positive correlation).

6. **The three-channel commodity-correlation framework in the knowledge base is consistent with the NK model but underspecifies the reaction function — the channel that dominates depends critically on the degree of monetary policy accommodation, which is endogenous to the inflation regime.** Channel A (transitory, negative correlation) requires a credible inflation-targeting central bank that "looks through" the shock — this is only available when inflation expectations are well-anchored (i.e., the Phillips Curve is relatively flat). Channel C (wage-price spiral, positive correlation) emerges when expectations de-anchor and the Phillips Curve steepens. The current state — partially re-anchored expectations after 2021-2023 — places the framework in an intermediate zone where the reaction function itself is uncertain.

7. **Under plausible NK model calibrations, the unconditional stock-bond correlation for the next 2-3 years is approximately -0.10 to +0.10, materially different from both the -0.30 to -0.40 of 2000-2020 and the +0.30 to +0.50 of 2022.** This near-zero unconditional correlation reflects the weighted average across demand-dominant and supply-dominant scenarios. The distribution is bimodal rather than normal: in demand-shock states (recession), correlation reverts to -0.30 to -0.50 as the Fed cuts aggressively; in supply-shock states (inflation resurgence), correlation rises to +0.20 to +0.40. Portfolio construction must address this bimodality, not the unconditional average.

8. **A New Keynesian framework predicts that the correlation regime is more likely to flip decisively negative in 2026-2027 than remain persistently positive, conditional on the base case that the output gap moves further into negative territory (probability: 55-60%).** The logic is as follows: with core inflation on a slow glide path toward target and the labor market loosening (unemployment rising from 3.7% toward 4.2-4.5%), demand-side weakness will increasingly dominate the shock taxonomy. The Fed's reaction function in this scenario is to cut rates, restoring the negative stock-bond correlation that prevailed in prior late-cycle environments. The risk case (35-40%) is that inflation proves sticky above 2.5% even as growth weakens — a stagflationary micro-episode that would sustain positive correlation.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Demand vs. Supply Shock Taxonomy
The theoretical foundation is the textbook New Keynesian three-equation model (IS curve, Phillips Curve, Taylor Rule). Under a positive demand shock: output rises, inflation rises, the central bank tightens, rates rise, equities rise on stronger earnings but fall on higher discount rates — net effect ambiguous but rates and equities move in opposite directions on the monetary policy leg. Under a negative supply shock: output falls, inflation rises, the central bank tightens, rates rise and equities fall — unambiguously positive correlation. Campbell, Pflueger, and Viceira (2020, "Macroeconomic Drivers of Bond and Equity Risks," JPE) formalize this: when inflation is driven by demand, nominal bonds are safe assets; when driven by supply, they become risky. Empirically, the positive stock-bond correlation of 1965-1998 coincided with supply shock dominance (oil, productivity slowdown), while the negative correlation of 2000-2020 coincided with demand shock dominance (tech bust, GFC, secular stagnation). The 2022 regime break followed the largest supply shock in decades (pandemic + energy crisis).

### Claim 2: Current Regime Assessment
**Output gap**: CBO estimates suggest the output gap closed by mid-2024 and is approximately 0% to -0.5% in Q1 2026. The unemployment rate has drifted up from 3.4% (April 2023) to approximately 4.0-4.1%, consistent with a modest negative output gap. The Beveridge curve has shifted back toward its pre-pandemic locus, suggesting labor market normalization rather than structural deterioration.

**Inflation dynamics**: Core PCE has declined from 5.6% (Feb 2022 peak) to approximately 2.3-2.5% (Q1 2026). However, the "last mile" from 2.5% to 2.0% has proven slow, consistent with the Christiano-Eichenbaum-Evans (2005) finding that inflation persistence increases near target due to downward nominal rigidity and the non-linear Phillips Curve. Supercore services inflation (services ex-housing ex-energy) remains elevated at ~3.0-3.5%, reflecting shelter lag effects and wage-intensive services.

**Shock classification**: The current environment features residual supply-side inflation (housing costs, insurance, healthcare) coexisting with moderating demand (retail sales softening, consumer confidence declining). This mixed signal is what produces the ambiguous correlation regime. The probability weight tilts toward demand dominance as the output gap widens, but the inflation residual prevents a clean classification.

### Claim 3: Maturity-Dependent Bifurcation
The knowledge base documents 2Y-SPX correlation at -0.15 to -0.35 and 30Y-SPX correlation at +0.05 to +0.15. Within the NK framework, this bifurcation maps directly to the decomposition of the yield curve into expectations (policy rate path) and term premium components.

**Front end (2Y)**: Dominated by expected policy rate over the next 8 quarters. Under a standard Taylor Rule, the expected policy rate responds counter-cyclically to the output gap and pro-cyclically to inflation, with the output gap response dominating for well-anchored inflation expectations. When equities fall due to growth fears, the expected policy path shifts down, 2Y yields fall, producing negative correlation. This mechanism requires central bank credibility — i.e., the market believes the Fed will cut in a downturn.

**Back end (30Y)**: Includes a substantial term premium that responds to fiscal supply expectations, inflation risk premium, and duration risk compensation. When fiscal deficits widen (5-7% of GDP structural deficits in US), the marginal buyer demands higher term premium for absorbing supply. This term premium component is positively correlated with equity risk premia because both respond to growth expectations and risk appetite. The Bianchi-Melosi (2022) "fiscal stagflation" channel predicts that when debt/GDP exceeds certain thresholds, the fiscal theory of the price level (FTPL) begins to influence inflation expectations at long horizons, converting long-duration bonds from safe assets into risky assets.

**Fiscal dominance indicator**: The bifurcation is a measurable leading indicator. Full fiscal dominance would manifest as positive correlation across the entire curve (including 2Y). The current bifurcation — negative at 2Y, positive at 30Y — suggests partial fiscal dominance, where the front end still trusts the central bank but the back end prices fiscal risk. The falsifiable prediction in the knowledge base (2Y-SPX turning positive = full regime shift) is exactly the test that the NK framework would prescribe.

### Claim 4: r* Level Shift
The pre-pandemic consensus estimate of r* was 0.25-0.75% (Laubach-Williams, Holston-Laubach-Williams models). Post-pandemic estimates have shifted to 1.0-1.75%, driven by:
- Higher trend fiscal deficits (+2-3pp of GDP structural deficit vs pre-pandemic)
- AI-driven investment demand (higher marginal product of capital)
- Deglobalization friction (reduced savings glut from global rebalancing)
- Demographic effects (labor supply constraints raising equilibrium wages)

The Fed's own Summary of Economic Projections shows the median longer-run dot at 3.0% nominal (implying r* ~1.0% with 2% inflation target), up from 2.5% (r* ~0.5%) in 2019.

**Correlation implications**: When r* is near zero, the effective lower bound truncates rate distributions asymmetrically — rates can rise but cannot fall much below zero. This truncation mechanically generates negative stock-bond correlation because in bad states (equities falling), rates are stuck near zero rather than falling. With r* at 1.0-1.5%, the policy rate in recession would fall to 1-2%, providing substantial space for bonds to rally. But the symmetric upside risk is also restored: if inflation resurges, rates can rise significantly. This two-sided rate risk raises the unconditional correlation toward zero. Del Negro et al. (2019, Fed NY) and Kiley (2024, Fed Board) formalize this ELB effect on asset correlations.

### Claim 5: Taylor Rule Assessment
Standard Taylor Rule: i = r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*)

Calibration:
- r* = 1.25% (midpoint of current estimates)
- π (core PCE) = 2.4%
- π* = 2.0%
- (y - y*) = -0.25% (modest negative output gap)

Prescribed rate: 1.25% + 2.4% + 0.5(0.4%) + 0.5(-0.25%) = 1.25 + 2.4 + 0.2 - 0.125 = 3.725%

Using the Mankiw-modified or balanced-approach rule with a higher output gap coefficient (1.0 instead of 0.5): 1.25 + 2.4 + 0.5(0.4%) + 1.0(-0.25%) = 3.45%

Current effective rate: 4.25-4.50%. This implies the Fed is 0-100bp above the Taylor Rule prescription, consistent with "slightly restrictive" but not dramatically so. The correlation implication: with the Fed near neutral, the next move is data-dependent rather than directionally obvious, creating the conditions for correlation to flip based on the marginal shock.

### Claim 6: Reaction Function Endogeneity
The three-channel framework from the knowledge base (iter_0005) correctly identifies the commodity-inflation-correlation mapping but treats the Fed reaction function as exogenous. In the NK framework, the reaction function is endogenous to the inflation regime:

- **Well-anchored expectations (flat Phillips Curve)**: The Fed can "look through" supply shocks → Channel A dominates → negative correlation. This was the 2014-2019 regime.
- **De-anchored expectations (steep Phillips Curve)**: The Fed must respond aggressively to supply shocks → Channel C dominates → positive correlation. This was the 2022 regime.
- **Partially re-anchored (current)**: The reaction function is uncertain — the Fed wants to look through but cannot if inflation expectations deteriorate. The Michigan 5Y inflation expectations survey at ~3.0% (above the 2.0-2.5% range consistent with target) suggests partial but incomplete re-anchoring.

This endogeneity means the probability-weighted pricing (~70% Channel A, ~30% Channel C per the KB framework assessment) is itself a function of inflation expectations dynamics, creating a feedback loop: if markets price Channel C, inflation expectations may rise, making Channel C more likely. This reflexivity is absent from the basic three-channel taxonomy.

### Claim 7: Bimodal Correlation Distribution
I model the correlation distribution as a mixture of two regimes:

**Regime D (demand-dominant)**: Stock-bond correlation = -0.30 to -0.50. Occurs when the output gap is the dominant macro variable and inflation expectations are anchored. Historical analogue: 2015-2019. Conditional on this regime, 60/40 portfolios perform well with Sharpe ratios of 0.8-1.0.

**Regime S (supply-dominant)**: Stock-bond correlation = +0.20 to +0.40. Occurs when inflation is the dominant variable and the central bank is tightening into weakness. Historical analogue: 2022, 1970s episodes. Conditional on this regime, 60/40 portfolios have Sharpe ratios of 0.2-0.4.

Current regime probabilities: 55-60% Regime D, 40-45% Regime S.
Unconditional correlation: 0.55 × (-0.35) + 0.45 × (+0.30) = -0.19 + 0.13 = **-0.06**

This near-zero unconditional correlation is consistent with my estimate and reflects the genuine uncertainty about which regime will dominate. Critically, portfolio optimization using the unconditional correlation would be suboptimal — the bimodality calls for regime-conditional allocation or dynamic hedging.

### Claim 8: Directional Forecast
The base case for 2026-2027 tilts toward Regime D (demand-dominant, negative correlation) for the following reasons:

1. **Labor market cooling**: The Sahm Rule indicator has been trending upward. Job openings/unemployed ratio has fallen from ~2.0 to ~1.2, approaching pre-pandemic equilibrium (~1.0-1.2). Continuation of this trend suggests rising unemployment and widening output gap.

2. **Fiscal impulse fading**: After years of expansionary fiscal policy, the impulse (change in deficit) is neutral to slightly contractionary in 2026 as pandemic-era programs expire and discretionary spending faces caps. The Brookings Hutchins Fiscal Impact Measure suggests near-zero impulse through mid-2026.

3. **Lagged monetary policy effects**: The 2022-2023 tightening cycle's full effects operate with "long and variable lags" (Friedman's estimate of 12-24 months, updated by Romer and Romer (2023) to 18-36 months for the post-GFC era due to fixed-rate mortgage stock). The full effects of cumulative tightening are still transmitting through the economy in 2026.

4. **Inflation glide path**: If core PCE continues declining at ~20-30bp per quarter, it reaches ~2.0-2.2% by late 2026, allowing the Fed to cut. Rate cuts in response to weakening demand = classic negative correlation regime.

**Risk scenario (35-40%)**: Inflation proves sticky above 2.5% due to shelter costs, insurance, or a new supply shock (energy, tariffs, geopolitical disruption). The Fed holds rates restrictive or hikes further, generating stagflationary positive correlation. Key indicators to monitor: Michigan 5Y expectations, trimmed-mean PCE momentum, wage growth tracker relative to productivity growth.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary Policy** (monetary_policy): The correlation regime is a direct function of the central bank reaction function. Any change in the Fed's reaction function — higher inflation tolerance, dual mandate re-weighting, or de facto yield curve control — would alter the correlation regime. The Taylor Rule assessment in Claim 5 connects directly to monetary policy stance evaluation. The 2025-2026 policy path is the single most important determinant of near-term correlation dynamics.

**Inflation Regimes** (inflation_regimes): The demand/supply shock taxonomy (Claim 1) maps directly to the inflation regime. A regime shift from "anchored low inflation" to "volatile inflation" would structurally shift correlation positive, as the 1970s demonstrated. The "last mile" inflation persistence is both a correlation determinant and an inflation regime diagnostic. The Phillips Curve slope — central to NK analysis — is the bridge between inflation regimes and correlation outcomes.

**Fiscal Dominance** (fiscal_dominance): Claim 3's maturity-dependent bifurcation is a direct manifestation of the fiscal dominance theme. The knowledge base's prior work on fiscal dominance (iter_0016, iter_0017) established the term premium and sovereign stress channels. This analysis extends that work by embedding it in the NK fiscal theory framework (Bianchi-Melosi). The falsifiable prediction (2Y-SPX correlation turning positive = full fiscal dominance) bridges both topics.

**Volatility Regimes** (volatility_regimes): The bimodal correlation distribution (Claim 7) implies distinct volatility regimes. Under Regime D (demand-dominant), equity vol and rate vol are negatively correlated (risk-off = vol up, rates down). Under Regime S (supply-dominant), both vol surfaces move together (inflation shock = rate vol up, equity vol up). This has direct implications for options pricing and cross-asset vol strategies.

**AI & Technology Disruption** (ai_and_tech_disruption): The knowledge base's stock_bond_correlation_regime concept (iter_0015) posits AI as a stage-dependent correlation driver. From the NK perspective, AI is primarily a supply-side shock (positive productivity shock) that is disinflationary and growth-enhancing — in a standard NK model, this would produce negative correlation (rates fall on lower inflation, equities rise on higher earnings). But the demand-side AI investment boom partially offsets this. The net effect on correlation depends on which channel dominates, connecting to the broader question of whether AI is primarily a supply or demand phenomenon.

**Commodity Inflation Transmission** (commodity_inflation_transmission): The three-channel framework (Claim 6) directly extends the knowledge base's commodity-correlation mapping by endogenizing the central bank reaction function. A commodity supply shock's effect on stock-bond correlation is not determined by the shock itself but by the monetary policy response, which depends on inflation expectations anchoring.

**Global Sovereign Debt Sustainability** (sovereign_debt): The r* level shift (Claim 4) is driven in part by the global sovereign debt trajectory. Higher r* raises debt servicing costs, which raises fiscal risk premia, which widens the maturity bifurcation, which reduces the hedging value of long-duration bonds. This creates a feedback loop where fiscal sustainability concerns directly alter the correlation regime.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the post-2020 r* shift permanent or cyclical?** If the r* increase is driven by a temporary fiscal expansion and pandemic-related supply disruption, it will revert, and the negative correlation regime will be restored. If it reflects structural forces (demographics, deglobalization, higher trend deficits), the near-zero correlation regime persists. This is the single most important unresolved question for multi-year correlation forecasting.

2. **What is the Phillips Curve slope in the current regime?** The NK framework's correlation predictions depend critically on the Phillips Curve slope. A flat curve (inflation insensitive to the output gap) means the Fed can cut in response to growth weakness without inflation constraint, supporting negative correlation. A steep curve means inflation responds quickly to output gaps, constraining the Fed's ability to cut and potentially sustaining positive correlation. The empirical literature is divided — estimates range from 0.1 to 0.5 for the output gap coefficient.

3. **How should the maturity bifurcation be modeled dynamically?** The knowledge base documents static correlation ranges for 2Y and 30Y, but the bifurcation itself evolves over time. Is the bifurcation widening, narrowing, or oscillating? What is the half-life of the current regime? Time-varying parameter models (e.g., DCC-GARCH with Markov switching) could address this but are notoriously difficult to estimate in real time.

4. **What is the threshold for fiscal dominance to reach the front end?** The falsifiable prediction (2Y-SPX correlation turning positive) identifies the diagnostic but not the trigger. What level of debt/GDP, primary deficit, or central bank credibility erosion would cause this? Historical analogues (Italy 2011, UK 2022, Japan's YCC exit) may provide calibration points, but the US fiscal position is unprecedented in scale among reserve currency issuers.

5. **Does index concentration (Magnificent 7, AI beneficiaries) alter the aggregate stock-bond correlation?** If a large fraction of equity market cap is in AI/tech companies whose earnings are relatively insensitive to interest rates (or even positively correlated with productivity-driven disinflation), the aggregate SPX-Treasury correlation may differ from the broad-economy-implied correlation. This composition effect is not captured in standard NK models that use a representative equity index.

6. **Can the correlation regime be identified in real time with sufficient lead time for portfolio adjustment?** Academic regime-switching models identify correlation regimes with 12-18 month lags. If the regime can only be identified ex-post, its usefulness for portfolio construction is limited. Real-time indicators (breakeven inflation momentum, labor market leading indicators, Fed communication text analysis) may provide earlier signals but with lower accuracy. The signal-to-noise ratio for real-time regime identification remains unquantified.

7. **How does the stock-bond correlation interact with the monetary policy transmission mechanism in a world of quantitative tightening (QT)?** The NK model's transmission mechanism runs through the short rate (expectations channel) and forward guidance. QT adds a supply channel that operates directly on term premium and may alter the correlation dynamics independently of the policy rate. If QT mechanically raises long-end term premium, it could sustain the maturity bifurcation regardless of the short-rate trajectory.
