### Claim 2: The 1973 Analogue Is Structurally Wrong

The iter_0009 macro_strategist invokes 1973-74 as the "closest analogue" and projects a 30-40% equity drawdown with bonds offering no offset. The structural comparison fails on three critical dimensions:

- **Energy intensity:** US GDP energy intensity (BTU per dollar of GDP) has fallen from ~15,000 BTU/$GDP in 1973 to ~5,500 BTU/$GDP in 2025 — a 63% decline. Each dollar of oil price increase has roughly 1/3 the GDP impact it had in 1973. The "oil at $106" headline is dramatic, but its macroeconomic punch is structurally smaller.

- **Net energy position:** The US was importing ~35% of its oil consumption in 1973 and had no domestic production response capacity. In 2025-2026, the US is a net energy producer — higher oil prices improve the US current account at the margin, support Permian Basin employment and investment, and generate windfall tax revenues from energy producers. The distributional effects are real (consumers pay more, producers earn more), but the aggregate GDP impact is ambiguous, not unambiguously negative.

- **Shale production elasticity:** The US shale industry can increase production by 500K-1M bbl/day within 6-12 months at $100+ oil, based on demonstrated behavior during the 2010-2014 shale boom and the 2017-2019 production ramp. This supply response did not exist in 1973, 1979, or even 2008. It functionally caps the duration of any oil supply shock by creating endogenous supply replacement.

The better analogue may be the 2022 Ukraine-related oil shock: oil spiked from ~$75 to $130 in 10 trading days, the "stagflation" narrative became consensus, and oil was back below $80 within 6 months as demand destruction, SPR releases, and supply adjustments took effect. Equities bottomed in October 2022 — 6 months after the oil peak — and the "stagflation" scenario did not materialize.

**What would prove this wrong:** A sustained disruption of >3 million bbl/day of supply (Strait of Hormuz closure scenario) lasting 6+ months, which would exceed the shale response capacity and SPR buffer.
