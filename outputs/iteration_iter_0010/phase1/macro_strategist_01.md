# Volatility Regime Analysis & Vol Surface Dynamics — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Volatility regimes are endogenous to the macroeconomic regime — specifically, they are determined by the interaction of the output gap, the inflation gap, and the stance of monetary policy relative to the Taylor Rule prescription.** Low-volatility regimes predominate when the output gap is closing under accommodative-but-credible monetary policy; high-volatility regimes emerge when either (a) the output gap is large and uncertain in sign, (b) inflation deviates substantially from target with ambiguous shock decomposition (demand vs. supply), or (c) the policy reaction function itself becomes uncertain. Volatility is not an exogenous "regime" that markets enter randomly — it is a reduced-form expression of macroeconomic uncertainty as priced through the stochastic discount factor.

2. **The implied-realized volatility spread (the variance risk premium, VRP) is a direct reflection of macroeconomic uncertainty about the output gap and the policy reaction function, not merely a compensation for jump risk or volatility-of-volatility.** In a New Keynesian model with time-varying uncertainty (Fernandez-Villaverde et al. 2015), the VRP widens when agents face greater uncertainty about the state of the economy — particularly when they cannot distinguish demand shocks from supply shocks, or when the central bank's reaction function is perceived to be in flux. The VRP therefore contains information about the probability distribution of future macro outcomes, not just about the distribution of asset prices per se.

3. **The VIX-MOVE correlation structure encodes the dominant shock type in the macro regime.** When demand shocks dominate (e.g., 2008-2009, 2020), VIX and MOVE rise together because both equity and rates markets reprice the output gap simultaneously, and the policy response is unambiguous (ease). When supply shocks dominate (e.g., 2022), VIX and MOVE can decouple or exhibit non-standard dynamics because the Taylor Rule gives conflicting signals — rates volatility surges on inflation uncertainty while equity volatility may initially remain contained if nominal earnings rise with prices. The correlation between VIX and MOVE is therefore a diagnostic for the macro shock mix, directly analogous to how stock-bond return correlations shift with the demand/supply shock composition (Campbell, Sunderam, and Viceira 2017).

4. **Rates volatility (MOVE index) is systematically underpriced relative to New Keynesian fundamentals in the current macro regime (as of early 2026), because markets are pricing a return to the low-r-star, low-inflation steady state that prevailed from 2010-2019, while the probability distribution over the natural rate and the long-run inflation anchor has materially widened.** The Laubach-Williams and Holston-Laubach-Williams r-star estimates carry 95% confidence intervals exceeding 300 basis points, and the post-pandemic structural shifts (deglobalization, fiscal expansion, AI-driven supply shifts) raise the probability of a higher terminal r-star. If markets are pricing a single-point r-star estimate while the true distribution is fat-tailed, rates implied volatility should be persistently higher than realized volatility — the opposite of the "rates vol is cheap" trade, which assumes realized will converge up to implied.

5. **The volatility surface in equity index options (skew, term structure) encodes the New Keynesian Phillips Curve trade-off.** Steep downside skew (out-of-the-money puts expensive relative to ATM) reflects the asymmetric output gap risk: negative output gaps trigger nonlinear financial accelerator dynamics (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist 1999) that generate fat left tails, while positive output gaps are bounded by capacity constraints and produce thinner right tails. The term structure of implied volatility (contango vs. backwardation) reflects the expected persistence of the current macro regime — steep contango during low-vol regimes signals confidence that the output gap will remain closed, while backwardation signals that the current elevated uncertainty is expected to persist or worsen.

6. **Central bank communication and forward guidance have become first-order drivers of volatility regimes, operating through what I term the "reaction function uncertainty channel."** In the standard NK model, the Taylor Rule is known to agents, and uncertainty derives from shocks to the economy. In practice, post-GFC monetary policy has introduced a new source of uncertainty: uncertainty about the reaction function itself. When the Fed pivots abruptly — as in the December 2018 dovish turn, the "transitory" inflation debacle of 2021, or the 2024 communication volatility around rate cut timing — it is not the economic data that is surprising but the mapping from data to policy. This "Taylor Rule uncertainty" increases the variance of the stochastic discount factor and produces volatility spikes that are disproportionate to the underlying economic news. The NK model with policy rule uncertainty (Bianchi 2012 on regime-switching monetary policy) formalizes this channel.

7. **Quantitative tightening (QT) operates as a volatility regime catalyst through two distinct channels: the reserve scarcity channel and the duration extraction channel.** The reserve scarcity channel (Acharya and Rajan 2022) predicts that as the central bank drains reserves, interbank market stress emerges at unpredictable thresholds — as demonstrated by the September 2019 repo spike. This creates discontinuous jumps in rates volatility. The duration extraction channel is the reverse of the QE portfolio rebalancing mechanism (Vayanos and Vila 2021): as the Fed returns duration to the market, the private sector must absorb it, requiring higher term premia as compensation. The interaction of these channels means QT does not simply unwind QE symmetrically — it introduces convex volatility risk because the reserve scarcity threshold is unknown and hit discontinuously.

8. **Volatility regime transitions exhibit a "macro trigger, micro amplification" structure that is well-captured by the NK model with financial frictions.** The initial impulse for a regime shift from low-vol to high-vol is typically macroeconomic: an unexpected inflation print, a policy surprise, a geopolitical shock that alters the output gap trajectory. But the amplification mechanism is microstructural: dealer balance sheet constraints, VaR-based deleveraging, gamma hedging feedback loops in options markets, and passive rebalancing flows. The NK financial accelerator (Christiano, Motto, and Rostagno 2014) provides the bridge — it explains why small macro shocks can produce outsized volatility responses when financial intermediary balance sheets are stretched, because the external finance premium is convex in the leverage ratio.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Volatility endogenous to macro regime:**
The theoretical foundation is the New Keynesian DSGE model with stochastic volatility (Fernandez-Villaverde, Guerron-Quintana, Rubio-Ramirez, and Uribe 2011; Bloom 2009). In these models, second-moment shocks (uncertainty shocks) interact with first-moment dynamics through precautionary savings and wait-and-see investment behavior. Empirically, the relationship between the output gap and volatility is robust: the VIX averages approximately 13-15 when the CBO output gap is between -1% and +1% (near potential), but rises to 20-30+ when the output gap exceeds -2%. Baker, Bloom, and Davis (2016) constructed the Economic Policy Uncertainty Index and demonstrated its strong co-movement with realized and implied market volatility. Crucially, the causation runs from macro uncertainty to market volatility — exogenous uncertainty shocks in calibrated NK models replicate the empirical impulse response functions of the VIX and credit spreads to FOMC announcements and macro data releases (Leduc and Liu 2016). The implication is that "volatility regimes" are not autonomous phenomena but are derivative of the macroeconomic state — they require no separate regime-switching model if the macro fundamentals are correctly specified.

**Claim 2 — VRP as macro uncertainty measure:**
The variance risk premium — the difference between implied variance (VIX²) and expected realized variance — averages approximately 4-5 volatility points (Bollerslev, Tauchen, and Zhou 2009). This premium is compensation for bearing variance risk, but its time-variation is informative about the macroeconomic state. Bekaert and Hoerova (2014) decomposed the VIX² into an expected variance component and a variance risk premium component, showing that the VRP is a significant predictor of future economic activity: a high VRP signals elevated macro uncertainty that precedes output gap deterioration. In the NK framework, this maps onto uncertainty about the state vector — when agents cannot cleanly identify whether a given shock is demand-side (requiring rate cuts) or supply-side (requiring rate hikes), the range of possible policy paths widens dramatically, and the price of bearing this uncertainty (the VRP) increases. The 2021-2022 period is illustrative: the VRP was elevated not because realized volatility was low, but because the decomposition of inflation into demand-pull (fiscal stimulus, pent-up demand) and cost-push (supply chains, energy) components was genuinely uncertain, making the policy path maximally ambiguous.

**Claim 3 — VIX-MOVE correlation and shock decomposition:**
The logic follows directly from the NK Phillips Curve. Under a demand shock: output and inflation move together → the Taylor Rule prescribes a clear directional response → rates and equity markets both move → VIX and MOVE correlate positively. Under a supply shock: output and inflation move in opposite directions → the Taylor Rule faces a trade-off → the policy path depends on the weight placed on inflation vs. output stabilization (the λ parameter) → rates volatility may surge (inflation uncertainty) while equity volatility is ambiguous (nominal earnings may be supported). Empirically, the VIX-MOVE 90-day rolling correlation averaged approximately 0.65 during 2008-2009 (pure demand shock) but fell to 0.25-0.35 during 2022 (supply shock dominated). In Q3 2022, MOVE spiked above 160 while VIX remained in the 25-30 range — consistent with the supply shock decomposition where rates uncertainty exceeded equity uncertainty. This diagnostic is operationally valuable: a low or negative VIX-MOVE correlation signals that the macro regime is supply-shock-dominated, which has distinct implications for hedging and portfolio construction.

**Claim 4 — Rates vol underpricing thesis:**
This is the most market-relevant and most debatable claim. The thesis rests on the Laubach-Williams framework: if the true distribution of r-star has shifted rightward and widened (Brand, Bielecki, and Penalver 2018 at the ECB; Bauer and Rudebusch 2023 at the SF Fed), then the range of possible terminal policy rates is wider than markets are pricing. As of early 2026, the Fed funds rate sits at approximately 4.00-4.25% following gradual easing, and the rates market implies a terminal rate around 3.25-3.50%. If r-star has genuinely risen from the 0.5% pre-pandemic estimate toward 1.5-2.0% (as some structural models suggest given fiscal deficits, AI capex, and green transition investment), then the neutral nominal rate is 3.5-4.0% — and rate cuts below this level would be inflationary, requiring reversal. This two-sided risk to the terminal rate — the possibility that the easing cycle undershoots or overshoots neutral — should be reflected in elevated rates implied volatility. The counter-argument is that the Fed's reaction function anchors rate expectations and compresses the distribution, but this only holds if the reaction function is itself well-understood, which Claim 6 argues is increasingly questionable.

**Claim 5 — Vol surface and the Phillips Curve:**
Equity index option skew reflects the probability-weighted distribution of returns, which in the NK framework is driven by the output gap distribution. The asymmetry of skew (downside puts persistently more expensive than equivalent upside calls) is consistent with the nonlinear financial accelerator: small positive output gaps generate moderate positive returns, but large negative output gaps generate outsized negative returns because balance sheet impairment amplifies the downturn. Bates (2000) and Bollerslev and Todorov (2011) demonstrated that the left tail of the risk-neutral distribution is fatter than the physical distribution, reflecting a "crash risk premium" that is consistent with the financial accelerator mechanism. The term structure of volatility connects to macro regime persistence: when the NK model is in the neighborhood of steady state (output gap near zero, inflation near target), future shocks are expected to be mean-reverting, and the vol term structure is upward-sloping (contango). When the model is far from steady state (large output gap, inflation well above target), shocks persist, and the vol term structure flattens or inverts.

**Claim 6 — Reaction function uncertainty:**
Bianchi (2012) estimated a regime-switching monetary policy model for the US and found that shifts between hawkish and dovish regimes — changes in the Taylor Rule coefficients — are a significant source of macroeconomic and financial volatility. Bauer, Lakdawala, and Mueller (2022) showed that FOMC communication shocks — news about the policy reaction function, as distinct from news about the economic outlook — are a major driver of asset price movements on announcement days. The implications for volatility regimes are profound: if the reaction function is time-varying and uncertain, then even in periods of stable macroeconomic data, volatility can surge when the market reprices the central bank's preference parameters. The 2021 "transitory" episode is a canonical example: the economic data (rising inflation) was observable, but the Fed's interpretation and intended response were uncertain for months, keeping rates volatility elevated despite relatively stable equity markets. This reaction function uncertainty is distinct from and additive to the standard macro uncertainty captured in NK models with known policy rules.

**Claim 7 — QT as volatility catalyst:**
The reserve scarcity channel was validated by the September 2019 repo market episode: the effective fed funds rate spiked above the target range when reserves crossed a threshold that was unknown ex ante. Copeland, Duffie, and Yang (2021) documented that the reserve demand curve is highly nonlinear — adequate reserves over a wide range, then a steep cliff. This nonlinearity means QT introduces jump risk in rates volatility: conditions are calm until they suddenly are not. The duration extraction channel works through Vayanos and Vila (2021) preferred habitat model: the Fed's QE removed net supply of duration from the market, compressing term premia; QT reverses this, and the repricing of term premia generates rates volatility during the adjustment period. The interaction of these channels is non-trivial: reserve scarcity can trigger funding stress that forces leveraged investors to sell duration, creating a double hit to rates markets. The March 2023 banking stress (SVB) can be partially understood through this lens — duration losses accumulated during QT interacted with deposit flight to create a volatility event that was disproportionate to the underlying macro news.

**Claim 8 — Macro trigger, micro amplification:**
The Christiano-Motto-Rostagno (2014) NK model with a banking sector demonstrates that financial intermediary leverage amplifies macro shocks in a state-dependent manner. When intermediary leverage is low, a given macro shock passes through to asset prices approximately one-for-one. When leverage is high, the same shock is amplified 2-4x through the balance sheet channel because the external finance premium is convex in the leverage ratio. Adrian and Shin (2014) provided the empirical complement: broker-dealer leverage is procyclical and co-moves with risk appetite, creating endogenous amplification. On the microstructure side, Barbon and Buraschi (2021) documented that gamma hedging by options dealers can amplify or dampen underlying market moves depending on their aggregate gamma exposure — negative gamma positions create a pro-cyclical feedback loop where volatility begets volatility. The implication is that any model of volatility regimes that does not incorporate the interaction of macro fundamentals with intermediary balance sheet positions will systematically underestimate tail risk.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Rates-Equity Correlation (rates_equity_correlation):** Volatility regimes and the stock-bond correlation are jointly determined by the macro shock composition. In demand-shock-dominated regimes, the stock-bond correlation is negative (bonds hedge equities), and VIX and MOVE correlate positively. In supply-shock-dominated regimes, the stock-bond correlation turns positive (both assets lose value), and VIX-MOVE dynamics decouple. The current regime (early 2026) is ambiguous: the disinflationary trend supports negative stock-bond correlation, but structural inflation risks (deglobalization, fiscal deficits) introduce supply-shock potential that could flip both the correlation structure and the volatility regime. Any portfolio that depends on negative stock-bond correlation for diversification is implicitly making a claim about the volatility regime persisting.

**Monetary Policy (monetary_policy):** The Taylor Rule framework is the transmission mechanism from monetary policy to volatility regimes. When the policy rate deviates substantially from the Taylor prescription — either too loose (fueling risk-taking and suppressing vol) or too tight (triggering the financial accelerator and spiking vol) — the deviation itself is the first-order driver of the volatility regime. Forward guidance and QE have added new channels: forward guidance collapses the conditional distribution of future rates (reducing rates vol in the short term but potentially increasing it when guidance changes), while QE/QT directly manipulates the quantity of duration risk the private sector must hold. The reaction function uncertainty channel (Claim 6) is the critical nexus — monetary policy drives volatility not only through its level and expected path, but through the market's confidence in the mapping from data to policy decisions.

**Risk Appetite Regimes (risk_appetite_regimes):** The connection is tight and bidirectional. Risk appetite regimes and volatility regimes are overlapping representations of the same underlying macro state: low vol ≈ risk-on, high vol ≈ risk-off, but they are not perfectly synonymous. Risk appetite can remain elevated even as volatility rises if the source of volatility is perceived as transitory (e.g., event risk around elections or FOMC meetings). Conversely, vol can remain suppressed even as risk appetite deteriorates if structural vol sellers (systematic strategies, overwriting programs) dominate the supply side of options markets. The distinction matters for the rates vol underpricing thesis (Claim 4): if risk appetite is high but macro uncertainty is also elevated, risk premia may be compressed across equities and credit while rates vol is underpriced — a regime that rewards selling equity vol and buying rates vol until the macro uncertainty resolves.

**Credit Cycles (credit_equity_linkage):** Volatility regimes are a leading indicator of credit cycle inflection points. Credit spreads lag the VIX by 1-3 months at cycle turning points because credit markets are less liquid and reprice more slowly. The financial accelerator channel provides the mechanism: rising equity volatility erodes collateral values and increases the external finance premium, which widens credit spreads with a lag. The vol surface also encodes credit risk: equity index skew is elevated when the financial accelerator is active, because the left tail of equity returns corresponds to the nonlinear impairment of corporate balance sheets that triggers credit events.

**FX Regimes (fx_rates_divergence):** The Mundell-Fleming framework links volatility regimes to FX dynamics through the capital flow channel. Low-vol regimes encourage carry trades (borrowing low-yielding, investing in high-yielding currencies), which suppress FX volatility and compress EM risk premia. The transition to high-vol regimes triggers carry unwinds — simultaneously increasing FX volatility, appreciating the USD (safe haven / funding currency), and widening EM spreads. The vol surface in FX options (risk reversals) directly encodes carry trade vulnerability: steep risk reversals favoring downside puts on EM currencies signal that the market anticipates a vol regime transition that would trigger carry unwinds.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the current implied volatility surface correctly pricing the probability of a return to the 2010-2019 low-vol macro regime?** The pre-pandemic regime was characterized by r-star near zero, inflation persistently below target, and QE as the marginal policy tool — all of which suppressed vol structurally. If the structural parameters have shifted (higher r-star, stickier inflation around target, QT as baseline), then the entire vol surface should be re-leveled higher. But markets may be correctly pricing a return to low vol if AI productivity gains are deflationary and fiscal consolidation eventually restores pre-pandemic dynamics. The answer depends on whether 2020-2025 was a transitory perturbation or a genuine structural break — and the NK framework, which is skeptical of structural breaks, would lean toward transitory, while the evidence is accumulating in the other direction.

2. **What is the "neutral" level of rates implied volatility in a regime where r-star uncertainty is permanently elevated?** If the Laubach-Williams 95% confidence interval for r-star remains 300+ basis points wide, and if fiscal policy becomes a more active and unpredictable co-determinant of the output gap, then the "equilibrium" level of MOVE may be structurally higher than its 2010-2019 average of approximately 60. But how much higher? The calibration depends on the elasticity of rates vol to r-star uncertainty, which is not well-estimated in the literature.

3. **How does the growth of 0DTE (zero-days-to-expiry) options alter the vol regime dynamics described above?** The explosion of 0DTE options volume since 2022 has introduced a new intraday vol feedback mechanism: dealer gamma exposure changes rapidly, and the intraday pinning/anti-pinning dynamics may be creating a new source of volatility that is unrelated to macro fundamentals. This microstructural development may weaken the macro-vol linkage described in Claim 1 at high frequencies while leaving it intact at lower frequencies (monthly/quarterly). The NK model has nothing to say about intraday dynamics, creating a genuine gap in the framework.

4. **Can the VRP be decomposed in real time into "macro uncertainty" and "risk aversion" components with sufficient precision to be tradeable?** The Bekaert-Hoerova (2014) decomposition is elegant but relies on reduced-form proxies. A structural decomposition within a calibrated NK model with heterogeneous agents would provide sharper identification, but the computational requirements and model uncertainty may make this impractical. The practical question is whether the VRP signals described in Claim 2 can be translated into a vol timing strategy with a Sharpe ratio above transaction costs.

5. **How should the NK framework incorporate geopolitical risk as a source of volatility regimes?** The standard model treats the economy as closed or semi-open with known trading partners. Events like the Russia-Ukraine conflict, US-China technology decoupling, and potential Taiwan contingencies represent "tail supply shocks" that are outside the model's shock taxonomy. Caldara and Iacoviello (2022) constructed a Geopolitical Risk Index that correlates with volatility, but integrating geopolitical scenarios into an NK-consistent volatility framework remains an open modeling challenge.

6. **What role does fiscal policy play in shaping the vol surface, and is the market correctly pricing fiscal tail risks?** Large structural deficits (US running 6-7% of GDP deficits at near-full employment as of 2025-2026) represent a potential supply-side shock to the Treasury market that could independently trigger a rates vol regime shift — a "fiscal volatility premium" distinct from the monetary policy-driven channels described above. The fiscal theory of the price level (Cochrane 2023) suggests that if fiscal sustainability is questioned, the entire term structure of rates vol shifts upward to reflect the possibility of fiscal dominance. Whether this is already in the price or represents an under-hedged tail risk is the key question for rates vol positioning.
