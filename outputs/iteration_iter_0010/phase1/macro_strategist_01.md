## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Accelerating demand-side transmission:**
The 1973-75 template showed approximately 3-4 quarters between the October 1973 oil embargo and the onset of demand-side contraction (real PCE began declining in Q2 1974). Modern financial transmission may compress this lag for three reasons: (a) algorithmic and passive investment strategies amplify the wealth effect velocity — the S&P 500 decline from the February peak translates to portfolio losses more rapidly through daily-rebalancing target-date funds and risk-parity strategies than through the 1970s' broker-mediated, quarterly-rebalanced accounts; (b) credit tightening propagates faster through automated underwriting systems (bank lending standards surveys in Q2 will be the key confirmation); (c) consumer spending responds faster due to real-time visibility of price increases and portfolio losses (behavioral finance evidence: loss aversion manifests faster in an environment with constant portfolio value monitoring). The counter-argument: automatic fiscal stabilizers (unemployment insurance, SNAP) and the 6% structural deficit provide a larger demand floor than existed in 1973. Net assessment: demand-side lag compressed to 1-3 quarters (vs. 3-4 quarters historically), placing the onset in Q2-Q3 2026.

**Claim 2 — Taylor Rule divergence and revealed reaction function:**

The practical implication: the first rate cut requires either (a) core PCE below 2.5% with a clear downward trajectory, or (b) unemployment above 4.5% with acceleration — neither of which is likely in Q2 2026 given the supply-shock inflation impulse. **Claim 3 — Output gap and NAIRU indeterminacy:**
The output gap estimation during supply shocks is a well-known problem in the NK literature. Orphanides (2003, AER) demonstrated that real-time output gap estimates were wrong by 2-3 percentage points during the 1970s oil shocks — the CBO contemporaneously estimated the output gap as moderately negative when it was actually near zero (because potential output had declined with the supply shock). The policy error was easing into what was misperceived as a large negative output gap, validating inflation. The modern parallel: if the CBO's potential output estimates have not yet incorporated the productivity loss from $100+ oil (energy-intensive sectors operating below optimal capacity), the measured output gap overstates the true gap, and any Taylor Rule prescription based on that gap is too accommodative. Ball and Mankiw (2002, "The NAIRU in Theory and Practice") document that the NAIRU shifts with supply shocks by 0.5-1.0 percentage points — meaning the "natural" unemployment rate may have risen from approximately 4.0% to 4.5-5.0%, making the current 4.0-4.1% unemployment rate potentially *below* NAIRU despite the supply shock. This would imply even more inflationary pressure than the headline numbers suggest.

**Claim 8 — Framework limitations:**
Blanchard and Galí (2007, "Real Wage Rigidities and the New Keynesian Model," JMCB) is the canonical reference for the divine coincidence breakdown: when real wage rigidities interact with supply shocks, stabilizing inflation and stabilizing the output gap become contradictory objectives. The NK model's prescriptive clarity depends on the divine coincidence — when it fails, the optimal policy involves a weighted compromise between inflation and output gap stabilization, with the weights determined by social preferences (which are unobservable). Woodford (2003, *Interest and Prices*) acknowledges that the "cashless economy" assumption underlying NK policy analysis becomes problematic when financial frictions are severe — precisely the condition during a supply-shock-driven credit tightening. The honest assessment: the NK framework correctly identifies the *problem* (supply shocks break the divine coincidence) but cannot prescribe the *solution* without knowing the social welfare function weights. For practical regime assessment, this means the NK framework is diagnostic (identifying the type of shock, the direction of gaps, the Taylor Rule implications) but not prescriptive (cannot specify optimal policy or therefore predict the policy response that determines risk appetite).

## Connections to Other Topics

**Credit Cycles:** The "r-star fog" (Claim 4) has a direct credit market implication: if the real policy rate gap is genuinely unknowable, then credit conditions are being determined more by endogenous credit dynamics (Minsky cycle position, CLO arbitrage economics, private credit deployment pressure) than by the policy rate channel. This validates the Post-Keynesian macro_strategist_02's core claim (iter_0009, Claim 1) that endogenous credit generates risk appetite — not because the NK framework is wrong, but because the NK transmission channel (policy rate → r* gap → IS curve → output → credit conditions) is temporarily broken by r* uncertainty. During the r-star fog, credit conditions are "decoupled" from policy — exactly the condition that produced the 2003-2007 anomaly where credit expanded despite Fed tightening.

**Volatility Regimes:** The supply-shock regime produces a specific volatility signature that differs from demand-driven risk-off. In a demand shock, equity vol rises while rates vol falls (as the market prices in easing). In a supply shock, both equity vol and rates vol rise simultaneously (because the policy response is ambiguous), while commodity vol spikes. The VIX-MOVE co-movement should be monitored as a real-time supply vs. demand shock discriminator: if VIX and MOVE are positively correlated with elevated commodity vol, the supply-shock regime persists; if VIX rises while MOVE falls, demand-side dynamics have taken over and the Fed pivot becomes possible.

**Fiscal Policy:** The Mundell-Fleming analysis (Claim 5) connects directly to the sectoral balances identity. The macro_strategist_02's claim that the 6.5% fiscal deficit provides a structural floor for risk appetite (via Kalecki profit equation) is being tested: if the supply shock forces fiscal expansion (energy subsidies, deficit widens to 7-8%), the Kalecki profit floor strengthens but term premium rises; if fiscal consolidation prevails (deficit narrows to 5%), the profit floor weakens and private sector financial balance shifts toward deficit — the precondition for Minsky instability. The fiscal response is the variable that determines whether the NK or Post-Keynesian warning system triggers first.

**FX and EM:** The Mundell-Fleming dollar appreciation prediction connects directly to the Global Financial Cycle (Miranda-Agrippino and Rey 2020). The dollar's appreciation compresses EM financial conditions independently of EM domestic policy, creating a correlated global risk-off that amplifies the developed-market supply-shock dynamics. Together they produce a "double squeeze" on EM risk appetite.

**Private Credit:** The output gap and NAIRU indeterminacy (Claim 3) has a specific private credit implication: if the output gap is more negative than currently measured (because potential output has declined with the supply shock), then the credit deterioration in private credit portfolios — particularly in energy-intensive mid-market companies — is more advanced than reported NAVs suggest. The mark-to-model suppression identified by iter_0008 (HC #7) interacts with the output gap measurement error: private credit NAVs are calibrated against *reported* economic conditions, and if those conditions are worse than reported, the NAV gap is wider than estimated.

**Term Premium and Rates:** The supply shock creates a specific term premium dynamic: inflation risk premium rises (because the inflation distribution has a fat right tail), while real-rate term premium is ambiguous (flight to safety compresses real-rate term premium, but fiscal supply expansion raises it). The net effect on the nominal term premium depends on which effect dominates — if inflation risk premium wins, the yield curve steepens and bonds fail as safe havens (1973-74 template); if flight-to-safety wins, the curve flattens and bonds partially hedge (2020 template). Current evidence (curves modestly steeper) favors the 1973-74 interpretation.
