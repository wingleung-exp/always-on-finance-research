## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Credit vol regimes map to credit cycle phases:**
The empirical basis is the Markov regime-switching literature applied to credit spreads (Ang-Bekaert 2002, Alexander-Kaeck 2008). HMM models fitted to IG and HY OAS time series consistently identify 2-3 volatility states with >0.85 persistence probabilities. The lag structure is mechanistic: equity markets price expectations forward, while credit spreads incorporate both expectations and actual funding conditions (new issuance pricing, bank lending surveys, covenant trigger levels). The 2-8 week equity-to-credit vol transmission is documented across the 2007-08, 2011, 2015-16, 2018, 2020, and 2022 episodes. The 6-12 month credit vol-to-default lead is driven by the channel: rising vol → wider new issue spreads → higher refinancing cost → coverage ratio erosion → eventual default.

**Claim 2 — Implied-realized credit vol compression:**
CDX HY 5Y option-implied vol vs. 3-month trailing realized spread vol has historically averaged a 15-25% premium (the variance risk premium in credit). When this compresses below 5%, it has reliably preceded spread widening events within 6 months (2007, 2014, 2018, 2021). The structural suppression mechanisms are well-documented: CLO mandate-driven buying (LCD/PitchBook data showing 60-70% absorption), systematic credit vol selling by insurance companies and pension funds seeking yield pickup on CDX puts, and private credit's amend-and-extend practices that delay observable distress. Each mechanism independently suppresses measured vol; combined, they create a regime where credit implied vol systematically understates true tail risk.

**Claim 3 — VIX-MOVE correlation as credit regime signal:**
The VIX-MOVE 60-day rolling correlation has historically oscillated between -0.2 and +0.8. Positive correlation regimes (both vol measures rising together) coincide with the most severe credit spread widening episodes: 2008 (+0.7 correlation, HY OAS >2000bp), March 2020 (+0.65, HY OAS >1100bp), 2022 rate shock (+0.5, HY OAS ~600bp). Negative or low correlation regimes (equity vol rising, rates vol stable) correspond to contained credit events: August 2015 (China deval, HY OAS +150bp then reverted), February 2018 (Volmageddon, credit barely moved), August 2024 (yen carry, HY OAS +100bp then reverted within 3 weeks). The mechanism: when rates vol rises simultaneously, the funding cost channel activates — repo rates spike, bank risk limits bind, CLO warehouse lines tighten — creating a second-order amplification that pure equity vol does not trigger.

**Claim 4 — Maturity wall as stored vol:**
The data is from Moody's, S&P LCD, and PitchBook: ~$350-400B in HY bonds and ~$500-700B in leveraged loans maturing 2025-2027. Weighted average coupons on 2019-2021 vintage issuance are 4.5-5.5%. Current all-in refinancing cost at prevailing spreads: 7.5-12% for BB/B-rated credits. The incremental debt service burden of $25-40B annually represents 5-8% of aggregate sub-IG EBITDA. Under a no-recession scenario, this alone pushes 3-5% of the HY universe into distress. Under a mild recession scenario, the figure rises to 8-12%. Covenant-lite documentation (>90% of institutional loans) means there is no early warning — the vol regime shift will be abrupt, not gradual, because borrowers won't trip maintenance covenants; they'll arrive at maturity unable to refinance.

**Claim 5 — Minsky cycle as credit vol regime transition:**
The theoretical framework links Minsky's three financing regimes (hedge, speculative, Ponzi) to observable credit vol states. In hedge finance (strong coverage, ample liquidity), credit vol is low and mean-reverting. In speculative finance (reliance on refinancing to service debt), credit vol becomes path-dependent — sensitive to rate and spread levels. In Ponzi finance (reliance on asset appreciation to service debt), credit vol becomes explosive and regime-switching. The current evidence for late-phase transition: leverage ratios by rating cohort have risen 0.3-0.5x since 2022, interest coverage ratios have declined 0.5-1.0x, EBITDA addbacks on new deals average 25-40% (per BDC disclosures), and the upgrade/downgrade ratio has been below 1.0 for five consecutive quarters. The HY-IG spread differential, currently at approximately 280-320bp, sits in the range that has historically preceded every credit cycle turn (it widens to 400-500bp before peak stress).

**Claim 6 — Rates vol underpricing and credit implications:**
The argument proceeds in two steps. First, MOVE is not subject to the 0DTE structural suppression that affects VIX: there is no equivalent of the massive short-dated options flow in rates that pins spot VIX below the level warranted by fundamental uncertainty. Second, the policy environment (potential fiscal dominance, uncertain central bank reaction functions, tariff-driven supply shocks) warrants structurally higher rates vol, yet MOVE has traded in a range suggesting markets price a return to the pre-2022 low-vol rates regime. This is independent of any macro deterioration — it is a pure vol-of-vol transmission.

**Claim 7 — CDX-VIX skew divergence:**
CDX HY 5Y 25-delta put skew vs. SPX 25-delta put skew for matched tenors. 1. **Has the 0DTE options explosion permanently altered the VIX's information content for credit analysis, or is this a structural shift that will itself regime-switch when market microstructure changes?** If VIX is structurally suppressed by 0DTE flows, credit analysts need to recalibrate every model that uses VIX as an input. 2. **What is the true "shadow" credit volatility when private credit marks are adjusted to market?** If $1.7-2.0T in private credit AUM is marked with 1-4 quarter lags and 25-40% EBITDA addbacks, the observable credit vol metrics (CDX realized vol, HY OAS vol) are understating the true dispersion in credit quality. Estimating the "mark-to-market" credit vol surface would require data that, by definition, does not exist in public markets.

3. **Can the maturity wall produce a gradual vol regime transition, or is the covenant-lite structure (>90% of institutional loans) inherently binary?** Historical credit cycles had maintenance covenants that created early, dispersed distress signals (covenant breaches → workouts → measured spread widening). The covenant-lite structure may mean the vol regime transition is compressed into a much shorter window, with more severe peak vol — but we have no full-cycle test of a predominantly covenant-lite loan universe.

4. **What is the forward-looking HY-IG spread differential threshold that would confirm the vol regime has shifted from "complacent carry" to "correction/rotation"?** The historical base rate suggests 350-400bp triggers serious portfolio repositioning. Current levels of ~280-320bp are elevated but not actionable. Is this threshold stable, or has it shifted due to structural changes in market composition (more BB, less CCC in HY; more BBB in IG)?

5. **How should credit vol regime models incorporate the feedback loop between spread widening and issuer funding costs in a world where ~40% of sub-IG credit is in private markets?** The traditional feedback loop (spreads widen → funding costs rise → fundamentals deteriorate → spreads widen further) assumed public market price discovery. When a large share of credit is privately held and marked to model, the feedback loop may be delayed but more violent when it eventually engages — or it may be permanently dampened by lender discretion and extend-and-pretend. We do not yet know which.

6. **Is the CDX-VIX skew divergence a reliable signal or a data-mining artifact?** Three to four episodes is insufficient for statistical inference. A rigorous out-of-sample test would require either more history (difficult — CDX options liquidity is relatively recent) or a cross-market validation (e.g., does the same signal work in European iTraxx vs. EuroStoxx skew?).
