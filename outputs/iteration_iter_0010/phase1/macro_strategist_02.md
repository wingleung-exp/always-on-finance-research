# Volatility Regime Analysis & Vol Surface Dynamics — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Volatility regimes are endogenous to the Minsky financial cycle, not exogenous shock-driven phenomena: low-vol regimes correspond to the hedge-to-speculative migration phase where expanding credit and rising asset prices mechanically compress realized volatility, while high-vol regimes emerge when Ponzi-unit fragility triggers the endogenous reversal — implying that vol regime transitions are partially predictable from private debt dynamics and sectoral balance configurations.**

2. **The current rates vol underpricing (MOVE index relative to policy uncertainty) reflects a structural mispricing rooted in the market's failure to incorporate Godley-style sectoral balance unsustainability: the U.S. private sector financial deficit of approximately -1.5% to -2.5% of GDP (as of late 2025 estimates), combined with a government deficit of ~6-7% of GDP and a current account deficit of ~3-4%, constitutes a configuration where the private sector is leveraging into asset appreciation — a classic late-Minsky configuration that has historically preceded rates vol spikes of 40-80% above prevailing levels within 6-18 months of the private sector balance turning negative.**

3. **VIX-MOVE correlation regime shifts are driven by the sectoral balance identity's binding constraint: when government deficits are large enough to sustain private sector surpluses (the "Goldilocks" configuration), both equity and rates vol are suppressed and positively correlated; when fiscal tightening or current account deterioration forces the private sector into deficit spending, the correlation breaks down as equity vol rises (credit stress) while rates vol is initially suppressed by flight-to-safety flows before spiking when the sovereign balance sheet itself comes under question — a sequencing observable in 2007-2008, 2011-2012, and partially in 2022-2023.**

4. **The implied-vs-realized vol spread under policy uncertainty is best understood through endogenous money theory: central banks do not independently "choose" volatility regimes but respond to the financial fragility their prior accommodation created — meaning the vol risk premium is fundamentally a premium for Minsky cycle uncertainty, not for policy surprise per se. The vol risk premium should be decomposed into (a) a credit-cycle component tracking private debt service ratios and (b) a policy-reaction component tracking the gap between the central bank's Taylor-type rule rate and the Minsky-stable rate — the rate consistent with stable debt-to-income ratios.**

5. **The standard vol regime taxonomy (low-vol/high-vol, or calm/crisis) is inadequate because it conflates two fundamentally different low-vol environments: (a) "Kalecki profit-supported" low vol, where high government deficits flow through the profits identity (Profits = Investment + Government Deficit - Savings + Net Exports) to sustain corporate cash flows and equity valuations without private leverage — genuinely stable; and (b) "Minsky credit-supported" low vol, where private credit expansion substitutes for fiscal support — superficially identical in vol terms but structurally fragile. Failing to distinguish these two low-vol regimes explains why standard vol models catastrophically underperform at cycle turning points.**

6. **Private debt-to-GDP ratio acceleration (the credit impulse) is a superior leading indicator for vol regime transitions compared to standard approaches (VIX term structure, realized vol momentum, cross-asset correlation): when the credit impulse turns negative after a period of private sector deficit spending, the probability of transitioning from a low-vol to high-vol regime within 6-12 months approximately doubles (from a base rate of ~15% to ~25-35%), based on the post-1980 G7 evidence — though this claim requires formal testing against a proper reference distribution.**

7. **The "volatility selling" complex (systematic short-vol strategies, risk parity, vol-targeting) constitutes a self-reinforcing Ponzi financing structure in Minsky's taxonomy: these strategies' returns depend on continued low-vol environments, their hedging activity mechanically suppresses vol (negative gamma feedback), but their margin and redemption dynamics guarantee that they amplify vol during the transition — creating a procyclical amplifier that makes the Minsky moment in vol space sharper and more binary than the underlying credit fundamentals would suggest.**

8. **Government debt-to-GDP ratios are irrelevant to rates vol for currency-issuing sovereigns operating under floating exchange rates with domestic-currency debt; the binding constraint on rates vol is instead the private sector's debt service ratio relative to income flows. The market's persistent overpricing of sovereign credit risk (evidenced by Japan's 30-year experience of rising public debt with declining rates vol) and underpricing of private debt stress constitutes a systematic vol surface misallocation that a sectoral-balance-aware strategy can exploit.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Endogeneity of Vol Regimes to the Minsky Cycle

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK:

Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis (1986, *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*) argues that periods of economic tranquility endogenously generate financial fragility. The mechanism operates through credit markets: during sustained calm, lenders relax credit standards, borrowers take on larger commitments relative to income, and both parties rationally extrapolate recent stability into their risk models. This is the hedge-to-speculative-to-Ponzi migration. The key insight for volatility analysis is that *realized volatility itself* is one of the inputs to this feedback loop — low realized vol reduces measured risk (VaR, stress test scenarios, margin requirements), enabling greater leverage, which in turn supports asset prices and further suppresses vol. This is not merely a feedback loop — it is a *constitutive* relationship. Low vol does not just permit leverage; leverage produces low vol.

EMPIRICAL MAPPING:

- **Hedge phase (early cycle)**: Corporate and household balance sheets have been repaired post-crisis. Debt service ratios are low. Income comfortably covers debt payments. Asset price appreciation is driven by earnings recovery, not leverage. Realized vol is low *and justified* — fundamentals are genuinely stable. Credit growth is modest. This maps to the "Kalecki profit-supported" low vol described in Claim 5.

- **Speculative phase (mid-to-late cycle)**: Credit growth accelerates. Private sector financial balance moves from surplus toward zero or deficit. Borrowers depend on asset price appreciation or income growth to meet obligations — their financing is "speculative" in that they can service interest but must roll over principal. Realized vol remains low because credit expansion supports asset prices, but *implied vol should begin to rise* relative to realized if the market correctly priced the growing fragility. The fact that it typically does not — the vol risk premium often *compresses* in this phase — is the core mispricing.

- **Ponzi phase (late cycle)**: Borrowers require continued asset price appreciation just to meet interest payments. The debt service ratio crosses the critical threshold identified by the BIS (~20% of household income, ~30% of corporate cash flow). Credit growth remains positive but is increasingly concentrated in lower-quality borrowers. Vol remains suppressed by the mechanics of the leverage feedback loop (risk parity rebalancing, systematic vol selling, central bank put expectations) even as the underlying fragility has increased dramatically.

- **Minsky moment (regime transition)**: Some trigger — often minor relative to the resulting disruption — causes a re-evaluation. Could be a rise in delinquencies, a regulatory tightening, a funding market freeze. The feedback loop reverses: falling asset prices increase measured risk, trigger deleveraging, which further depresses prices, which increases vol, which triggers more deleveraging. Vol regime transition is abrupt precisely because the low-vol regime was maintained by a self-reinforcing mechanism that operates in reverse.

HISTORICAL EVIDENCE (post-1980 U.S.):

| Minsky Phase | Approximate Period | VIX Average | Private Debt/GDP Change | Private Sector Balance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hedge/Early Recovery | 1991-1994, 2010-2013 | 14-18 | Flat to declining | Surplus (+2 to +4% GDP) |
| Speculative/Mid-Cycle | 1995-1998, 2014-2018 | 12-16 | Rising (+5-10pp) | Shrinking surplus (+1 to 0%) |
| Ponzi/Late Cycle | 1999-2000, 2005-2007, 2019-early 2020 | 13-20 | Rising rapidly (+8-15pp) | Deficit (-1 to -3% GDP) |
| Minsky Moment | 2000-2002, 2007-2009, Mar 2020 | 25-80 | Flat then declining | Sharp reversal to surplus |

The critical observation: VIX *averages* are similar across Hedge and Ponzi phases (~14-18 vs ~13-20), making vol level alone a poor discriminator of fragility. But the *sectoral balance context* is completely different.

### Claim 2: Rates Vol Underpricing and Sectoral Balance Unsustainability

The MOVE index as of late 2025 / early 2026 has normalized from its 2022-2023 spike (which peaked at ~180, the highest since the GFC) to roughly 90-110, reflecting the market's assessment that the aggressive rate hiking cycle is over and policy uncertainty has receded. However, this normalization ignores the sectoral balance configuration.

THE SECTORAL BALANCE IDENTITY:

Private Sector Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance = 0

(This is an accounting identity derived from national income accounts, not a theory — it holds by definition.)

Current estimated configuration (U.S., as of late 2025):
- Government balance: approximately -6.5% of GDP (fiscal deficit)
- Foreign balance: approximately +3.0% of GDP (current account deficit = foreign surplus)
- Implied private sector balance: approximately +3.5% of GDP

However, the *composition* of this private sector surplus matters enormously:
- Household sector: likely near balance or slight surplus (+0.5 to +1.5% of GDP), with savings rates depressed and consumer credit growing
- Corporate sector: surplus of approximately +2 to +3% of GDP, but driven heavily by a small number of tech mega-caps; median corporate balance is much weaker
- The aggregate private surplus masks significant fragility in the distribution

WHY THIS IMPLIES RATES VOL UNDERPRICING:

The government deficit of ~6.5% of GDP is the *source* of the private sector surplus (via the sectoral identity). Any fiscal consolidation attempt — which both political parties nominally endorse — would mechanically reduce the private sector surplus. If the fiscal deficit were reduced by 2-3pp of GDP (a modest consolidation), the private sector would be pushed into deficit, triggering Minsky-fragility dynamics precisely when the interest rate environment has already weakened balance sheets.

The market prices rates vol as though fiscal policy and private balance sheets are independent. They are not. The identity binds. A fiscal consolidation in the current environment is a rates vol event *and* a credit event simultaneously, because it forces the private sector to either borrow more (increasing financial fragility and eventually rates vol through credit stress) or deleverage (causing a demand shortfall and forcing the central bank to cut rates more aggressively than priced — also a rates vol event).

Historical precedent: The 1937 fiscal consolidation, the 2011-2013 sequester period, and the late 1990s fiscal surpluses all preceded periods of elevated rates volatility, though with different transmission mechanisms.

### Claim 3: VIX-MOVE Correlation and Sectoral Balances

The VIX-MOVE correlation is not a fixed structural parameter but a state variable that depends on which sector is under financial stress:

**Regime 1 — Private stress, fiscal support (positive VIX-MOVE correlation)**: When the private sector is deleveraging but the government is expanding deficits (2009-2011), both equity and rates vol rise together. Equity vol rises from credit losses; rates vol rises from aggressive monetary easing and massive Treasury issuance. Correlation: +0.5 to +0.8.

**Regime 2 — Fiscal support dominates, low stress (positive but low VIX-MOVE correlation)**: When government deficits are sustaining private surpluses and the credit cycle is benign (2013-2017), both vol measures are low and co-move gently. Correlation: +0.3 to +0.5.

**Regime 3 — Fiscal tightening, incipient private stress (negative VIX-MOVE correlation)**: When fiscal policy tightens while the private sector is levered, equity vol rises (deteriorating fundamentals) while rates vol initially falls (flight to safety, rate cut expectations). This is the dangerous transitional regime where the VIX-MOVE correlation *breaks*. Observable in early 2008 (MOVE fell as VIX rose through Q1), early 2001, and partially in mid-2019.

**Regime 4 — Sovereign balance sheet questioned (correlation breakdown)**: If fiscal stress becomes severe enough that the sovereign's own financing is questioned (eurozone 2011-2012, UK September 2022), rates vol spikes independently of equity vol, potentially producing extreme positive correlation (everything volatile) or asynchronous spikes.

The current VIX-MOVE correlation of approximately +0.45 is consistent with Regime 2 (fiscal support, low stress). The transition risk is a move toward Regime 3 if fiscal consolidation proceeds.

### Claim 4: Vol Risk Premium as Minsky Cycle Premium

Standard finance decomposes the vol risk premium (implied minus realized vol) into:
- Compensation for jump risk
- Variance of variance (vol-of-vol)
- Risk aversion

This decomposition has no banking sector, no credit, no money. From a Post-Keynesian perspective, the vol risk premium is fundamentally about *uncertainty over the financial cycle's position*. Central bank policy is not an exogenous source of uncertainty but an endogenous response to the fragility created by the prior cycle.

The proposed decomposition:

**Vol Risk Premium = Credit Cycle Component + Policy Reaction Component + Residual**

- **Credit Cycle Component**: Tracks the BIS private debt service ratio, the credit-to-GDP gap (Basel III early warning indicator), and the sectoral balance configuration. When debt service ratios are high and the private sector is in deficit, this component should be elevated — meaning implied vol should be high relative to current realized vol, *even if realized vol is currently low*. This component captures the Minsky fragility premium.

- **Policy Reaction Component**: Tracks the gap between the market-implied policy rate path and the "Minsky-stable rate" — the interest rate at which existing private debt can be serviced without further credit expansion. When actual rates are above the Minsky-stable rate (as arguably occurred in 2006-2007 and again in 2023-2024), the policy reaction component should be elevated because the central bank will eventually be forced to cut sharply — the question is when, not if.

Empirical support: Drehmann and Juselius (2014, BIS Working Paper 455) show that the debt service ratio is the single best early warning indicator of financial crises, outperforming credit-to-GDP gaps. This paper, while not framed in Post-Keynesian terms, is directly consistent with the Minsky framework.

### Claim 5: Two Types of Low-Vol Environments

This is the most operationally important claim. The Kalecki profits equation provides the theoretical basis:

**Aggregate Profits = Investment + Government Deficit - Worker Saving + Capitalist Consumption + Net Exports**

(Simplified version of Kalecki 1954/Levy-Kalecki identity)

**Type A: "Kalecki profit-supported" low vol**
- Government deficits large enough to sustain private sector surplus
- Corporate profits supported by fiscal flows, not leverage
- Investment moderate, funded from retained earnings
- Private debt-to-GDP stable or declining
- Exemplar: 2010-2013 (U.S.), 2013-2017 (U.S.)
- Vol characteristics: Low VIX (12-16), low MOVE (80-100), stable vol term structure, vol selling is profitable and *appropriately* profitable — the low vol is informationally correct

**Type B: "Minsky credit-supported" low vol**
- Fiscal policy neutral or tightening
- Corporate profits increasingly dependent on share buybacks (funded by debt) and financial engineering
- Investment driven by credit expansion
- Private debt-to-GDP rising, credit impulse positive
- Exemplar: 2005-2007 (U.S.), 2018-2019 (U.S.)
- Vol characteristics: Low VIX (12-16), low MOVE (80-100), stable vol term structure — *superficially identical to Type A* — but vol selling is a *Ponzi strategy* because the underlying stability is fragile

CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Both types produce identical vol surface shapes, identical realized vol, and similar implied vol levels. Standard vol models cannot distinguish them because they lack a credit/money dimension. The *only* way to distinguish them is through sectoral balance and flow-of-funds analysis.

DIAGNOSTIC TOOL: The discriminating variable is (Government Deficit / GDP) - (Change in Private Debt / GDP). When this is positive, government flows exceed private credit expansion — Type A. When negative, private credit expansion is filling a "fiscal gap" — Type B.

### Claim 6: Credit Impulse as Vol Regime Transition Indicator

The credit impulse is the change in the flow of credit (second derivative of credit stock). A positive credit impulse means credit growth is accelerating; a negative impulse means it is decelerating.

THEORETICAL MECHANISM: When the credit impulse turns negative after a period of private sector deficit spending, it means the *rate of new borrowing is declining*. In a Minsky-fragile environment, this is the proximate trigger for the vol regime transition because:
1. Asset prices were supported by the *flow* of new credit, not the *stock*
2. Slowing credit growth reduces demand, reduces incomes, increases default rates
3. This triggers the Minsky feedback loop (asset price decline → increased measured risk → deleveraging → further decline)

EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE (partial, requiring formal testing):

The credit impulse turned negative prior to:
- 2000 dot-com bust: turned negative Q4 1999, VIX rose from ~20 to ~35 within 12 months
- 2007-2008 GFC: turned negative Q3 2007, VIX rose from ~12 to ~80 within 15 months
- 2019-2020: turned negative Q3 2019 (pre-COVID), VIX was already rising before the pandemic shock

FALSE POSITIVES: Credit impulse turned negative in 2014 and 2016 without producing sustained high-vol regimes. In both cases, the private sector was in *surplus* (Type A low vol), so the credit impulse decline did not trigger Minsky dynamics. This confirms the need to condition on the sectoral balance configuration — the credit impulse is only a vol regime indicator when the private sector is in deficit or near-deficit.

ESTIMATED CONDITIONAL PROBABILITIES:
- P(vol regime transition | negative credit impulse AND private sector deficit) ≈ 25-35%
- P(vol regime transition | negative credit impulse AND private sector surplus) ≈ 10-15% (near base rate)
- Base rate P(vol regime transition in any 12-month window) ≈ 12-18%

These estimates are rough and based on a small number of G7 episodes (n ≈ 8-12 qualifying events). Formal testing is needed.

### Claim 7: Vol-Selling Complex as Ponzi Finance

Minsky classifies financial positions by their cash flow characteristics:
- **Hedge**: Income covers both interest and principal payments
- **Speculative**: Income covers interest but principal must be rolled over
- **Ponzi**: Income does not cover even interest; the position depends on capital gains or continued access to financing

Systematic vol-selling strategies are Ponzi units in the following precise sense:
- Their income (option premium collected) covers their *expected* losses in the low-vol regime
- They cannot cover losses during vol regime transitions — the payout is highly convex against them
- Their *continued viability* depends on the persistence of the low-vol regime, which their own activity helps sustain
- When the regime transitions, they face margin calls or redemptions that force liquidation, amplifying the vol spike

The AUM in systematic short-vol strategies (broadly defined to include risk parity's vol-targeting component, CTA vol overlay, insurance company annuity hedging, and explicit short-vol funds) has grown from approximately $50-100bn in 2010 to an estimated $500bn-$1.5tn by 2025, depending on definitional scope (this estimate draws on BIS and CFTC flow data, not a single authoritative source).

The "Volmageddon" event of February 2018 was a small-scale Minsky moment in vol space — a minor equity selloff triggered reflexive vol amplification that destroyed XIV and similar products. But the broader vol-selling complex survived because the underlying credit cycle was still in the speculative (not Ponzi) phase. The next vol regime transition, occurring from a late-Minsky configuration, would likely be larger and more persistent.

### Claim 8: Sovereign Debt Irrelevance for Currency-Issuer Rates Vol

This is an application of Modern Monetary Theory's (MMT) core insight, grounded in Lerner's functional finance and Godley's monetary stock-flow consistent models.

A government that issues its own floating-rate fiat currency:
- Cannot involuntarily default on domestic-currency obligations
- Sets the short-term interest rate as a policy variable
- Issues bonds as a monetary policy operation (reserve drain), not as financing

Therefore, government debt-to-GDP ratios have *no theoretical mechanism* for increasing rates vol for currency issuers. The empirical evidence is overwhelming:

- Japan: Government debt/GDP rose from ~70% (1995) to ~260% (2025). JGB vol (10y) *declined* over this period, averaging lower in the 2010s and early 2020s than in the 1990s.
- U.S.: Government debt/GDP rose from ~60% (2007) to ~125% (2025). Treasury vol is higher in 2022-2025, but this is entirely attributable to the *interest rate cycle* (hiking from 0 to 5.5%), not to debt sustainability concerns.
- UK: Government debt/GDP rose from ~40% (2007) to ~100% (2025). Gilt vol spikes (September 2022) were caused by private pension leverage (LDI crisis), not sovereign credit concerns.

By contrast, rates vol for *currency users* (eurozone members, EM with FX pegs, dollarized economies) is highly sensitive to debt ratios because default is possible. This is the key distinction that mainstream vol models conflate.

THE SYSTEMATIC MISPRICING: The market persistently prices some probability of fiscal crisis for currency-issuing sovereigns (observable in CDS spreads on U.S., Japan, UK sovereign debt that are persistently above zero). This "impossible default premium" in rates vol space is extractable by strategies that sell rates vol contingent on sovereign credit events that cannot occur in the absence of a political choice to default.

Conversely, the market *underprices* rates vol from private debt dynamics. The September 2022 UK LDI crisis is the perfect illustration: gilts experienced their worst selloff in decades, but the trigger was *private sector leverage* (pension fund duration mismatch), not sovereign insolvency. A sectoral-balance-aware vol model would have correctly identified the private sector as the source of rates vol risk.

## Confidence Assessment

1. **Vol regimes endogenous to Minsky cycle**: 7/10. The theoretical framework is well-articulated (Minsky 1986, Wray 2016, Keen 2011), and the qualitative mapping to historical vol regimes is persuasive. Marked down because formal quantitative testing of the specific phase-to-vol mapping is lacking — the historical table in Evidence is illustrative, not a regression. The endogeneity thesis is also difficult to falsify because it is partly tautological (any vol spike can be retrospectively attributed to "fragility building").

2. **Rates vol underpricing from sectoral balance unsustainability**: 5/10. The theoretical logic is sound — the sectoral identity binds, and fiscal consolidation mechanically stresses the private sector. But the timing is highly uncertain. The U.S. has maintained a fiscal deficit of 5-7% of GDP for several years without triggering the predicted private sector stress, partly because the large deficit *is* the mechanism sustaining the private surplus. The claim depends on fiscal consolidation actually occurring, which is a political judgment. If fiscal deficits persist, the rates vol "underpricing" may be correct.

3. **VIX-MOVE correlation driven by sectoral balances**: 6/10. The regime taxonomy is logically consistent with the sectoral identity, and the historical examples (2008 correlation breakdown, 2011-2012 eurozone, 2022 UK) are supportive. But the sample of clear regime transitions is very small (n ≈ 4-6), making formal statistical testing weak. The proposed four-regime model is also difficult to test because regime assignment requires real-time sectoral balance data that is published with long lags.

4. **Vol risk premium as Minsky cycle premium**: 5/10. This is a reframing rather than a new empirical claim. The decomposition into credit cycle and policy reaction components is conceptually appealing but would require significant econometric work to implement and test. The Drehmann-Juselius (2014) finding on debt service ratios is supportive but was not designed to explain vol premia specifically. This claim is more of a research agenda than a testable assertion.

5. **Two types of low-vol environments**: 7/10. This is the most practically useful claim. The Kalecki profits identity provides a rigorous theoretical basis, and the proposed diagnostic variable (government deficit gap vs. private credit expansion) is computable from available data. The challenge is that the distinction has only been "tested" across ~3-4 full cycles in the U.S. data, and the Type A vs. Type B classification involves judgment calls at the boundaries.

6. **Credit impulse as vol regime transition indicator**: 5/10. The mechanism is theoretically sound, and the anecdotal evidence (2000, 2007, 2020) is suggestive. But the false positive problem is serious — credit impulse turns negative frequently without vol regime transitions — and the proposed conditioning on private sector balance (to reduce false positives) has not been formally tested. The conditional probability estimates (25-35%) are rough extrapolations from very small samples.

7. **Vol-selling complex as Ponzi finance**: 6/10. The Minsky classification is analytically precise and the Volmageddon precedent is directly supportive. The AUM estimates are imprecise and the definitional scope is uncertain. The claim that the *next* vol event will be larger than 2018 depends on the Minsky cycle position, creating a circular dependency with Claims 1-2. The amplification mechanism (reflexive short-vol liquidation) is well-documented in the microstructure literature.

8. **Sovereign debt irrelevance for rates vol**: 8/10. This is the highest-confidence claim because it rests on the most robust empirical record (Japan 30 years, U.S. and UK multi-decade) and the clearest theoretical mechanism (currency issuer vs. user distinction). The market's persistent mispricing of this relationship is documented in the sovereign CDS literature. Marked down from 9 because political risk (a government *choosing* to default for ideological reasons) is non-zero, and because the mechanism through which private debt creates rates vol (Claim 8's corollary) is less precisely specified.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Rates-Equity Correlation (rates_equity_correlation)**: The stock-bond correlation regime is a direct expression of the sectoral balance dynamics described here. The Post-Keynesian framework predicts that stock-bond correlation is *positive* when the economy is in a Type B (credit-supported) low-vol regime (because both assets are being inflated by the same credit expansion and will deflate together) and *negative* when in a Type A (fiscally-supported) regime (because government bonds act as a genuine safe haven when the private sector is not leveraged). This provides a structural explanation for the observed regime shifts in stock-bond correlation that cross-asset strategists have documented.

**Monetary Policy (monetary_policy)**: Central bank policy is endogenous in this framework — the central bank's rate-setting is a *response* to the Minsky cycle position, not an independent driver. The "rates vol underpricing" thesis (Claim 2) is therefore intimately connected to the monetary policy transmission mechanism: if the Fed is constrained by a Minsky-fragile private sector, its future rate path is more volatile than forward curves suggest, because it will be forced to react more aggressively (in either direction) than a smooth Taylor rule would imply. The policy rate is not a freely chosen variable but a constrained response to financial fragility.

**Risk Appetite Regimes (risk_appetite_regimes)**: "Risk appetite" in the Post-Keynesian framework is not a psychological variable but a balance-sheet-driven variable. Risk appetite is high when private sector balance sheets are healthy (high net worth, low debt service ratios) and low when they are fragile. The Minsky cycle *is* the risk appetite cycle. This means that risk appetite regime shifts should be predictable from the same credit impulse and sectoral balance indicators proposed here for vol regime prediction. The causation runs from balance sheets → risk appetite → vol, not from vol → risk appetite or from some exogenous "sentiment" shock.

**FX-Rates Divergence & Carry Dynamics (prior iterations)**: The carry trade is a Ponzi financing structure in currencies with structurally negative private sector balances. Carry vol and FX vol are therefore connected to the same Minsky dynamics. The "compressed spring" thesis from prior iterations is consistent with the Minsky framework: large rate differentials sustained by divergent Minsky cycle positions across countries are inherently fragile because the high-rate country's private sector is typically more leveraged (requiring higher rates to control credit growth), making the carry return dependent on a fragility that will eventually reverse.

**Fiscal Policy / Fiscal Dominance**: If the primary driver of vol regimes is the interaction between fiscal deficits and private debt dynamics (as argued here), then fiscal policy is not merely "background" for vol analysis but a *first-order determinant*. The fiscal dominance literature — examining whether fiscal sustainability constrains monetary policy — connects directly to the rates vol thesis: in a fiscally-dominant regime, the central bank's rate path is constrained by the government's financing needs, making rates vol a *fiscal* variable masquerading as a monetary variable.

## Open Questions

1. **How do we measure the private sector financial balance in real time?** Flow-of-funds data (Federal Reserve Z.1) is published quarterly with a 2-3 month lag. For a vol regime transition indicator to be tradeable, we need a higher-frequency proxy. Candidates include bank lending surveys, consumer credit data (monthly), and corporate bond issuance patterns — but their mapping to the aggregate sectoral balance is imprecise. Can we construct a monthly "shadow sectoral balance" that is sufficiently accurate for vol regime prediction?

2. **What is the Minsky-stable interest rate, and can it be estimated?** Claim 4 posits a "Minsky-stable rate" — the policy rate consistent with stable private debt-to-income ratios. This is conceptually distinct from the "natural rate" (r*) of mainstream economics because it depends on the *existing stock* of private debt, not on structural parameters. Estimating this rate requires a stock-flow consistent model of the economy with an explicit banking sector. Has anyone done this? Keen (2011) and Godley-Lavoie (2007) provide theoretical models but not real-time estimates suitable for market application.

3. **Does the two-type low-vol framework hold outside the U.S.?** The evidence cited is predominantly U.S.-centric. Do Type A and Type B low-vol regimes exist in Europe (where fiscal rules constrain the "Kalecki profit-supported" pathway), Japan (where the private sector has been in surplus for 30 years), and EM? The eurozone's structural design, which constrains member states from running sufficient fiscal deficits, may mean that *all* eurozone low-vol is Type B — which would explain the eurozone's chronic tendency toward financial crises.

4. **How precisely does the credit impulse predict vol regime transitions versus level shifts?** The credit impulse may predict *when* vol will rise but not *how much*. A sharp credit impulse reversal from a deeply leveraged state (2007) produces a different vol outcome than a mild reversal from moderate leverage (2014). What is the right functional form? Is it the credit impulse interacted with the debt-to-GDP level? The debt service ratio? The Minsky classification of the marginal borrower?

5. **Can the Minsky phase be identified in real time, or is it only observable retrospectively?** The greatest weakness of the Minsky framework for practical vol trading is the classification problem. Calling the current phase "speculative" or "Ponzi" requires knowing the marginal borrower's cash flow characteristics, which are not directly observable in aggregate data. Credit bureau data, bank earnings reports, and structured credit delinquency rates are all proxies, but their mapping to Minsky classifications is not standardized. A systematic, data-driven Minsky phase indicator — using, say, the distribution of interest coverage ratios across listed firms — would be immensely valuable but does not exist in published form.

6. **How does the vol-selling complex's AUM relate to the vol regime transition's severity?** Claim 7 argues that systematic short-vol is a Ponzi structure, but the quantitative relationship between short-vol AUM and the subsequent vol spike magnitude is unexplored. Is there a threshold below which short-vol is merely a Ponzi *position* within a stable system, and above which it becomes *systemically destabilizing*? The BIS has flagged this concern but has not produced estimates.

7. **What role does the international dimension of the sectoral balance play in vol regime transmission?** If the U.S. private sector is sustained by a government deficit that is partly funded by foreign reserve accumulation (the foreign balance), then a shift in foreign reserve policy (e.g., reduced demand for Treasuries from China or OPEC) would simultaneously stress the sectoral balance *and* rates vol. This connects the vol regime question to geopolitical dynamics that are fundamentally outside the domain of financial economics. How should this channel be modeled?
