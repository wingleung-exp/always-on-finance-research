# Global Sovereign Debt Sustainability — EM FX & Capital Flows Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The global sovereign debt expansion is primarily an EM FX event disguised as a fiscal event: the binding constraint for 60-70% of the world's economies is not debt/GDP ratios but the external financing gap — the difference between current account deficits plus external debt amortization versus available foreign capital inflows plus FX reserves.** DM sovereign debt sustainability is measured in fiscal space; EM sovereign debt sustainability is measured in dollars. This distinction is critical because EM sovereign crises historically trigger not when debt/GDP crosses a threshold (the KB correctly debunks this at confidence 8) but when external dollar liquidity dries up — a sudden stop. The current global environment combines record DM sovereign issuance competing for the same savings pool with EM external financing needs, creating a crowding-out dynamic where EM sovereigns lose access to dollar funding precisely when DM issuance absorbs marginal demand. This is the under-analyzed transmission channel from DM fiscal deterioration to EM sovereign stress.

2. **EM sovereign debt composition has shifted dramatically toward local currency issuance since 2010 (from ~45% to ~70% of total EM government debt), which reduces classic FX mismatch risk but creates a new vulnerability: the "local currency trap" where foreign investors in local currency EM bonds face FX risk that DM sovereign stress can trigger, producing the same capital outflows as a traditional external debt crisis but through a different mechanism.** The shift to local currency debt was supposed to eliminate original sin. It did not — it transferred FX risk from the sovereign balance sheet to the foreign investor, who now bears the depreciation risk and will sell when global risk appetite turns. EPFR data shows $45B outflows from EM local currency bond funds when US yields spike 100bp+. The EM sovereign doesn't default in local currency terms but the capital outflow forces the same reserve depletion and monetary tightening as a traditional external debt crisis. The vulnerability is disguised, not eliminated.

3. **The Guidotti-Greenspan rule (FX reserves should cover 100% of short-term external debt) is dangerously insufficient as a reserve adequacy metric in the current environment, because it ignores portfolio flow reversibility: countries meeting Guidotti-Greenspan but holding $50-100B+ in foreign-owned local currency bonds face effective reserve inadequacy when portfolio outflows are included.** The IMF's Assessing Reserve Adequacy (ARA) metric partially addresses this by including a portfolio flow component (10% of broad money), but even ARA understates risk for countries with large foreign participation in local markets. South Africa (foreign ownership ~25% of local govies, reserves ~$60B, foreign LC holdings ~$40-50B) and Indonesia (foreign ownership declining from ~40% to ~15% but reserves of ~$145B against $200B+ in foreign-held LC bonds plus external debt) illustrate the gap. A proper EM sovereign vulnerability assessment must add "portfolio flow reversal coverage" — reserves minus short-term external debt minus a stress-scenario share (30-50%) of foreign-held local currency bonds.

4. **US fiscal deterioration and the resulting term premium expansion create an asymmetric negative shock for EM sovereigns through three channels: (a) direct crowding-out of EM bond demand, (b) dollar strength from higher US yields, and (c) tightening global financial conditions that compress EM risk appetite — and these three channels are positively correlated, producing a multiplicative rather than additive impact.** When US Treasury issuance rises and term premium expands, the marginal dollar of global savings is redirected from EM to DM. Simultaneously, higher US yields strengthen the dollar (despite the KB finding that AI inflows currently drive DXY more than rates), which raises EM external debt service costs in local currency terms. And tighter global financial conditions reduce risk appetite, widening EM spreads. The critical point is that these three channels activate simultaneously and reinforce each other — a 100bp US term premium shock doesn't produce a linear EM response; it produces a convex one. Historical evidence: the 2013 Taper Tantrum saw 10Y UST rise ~130bp, EM spreads widen ~100bp, EM FX depreciate 5-15%, and EM reserve losses of $100B+ — and the US was running a 4% deficit then versus 6.5-7.0% now with the deficit-term premium doom loop in play.

5. **Japan's sovereign debt endgame is the single most consequential scenario for EM FX because Japan is simultaneously the world's largest net creditor nation (~$3.3T net international investment position) and the anchor of the global carry trade — a disorderly Japanese repatriation would be the largest sudden stop in history, but for EM borrowers rather than Japan itself.** Japan's Government Pension Investment Fund (GPIF), life insurers, and banks hold ~$2-3T in foreign assets. A Japanese sovereign stress scenario (BoJ forced to tighten aggressively, JGB market dysfunction, or yen funding squeeze) would trigger repatriation of these assets. The transmission to EM: Japanese investors hold significant positions in EM sovereign debt (particularly MXN, BRL, IDR, ZAR, INR-denominated bonds, which offer carry over JGBs). A Japan-driven carry unwind would produce simultaneous EM currency depreciation and sovereign spread widening across the carry complex. The KB's carry unwind sequence framework (confidence 8.5) identifies this as a plausible trigger — all four prerequisites (rate divergence extreme, vol suppression, positioning overhang, credit cycle intermediate) are currently satisfied.

6. **EM sovereign creditworthiness is bifurcating along a new axis that traditional rating methodologies underweight: commodity-exporter sovereigns with revenue windfalls from energy transition metals (Chile, Indonesia, DRC for copper/nickel/cobalt; Saudi Arabia, UAE for petrochemical diversification) are structurally improving their external positions, while commodity-importing EM sovereigns dependent on services exports face gradual current account erosion from AI automation — creating a 10-year divergence in sovereign credit trajectories that is not yet priced in CDS or FX.** The KB identifies the two-tier EM FX regime (AI-adjacent vs AI-disrupted, confidence 7) and BPO/IT services current account erosion (India $200B, Philippines $33-35B, confidence 5.5). From a sovereign debt perspective, this bifurcation means commodity exporters are building FX reserves and reducing external vulnerability while services exporters face a slow structural deterioration in their balance of payments. The sovereign CDS market does not differentiate — 5Y CDS for Indonesia (~85bp) and Philippines (~65bp) imply similar credit trajectories, but their structural paths are diverging. FX forward curves similarly do not reflect this divergence.

7. **The "original sin redux" — EM sovereign borrowing in dollars at artificially compressed spreads during the 2020-2021 ZIRP era — has created a wall of hard-currency maturities in 2025-2028 totaling ~$300-400B across frontier and lower-rated EM sovereigns, and the refinancing of this debt at current spreads (200-600bp wider) will force a fiscal adjustment that markets have not priced because EM debt amortization schedules are not widely tracked by macro investors.** Between 2020-2021, EM hard currency issuance surged to record levels (~$200B/year) as spread compression made external borrowing artificially cheap. Much of this issuance was 3-5Y maturity, creating a refinancing cliff in 2025-2028. Countries like Ghana (already restructured), Egypt (narrowly avoided default with IMF + Gulf support), Pakistan, Kenya, Ethiopia (restructuring under Common Framework), and Sri Lanka (restructuring ongoing) are the canaries. But the next tier — Nigeria, Tunisia, El Salvador, Bahrain — faces refinancing at spreads 300-500bp wider than issuance, which adds 2-4% GDP to annual debt service. The FX implication: these countries will either draw down reserves (FX negative), seek IMF programs (conditionally neutral), restructure (FX very negative in short term), or impose capital controls (FX negative with contagion risk).

8. **China's sovereign and quasi-sovereign debt ($15T+ including LGFV) is the elephant in the EM sovereign debt room, and while it does not present classic external vulnerability (current account surplus, $3.2T reserves, capital controls), its resolution through financial repression and managed depreciation creates a persistent deflationary export impulse that worsens sovereign debt sustainability for every other EM competitor economy — the China deflation export channel is an underpriced EM sovereign risk factor.** China's local government financing vehicle debt (~$8-9T) is being resolved through swap programs, special bonds, and implicit central government backstops. The cost is paid through financial repression (low deposit rates squeezing household consumption) and weak domestic demand, which forces excess production onto global markets. For competing EM sovereigns — particularly in ASEAN manufacturing (Vietnam, Thailand, Malaysia) and EM commodity processors (Brazil steel, India manufacturing) — this means margin compression, weaker corporate tax revenues, and deteriorating fiscal positions. The RMB's managed weakness (CNY 7.2-7.5 range) further compounds this by forcing competitive depreciation responses from EM peers, which in turn worsens their dollar-denominated debt service.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 (External Financing Gap as Binding Constraint)
- KB: debt_gdp_threshold_debunked (confidence 8) — no robust nonlinear threshold for debt/GDP; the binding constraint must lie elsewhere for EM
- Reinhart-Rogoff-Savastano "debt intolerance" literature: EM crises occur at much lower debt/GDP (40-60%) than DM crises because external financing dependence creates a rollover vulnerability absent for monetary sovereigns
- DM sovereign issuance in 2025-2026: US ~$2T net new issuance, EU ~€500B for defense/NextGenEU, Japan ~¥200T gross — this absorbs global savings pool
- EM gross external financing needs (amortization + current account deficit): ~$1.5-2.0T annually across major EM, competing for the same marginal dollar
- Historical precedent: 1997-1998 Asian crisis triggered not by debt/GDP thresholds but by sudden stop in external financing, with Thailand's short-term external debt exceeding reserves as the proximate trigger
- The crowding-out channel is measurable: when US 10Y rises 100bp, EM hard currency bond fund inflows fall by ~$15-20B over subsequent 3 months (EPFR data, 2013-2024 sample)

### Claim 2 (Local Currency Trap)
- EM local currency bond stock: ~$25T total, up from ~$8T in 2010
- Foreign ownership varies: Brazil ~10%, South Africa ~25%, Indonesia ~15% (down from ~40% in 2019), Mexico ~15%, India ~5% (rising post-index inclusion)
- EPFR EM local currency bond fund flows: -$45B during 2022 US rate hiking cycle, -$25B during 2013 taper tantrum
- The mechanism: foreign investor sells LC bond → converts proceeds to USD → CB must either (a) let currency depreciate, raising imported inflation and forcing rate hikes, or (b) intervene, depleting reserves → both outcomes tighten domestic financial conditions, analogous to traditional external debt crisis
- Key difference from traditional crisis: no formal default event, so the stress is less visible but equally contractionary
- Indonesia case study: foreign ownership of IndoGBs fell from ~40% (2019) to ~15% (2024), representing ~IDR 400T in outflows, which Bank Indonesia offset with ~$35B in reserve drawdown and heavy intervention, raising domestic rates 250bp — achieving the same macroeconomic tightening as a traditional crisis would

### Claim 3 (Reserve Adequacy Gaps)
- Guidotti-Greenspan rule: reserves ≥ 100% of short-term external debt — satisfied by most major EM
- IMF ARA metric: weighted composite of short-term debt (30%), other portfolio liabilities (10-15%), broad money (10%), exports (5-10%) — more comprehensive but still underweights portfolio flow reversal
- South Africa: reserves ~$60B, short-term external debt ~$25B → Guidotti ratio 240% (comfortable). But foreign-held LC bonds ~$40-50B. Under stress scenario (30% portfolio exit), effective reserve coverage falls to $60B / ($25B + $15B) = 150% — still adequate but much tighter
- Turkey (pre-2023 election): reserves ~$60B gross but ~$20B net of swaps, short-term external debt ~$180B → Guidotti ratio <15% — textbook vulnerability that materialized in the lira's 40%+ depreciation
- Enhanced metric proposal: reserves / (short-term external debt + 0.3 × foreign-held LC bonds + 0.1 × M2) — this would reclassify several "adequate" reserve countries as "marginal"
- The 30-50% stress scenario for portfolio outflows is derived from the maximum 12-month outflow observed in the 2013-2022 period across major EM LC bond markets

### Claim 4 (US Fiscal Spillover Asymmetry)
- KB: deficit_term_premium_doom_loop — net interest $350B (FY2020) → ~$1T (FY2025/26), 20-40bp term premium per 1pp deficit/GDP above 3-4% threshold (confidence 8)
- KB: dollar_overvaluation_ai_inflows — DXY 103-107, AI tech flows ~$40-60B/month (confidence 8)
- 2013 Taper Tantrum impact on EM: 10Y UST +130bp; MSCI EM FX index -8%; EM hard currency spreads (EMBI) +100bp; EM reserve losses ~$100B; "Fragile Five" (BRL, INR, IDR, ZAR, TRY) depreciated 10-20%
- The three channels are empirically correlated: correlation between US term premium changes and EM spread changes is 0.55-0.65 (2010-2024); correlation between DXY and EM spreads is 0.45-0.55; these produce a multiplicative effect — a 100bp term premium shock with simultaneous 3-5% dollar appreciation and 50bp EM spread widening produces aggregate EM tightening of ~200-250bp equivalent
- Current vulnerability is higher than 2013: US deficit 6.5-7% vs 4% in 2013; EM external debt stock higher ($4.5T vs $3.5T); basis trade amplification mechanism ($800B-$1T) was absent in 2013
- Non-linearity: EM spread response to UST moves is convex — the second 50bp of UST widening produces ~1.5x the EM spread response of the first 50bp, because the second increment triggers positioning adjustments and risk limit breaches

### Claim 5 (Japan Repatriation Risk)
- Japan net international investment position (NIIP): ~$3.3T (largest globally)
- GPIF foreign asset allocation: ~50% of ~$1.6T AUM = ~$800B in foreign bonds and equities
- Japanese life insurers: ~$500-700B in foreign bonds (hedged and unhedged)
- Japanese bank cross-border lending: ~$4.5T (BIS data), significant share to EM and Asian economies
- KB: carry_unwind_sequence (confidence 8.5) — six-stage pattern, all four prerequisites currently satisfied
- Historical precedent: October 1998 yen carry unwind triggered by LTCM/Russia crisis produced 15% yen appreciation in 2 months, with cascading EM currency depreciation
- BoJ normalization scenario: if BoJ raises rates to 1-1.5% (from current ~0.5%), the carry differential narrows, Japanese investors repatriate, and EM carry currencies (MXN, BRL, IDR, ZAR, INR, HUF, PLN) face simultaneous selling pressure
- The scale concern: $2-3T in Japanese foreign assets is ~10x the size of the 1998 yen carry position; even a 10% repatriation ($200-300B) would overwhelm EM currency markets
- MXN particularly vulnerable: Japanese retail investors ("Mrs. Watanabe") hold significant MXN-denominated uridashi/samurai bonds; MXN carry of ~600bp over JGBs has attracted systematic positioning

### Claim 6 (Commodity vs Services Bifurcation)
- KB: two_tier_em_fx_ai_regime (confidence 7) — AI-adjacent vs AI-disrupted EM
- KB: bpo_it_services_current_account_erosion (confidence 5.5) — India IT $200B, Philippines BPO $33-35B, 60% automatable by 2030-2040
- Copper/nickel demand from energy transition: IEA projects 2-3x demand growth by 2030 for clean energy applications
- Chile copper revenue: ~10% of GDP, ~55% of exports — energy transition is a structural positive for fiscal and external accounts
- Indonesia nickel: export revenue doubled from ~$15B (2020) to ~$30B (2023) on EV battery demand; current account shifted from deficit to surplus
- Contrast with Philippines: BPO ~8% of GDP, ~15% of current account credits; AI automation risk threatens the single largest source of FX inflows
- CDS market: Indonesia 5Y ~85bp, Philippines 5Y ~65bp — Philippines trades *tighter* despite arguably worse structural trajectory, because current snapshot metrics (lower debt/GDP, better fiscal balance today) dominate rating methodologies
- FX forward curve: 12M NDF premium for PHP (~1.5%) vs IDR (~2.5%) reflects carry differential, not structural divergence — this is the mispricing

### Claim 7 (EM Hard Currency Maturity Wall)
- EM hard currency sovereign issuance: ~$200B/year in 2020-2021 (record), ~$150B/year in 2022-2023
- Average maturity of 2020-2021 issuance: 5-7Y for investment grade, 3-5Y for high yield/frontier
- This creates a refinancing concentration in 2025-2028
- Ghana: defaulted 2022, restructuring completed 2024 with ~37% NPV haircut
- Egypt: avoided default through $35B Ras El-Hekma deal + IMF $8B EFF; EGPT 5Y CDS still ~800bp
- Kenya: Eurobond 2024 maturity ($2B) refinanced at 10.375% vs original 6.875% — cost increase of 350bp
- Pakistan: $1B Sukuk and $1.5B Eurobond maturities in 2025-2026; 5Y CDS ~1,200bp
- Ethiopia: restructuring under Common Framework since 2021, still unresolved — the Common Framework's slowness itself is a sovereign risk factor
- Next tier concern: Nigeria ($5B Eurobonds maturing 2025-2029), Tunisia ($1B+ annual needs, CDS ~1,000bp), El Salvador ($800M 2025, $600M 2027 — restructuring risk elevated under dollarization constraint)
- Aggregate: ~$80-120B in frontier/lower-rated EM hard currency maturities over 2025-2028, with average spread widening of 200-500bp from issuance — this adds $2-6B/year in additional debt service costs across the cohort

### Claim 8 (China Deflation Export Channel)
- LGFV total debt: estimates range from $7-9T (IMF, ~45% of GDP) to $12-13T (broader definition including off-balance-sheet)
- China's swap program: ~RMB 2T in LGFV debt swapped to lower-cost provincial bonds in 2024-2025
- PPI deflation: China PPI negative for 20+ consecutive months through early 2025, reflecting excess capacity
- Export price deflation: China export prices fell 5-8% YoY in key manufactured goods categories (steel, solar panels, EVs, chemicals) through 2024
- Impact on EM competitors: Vietnam's manufacturing export margins compressed 200-400bp; Thailand's auto sector facing Chinese EV competition; Brazil's steel sector under pressure from Chinese dumping
- RMB depreciation: CNY/USD moved from 6.3 (2022) to 7.2-7.5 range (2024-2025); each 5% RMB depreciation puts 2-3% depreciation pressure on ASEAN and commodity-exporter EM currencies
- Fiscal transmission: weaker corporate margins → lower corporate tax revenue → wider fiscal deficits → sovereign credit pressure — this channel operates with a 12-18 month lag
- The key insight: China's sovereign debt resolution is externalizing its cost to other EM sovereigns through the trade channel, and this is not captured in any standard sovereign debt sustainability framework

## Connections to Other Topics

### Fiscal Dominance (fiscal_dominance)
The DM fiscal dominance dynamic (KB: fiscal_dominance_early_stage, US at 2.0-2.5 of 5 signals) directly feeds EM sovereign vulnerability through the crowding-out and term premium spillover channels described in Claim 4. If US fiscal dominance advances to stage 3+ (implicit Fed coordination, regulatory forced buying), the dollar's safe-haven status erodes (KB: fiscal_dollar_smirk), which paradoxically could *help* EM by weakening the dollar — but only if the transition is gradual. A disorderly US fiscal crisis would be catastrophic for EM due to global risk appetite collapse. The EM FX implication: the optimal EM FX regime depends critically on *which path* DM fiscal dominance takes — Japanese-style gradual (EM positive: weaker DM currencies, search for yield) vs Truss-style acute (EM catastrophic: risk-off contagion). This bimodality is the most important single variable for EM FX allocation.

### Yield Curve Dynamics (yield_curve_dynamics)
The US term premium expansion (KB: term_premium_fiscal_signal, confidence 7) is the primary transmission mechanism from DM sovereign debt to EM FX. The shape of the US yield curve determines the cost of EM dollar hedging (which is priced off the short end) versus the incentive to hold EM duration (which competes with the US long end). A steepening US curve (short rates falling, long rates rising on term premium) is the worst scenario for EM FX because it simultaneously cheapens short-end hedges (encouraging dollar longs) and raises long-end competition (discouraging EM bond allocation).

### FX Regimes (fx_regimes)
EM sovereign debt sustainability is inseparable from FX regime choice. Countries with managed exchange rates (Egypt, Nigeria, Pakistan pre-crisis) accumulate hidden external vulnerability because the pegged rate encourages unhedged foreign borrowing. When the peg breaks, the currency overshoots, external debt service spikes, and the sovereign enters a debt-depreciation doom loop. The KB's em_crisis_archetype_scoring framework correctly identifies that managed/pegged regimes produce 30-60% discrete devaluation events versus gradual adjustment under floating regimes. For the sovereign debt topic, this means that EM countries with managed rates and high external debt are systematically underpriced in risk markets.

### Fiscal Policy (fiscal_policy)
EM fiscal space is constrained by external vulnerability in a way DM fiscal space is not. An EM sovereign running countercyclical fiscal policy during a global downturn faces the paradox that wider fiscal deficits signal external vulnerability, trigger capital outflows, and force procyclical tightening — the opposite of the intended effect. This "fiscal trap" is the core reason EM sovereign debt has different dynamics than DM. The KB's fiscal policy topic should incorporate this as a fundamental constraint on EM macro stabilization capacity.

### Carry Trade & Positioning (carry_unwind)
The KB's carry unwind sequence framework (confidence 8.5) is directly relevant because EM sovereign debt is the primary vehicle for carry trade expression. Carry positioning inflates EM sovereign bond demand during accumulation phases, compresses spreads below fundamental fair value, and then reverses violently during unwinds. The sovereign debt sustainability assessment must be carry-cycle adjusted: an EM sovereign that looks well-financed during a carry accumulation phase may be fundamentally vulnerable once carry positioning normalizes. Current positioning overhang (90th+ percentile per KB) means that current EM sovereign spreads likely understate true risk.

### Commodity Dynamics (commodity_dynamics)
Terms of trade volatility is the dominant driver of EM sovereign creditworthiness for commodity exporters (which constitute ~60% of EM by market weight). A commodity price crash produces simultaneous fiscal deterioration (lower royalty/tax revenue), current account deterioration (lower export receipts), and currency depreciation (terms of trade shock) — all three reinforcing sovereign stress. The energy transition creates structural winners (copper, nickel, lithium exporters) and losers (oil exporters without diversification), overlaying a secular shift on the cyclical commodity-sovereign relationship.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the true size of the "shadow" EM external debt?** Corporate external borrowing in EM is often intermediated through offshore subsidiaries or structured vehicles that don't appear in sovereign external debt statistics but create contingent sovereign liabilities (because governments backstop systemically important corporates). China's LGFV debt is the largest example, but Turkey's corporate FX debt, India's Adani-linked external borrowing, and Brazil's Petrobras dollar debt all create sovereign-corporate contingency links that are poorly measured. I need better data on total EM external exposure including quasi-sovereign and private-sector-with-sovereign-backstop debt.

2. **How will India's inclusion in global bond indices (JPM GBI-EM, Bloomberg, FTSE) change the EM sovereign debt vulnerability landscape?** India's index inclusion is expected to attract $25-40B in passive inflows over 2024-2026. This transforms India from a ~5% foreign ownership market to potentially 8-12%. Does this make India more vulnerable to the "local currency trap" described in Claim 2, or does India's massive domestic savings base provide a buffer that smaller EM markets lack? The answer has significant implications for the entire EM LC bond asset class because India will become the largest single weight.

3. **Can the Common Framework for sovereign debt restructuring actually work at scale?** Ethiopia, Zambia, Chad, and Ghana have tested the G20 Common Framework with mixed results (Chad resolved quickly, Ethiopia took 3+ years, Zambia took 2+ years). If the framework is too slow to provide liquidity relief, distressed frontier sovereigns may prefer to muddle through with bilateral deals (Egypt/Gulf model) or impose capital controls rather than enter a formal process. This has implications for pricing — if restructuring is uncertain and slow, the recovery value in EM sovereign bonds is more discounted than it should be, which raises new issuance costs for the entire frontier EM asset class.

4. **What is the feedback loop between EM sovereign stress and DM bank balance sheets?** European banks (particularly French, Spanish, and UK banks) have significant EM sovereign and corporate exposures. A wave of EM sovereign stress (multiple restructurings, reserve depletion, capital controls) could impair DM bank capital, tighten DM lending conditions, and create reverse contagion from EM to DM. This feedback loop operated during the 1997-1998 Asian crisis (via LTCM and bank exposures) and during the 2001 Argentine crisis (via Spanish bank exposures). The current magnitude of this channel is unclear.

5. **How do geopolitical alignment shifts (friend-shoring, de-risking) alter traditional EM sovereign vulnerability patterns?** Countries aligned with US/Western interests may receive implicit financial support (IMF programs, bilateral swaps, World Bank lending) that insulates them from market discipline, while countries aligned with China/Russia may face sanction risk that amplifies market discipline. This geopolitical overlay may mean that traditional fundamental analysis (debt ratios, reserves, current account) overstates risk for aligned countries and understates risk for non-aligned ones. Vietnam (geopolitically favored), India (favored), and Turkey (ambiguous) are test cases.

6. **At what point does EM central bank gold accumulation (1,037-1,045t annually per KB) become large enough to meaningfully alter FX reserve adequacy calculations?** If EM central banks are systematically shifting reserves from UST to gold, this changes the liquidity profile of their reserve buffers — gold is less liquid than UST in a crisis, but more robust against a US fiscal/sanctions risk scenario. The reserve adequacy framework needs to differentiate between liquid reserves (UST, other DM govies) and strategic reserves (gold, bilateral swap lines) when assessing sudden stop vulnerability.
