# Global Sovereign Debt Sustainability — Central Bank Policy Transmission Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The primary risk from elevated sovereign debt is not default but the progressive impairment of the monetary policy transmission mechanism itself — specifically, the interest rate channel becomes self-referential when rate hikes designed to tighten financial conditions simultaneously worsen sovereign debt dynamics, creating a fiscal feedback loop that narrows the effective policy space.** At current US debt levels (~124% debt/GDP, ~$1T annual interest expense), each 100bp of Fed tightening adds roughly $250-320B in annual debt service over a 6-year average maturity rollover horizon, partially offsetting the intended contractionary effect through a fiscal impulse channel that standard Taylor Rule frameworks ignore.

2. **Financial conditions indices (FCI) have become structurally harder for central banks to control in high-debt environments because the asset price channel and the sovereign risk channel now operate in opposition.** When central banks tighten, higher rates should tighten FCIs — but if tightening triggers sovereign risk repricing, equity rallies on "fiscal put" expectations, or currency appreciation stalls because foreigners question sovereign sustainability, the FCI may not respond as intended. The 2022-2024 Fed tightening cycle exhibited this: despite 525bp of hikes, Goldman Sachs FCI spent extended periods looser than pre-hike levels, driven partly by equity market resilience rooted in fiscal-deficit-supported corporate profits (the Kalecki channel).

3. **The central bank reaction function is observably different across major DM central banks as a function of sovereign debt exposure, creating a hierarchy of monetary policy credibility: the Fed retains the most operational independence (but faces growing fiscal constraints), the ECB operates under fragmentation risk that forces it to develop backstop tools (TPI/OMT) that are fundamentally fiscal-monetary hybrids, the BoJ has effectively subordinated monetary policy to debt management for a decade, and the BoE's 2022 episode revealed that even a credible central bank can be forced into emergency operations that contradict its stated policy stance within days.**

4. **The expectations channel of monetary policy — forward guidance — is being systematically undermined by sovereign debt dynamics across the G7.** Central bank forward guidance operates on the assumption that the central bank's reaction function is primarily determined by inflation and output. When fiscal sustainability becomes a binding constraint, forward guidance becomes state-contingent on a variable (fiscal trajectory) that central banks do not control, reducing the informational content of guidance and forcing markets to model a central bank that cannot credibly commit to its stated path. This manifests as elevated rate vol (MOVE index sustained above 100) even in periods of ostensible policy stability.

5. **The credit channel transmission is being rewired by sovereign debt in ways that reduce its effectiveness for real-economy stimulus while increasing its role as a sovereign financing mechanism.** Basel III/IV liquidity regulations (LCR, NSFR) effectively mandate bank holdings of sovereign bonds, creating a "regulatory crowding-in" that channels bank balance sheets toward sovereign funding and away from private credit creation. This resembles the financial repression of the 1940s-50s, but operates through regulatory architecture rather than explicit interest rate caps. For the US, bank holdings of Treasuries/Agencies (~$4T) represent a de facto captive buyer base that suppresses term premia but reduces the responsiveness of bank lending to policy rate changes.

6. **The most dangerous near-term transmission risk is not a sovereign debt crisis but a "transmission fracture" — a scenario where central banks lose the ability to simultaneously manage inflation, financial stability, and sovereign funding costs.** This three-body problem is already partially active: the Fed's 2023 BTFP facility was functionally a sovereign-debt-linked bailout of bank unrealized losses on Treasuries; the BoE's 2022 gilt intervention contradicted its QT and tightening stance; the ECB's TPI was created explicitly to prevent sovereign spreads from defeating its tightening cycle. Each intervention solved one problem while creating moral hazard for the other two.

7. **Rate markets are systematically mispricing the asymmetry in the Fed's reaction function that sovereign debt creates: the Fed can tighten less than warranted by inflation (fiscal sensitivity limits the ceiling) but faces limited constraint on easing when needed (fiscal dynamics welcome lower rates).** This produces a structural asymmetry in the distribution of policy rate outcomes — the right tail (aggressive tightening) is truncated by fiscal arithmetic while the left tail (aggressive easing) is unconstrained. Front-end rate vol should price higher for puts than calls, and the terminal rate in any tightening cycle is endogenously lower than conventional Taylor Rule estimates would suggest.

8. **Global monetary policy divergence is being compressed by shared sovereign debt vulnerability, reducing the degrees of freedom available for independent monetary policy and creating a coordination problem with no institutional mechanism for resolution.** In the 1990s-2000s, major central banks could diverge significantly (Fed at 6.5% while BoJ at 0%). Today, the fiscal constraints on tightening create an implicit ceiling that makes extreme divergence destabilizing — a wide rate differential between the US and Japan, for instance, creates carry-trade-amplified capital flows that stress the deficit country's funding model. This means each central bank's reaction function now partially incorporates the other's fiscal constraint, even without explicit coordination.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 — Self-Referential Interest Rate Channel

The standard textbook model of monetary policy transmission assumes the interest rate channel operates independently of fiscal dynamics. The central bank raises the policy rate, this passes through to deposit rates, lending rates, and bond yields, tightening financial conditions and slowing aggregate demand.

This model breaks down when government debt is large enough that interest rate changes materially affect the fiscal position. The arithmetic:

- US federal debt held by the public: ~$28T (early 2026)
- Average maturity: ~6.2 years, meaning ~16% of the stock reprices annually (~$4.5T)
- Each 100bp increase in new issuance rates adds ~$45B in the first year, ~$90B in year two, reaching ~$250-320B in additional annual interest cost by the time the full stock has turned over
- At 6-7% of GDP deficits, this additional interest cost is not self-financing — it requires additional borrowing, creating a compound interest dynamic

This produces a **fiscal multiplier offset to monetary tightening**. When the government borrows $250-320B more to pay interest, that money enters the economy as income to bondholders — a transfer payment that supports aggregate demand, partially offsetting the contractionary effect of higher rates. The net tightening is less than the gross rate increase implies.

**Quantitative estimate:** Using a fiscal multiplier of 0.5-0.7 for transfer-like payments (conservative, as bondholder marginal propensity to consume is lower than average), 100bp of rate hikes produces an offsetting fiscal impulse of ~$125-225B (0.4-0.7% of GDP). This is not trivial — it represents 25-50% of the GDP drag that conventional estimates attribute to 100bp of tightening (~1.0-1.5% of GDP over 2 years).

**Empirical support:** The 2022-2024 tightening cycle produced a much shallower recession signal than historical 500bp+ tightening campaigns. While multiple factors contributed (post-Covid labor market strength, excess savings), the fiscal impulse from deficit spending (including rising interest costs) is a quantitatively significant explanation that most monetary policy models do not capture.

**Cross-country comparison:** Japan provides the extreme case — with debt/GDP at ~250% and BoJ owning >50% of JGBs, the interest rate channel is almost entirely self-referential. BoJ rate hikes would simultaneously raise government interest costs (fiscal easing) and reduce the value of the BoJ's own JGB holdings (balance sheet impairment). This circularity is a key reason BoJ normalization has been so cautious (~0.5% policy rate as of early 2026 vs. ~5.5% for the Fed at peak).

### Claim 2 — FCI Divergence from Policy Rate

The Goldman Sachs Financial Conditions Index (GSFCI) and the Chicago Fed's National Financial Conditions Index (NFCI) are the two most-watched summary statistics of how tight or easy actual monetary conditions are. The key insight is that the FCI integrates the effects of the policy rate with equities, credit spreads, the dollar, and real rates — and these components can move in ways that offset the policy rate.

**2022-2024 episode:** Despite 525bp of cumulative tightening (March 2022 to July 2023), the GSFCI spent significant periods in 2023-24 at levels consistent with neutral or even slightly easy financial conditions. Contributing factors:

- **S&P 500 rally:** From the October 2022 lows (~3,500) to early 2026 levels (~5,500+), the equity rally offset much of the FCI tightening from higher rates
- **Credit spreads:** IG and HY spreads compressed below historical averages despite the rate hiking cycle, reflecting strong corporate balance sheets supported by fiscal-deficit-sustained revenue growth
- **Dollar dynamics:** The dollar appreciated initially (tightening FCIs) but consolidated in 2024-25, reducing the FCI tightening contribution from FX

**The Kalecki connection:** The fiscal deficit channel explains why equities and credit remained resilient despite aggressive tightening. Under the Kalecki-Levy profit identity, government deficits flow directly to corporate profits (by accounting identity). A 6-7% GDP deficit sustains corporate revenue and margins, supporting equity prices and credit quality, which keeps the FCI looser than the policy rate alone would suggest.

**Implication for central banks:** This means that in high-debt environments, central banks may need to tighten MORE to achieve the same real-economy impact — but tightening more worsens the fiscal dynamics that are supporting the equity rally, creating an unstable feedback loop. The reaction function must now account for fiscal policy's effect on the FCI, not just the direct pass-through from the policy rate.

### Claim 3 — Central Bank Credibility Hierarchy

**Fed (High but eroding credibility):**
- Operational independence legally protected but subject to political pressure
- Balance sheet: ~$7T (down from ~$9T peak), QT ongoing at ~$60B/month
- Key constraint: Fed holds ~$4.5T in Treasuries — unrealized losses of ~$500B at current rates. This does not impair operations (central bank accounting differs from commercial banks) but creates political vulnerability
- Reaction function: Still primarily inflation/employment focused, but the March 2023 BTFP episode showed willingness to provide emergency liquidity when sovereign-debt-related bank stress emerged
- Assessment: Policy rate decisions remain independent, but the pace of QT and the management of the balance sheet are increasingly influenced by Treasury funding needs (e.g., the 2023 shift toward T-bill issuance was partially coordinated)

**ECB (Fragmentation-constrained credibility):**
- Must maintain rate policy for the entire eurozone while managing sovereign spread divergence among 20 members
- TPI (Transmission Protection Instrument, 2022) is explicitly a tool to prevent sovereign spreads from impairing monetary policy transmission — this is a de facto admission that sovereign debt dynamics can break the transmission mechanism
- OMT (Outright Monetary Transactions, 2012) was never activated but its existence prevents extreme spread widening — a permanent fiscal backstop embedded in monetary architecture
- Italy at ~140% debt/GDP is the binding constraint: ECB cannot tighten aggressively without risking Italian spread widening that could become self-fulfilling
- Assessment: Monetary policy is operationally subordinated to fragmentation management. Every rate decision implicitly incorporates Italian/peripheral spread stability

**BoJ (Fully subordinated to debt management):**
- YCC (Yield Curve Control, 2016-2024) was functionally a sovereign debt management tool: capping 10Y JGB yields at 0-1% while debt/GDP exceeded 250%
- BoJ owns >50% of JGBs — the market for Japanese sovereign debt is effectively an administered market, not a price-discovery market
- Policy rate normalization limited to ~0.5% (early 2026) despite rising inflation, precisely because higher rates would destabilize the sovereign debt arithmetic
- Assessment: BoJ is the clearest current example of fiscal dominance in a major economy. Monetary policy has been an instrument of debt management for over a decade

**BoE (Credibility validated but stress-tested):**
- September 2022 gilt crisis forced the BoE to intervene with emergency purchases while simultaneously maintaining its tightening bias
- The contradiction was resolved only because the fiscal trigger (Truss mini-budget) was reversed politically — the BoE could not have sustained both operations indefinitely
- Assessment: The BoE demonstrated that central bank independence can survive a sovereign debt shock, but only if the fiscal authorities capitulate quickly. The counterfactual (Truss stays, persists with unfunded fiscal expansion) would have produced a genuine credibility crisis

### Claim 4 — Forward Guidance Degradation

Forward guidance works through the expectations channel: by communicating the likely path of the policy rate, the central bank can influence long-term rates, asset prices, and economic decisions without changing the current policy rate.

**The sovereign debt problem for forward guidance:**

Forward guidance is credible only if markets believe the central bank will follow through. When sovereign debt dynamics constrain the reaction function, three degradation mechanisms operate:

1. **Path uncertainty increases:** If the central bank cannot raise rates as high as inflation warrants (because of fiscal sustainability concerns), the ceiling of the rate path becomes uncertain. Markets must model not just the inflation/output reaction function but also the fiscal constraint — an additional state variable that the central bank does not control.

2. **Time inconsistency intensifies:** A central bank may guide for higher rates but face pressure to cut when debt service costs mount. This is the classic Kydland-Prescott time inconsistency problem, amplified by fiscal dynamics. Markets rationally discount forward guidance that they expect to be revised for fiscal reasons.

3. **Communication becomes more ambiguous:** Central bankers cannot publicly acknowledge fiscal constraints on policy without undermining their independence narrative. This forces deliberately vague communication that reduces the informational content of forward guidance.

**Observable evidence:**
- MOVE index (Treasury rate volatility) has remained structurally elevated above 100 since 2022, compared to the pre-2022 average of ~60-80. This persistence of rate vol in a period of stable policy rate (no changes since mid-2024) suggests markets are pricing elevated uncertainty about the future path — consistent with forward guidance degradation.
- OIS-derived terminal rate expectations have become more dispersed: the width of the 80% confidence interval for the terminal rate has roughly doubled since 2019.
- Fed dot plots have consistently missed actuals by wider margins since 2021 (median dot error of ~150bp in the December 2021 dots for end-2023, compared to ~50bp average error pre-2020). While this partly reflects forecast difficulty in the post-Covid environment, it also reflects a reaction function that is harder to predict because it has more binding constraints.

### Claim 5 — Credit Channel Rewiring

The credit channel transmits monetary policy through bank lending: lower rates reduce funding costs, expand bank margins on new lending, and ease credit standards. Higher rates do the reverse.

**How sovereign debt rewires this channel:**

**Regulatory crowding-in:** Basel III liquidity requirements (LCR — Liquidity Coverage Ratio; NSFR — Net Stable Funding Ratio) assign 0% risk weight to domestic sovereign bonds and require banks to hold large liquid asset buffers. For US banks, this has produced ~$4T in combined Treasury/Agency holdings. Each dollar of bank balance sheet allocated to sovereign bonds is a dollar not available for private credit creation.

This is economically equivalent to the financial repression of the 1940s-50s, when Regulation Q and direct investment mandates channeled bank and insurance company assets into government bonds. The difference is that the modern version operates through "voluntarily" adopted regulatory architecture rather than explicit mandates — making it politically sustainable but economically identical in its effects on private credit availability.

**Quantitative evidence:**
- US bank lending growth has trailed nominal GDP growth since 2022 despite strong economic activity. C&I loan growth was ~2% in 2024 vs. ~5-6% nominal GDP growth — a gap that conventional monetary policy models (which assume credit growth roughly tracks nominal activity) cannot fully explain.
- The bank lending spread (prime rate minus deposit rate) has compressed, reducing the incentive for banks to lend at the margin. Banks can earn 4-5% on Treasuries with zero capital charge vs. ~7-8% on C&I loans with meaningful capital charge — the risk-adjusted return differential has narrowed.
- European banks exhibit this more acutely: sovereign bond holdings as a share of total assets are ~15-20% for Italian and Spanish banks (vs. ~10% for German banks), reflecting the sovereign-bank nexus that the ECB's backstop architecture was designed to manage.

**Transmission impairment assessment:** The credit channel is partially impaired in the current environment. Rate cuts do not fully transmit to private lending because banks prefer the risk-free return on Treasuries, especially when the yield is historically elevated. Rate hikes do not fully restrict credit because fiscal-deficit-supported demand keeps credit quality strong. The result is a credit channel that is dampened in both directions — the policy rate has less traction on real lending activity than historical relationships would suggest.

### Claim 6 — The "Transmission Fracture" Three-Body Problem

Central banks have traditionally managed two objectives (dual mandate: inflation and employment for the Fed; price stability for the ECB) using one instrument (the policy rate). Sovereign debt sustainability introduces a third objective — maintaining orderly government funding — that can conflict with the first two.

**The three-body problem:**

| Scenario | Inflation Mandate | Financial Stability | Sovereign Funding | Central Bank Response | Contradiction |
|----------|------------------|--------------------|--------------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Inflation surge + sovereign stress | Raise rates | May need liquidity support | Higher rates worsen funding | Cannot simultaneously tighten AND provide liquidity backstop | **BoE Sep 2022** |
| Bank losses on sovereign bonds | Not directly relevant | Requires liquidity support | Support = implicit sovereign backstop | Backstop enables fiscal laxity (moral hazard) | **Fed BTFP Mar 2023** |
| Peripheral spread widening | May require tightening | Fragmentation threatens financial stability | Rate hikes widen spreads | Cannot tighten without fragmenting | **ECB TPI 2022** |
| Recession + high debt | Should ease | Not acutely stressed | Would benefit from easing | No contradiction — but debt constrains future tightening capacity | **Post-Covid all DM** |

Each of the three actual episodes (BoE Sep 2022, Fed BTFP, ECB TPI) was resolved through ad hoc interventions — emergency facilities designed to address one corner of the three-body problem without formally abandoning the other two objectives. But each resolution created precedent and moral hazard:

- **BoE precedent:** Sovereign bond holders now expect central bank intervention if fiscal shocks cause rapid repricing. This reduces the market discipline on sovereign fiscal policy.
- **BTFP precedent:** Banks now expect Fed backstop for unrealized losses on sovereign bond holdings. This reduces the incentive for banks to manage duration risk on sovereign portfolios.
- **TPI precedent:** Euro-area sovereigns now expect ECB spread management. This reduces the incentive for fiscal consolidation in high-debt members.

### Claim 7 — Asymmetric Policy Rate Distribution

**The fiscal arithmetic ceiling on tightening:**

Consider the Fed's reaction function. A standard Taylor Rule would prescribe:

r* = r_neutral + 0.5*(π - π*) + 0.5*(y - y*)

With neutral rate ~2.5%, inflation above target, and positive output gap, the Taylor Rule might prescribe a policy rate of 5-6%. But the fiscal arithmetic creates an implicit ceiling:

- At 5.5% Fed funds, new Treasury issuance rates are ~4.5-5.5% (weighted average across maturities)
- At ~$28T debt, ~16% annual rollover, the blended rate converges toward the current issuance rate at ~4.5% over 6 years
- When blended rate approaches nominal GDP growth (~5.0-5.5%), the debt/GDP ratio becomes dynamically unstable absent primary surpluses
- The US runs a 3-4% primary deficit — there are no primary surpluses in any baseline projection

This arithmetic creates a **fiscal ceiling** on the policy rate: the Fed cannot sustain a policy rate much above 5-5.5% for extended periods without triggering the doom-loop dynamics identified in the KB (deficit → issuance → term premium → more deficit). The ceiling is not a hard constraint but a zone of increasing discomfort.

**No equivalent floor on easing:**

In contrast, when the Fed needs to ease (recession, financial stress), lower rates improve sovereign debt dynamics — lower debt service, easier funding, reduced term premium. There is no fiscal constraint on easing. This produces a structurally asymmetric reaction function.

**Market implications:**
- The terminal rate in tightening cycles is endogenously capped at ~5-5.5%, below what unconstrained Taylor Rule estimates might prescribe
- The duration of tightening cycles is limited — the Fed cannot sustain high rates for multiple years without fiscal dynamics forcing a pivot
- Front-end rate options should price this asymmetry: OTM puts on SOFR (pricing aggressive easing) should trade rich to OTM calls (pricing aggressive tightening), more than historical patterns would suggest
- The "higher for longer" narrative (2023-2024) has a fiscal expiration date — the Fed's willingness to hold rates at 5.25-5.5% is time-limited by the pace at which the debt stock reprices to current rates

**Observable evidence:** The Fed's shift from "higher for longer" (late 2023) to cutting (September 2024) occurred faster than pure inflation data would have warranted. While labor market softening was the stated reason, the fiscal arithmetic — with net interest exceeding $1T annualized — was plausibly a contributing factor that cannot be acknowledged publicly.

### Claim 8 — Compressed Global Monetary Divergence

In a world of low sovereign debt, central banks can pursue independent monetary policies bounded mainly by the exchange rate (Mundell-Fleming trilemma). The US can run 6.5% rates while Japan runs 0% — the exchange rate adjusts, but both policy stances are sustainable.

**High sovereign debt compresses this:**

- **The tightening ceiling compresses:** If the Fed's fiscal ceiling is ~5-5.5% and the ECB's is ~4-4.5% (lower because the ECB must manage peripheral spreads), the maximum divergence is ~100-150bp, not the ~300-650bp divergence observed in the 2000s.
- **The easing floor converges:** When multiple sovereigns need lower rates for fiscal sustainability, they ease toward similar levels — zero or slightly positive — compressing divergence from below.
- **Capital flow amplification:** Wide rate differentials in a high-debt world trigger carry trades that disproportionately affect sovereign funding. Japan's ~$1T in US Treasury holdings means that yen strengthening (from BoJ tightening or dollar weakening) creates unrealized losses that amplify balance-of-payments stress. The carry trade is simultaneously a monetary policy arbitrage AND a sovereign funding flow.

**The coordination problem:**

No institutional mechanism exists for coordinating monetary policy across sovereign-debt-constrained central banks. The G7/G20 communiqués avoid explicit monetary coordination. The BIS provides a forum but not a decision-making framework. The result is that each central bank privately factors in the others' constraints without explicitly acknowledging them — producing a kind of implicit coordination that is fragile because it cannot be formally committed to.

**Historical comparison:** The 1985 Plaza Accord and 1987 Louvre Accord coordinated FX intervention across G5 central banks to manage exchange rate imbalances. The current sovereign debt coordination problem is more complex because it requires sustained monetary policy convergence, not one-time FX intervention. The closest historical precedent is the Bretton Woods system's implicit coordination through fixed exchange rates — but that system required capital controls that no longer exist.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Fiscal Dominance (iter_0016):** This analysis provides the transmission mechanism micro-foundations for the fiscal dominance regime described in iter_0016. The five-signal sequence tracks regime progression; this analysis explains *why* each signal emerges (e.g., forward guidance degradation → elevated rate vol → wider term premium → fiscal dominance signal). The "2.0-2.5 on the five-signal scale" translates to "partial but increasing transmission impairment" in this framework.

- **Yield Curve Dynamics / Term Premium:** Term premium is the market's pricing of transmission mechanism uncertainty. When the credit channel is rewired and the expectations channel is degraded, investors demand more compensation for holding duration — not just for inflation risk but for *reaction function uncertainty*. The ACM term premium move from -50bp to +50-100bp partially reflects this pricing of monetary policy credibility loss. The term premium decomposition framework should be extended to include a "fiscal credibility" component.

- **Currency Regimes & FX (iter_0012):** The exchange rate channel of monetary policy is distorted by sovereign debt dynamics. In the standard model, rate hikes → currency appreciation → FCI tightening via import prices. But if rate hikes are perceived as fiscally destabilizing, the currency may not appreciate (or may depreciate), breaking the FX transmission channel. This connects to the "fiscal dollar smirk" concept — the dollar may not follow the normal rate-differential model when fiscal sustainability is questioned.

- **Central Bank Balance Sheet Normalization (iter_0013):** QT is itself a sovereign debt operation — it returns Treasuries to the private market, requiring price-sensitive buyers to absorb supply. QT pace is constrained by the same fiscal dynamics described here: too-fast QT creates Treasury market stress (October 2023 term premium spike, September 2019 repo crisis). The three-body problem (Claim 6) means QT decisions are now partially subordinated to sovereign funding considerations.

- **AI & Technology Disruption (iter_0014-15):** AI-driven productivity growth is the single most important potential release valve for the transmission impairment described here. If AI adds 1-2pp to trend growth, it raises the fiscal ceiling on rates (higher growth makes any debt/GDP ratio more sustainable), restores the denominator effect, and potentially allows central banks to maintain higher rates without triggering doom-loop dynamics. This is the "escape velocity" scenario for transmission mechanism restoration.

- **Inflation Regimes:** The transmission fracture is most dangerous when inflation is elevated simultaneously with sovereign debt stress. The central bank cannot fight inflation without worsening fiscal dynamics — this is the precise definition of fiscal dominance. The modal outcome of the transmission fracture is not hyperinflation or crisis but a prolonged period of above-target inflation (3-4%) that central banks tolerate because the alternative (aggressive tightening) is fiscally destabilizing. This connects to the financial repression thesis.

- **Volatility Regimes:** Transmission mechanism impairment manifests as structurally higher rate volatility (MOVE index). This is not temporary event-driven vol but a regime shift in the volatility of the policy rate path itself. Cross-asset vol is affected because rate vol is a key input to equity and credit vol models — if rate vol is structurally higher, the entire vol surface across asset classes should reprice.

## Open Questions

1. **At what debt/GDP level does the fiscal ceiling on the policy rate become operationally binding for the Fed?** The arithmetic suggests the constraint is already partially binding (~124% debt/GDP), but there is no precise threshold — it operates as a gradient, not a cliff. Historical comparison is limited because the US has only operated at these debt levels during and immediately after WWII (when the constraint was binding and resulted in explicit yield curve caps). Can the fiscal ceiling be estimated more precisely using the r-g framework and rollover dynamics?

2. **How should central banks communicate about fiscal constraints on monetary policy without undermining independence?** Current institutional norms require central bankers to deny fiscal influence on rate decisions. This creates a systematic communication bias that degrades forward guidance. Is there a communication framework that allows honest acknowledgment of fiscal constraints without triggering the self-fulfilling dynamics that such acknowledgment might create? The ECB's approach (creating explicit tools like TPI) is more transparent than the Fed's approach (ad hoc facilities like BTFP), but both avoid explicit acknowledgment.

3. **Is the basis trade (~$1T notional) a symptom or a cause of transmission impairment?** The basis trade exists partly because of the sovereign debt supply/demand imbalance — there are not enough natural buyers at current rates, so leveraged relative-value traders fill the gap. If the transmission mechanism were working normally (central bank signals → predictable rate path → low vol → compressed basis), the trade would be less profitable and smaller. The trade's existence may itself impair transmission by creating a fragility that constrains both Fed and Treasury behavior.

4. **Can the credit channel be de-linked from sovereign funding through regulatory reform?** If Basel III/IV's 0% risk weight for sovereign bonds were modified (e.g., risk-weighted based on debt/GDP or fiscal trajectory), banks would hold fewer sovereigns and more private credit, restoring some credit channel effectiveness. But this would simultaneously remove the "captive buyer" suppression of term premia, potentially destabilizing sovereign funding. Is there a regulatory design that threads this needle?

5. **What is the interaction between digital currencies (CBDC) and the sovereign debt transmission mechanism?** If CBDC deposits are held directly at the central bank rather than through commercial banks, the credit channel is further impaired (disintermediation), but the central bank gains more direct control over financial conditions (through CBDC rate setting). Does CBDC make the transmission fracture better or worse? The answer may depend on whether it is designed as a complement to or substitute for commercial bank deposits.

6. **How should the Fed model the Kalecki-Levy profit identity's implications for the FCI when setting policy?** If fiscal deficits mechanically support corporate profits and thereby keep the FCI looser than the policy rate implies, should the Fed's internal FCI models include a fiscal deficit adjustment? And if so, should the Fed target a tighter FCI (requiring more aggressive rate action) or accept that the deficit-supported FCI is the relevant measure of financial conditions for the real economy?
