# Sovereign Debt Sustainability — Panel Synthesis

**Topic:** sovereign_debt | **Category:** credit_and_rates | **Iteration:** iter_0017

## HIGH_CONFIDENCE

**1. Currency regime is the primary classifier of sovereign debt risk — not debt/GDP level.**

**Confidence: 9/10**

Nations borrowing in their own free-floating currency face a categorically different constraint (inflation tolerance, political willingness) than those borrowing in foreign currency or under fixed/pegged regimes. The historical base rate for sovereign default among own-currency issuers with domestic-law debt is near zero in the modern era. The actual risk for these issuers is real value erosion — inflation, financial repression, currency depreciation — not default.

- **Originating agents:** challenger_02 (Claims 1, 4 — 8/10, 9/10), fx_strategist_01 (Claim 1, 9/10), fx_strategist_02 (Claim 1, 8/10), generalist_02 (Claim 6, 8/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Strongest shared finding in pair_2 debate. Pair_3 debate agreement between rates_strategist_02 and challenger_02. Unchallenged across all four debates.
- **Surviving evidence:** Zero instances of sovereign default by a free-floating domestic-currency issuer in the modern record. Russia 1998, Argentina 2001, Greece 2012 all involved foreign-currency debt, currency boards, or eurozone membership. CDS markets price US/UK/Japan default risk at <2% cumulative 5-year — markets clearly make this distinction even if analytical frameworks often don't.
- **Caveat from pair_3:** The 0/6 base rate comes from a comparison class that may not include the current US configuration (high debt + persistent primary deficits + r≈g + above-target inflation simultaneously). The base rate is informative but not dispositive.

**2. Financial repression / slow erosion is the modal outcome for high-debt advanced economies, not acute crisis.**

**Confidence: 8/10**

Among 23 historical episodes where DM debt/GDP exceeded 90%, acute crisis occurred in 17% of cases — and all in non-monetary-sovereign contexts. Slow erosion (debt service >15% of revenue for >10 years, crowding out) occurred in 61%. Successful deleveraging occurred in 22%. The most probable resolution for current DM sovereign debt is decades of above-target inflation (3-6%), negative real bond returns, and subpar growth — costly but not dramatic.

- **Originating agents:** generalist_02 (Claim 2, 6/10), generalist_01 (Claim 8, 7/10), challenger_02 (Claim 4, 9/10; "financial repression modal outcome"), equity_analyst_01 (40-50% probability for repression path), rates_strategist_02 (Claim 5, 7/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Combined confidence 8/10 in pair_0 debate. Pair_3: challenger_02 won outcomes debate (slow erosion, not fracture). Convergence across all four debates.
- **Surviving evidence:** Post-WWII UK (270% → 50% over 40 years via repression + growth), US (120% → 30% over 35 years), Japan (250%+ for 30 years, stagnation not crisis), Belgium (140% → gradual consolidation), Italy (120-155% for 30+ years, ECB backstop). The KB's own `financial_repression_modal_outcome` concept (confidence 7) aligns.

**3. The r-g differential is the governing variable for sovereign debt trajectories, and it is shifting adversely for the US.**

**Confidence: 9/10 on the identity, 6/10 on specific trajectory projections**

The debt sustainability equation Δ(Debt/GDP) = (r - g) × (Debt/GDP) - Primary Balance/GDP is an accounting identity. When r<g, debt can stabilize with primary deficits. When r>g, stabilization requires primary surpluses. US effective rate rising from ~1.5% (2021) to ~3.3% (2024), converging toward 4.5-5.0% as ~$8T in sub-2% pandemic-era issuance rolls over 2025-2028, against nominal GDP growth of ~5-5.5%.

- **Originating agents:** equity_analyst_01 (Claim 1, 8/10), equity_analyst_02 (Claim 2, 9/10), generalist_01 (Claim 9, 8/10), generalist_02 (throughout), rates_strategist_02 (throughout)
- **Debate outcome:** Universal convergence. Pair_1 combined confidence 9/10. Pair_0 agreement. The direction is arithmetically certain; timing is the uncertainty.
- **Surviving evidence:** No G7 economy currently generates a primary surplus sufficient to stabilize debt/GDP at market-clearing rates. US primary deficit of ~3.5-4.0% GDP requires a swing of 4.5-5.5pp to achieve stabilization — top decile of historical consolidation difficulty. Median historical consolidation achieved: 2.5pp over 5 years (IMF database).

**4. Japan is structurally non-generalizable to the US and should not be used to argue US debt is indefinitely sustainable.**

**Confidence: 8/10**

Japan's sustainability rests on a unique configuration: NIIP +70% GDP (largest net creditor), >90% domestic JGB ownership, BoJ owns >50% of outstanding JGBs, persistent current account surplus, deflationary backdrop keeping r<g. The US fails every one of these conditions: negative NIIP (~-$20T), ~70% domestic ownership (declining), Fed owns ~20% of Treasuries (declining via QT), current account deficit ~3% GDP, moderately inflationary environment.

- **Originating agents:** fx_strategist_01 (Claim 4, 9/10), generalist_02 (Claim 4, 8/10), challenger_02 (Claim 5, 7/10), equity_analyst_02 (Claim 7, 8/10), generalist_01 (Claim 8, 7/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Agreement in pair_0. Agreement in pair_2. Pair_3: both agents identified Japan as critical but drew different implications (challenger_02: Japan disproves crisis narratives; rates_strategist_02: Japan is fiscal dominance). Pair_1: equity_analyst_01's claim that Japan is "more sustainable" than US was refuted.
- **Critical nuance from pair_3:** Japan's 30+ years coincided with deflation — it never faced the three-body problem of inflation + high debt + tight monetary policy simultaneously. The current US environment (3-4% inflation + 124% debt/GDP) is genuinely different from Japan's experience.

**5. The fiscal deficit mechanically supports corporate earnings via the Kalecki-Levy profit identity, creating a consolidation paradox.**

**Confidence: 8/10 on mechanism, 6/10 on magnitude**

The Kalecki-Levy profit equation is an accounting identity: Corporate Profits = Investment + Fiscal Deficit + Net Exports + Consumption from Profit Income - Worker Savings. The 6-7% US deficit (~$1.8-2.0T) is the dominant contributor, estimated at ~$20-40/share of S&P 500 EPS (7-15%). Any fiscal consolidation sufficient to stabilize debt/GDP (2-3pp GDP improvement) would directly reduce the earnings base, turning "fiscal improvement" into an earnings recession.

- **Originating agents:** equity_analyst_01 (Claim 6, 7/10), generalist_01 (referenced as "carry trap"), rates_strategist_02 (Claim 2, FCI connection)
- **Debate outcome:** Identified as "single most valuable insight across both analyses" in pair_1 debate. Pair_3: rates_strategist_02's Kalecki-FCI link identified as "most original analytical contribution." equity_analyst_02 entirely missed this channel — flagged as a "glaring omission for a self-described earnings specialist."
- **Surviving evidence:** The accounting identity is definitional. The $20-40/share estimate has wide uncertainty (±50%), but the direction is high-confidence. European austerity 2011-2014: EPS fell 10-15%, multiples barely rose, indices flat to down — historical validation of the consolidation-earnings headwind.

**6. The Treasury basis trade ($800B-$1T+) is a systemic amplifier that could transform gradual sovereign stress into an acute equity crash.**

**Confidence: 7/10**

The 8-step transmission chain: sovereign debt supply concern → term premium rise → Treasury basis widens → leveraged unwind (50-100x leverage) → forced Treasury selling → repo spike → cross-asset collateral haircuts → equity margin calls → broad deleveraging. The "Fed dilemma" is that intervention (unlimited Treasury purchases) would validate fiscal dominance fears.

- **Originating agents:** equity_analyst_01 (Claim 9, 6/10), generalist_01 (Claim 7, 7/10), generalist_02 (Claim 7, 8/10 via Truss validation)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_0: "one of the strongest claims in either analysis." Pair_1: basis trade amplification channel identified as original and well-constructed. Truss gilt crisis validated: supply channel (~120bp in 4 days), LDI leverage amplifier, central bank arrest capability.
- **Surviving evidence:** March 2020 required $1.6T in emergency Fed purchases when the basis trade unwound at ~$300B. Current notional is 2-3x larger. UK 2022 validated the LDI amplifier mechanism. SEC clearing mandate (potentially Q2 2026) is a calendar-identifiable catalyst.
- **Partially refuted:** generalist_01's claim that the basis trade compresses term premium by 20-40bp and creates a "reflexive loop" where cheap funding → larger deficits was challenged in pair_0 (deficits are driven by entitlement spending, not marginal term premium changes).

**7. The stock-bond correlation has bifurcated by maturity — the long end no longer hedges equity risk.**

**Confidence: 7/10**

2Y-SPX correlation: -0.15 to -0.30 (reflects Fed credibility). 30Y-SPX correlation: +0.05 to +0.15 (reflects fiscal supply dynamics). This bifurcation is the signature of sovereign debt supply as a distinct pricing factor. It means long-duration Treasuries no longer reliably offset equity drawdowns, breaking the 60/40 portfolio's diversification benefit.

- **Originating agents:** equity_analyst_01 (Claim 4, 8/10), generalist_01 (Claim 2, 7/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_0: confirmed by two independent agents, identified as "Agent A's single strongest original contribution." Historical precedent: observed in UK during Truss (short gilts rallied, long gilts collapsed) and Italy 2011.
- **Portfolio implications:** Vol-targeting strategies ($500B-$1T AUM) mechanically reduce equity exposure when stock-bond correlations rise, creating forced selling during fiscal stress — an amplification mechanism absent pre-2020.
- **Limitation:** Sample period is short (~3 years of post-QE data).

**8. The dollar cycle is the primary exogenous driver of EM sovereign stress, operating through multiple correlated channels.**

**Confidence: 8/10**

Three transmission channels: (a) balance-sheet effects on USD-denominated liabilities (~$13T outside the US), (b) tightening global financial conditions via the "dollar as global risk factor," (c) commodity price suppression. These channels are positively correlated, producing convex (not linear) EM responses — a 100bp US term premium shock generates ~200-250bp equivalent EM tightening.

- **Originating agents:** fx_strategist_01 (Claim 2, 8/10; Claim 5, 7/10), fx_strategist_02 (Claim 4, 8/10), generalist_01 (Claim 6, 7/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_2 agreement as second-strongest shared finding. Every major EM crisis cluster was preceded by sustained dollar strengthening (1982, 1997-98, 2013-15, 2021-22). 2013 Taper Tantrum: UST +130bp → EM spreads +100bp, EM FX -5-15%, reserve losses ~$100B.
- **Modifying factor from pair_2:** EM local-currency debt shift (45% → 70%) has *disguised* rather than eliminated vulnerability. FX risk transferred to foreign investors who sell during stress, producing equivalent outflows (fx_strategist_02 won this point).

**Confidence: 9/10**

US ~120% debt/GDP with 6-7% deficits, Japan ~260%, Italy ~140%, France ~115% (rising), UK ~100%, Germany ~65%. The dispersion and divergent trajectories are directly observable from national statistics.

- **Originating agents:** generalist_01 (Claim 5, 9/10), generalist_02 (confirmed), equity_analyst_01 (Claim 7)

**10. Threshold-seeking in sovereign debt analysis persists and produces systematically miscalibrated risk assessments.**

**Confidence: 8/10**

After the Reinhart-Rogoff 90% threshold was debunked, the field shifted to subtler thresholds: 5-signal fiscal dominance sequences, r>g "activation points," "4% absorption" triggers. All suffer from the same fundamental problem: imposing discontinuities on continuous processes using small samples (N=3-4 for the 5-signal sequence, N=7 for the absorption threshold).

- **Originating agents:** challenger_02 (Claim 2, 8/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Won decisively in pair_3. rates_strategist_02 relied on the 5-signal framework without interrogating its statistical foundations; challenger_02's critique was "nearly unanswerable." The psychological mechanism (anchoring bias, narrative convenience) is well-documented.
- **Implication:** Confidence intervals on all threshold-based predictions should be substantially wider than currently stated. The r-g identity is directionally useful; quantitative precision in base rates derived from heterogeneous panels is illusory.

## MODERATE_CONFIDENCE

**1. The post-WWII deleveraging toolkit is only ~25-40% available today.**

**Confidence: 6/10**

Of four historical channels — financial repression (35% contribution), negative real rates (25%), strong real growth (25%), primary surplus (15%) — only sustained moderate inflation remains clearly available. Forced-savings regulation is politically infeasible, demographic tailwinds have reversed, no current productivity equivalent to the post-war boom (AI is a candidate but unproven).

- **Originating agents:** generalist_02 (Claim 5, 7/10), generalist_01 (caveat), equity_analyst_01 (mentioned)
- **Debate outcome:** Directional finding survived pair_0. Specific percentages (35/25/25/15) refuted for lack of identification strategy — "illustrative at best." Implication: deleveraging would be 2-3x slower than 1945-55.

**2. The UK Truss gilt crisis is the most operationally relevant DM sovereign stress analogue.**

**Confidence: 7/10**

Validated three propositions: (a) unfunded fiscal expansion can produce ~120bp long-end moves in 4 days, (b) leveraged sovereign bond positioning (LDI) amplifies, (c) central banks can arrest crises via emergency operations but at cost of monetary credibility. Annual probability of Truss-equivalent US event: 5-10%.

- **Originating agents:** generalist_02 (Claim 7, 8/10), generalist_01 (referenced), rates_strategist_02 (cited)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_0: "most recent and operationally relevant analogue." Pair_3: both agents cite it. The 5-10% annual probability estimate is among the most useful calibrated numbers in the analysis.

**3. The monetary policy transmission mechanism is partially impaired by sovereign debt dynamics.**

**Confidence: 7/10 on mechanism, 5/10 on severity**

Rate hikes generate a fiscal impulse that partially offsets contractionary intent (each 100bp adds ~$250-320B in debt service over the rollover horizon). The Kalecki channel keeps FCIs looser than the policy rate implies. Basel III creates "regulatory crowding-in" equivalent to 1940s financial repression. Central banks face a three-body problem: inflation vs. financial stability vs. sovereign funding.

- **Originating agents:** rates_strategist_02 (Claims 1-6, 7-8/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_3: "Agent A wins the mechanism debate; Agent B wins the outcomes debate." The impairment is real but produces decades of muddling through, not a dramatic fracture. The fiscal impulse offset magnitude was partially refuted: realistic bondholder MPC yields 10-25% offset, not 25-50%.

**4. EM sovereign stress operates through common-cause exposure, not contagion.**

**Confidence: 7/10**

Historical falsification across four major episodes (Tequila 1994, Asia 1997-98, Argentina 2001, Taper Tantrum 2013) consistently supports common-cause over contagion. Countries sharing external vulnerabilities (pegged FX, current account deficits, USD debt, low reserves) are hit simultaneously by common shocks (DM tightening, dollar strength). Countries lacking these vulnerabilities are unaffected regardless of "proximity."

- **Originating agents:** challenger_02 (Claim 6, 8/10), fx_strategist_01 (Claim 8, implicitly), fx_strategist_02 (Claim 4, implicitly)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_3: identified as novel insight with "operationally useful" reframing. Supported by Forbes & Rigobon (2002). Practical implication: hedge common-factor exposure, not sequential cascading.

**5. An EM hard-currency maturity wall in 2025-2028 creates concentrated refinancing risk for frontier sovereigns.**

**Confidence: 7/10**

~$300-400B in frontier/lower-rated EM maturities at spreads 200-600bp wider than issuance. Kenya refinanced 2024 Eurobond at 10.375% vs. original 6.875%. Next tier at risk: Nigeria ($5B 2025-2029), Tunisia (CDS ~1,000bp), El Salvador ($800M 2025), Bahrain. Adds $2-6B/year in debt service across the cohort.

- **Originating agents:** fx_strategist_02 (Claim 7, 8/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_2: "most actionable insight in either analysis." Concrete, verifiable, and underpriced by macro investors who don't track EM amortization schedules.

**6. The reverse-DCF framework reveals the fiscal trajectory implied by current Treasury yields is implausible.**

**Confidence: 6/10**

10Y UST at 4.3-4.5% implies a terminal primary surplus of +1.0-1.5% GDP within 10 years. Historical base rate for fiscal adjustments of that magnitude: <10% of attempts succeed. Either the market is mispricing, or reserves currency status justifies more complacency than fundamentals suggest.

- **Originating agents:** equity_analyst_02 (Claim 3, 6/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_1: "the right analytical move" — framework is novel and useful. But the conclusion depends on the assumption that fiscal consolidation is politically infeasible. The "exorbitant privilege" discount is real and hard to quantify.

**7. The Fed's reaction function is asymmetrically constrained: fiscal ceiling on tightening, no floor on easing.**

**Confidence: 6/10**

The fiscal arithmetic creates an implicit ceiling at ~5-5.5%: above this, the doom-loop dynamics activate as rollover costs approach nominal GDP growth against persistent primary deficits. No equivalent constraint on easing. This truncates the right tail of the policy rate distribution.

- **Originating agents:** rates_strategist_02 (Claim 7, 7/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_3: identified as novel and tradeable insight. The claim that September 2024 cut was partially fiscally motivated is speculative but consistent. Rate option skew implications are testable.

**8. The sovereign stress sequencing follows a consistent historical pattern: term premium → steepening → FX → equity underperformance → rating downgrades → acute crisis (if at all).**

**Confidence: 6/10**

Consistent across 11 episodes. US currently at stage 1-2 (term premium rising, curve steepening). Conditional probability of progression from stage 2 to stage 5+ is ~50-60% in full sample, but ~20-30% for monetary-sovereign reserve-currency issuers. 4 of 11 episodes stopped at stage 1-2 and reversed.

- **Originating agents:** generalist_02 (Claim 3, 7/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_0: "Agent B is stronger" — empirically grounded with conditional probabilities. The 20-30% conditional progression probability is more useful than generalist_01's "stability 3/10" rating.

**9. DM sovereign debt analysis exhibits a systematic DM-EM asymmetry bias.**

**Confidence: 7/10**

DM risk analyzed through "slow deterioration" lens (5-20 year horizon); EM through "sudden stop" lens (6-24 months). But DM sovereigns CAN face sudden stops (UK 2022, Italy 2011) and EM sovereigns CAN persist for decades at "unsustainable" levels (India 80-85%, Brazil persistent deficits).

- **Originating agents:** challenger_02 (Claim 3, 7/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Not directly challenged. The asymmetry is partially justified by structural differences but introduces systematic biases in both directions — overconfidence about DM resilience timelines and EM vulnerability timelines.

**10. US fiscal spillover creates multiplicative (not additive) EM tightening through three correlated channels.**

**Confidence: 7/10**

US term premium expansion → (a) direct crowding-out of EM bond demand, (b) dollar strengthening, (c) global financial conditions tightening. The three channels activate simultaneously and reinforce each other. Current vulnerability exceeds 2013 Taper Tantrum (deficit 6.5-7% vs. 4%, basis trade amplification absent then).

- **Originating agents:** fx_strategist_02 (Claim 4, 8/10)
- **Debate outcome:** Pair_2: "B adds a genuinely underappreciated dimension" via the crowding-out channel (quantity of DM issuance, not just dollar level). The convexity claim (second 50bp produces 1.5x the response) is consistent with microstructure theory but hard to prove statistically.

## REFUTED

**1. "At most one of [cross-asset prices] can be correct."** (generalist_01 Claim 1)

Refuted by the agent's own subsequent analysis in the same claim. If credit prices 1-2Y correctly, rates prices 3-5Y, and gold prices 5-10Y tail risk, all are simultaneously coherent across time horizons. Rhetorical overreach.

**2. "Japan at 250% debt/GDP may actually be a more sustainable configuration than the US at 125%."** (equity_analyst_01 Claim 7)

Internally contradicted: equity_analyst_01 simultaneously argues financial repression delivers -1 to -3% real annualized returns (Claim 2, scenario B) while calling Japan's 25-year financial repression "more sustainable." Confuses "has not yet exploded" with "viable long-term path." Refuted in pair_1.

**3. "High-quality IG corporates now offer superior credit fundamentals to several G7 sovereigns."** (equity_analyst_02 Claim 6)

Conflates standalone balance sheet metrics with systemic credit independence. Microsoft's creditworthiness is nested within the US sovereign's institutional, legal, and monetary framework. In sovereign distress, corporate tax rates rise, regulatory environment shifts, domestic revenue falls. The sovereign ceiling reflects real economic dependency, not an analytical artifact. Refuted in pair_1.

**4. Probability-weighted S&P 500 expected return of ~3.6% nominal / ~0% real.** (equity_analyst_01 Claim 10)

Mechanically sensitive to scenario weights: shifting financial repression probability from 45% to 30% inverts the conclusion (expected return rises above risk-free rate). Agent's own confidence was 5/10. The qualitative point (sovereign headwinds for equity real returns) survives; the specific numbers and the "structural equity underweight" conclusion do not. Refuted in pair_1.

**5. Post-WWII deleveraging decomposition: 35% financial repression / 25% negative real rates / 25% growth / 15% primary surplus.** (generalist_02 Claim 5 — specific percentages)

No clean identification strategy. All four channels operated concurrently and interacted (growth was partially caused by financial repression enabling low borrowing costs). The directional finding ("less toolkit available today") survives; the quantitative attribution does not. Refuted in pair_0.

**6. Philippines vs. Indonesia CDS differential is a "mispricing."** (fx_strategist_02 Claim 6)

Ignores legitimate pricing factors: Philippines has lower external debt/GDP, stronger governance scores, $35B/year remittance flows, and more diversified services exports. Calling a 20bp CDS differential a mispricing based on a structural hypothesis at confidence 6 over a 10+ year timeline is overreaching. Refuted in pair_2.

**7. "5Y5Y forward breakeven above 3.0% is the single most important threshold to monitor."** (generalist_01 Claim 9)

Analytically arbitrary. Cannot discriminate between "market expects financial repression" and "market expects sticky inflation from supply shocks." The 5Y5Y breakeven reflects liquidity premia, risk premia, and actual inflation expectations — it is one useful input, not a decisive threshold. Refuted in pair_0.

**8. Fiscal impulse offsets 25-50% of monetary tightening.** (rates_strategist_02 Claim 1 — magnitude)

Assumes fiscal multiplier of 0.5-0.7 for interest payments to bondholders. Realistic bondholder MPC (institutional investors, foreign central banks, HNW individuals) is 0.2-0.4, reducing the offset to ~10-25%. Direction survives; magnitude overstated by roughly 2x. Partially refuted in pair_3.

**9. "Markets tend to over-price the acute crisis scenario and under-price the slow erosion scenario."** (generalist_02 Claim 2)

Asserted without market pricing evidence. What would it mean for markets to "price" the slow erosion scenario? The claim may be true but is unsupported by the evidence presented. Refuted in pair_0.

## KEY_DISAGREEMENTS

**1. Is Japan the central case or the boundary condition?**

challenger_02 argues Japan should be the prior for all advanced economies — theories must explain Japan first. rates_strategist_02 argues Japan is the extreme case of fiscal dominance. generalist_02 documents specific non-replicable conditions.

**Unresolved because:** Japan's 30+ years at extreme debt coincided with *deflation*, not the inflation + high debt combination facing the US. Whether Japan's structural features (domestic ownership, current account surplus, savings culture) are load-bearing rather than coincidental remains genuinely uncertain. The burden-of-proof question (is Japan special, or is it the base case?) is epistemically sound but empirically unresolvable with available data.

**2. What is the US-specific probability of fiscal stress at r≈g?**

The cross-country panel suggests 35-40%, but challenger_02 demonstrates this is drawn from a heterogeneous panel including Eurozone members with genuine solvency constraints. For own-currency reserve issuers, challenger_02 estimates 10-20% (market-forced) vs. 50-60% (politically-driven adjustment). No agent provides a rigorous US-specific estimate because the comparison class has n≈3.

**3. Can financial repression operate in modern liberalized capital markets?**

Historical repression required captive creditor bases (Regulation Q, directed investment). Modern equivalents (Basel III regulatory crowding, ~$4T in bank sovereign holdings) are partial. generalist_01 raises whether crypto, stablecoins, and digital capital mobility structurally weaken the repression toolkit. equity_analyst_02 notes modern bondholders can exit more easily. No agent resolves this.

**4. How does AI productivity interact with sovereign debt sustainability?**

Identified by 5+ agents as the single biggest wildcard. If AI adds 0.5-1.0pp to real GDP, r-g stays favorable and current debt levels may be sustainable without consolidation or repression. But timing, magnitude, distribution, and fiscal impact (tax revenue on AI profits vs. costs of displaced workers) are all deeply uncertain. This is "the single biggest uncertainty that cannot be resolved by looking at historical base rates."

**5. What triggers the transition from gradual sovereign stress to acute?**

generalist_01 proposes three conditions (inflation >3.5%, tight labor market, Treasury dislocation). generalist_02 gives 20-30% conditional probability of progression from stage 2 to stage 5+. No consensus on specific catalyst, timing, or whether the transition is a phase change (discontinuous) or a tipping point (continuous but nonlinear). The basis trade unwind is the most-cited mechanism but has never been triggered by fiscal (rather than exogenous) causes.

**6. The "nowhere else to go" paradox for simultaneous G7 fiscal deterioration.**

If all major sovereigns deteriorate simultaneously, reserve managers face a substitution problem — there is no AAA alternative at scale. This could paradoxically support each sovereign's funding, or trigger flight to gold, crypto, and other non-sovereign stores of value. The post-WWII analogue (simultaneous high debt resolved via coordinated repression) lacks a modern institutional coordination mechanism. Raised by generalist_02; unaddressed by other agents.

## NOVEL_CONTRIBUTIONS

**challenger_02:**
- Conceptual instability of "sustainability" — disambiguating solvency, liquidity, and political willingness as three failure modes with different dynamics, hedges, and consequences
- Survivorship bias in default studies: enumerating high-debt non-defaulters (Belgium, Canada, post-WWII US/UK, India) as systematically underweighted evidence
- Contagion vs. common-cause reframing with historical falsification across four major EM episodes
- "Nothing happened" as evidence: the epistemological point that negative evidence is systematically underweighted because it doesn't generate narratives
- Japan-as-central-case epistemological argument (burden of proof on those claiming Japan is special)
- CBO projection bias documentation with historical error margins

**equity_analyst_01:**
