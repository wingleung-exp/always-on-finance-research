## Key Claims

CDX IG at ~55-65bp implies 12-month default probability of ~0.9-1.1%. MOVE index at ~95-110 implies daily 10Y moves of ~6-7bp. 3. **The equity-credit basis in AI-adjacent names has reached levels last seen in 2007 and late 1999, signaling either credit complacency or equity euphoria — and the direction of convergence matters enormously.** Equity-implied CDS for Mag-7 names (derived from Merton model using equity vol, leverage, and asset value) runs 15-25bp. Force 2: Potential AI supply-side productivity gains (disinflationary, negative stock-bond price correlation — the portfolio diversification that worked 2000-2020). Currently, Forces 1 and 3 are dominant — the rolling 6-month stock-bond return correlation is +0.15 to +0.35, consistent with a regime where inflation expectations matter more than growth expectations. The KB identifies this as "ambiguous" — I upgrade this to "structurally positive with intermittent negative episodes during growth scares." A capex cut of 20-25% breaks the loop at the Kalecki node: profits fall → equity prices fall → capital outflows → dollar weakens → imported input costs rise → margins compress further → MORE capex cuts. The key insight: the feedback loop means the "gradual adjustment" scenario (KB: dollar_cycle_plateau_correction at 55% probability) is less likely than either continued appreciation OR sharp correction. Cross-asset pricing consistency requires that the macro story embedded in each asset class's price is mutually compatible. Currently: equities say "AI revolution imminent, growth acceleration," rates say "muddle-through, no structural change," credit says "low default risk, soft landing," FX says "US exceptionalism justified by capital inflows." ### Claim 3: Equity-Credit Basis Divergence

The equity-credit basis uses the Merton (1974) structural model to derive "equity-implied" credit spreads from equity volatility, leverage, and distance-to-default.

For Mag-7 specifically:
- Ultra-low leverage (net cash positions totaling ~$300B+, per KB: hyperscaler_self_funded_capex)
- Moderate equity vol (~20-25% implied)
- Merton-implied 5Y CDS: 15-25bp (essentially risk-free)
- Actual 5Y CDS: 30-50bp (slightly wider, reflecting name-specific event risk)
- Negative basis (credit wider than equity implies) = healthy skepticism in credit market

For second-tier AI ecosystem:
- Higher leverage (many leveraged loan issuers in AI infrastructure, cloud software, AI services)

Historical precedent: positive equity-credit basis in the 75th+ percentile has preceded credit spread widening of 150-300bp within 12 months in 2007 (pre-GFC), 2000 (pre-tech bust), and 2019 (pre-COVID, though interrupted by exogenous shock).

### Claim 5: Kalecki-FX-Equity Feedback Loop

The KB identifies the Kalecki channel (kalecki_identity_capex_profits) and dollar support (dollar_overvaluation_ai_inflows) as related concepts. My contribution is identifying the feedback loop dynamics:

Loop nodes:
1. AI capex ($250B) → Kalecki profit sustenance (entire growth margin of private investment, KB)
2. Profit growth → equity price appreciation → capital inflows ($40-60B/month, TIC data)
3. Capital inflows → dollar strength (DXY 103-107 despite Fed cuts)
4. Dollar strength → cheaper imported inputs (semiconductor equipment, energy) → margin support
5. Margin support → capex continuation (OCF coverage 1.5-1.7x, KB) → back to node 1

This is a self-reinforcing loop. The critical property of positive feedback loops: they are non-linear. They do NOT "gradually unwind." They sustain until one node breaks, then the entire loop reverses.

Historical analogy: the 1997-2001 dollar-tech loop. Dollar appreciated ~25% as tech capex sustained profits sustained inflows. The reversal was NOT gradual: DXY fell ~33% from 2002-2008 peak-to-trough, with the sharpest decline in the first 18 months after the loop broke.

Implication: the KB's 55% probability for "plateau-then-correction" over 3-5 years (dollar_cycle_plateau_correction) understates the tail risk of a sharp correction. The feedback loop makes a bimodal distribution more appropriate: 40% continued strength, 35% sharp correction (>15% in 12 months), 25% gradual correction.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the VIX/MOVE ratio a reliable cross-asset signal, or has the structural shift toward 0DTE options and systematic vol selling permanently altered the equilibrium?** Historical precedent covers only 3 episodes; the options microstructure has changed dramatically since 2020. If 0DTE options create a structural VIX discount of 2-3 points, the current VIX/MOVE ratio may be less anomalous than it appears.

2. **Can the Kalecki-FX feedback loop sustain for another 2-3 years even without capex-revenue validation?** The KB identifies hyperscaler capex as hedge-financed (OCF coverage 1.5-1.7x). If OCF coverage remains above 1.0x, the loop could persist despite poor incremental ROIC, extending the duration of overvaluation. The Japanese corporate analogy (KB: hedge_destructive_minsky_category) suggests decades of sub-WACC investment without forced liquidation.

3. **What is the actual capital at risk from the hidden short-vol position, and what is the realistic drawdown in a 2-sigma AI selloff?** My estimate of $200-500B forced selling over 5-10 days is rough. A more precise answer requires modeling the actual portfolio rebalancing rules of passive, momentum, risk-parity, and vol-targeting strategies — data that is not publicly available.

4. **Does the stock-bond correlation regime shift during an AI-specific risk event differently than during a broad macro risk event?** An AI capex bust is a demand shock to the AI sector but potentially a supply shock to the broader economy (if AI reduces costs for non-tech firms). The correlation regime during such an event could be different from a traditional recession.

5. **How should the relative value scorecard account for the "AI miracle" tail scenario?** If TFP acceleration materializes (15-25% probability within 3-5 years per KB), the entire scorecard reverses. Should the portfolio construction overweight the base case and hedge the tail, or should it attempt to be Kelly-optimal across both scenarios?

6. **Is the Fed framework review truly under-priced, or is the market correctly pricing a near-zero probability of substantive changes?** The base rate for meaningful central bank framework changes is low (once per cycle at most). The carry cost of positioning is manageable but not zero. The option value depends on a probability judgment I cannot make with high confidence.

7. **At what real rate level does the cross-asset inconsistency resolve?** If real rates rise 50bp to ~2.5-2.7%, equity multiples compress sufficiently to narrow the inconsistency. If real rates fall 50bp to ~1.5-1.7%, the dollar weakens and the feedback loop faces stress. The current ~2.0-2.2% is the "Goldilocks" level that sustains the tension. What catalysts would push real rates out of this range?
