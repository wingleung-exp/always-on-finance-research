# Credit-Equity Linkage — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Term premium is the overlooked common discount-rate factor driving credit-equity co-movement.** Term-premium-driven yield rises hit both credit spreads and equity multiples simultaneously without any growth offset, producing pure valuation compression. Supply-driven term premium increases (2013 Taper Tantrum, 2018 Q4, 2023 Q3) are qualitatively worse for credit-equity linkage than expectations-driven rate rises, because expectations-driven rises at least reflect improved growth prospects that partially offset higher discount rates. The yield curve decomposition — not the raw level or slope — is the essential diagnostic for distinguishing benign from malignant rate regimes for cross-asset contagion.

2. **The current term premium / credit premium divergence is an internal market inconsistency that must resolve.** ACM term premium sits at +80-130bp, pricing fiscal supply risk and duration uncertainty as expensive. Simultaneously, HY OAS at 350-420bp (40th-55th percentile) prices credit risk as cheap. These two premia should, over medium horizons, reflect a broadly consistent macro risk state. They currently do not. In the three prior episodes of comparable divergence, two resolved via credit widening. This divergence is the single most actionable cross-asset signal from the rates perspective.

3. **The yield curve's current steepening is a term premium steepener, not an expectations steepener, and this distinction is critical for credit-equity assessment.** ACM and Kim-Wright decompositions confirm term premium accounts for the majority of the positive 2s10s slope. A genuine mid-cycle expectations steepener (like 2015-2017) would signal growth acceleration, supporting credit quality and equity earnings. A term premium steepener signals rising duration risk compensation — consistent with fiscal supply pressure, diminished foreign demand, and QT — which compresses credit and equity valuations simultaneously. Interpreting the current positive slope as a "growth signal" without decomposition is a category error.

4. **The basis trade ($800B-$1T+ at 50-100x leverage) has converted the Treasury market from safe haven to contagion source, fundamentally rewiring credit-equity linkage during stress.** The basis trade creates a dual distortion: suppressing measured term premium by 20-40bp in calm markets (acting as synthetic QE) while amplifying term premium spikes during stress. The contagion chain runs: term premium shock → basis widens → forced Treasury selling (50-100x deleveraging) → repo rate spike → collateral haircuts → dealer liquidity withdrawal → credit bid-ask widening → forced selling by levered credit funds → VIX spike → equity risk-parity deleveraging. March 2020 validated this chain at smaller scale ($1.6T Fed emergency intervention required). Current notional is 2-3x the March 2020 level. The SEC central clearing mandate (Q2 2026) is a calendar-identifiable catalyst for potential position adjustment.

5. **Supply-demand technicals have structurally shifted the term premium's equilibrium level higher, with direct transmission to credit and equity.** Private sector duration absorption requirements have risen from a pre-pandemic $500-800B/year to $2.0-2.6T/year (net issuance + QT runoff). The marginal buyer has shifted from price-insensitive (central banks, foreign official sector) to price-sensitive (hedge funds via basis trades, asset managers, households). The deficit-term premium relationship is non-linear: insensitive below ~3-4% structural deficit for a reserve currency issuer but highly sensitive above, with central estimates of 20-40bp per 1pp deficit/GDP. At 6-7% deficits, the US is firmly in the sensitive zone. Indirect bidder share declining from 72% to 65% and T-bill share rising from 15% to 22% are observable confirmation.

6. **The deficit → term premium → debt service → deficit reflexive loop is activating, with direct implications for credit-equity stability via the Kalecki channel.** US net interest has risen from ~$350B (FY2020) to ~$1T (FY2025/26). The loop activates when blended borrowing rate (~4.5%) exceeds nominal GDP growth (~5.5%) — currently a margin of only ~100bp. This reflexive loop interacts with the Kalecki fiscal channel in a paradoxical way: the same deficits that sustain corporate profits (supporting credit quality and equity earnings) require $2T+ annual Treasury issuance that elevates term premium (compressing valuations). Both channels are simultaneously active, creating an unstable equilibrium where the supporting mechanism (fiscal deficits → profits) generates its own destabilizing byproduct (fiscal deficits → issuance → term premium).

7. **The yield-curve-mediated contagion sequence — inversion → bear steepener → credit blowout → equity drawdown — is the canonical transmission pattern, and the current curve has already completed the first two stages.** The 2022 inversion (-108bp on 2s10s) has now resolved into a bear steepener driven primarily by term premium expansion. In prior cycles (2000, 2007), this transition preceded credit stress by 6-18 months. The critical diagnostic is whether the steepening accelerates via falling front-end rates (recession anticipation, benign for credit-equity initially) or rising back-end rates (term premium expansion, immediately negative for credit-equity). The current environment shows the latter — back-end-driven steepening — which is the more dangerous configuration.

8. **The Fed's implicit credit-spread response function (modified Taylor rule) creates a put option that partially endogenizes the contagion threshold, but is currently constrained by inflation.** Approximately 100bp HY spread widening equates to 25-50bp FFR easing equivalent per the Curdia-Woodford formalization. This means the Fed would typically ease before primary markets shut down. However, with services inflation persistent and the 5y5y breakeven at 2.3-2.5% (below the 3.0% de-anchoring trigger but above target), the Fed's ability to respond to credit stress is constrained. An inflation-constrained Fed facing credit stress is the worst-case scenario for credit-equity contagion — the safety net has holes precisely when you might need it.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Term premium as common factor):** Three clean episodes confirm the mechanism. The 2013 Taper Tantrum produced 100bp+ term premium expansion with synchronized credit widening (CDX IG +30bp, HY +80bp) and equity decline (S&P -5.8%) despite no fundamental earnings deterioration. 2018 Q4 saw term premium spike alongside S&P -19.8% and HY OAS widening to 533bp. 2023 Q3 produced the same pattern: ACM term premium rising ~80bp, HY OAS widening from 380 to 500bp, S&P declining 8%. In each case, the mechanism was pure discount-rate compression without growth deterioration — the hallmark of supply-driven term premium transmission. Credit-specific channel: supply-driven term premium raises CLO AAA yields 1:1 without improving borrower cash flows, directly inverting CLO arbitrage economics.

**Claim 2 (Term premium / credit premium divergence):** The divergence is observable in real-time: 10Y ACM term premium at approximately +80-130bp versus HY OAS at 350-420bp. If term premium is correctly pricing fiscal/duration risk as elevated, credit spreads should reflect some component of that risk through higher funding costs and discount rates. The fact that credit spreads remain compressed suggests either: (a) term premium is overstating risk (rates market is wrong), (b) the Kalecki fiscal buffer is genuinely suppressing credit risk (credit market is right for now but fragile), or (c) private credit has absorbed enough of the riskiest borrowers that public HY OAS no longer captures the true risk distribution. Resolution pathway analysis: 2/3 prior episodes resolved via credit widening, 1/3 via term premium compression.

**Claim 3 (Term premium steepener diagnostic):** In the 2015-2017 mid-cycle, the positive 2s10s slope was driven by rate expectations (market pricing a normalization path for the funds rate). Term premium was negative or near-zero. The current positive slope has the opposite composition: rate expectations are for cuts (implying curve inversion from that component alone), while term premium expansion more than offsets to produce a positive slope. This means the curve's positive slope is entirely a risk compensation phenomenon, not a growth signal. Operationally, this means traditional curve-steepening trades that are long growth/short recession are mispriced if they don't account for the decomposition.

**Claim 4 (Basis trade contagion):** The 8-step amplification chain has been partially validated by March 2020. At that point, basis trade notional was estimated at $500-600B. The current $800B-$1T+ at 50-100x leverage represents significantly larger systemic exposure. The dual distortion is critical for understanding measured term premium: in calm markets, basis trades compress measured term premium by 20-40bp (because levered buyers absorb duration), meaning true equilibrium term premium is higher than models report. In stress, the unwind amplifies the term premium spike beyond what fundamentals would justify. This creates a bimodal distribution of outcomes rather than a smooth distribution — calm is calmer than justified, stress is more violent than justified.

**Claim 5 (Supply-demand structural shift):** The arithmetic is straightforward. Pre-pandemic, the Fed was a net buyer (QE) and foreign official sector held ~$4.1T (growing). Currently, the Fed is a net seller (QT at ~$60B/month), foreign official holdings have plateaued, and net issuance is $2T+/year. The gap is filled by price-sensitive buyers who demand compensation. The non-linear relationship between deficits and term premium (insensitive below 3-4%, highly sensitive above) is supported by pre-QE regressions (20-40bp per 1pp deficit/GDP) and event studies (Clinton consolidation: 80-100bp compression; Truss fiscal expansion: 100bp spike in 4 days). The US at 6-7% deficit/GDP is firmly in the sensitive zone.

**Claim 6 (Deficit-term premium doom loop):** Net interest of ~$1T represents ~15% of federal revenue and ~3.5% of GDP. With average debt maturity of ~6.2 years, approximately 16% of the stock reprices annually at current market rates. Each 100bp of additional rate hikes generates ~$250-320B in additional interest payments. The blended rate (~4.5%) versus nominal GDP growth (~5.5%) margin of ~100bp is historically thin. The interaction with Kalecki is the key insight: deficits simultaneously sustain the earnings that keep credit spreads tight AND generate the issuance that elevates term premium. This is an inherently unstable equilibrium — the two effects pull in opposite directions, and their relative magnitudes determine whether the system holds or breaks.

**Claim 7 (Yield curve contagion sequence):** The historical pattern is well-documented. 2006-2009: inversion (Nov 2006) → bear steepener begins (mid-2007) → credit blowout (late 2007-2008) → equity collapse (2008). 2000-2001: inversion (Feb 2000) → bear steepener (late 2000) → credit widening (early 2001) → equity decline (2001-2002). The current cycle: inversion (July 2022, -108bp) → steepener underway (positive 2s10s as of late 2024). The critical variable is whether Stage 3 (credit blowout) activates. The term premium composition of the current steepener makes it more analogous to the pre-crisis patterns than to benign mid-cycle steepening.

**Claim 8 (Constrained Fed put):** The Fed's revealed behavior is consistent with a credit-spread reaction function: 2018 Q4 pivot (HY at 533bp), March 2020 emergency response, 2023 pause. But the current inflation constraint is real: core PCE remains above target, services inflation is persistent, and the 5y5y forward at 2.3-2.5% signals that long-run inflation expectations are elevated relative to target (though below the 3.0% de-anchoring threshold). If credit stress emerges while inflation remains above target, the Fed faces a genuine dilemma that did not exist in 2018 or 2020 (when inflation was below target). The Gilchrist-Zakrajsek Excess Bond Premium provides a real-time monitor of this constraint: if it rises while the Fed is on hold due to inflation, the safety net is effectively absent.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Credit Cycles:** The term premium channel provides the "why now" for credit cycle turning points. The credit cycle literature (maturity wall, CLO OC test thresholds, fallen angel risk) identifies the mechanisms that can generate credit stress, but the term premium regime determines whether those mechanisms activate in a benign or malignant rate environment. Supply-driven term premium expansion makes every credit vulnerability worse by raising refinancing costs without improving cash flows. The $900B-$1.1T maturity wall meets its worst-case scenario when term premium is elevated and rising — every dollar of refinancing costs more, and the CLO arbitrage that absorbs leveraged loans inverts at lower spread levels.

**Equity Cycles:** The term premium steepener diagnostic connects directly to equity valuation frameworks. If the curve's positive slope is a risk compensation signal rather than a growth signal, equity multiples should not expand as they would in a genuine mid-cycle. The 5y5y forward rate — currently pricing long-run neutral at elevated levels — acts as a constraint on sustainable equity multiples via the equity risk premium framework. Supply-driven term premium compresses equity multiples through higher discount rates while the Kalecki fiscal channel supports the numerator (earnings). This tension is the central dynamic of the current equity regime.

**Fiscal Dominance / Sovereign Debt:** The deficit-term premium doom loop is the primary transmission channel from fiscal dynamics to financial markets. The non-linear relationship (insensitive below 3-4% deficit/GDP, highly sensitive above) means the US has crossed into the sensitive zone. The interaction between fiscal issuance and term premium is the mechanism by which fiscal dominance manifests in market prices before it manifests in institutional changes. The 5-signal fiscal dominance sequence (debt/GDP >100% YES, interest expense > defense YES, implicit coordination DEBATABLE, regulatory forced buying NO, inflation de-anchoring NO) positions the US at 2.0-2.5 on the sequence.

**Real Estate Cycles:** Term premium is a first-order driver of CRE capitalization rates. The term premium steepener directly compounds CRE repricing pressures: rising long-end yields driven by risk compensation (not growth) increase required cap rates without improving NOI growth prospects. The CRE-to-bank contagion chain (office down 25-40% → regional bank provisioning → lending pullback) is amplified by elevated term premium because it raises the refinancing rate for CRE debt simultaneously.

**Risk Appetite Regimes:** The basis trade creates a novel volatility regime: compressed vol in calm markets (basis trade acts as synthetic QE, suppressing term premium and providing liquidity) and amplified vol in stress (basis trade unwind creates forced selling across rates, credit, and equity simultaneously). This produces a bimodal distribution of outcomes inconsistent with standard risk models. The regime transition from low-vol-compressed to high-vol-amplified is likely to be sudden and driven by Treasury market dynamics rather than traditional risk appetite indicators.

**Central Bank Balance Sheets:** QT is the mechanism by which the central bank balance sheet transmits to financial conditions via term premium. The 30-60bp QT-specific term premium impact is additive with fiscal supply pressure. The QE/QT asymmetry (QE compressed term premium by 100-150bp, QT has expanded it by 30-60bp) means the net effect of the full QE-then-QT cycle is a permanent increase in term premium's sensitivity to fiscal supply — the price-insensitive buyer was replaced by price-sensitive buyers, and even QT's end won't reverse that substitution.

## Open Questions

1. **Model divergence on term premium magnitude:** ACM and Kim-Wright estimates diverge by 30-50bp. At the margin, this uncertainty is large enough to affect whether the term premium / credit premium divergence is genuine or within noise. What independent validation can resolve this?

2. **Is the Kalecki fiscal buffer strong enough to indefinitely prevent term premium transmission to credit spreads?** If fiscal deficits sustain corporate earnings sufficiently to keep distance-to-default wide, credit spreads can remain compressed even as term premium rises. The question is whether this divergence can persist for years or must resolve within quarters.

3. **What is the basis trade's current true size and leverage?** Estimates range from $800B to $1.5T with leverage of 20-100x. The range is too wide for precise risk assessment. Has post-2020 regulatory attention (including the pending SEC clearing mandate in Q2 2026) reduced net exposure, or has the trade continued to grow?

4. **Does the yield-curve-mediated contagion sequence (inversion → steepener → credit blowout → equity drawdown) still hold in a regime where the steepening is term-premium-driven rather than expectations-driven?** The historical pattern may not map cleanly because the causal mechanism is different. Term premium steepening does not necessarily signal recession expectations the way expectations-driven steepening does.

5. **How does private credit's opacity interact with the term premium channel?** If $1.7T in private credit is outside the term structure's direct influence (floating rate, not mark-to-market), the term premium channel operates on a shrinking share of the credit universe. Does this reduce the credit-equity linkage via rates, or does it merely shift the transmission from price to quantity (private credit volume rather than spread)?

6. **What is the US-specific activation threshold for the deficit-term premium doom loop given reserve currency status?** The UK gilt crisis activated at ~100% debt/GDP with ~2pp fiscal expansion. The US has deeper capital markets, reserve currency demand, and a different institutional framework. Is the threshold 150% debt/GDP? 200%? Or is the mechanism entirely different for the reserve currency issuer?

7. **Can the Fed credibly respond to credit stress while inflation remains above target?** The 2018 and 2020 pivots occurred with below-target inflation. The current environment is structurally different. If HY OAS widens to 500bp+ while core PCE is 3%+, does the Fed ease? And if it does, what happens to term premium and inflation expectations?

8. **Is the synthetic credit reverse causality (equity vol → CDX protection buying → mechanical spread widening) contaminating the term premium / credit premium divergence signal?** If a portion of measured credit spreads reflects equity hedging demand rather than fundamental credit risk, the "divergence" between term premium and credit premium may be smaller than it appears — credit spreads may actually be tighter on a fundamentals-adjusted basis than headline OAS suggests.
