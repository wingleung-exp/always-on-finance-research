# Deglobalization, Supply Chain Rewiring, & Trade Fragmentation — Geopolitical Risk & Political Economy Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

**1. Deglobalization is not a single phenomenon but three distinct geopolitical processes operating on different timescales, with different market transmission mechanisms, that are analytically conflated at investors' peril.**
- (a) Strategic decoupling: deliberate state-directed severance of technology and critical supply chains between US/allied and China/Russia blocs (5-15 year horizon, driven by security logic)
- (b) Tariff-driven trade fragmentation: episodic, politically-motivated tariff escalation cycles (1-3 year horizon, driven by electoral and mercantilist logic)
- (c) Supply chain resilience restructuring: private-sector risk-repricing of geographic concentration after COVID/Ukraine shocks (3-7 year horizon, driven by corporate risk management)

These three processes sometimes reinforce each other but can also conflict—e.g., tariff escalation against allied countries (Canada, EU, Mexico) undermines the friendshoring logic of strategic decoupling.

**2. Friendshoring economics produces a trilemma: you can optimize for two of {cost efficiency, supply security, allied sourcing} but not all three simultaneously. The revealed preference of governments suggests security is winning over cost, implying a durable 15-30% cost premium on strategically sensitive supply chains.**
- The "nearshoring capex multiplier" is not a single number but is regime-dependent: semiconductor fabrication shows 30-50% cost premium for US/EU vs. Taiwan/Korea (TSMC Arizona documenting 50%+ cost overruns); battery manufacturing shows 20-35% premium for US/EU vs. China; rare earth processing shows 40-60% premium for non-Chinese sources given decades of neglected processing infrastructure outside China.
- The capex multiplier is front-loaded (construction/equipment costs), while the opex multiplier persists but narrows over time as learning curves develop. Total lifecycle cost premium likely settles at 15-25% for mature reshored operations, but the upfront capital requirement is 2-3x what equivalent Asian capacity would cost.

**3. Rare earth and critical mineral supply chain vulnerability represents the single most underpriced geopolitical risk in commodity markets, with China controlling 60-70% of mining and 85-90% of processing for rare earths, and >80% of processing for lithium, cobalt, and graphite into battery-grade materials.**
- China's 2023-2025 export controls on gallium, germanium, antimony, and graphite processing technology signal a shift from passive dominance to active weaponization of mineral supply chains.
- Diversification timelines are 7-15 years for mining and 5-10 years for processing, creating a structural vulnerability window that cannot be closed before the peak risk period for Taiwan contingency scenarios.
- The asymmetry is critical: China can impose mineral export restrictions in weeks; Western alternatives require years and billions in capex. This is not priced into defense/aerospace equities or the broad commodity complex.

**4. Trade fragmentation costs are non-linear and path-dependent: the first 10-15% of trade rerouting produces modest efficiency losses (0.5-1.5% of GDP), but beyond a tipping point (~25-30% trade disruption), costs escalate sharply as redundant supply chains, compliance overhead, and logistical friction compound.**
- Current fragmentation is in the "manageable cost" zone for most sectors, with the notable exception of advanced semiconductors (already at acute disruption) and critical minerals (approaching the tipping point).
- A Taiwan contingency would instantly push global trade fragmentation past the tipping point, with estimated GDP impact of 5-10% globally and 15-25% for semiconductor-dependent manufacturing chains.

**5. The political economy of deglobalization creates a self-reinforcing escalation dynamic: tariffs generate domestic constituencies (protected industries, subsidized reshoring firms) that lobby for their continuation and expansion, while trade partners retaliate, creating mirror constituencies. This makes deglobalization significantly easier to start than to reverse.**
- The geopolitical correlation ratchet (KB confidence 4.67/10) is the financial markets manifestation of this political economy dynamic. I assign higher confidence (6/10) to the ratchet mechanism specifically because the political economy of trade protection creates institutional lock-in beyond the pure fiscal/sunk-cost channel previously identified.
- CHIPS Act, IRA, and EU critical minerals initiatives have created "strategic geopolitical zombies" (KB concept) — firms whose viability depends on continued subsidy. These firms become a political constituency for maintained protection, independent of the original security rationale.

**6. The 2025-2027 tariff escalation scenario tree has shifted decisively toward higher equilibrium tariff levels regardless of political outcomes, because both US political parties have converged on protectionist postures and retaliatory dynamics have hardened.**
- Base case (55% probability): current tariff architecture largely maintained with tactical adjustments; effective average US tariff rate stabilizes at 15-20% (up from ~3% pre-2018)
- Escalation case (30% probability): reciprocal retaliation spirals push effective tariffs to 25-35%, triggering measurable GDP drag of 1.5-2.5% over 2 years
- De-escalation case (15% probability): bilateral deals reduce tariffs to 8-12% average, but structural export controls and investment restrictions remain

**7. The inflation transmission of deglobalization operates through three distinct channels with different time horizons, and the KB's existing estimate of 10-20bp structural inflation contribution understates the full impact because it captures only the steady-state cost premium, not the transition dynamics.**
- Level shift channel (one-time, 0.3-0.8pp CPI): tariff-induced price increases pass through directly, as documented in KB via Amiti-Redding-Weinstein calibration
- Persistent premium channel (ongoing, 10-20bp/year): higher steady-state production costs from reshored supply chains, as currently estimated in KB
- Volatility channel (episodic, variable): reduced supply chain diversification increases the variance of supply shocks, creating more frequent inflationary episodes that ratchet up inflation expectations over time
- Combined impact over a 5-year deglobalization transition: cumulative 1.5-3.0pp of additional inflation relative to the counterfactual of maintained globalization

**8. EM geopolitical bifurcation (KB: 25-40% cumulative performance divergence since 2022) is entering a second phase where the initial "aligned vs. non-aligned" binary is fragmenting into a more complex four-tier structure.**
- Tier 1 — Strategic beneficiaries: countries that gain both trade diversion AND strategic subsidy (India, Vietnam, Mexico before tariff escalation, Poland) — these see both FDI inflows and favorable trade terms
- Tier 2 — Trade diversion beneficiaries without strategic alignment: countries that absorb rerouted Chinese exports (Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia) — gains are real but vulnerable to "transshipment crackdown" risk
- Tier 3 — Caught in crossfire: countries with deep trade linkages to both blocs (South Korea, Taiwan, Germany) — forced into costly hedging and compliance, facing the friendshoring trilemma most acutely
- Tier 4 — Sanctioned/excluded: countries facing direct restrictions (Russia, potentially China under escalation scenarios) — permanent investable universe shrinkage

**9. Mexico's friendshoring premium is at severe risk of reversal following 2025 tariff escalation, representing the single largest mispricing in the EM friendshoring trade.**
- Mexico was the consensus friendshoring beneficiary (nearshoring from China), but 25% tariffs on Mexican imports fundamentally undermine the economic logic of manufacturing in Mexico for US consumption.
- The policy incoherence — simultaneously pursuing friendshoring and imposing tariffs on the primary friendshoring destination — reveals that the tariff agenda is driven by mercantilist logic, not security logic, when the two conflict.
- MXN and Mexican manufacturing equities have not fully repriced this contradiction. The KB's EM bifurcation framework needs updating to account for the possibility that "aligned" status does not protect against tariff-driven trade disruption.

**10. De-dollarization is a real but vastly overhyped risk on market-relevant timescales (5-10 years), while the parallel financial infrastructure being built (CIPS, mBridge, bilateral swap lines, gold accumulation) is a meaningful structural shift on longer horizons (10-25 years).**
- Central bank gold purchases (1,000+ tonnes/year in 2023-2024 vs. ~400-500 tonne pre-2022 average) represent genuine diversification away from dollar reserves, but from a base of ~$12T in global reserves, the flow impact is marginal.
- CIPS transaction volumes are growing (~$15-20T annual throughput estimated, vs. SWIFT ~$150T+) but are concentrated in bilateral China trade, not genuine multilateral alternative payments.
- The dollar's share of FX reserves has declined from ~72% (2000) to ~58% (2024), but the decline has been to a basket of smaller currencies (AUD, CAD, CHF, CNY), not a single challenger. This is gradual reserve diversification, not weaponization-driven flight.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 (Three distinct processes)
The analytical conflation of strategic decoupling, tariff wars, and resilience restructuring produces confused market pricing. Evidence: US tariffs on Canadian aluminum (mercantilist) directly conflict with Canada as a friendshoring partner for critical minerals (security logic). EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) is climate policy but functions as trade fragmentation. CHIPS Act reshoring subsidies (security) coexist with tariff threats against TSMC's home country Taiwan. These contradictions are only explicable when you decompose deglobalization into its three constituent political logics.

### Claim 2 (Friendshoring trilemma and capex multiplier)
TSMC Arizona fab reported 50%+ cost overruns relative to Taiwan benchmarks, with labor productivity 30-40% lower during ramp-up. Intel's Ohio and German fab projects face similar dynamics. Samsung's Taylor, Texas fab experienced comparable cost escalation. The common denominator: mature semiconductor ecosystem efficiencies in East Asia (equipment vendors, specialized labor, chemical supply) cannot be replicated at comparable cost in greenfield locations. For batteries, CATL's German plant and LG/Samsung US plants show 20-35% cost premium, partially offset by IRA subsidies. For rare earth processing, Lynas (Malaysia/Australia) and MP Materials (US) face 40-60% processing cost premiums relative to Chinese operations, reflecting decades of Chinese process optimization and environmental cost externalization.

### Claim 3 (Rare earth vulnerability)
China's sequential export control actions provide the evidence trail: gallium/germanium controls (July 2023), expanded graphite export controls (October 2023), antimony controls (August 2024), critical mineral processing technology export ban (2025). Each action tests Western response capacity and demonstrates asymmetric escalation potential. US Geological Survey data: China produces 60-70% of rare earth mine output and processes 85-90%. For battery-grade materials: China processes >65% of lithium into battery-grade compounds, >70% of cobalt, >90% of graphite into anode material. Diversification projects (Lynas, MP Materials, Energy Fuels, various Australian/Canadian miners) collectively represent <15% of Chinese processing capacity and require 5-10 years to reach scale.

### Claim 4 (Non-linear fragmentation costs)
IMF World Economic Outlook estimates: "severe fragmentation" scenario reduces global GDP by 7% in the long run, with low-income countries losing up to 12%. WTO estimates: full decoupling into two trading blocs reduces global welfare by 5%. These aggregate numbers mask the non-linearity: the Kaldor-Hicks framework shows that initial trade rerouting involves modest deadweight loss as goods flow through slightly less efficient channels (Vietnam vs. China for textiles), but systemic disruption to integrated supply chains (semiconductors, aerospace, pharma) produces cascading losses as just-in-time networks cannot be rebuilt just-in-case without massive capital expenditure. The COVID semiconductor shortage (estimated $240B in lost auto industry revenue in 2021 alone) was a preview of acute fragmentation impact on a single sector.

### Claim 5 (Self-reinforcing political economy)
Smoot-Hawley analogy is imperfect but instructive on the ratchet mechanism: once tariffs create protected domestic producers, those producers become a concentrated lobbying interest for maintained protection, while the diffuse consumers who bear the cost lack organized representation (Olson's collective action problem). Modern evidence: US steel tariffs imposed in 2018 remain in place in 2026 under a different administration, with no serious political movement to remove them. Solar panel tariffs have been renewed and expanded across multiple administrations. Agricultural subsidies to offset retaliatory tariffs created their own constituency. The "strategic geopolitical zombie" mechanism identified in the KB (CHIPS Act firms, defense industrial base) is the corporate manifestation of this political economy trap.

### Claim 6 (Tariff scenario tree)
The probability weighting reflects: (a) bipartisan protectionist convergence in US politics (both parties competing on "tough on trade" positioning), (b) retaliatory dynamics that create political costs for de-escalation (EU, China, Canada all face domestic pressure to maintain counter-tariffs), (c) the institutional stickiness described in Claim 5, (d) the absence of a credible international mechanism (WTO Appellate Body non-functional since 2019) to arbitrate and reduce tariffs. The 15% de-escalation probability reflects the possibility of bilateral "deals" that reduce headline tariff rates while maintaining the structural apparatus of export controls, investment restrictions, and strategic subsidies.

### Claim 7 (Inflation transmission channels)
The KB correctly identifies the 10-20bp steady-state structural inflation contribution and the 0.3-0.8pp tariff passthrough. The underappreciated third channel — supply chain volatility — operates through reduced buffer capacity. Pre-2020 globalization provided natural hedging: if one supplier was disrupted, alternatives existed within integrated global networks. Deglobalization reduces this optionality, meaning each supply disruption (weather, political, logistics) has a larger price impact. Evidence: post-2020 supply shock frequency has increased (COVID, Suez, Red Sea, Ukraine energy, export controls) and each shock produces larger, longer-lasting price responses than pre-2020 comparables. This is not coincidence — it reflects the reduced absorptive capacity of a fragmenting trade system.

### Claim 8 (Four-tier EM structure)
The initial binary (aligned vs. non-aligned) was an adequate first approximation but misses critical nuances. The "transshipment crackdown" risk for Tier 2 is evidenced by: US Customs and Border Protection increased investigations of Chinese goods rerouted through Vietnam, Malaysia, and Thailand; anti-circumvention duties imposed on solar panels from Southeast Asian countries; proposed "de minimis" threshold changes targeting low-value shipments. Tier 3 countries face the most complex positioning: South Korea must maintain semiconductor exports to China (~25% of Samsung/SK Hynix revenue) while complying with US export controls; Germany's auto sector depends on Chinese market (~35% of BMW/Mercedes profits from China) while aligning with EU strategic autonomy policies.

### Claim 9 (Mexico mispricing)
Pre-tariff escalation, Mexico was absorbing manufacturing FDI at record rates: $36B in 2023, with nearshoring-driven industrial construction up 45% YoY. Northern Mexico industrial vacancy rates fell below 2%. The investment thesis: produce in Mexico, sell tariff-free to the US under USMCA. The 25% tariff on Mexican goods fundamentally breaks this arbitrage. Manufacturing capex decisions with 5-10 year payback periods cannot be based on tariff regimes that change quarterly. The uncertainty premium — not just the tariff level — is the primary damage mechanism, as it freezes new investment decisions. MXN has depreciated ~15-20% from the pre-tariff escalation peak, but manufacturing equity multiples have not fully reflected the structural impairment of the nearshoring thesis.

### Claim 10 (De-dollarization calibration)
The dollar's dominance rests on four pillars: (1) deep, liquid capital markets — US Treasury market is $27T+, no alternative comes close; (2) rule of law and property rights — despite concerns, still superior to alternatives; (3) network effects in trade invoicing and settlement; (4) military/security alliances that create dollar-demand through petrodollar recycling and reserve accumulation. De-dollarization efforts chip at pillar 3 (CIPS, bilateral swap lines) and marginally at pillar 4 (Saudi/China oil-for-yuan deals), but pillars 1 and 2 remain overwhelming advantages. The euro's share has been flat at ~20% for two decades. The yuan's reserve share is ~2.3% — up from zero but still trivial. Gold purchases are the most significant diversification vector but represent a shift of ~$80-100B/year relative to a $12T reserve base.

## POLITICAL ECONOMY ASSESSMENT

**United States**: Bipartisan protectionist convergence is the single most important political economy fact for trade fragmentation analysis. The 2024-2025 tariff escalation represents the continuation and expansion of the 2018-2020 trade war, not a new phenomenon. The political economy constraint is binding: no US politician faces electoral incentive to advocate tariff reduction. Industrial policy subsidies (CHIPS Act, IRA) create concentrated beneficiaries who will defend their subsidies regardless of which party controls Congress. The fiscal sustainability question is whether $200B+ in combined industrial policy subsidies can survive the next deficit reduction cycle — I assess 70% probability they survive in some form, because national security framing is bipartisan.

**China**: Xi's "dual circulation" strategy is the mirror image of US decoupling policy — deliberate reduction of external dependency, technology self-sufficiency push, and weaponization of existing supply chain dominance. The political economy differs from the US: centralized decision-making allows faster strategic repositioning but creates information problems (no feedback mechanism for policy failure). China's overcapacity in EVs, solar, and steel is both an economic problem and a geopolitical weapon — the ability to flood global markets with subsidized goods creates leverage in trade negotiations. The demographic constraint (shrinking working-age population from 2024) adds urgency to China's timeline for establishing technological self-sufficiency, creating a "window of vulnerability" dynamic.

**European Union**: Strategic autonomy rhetoric exceeds implementation capacity. The EU faces a three-body problem: (a) maintain transatlantic alliance and market access, (b) preserve economic relationship with China (Germany's auto sector, Southern Europe's luxury goods exports), (c) build independent strategic capacity. The political economy of EU trade policy is complicated by member-state divergence: Germany prioritizes Chinese market access, Eastern Europe prioritizes US security alignment, Southern Europe prioritizes fiscal flexibility. CBAM is the EU's most significant trade fragmentation initiative — it's climate-labeled but functions as a tariff on carbon-intensive imports, primarily affecting developing countries and energy-intensive exporters.

**Populist pressure gauge**: Running hot globally. Supply chain disruption, cost-of-living pressures, and visible factory closures in import-competing sectors create fertile ground for protectionist politics in every major economy. The feedback loop is tight: globalization skepticism → protectionist candidates → tariff escalation → trade partner retaliation → further economic disruption → deeper globalization skepticism.

## SANCTIONS AND ECONOMIC STATECRAFT

**Current architecture**: US sanctions designations at ~12,000+ entities (KB data). The sanctions toolkit has expanded from targeted financial sanctions to comprehensive sectoral sanctions (Russia), semiconductor export controls (China), secondary sanctions enforcement, and outbound investment screening. The compliance cost (~$40-60B/year globally, KB data) functions as a friction tax on all cross-border transactions, not just those involving sanctioned entities.

**Export control escalation trajectory**: The October 2022/2023/2024 tightening of semiconductor export controls to China follows a ratcheting pattern — each round closes loopholes from the previous round. I assess 75% probability of further tightening in 2025-2026, potentially extending to AI model weights, quantum computing components, and biotechnology equipment. The extraterritorial application of these controls (foreign direct product rule) forces allied countries into compliance, creating the infrastructure for broader technology bifurcation.

**China's counter-escalation**: Critical mineral export controls represent China's most credible retaliatory instrument. Unlike tariffs (which hurt Chinese exporters too), mineral export controls exploit a genuine bottleneck with no short-term substitute. The escalation ladder: gallium/germanium (2023) → graphite (2023) → antimony (2024) → rare earth processing technology (2025) → potential future restrictions on lithium/cobalt processing, permanent magnets, or battery cathode/anode materials. Each step has been calibrated to demonstrate capability without triggering maximum Western response.

**Secondary sanctions risk**: The expansion of secondary sanctions enforcement — penalizing non-US entities for transactions with sanctioned parties — is the most significant under-discussed development. Major Chinese and Indian banks have curtailed Russia-related transactions under US pressure. The precedent: secondary sanctions can be applied to China-related transactions under a Taiwan contingency scenario, which would represent an order-of-magnitude escalation in economic warfare.

## HEDGING AND POSITIONING

**Underpriced geopolitical risks:**
1. **Rare earth weaponization** (most underpriced): Markets price rare earth supply as a known but manageable risk. The asymmetry between China's ability to restrict supply (weeks) and Western alternatives (years) is not reflected in defense/aerospace/EV equity multiples or commodity forward curves. Position: long Lynas, MP Materials, and rare earth ETFs as convex exposure.
2. **Transshipment crackdown risk**: Tier 2 EM countries (Vietnam, Malaysia, Thailand) are priced as unambiguous friendshoring winners, but their role as transshipment hubs for Chinese goods creates regulatory risk. Position: underweight relative to Tier 1 (India, Poland) on a relative value basis.
3. **Mexico friendshoring reversal**: As detailed in Claim 9, the tariff-friendshoring incoherence is underpriced. Position: underweight MXN and Mexican industrial REITs relative to consensus.
4. **Supply chain volatility premium**: The increased frequency of supply disruptions in a fragmenting trade system is not adequately priced into options markets. Position: systematic long gamma on commodity and freight indices.

**Overpriced geopolitical risks:**
1. **De-dollarization**: Narrative vastly exceeds near-term structural reality. Dollar assets are overhedged relative to actual de-dollarization flow impact. Position: fade extreme dollar bearishness on 5-year horizon.
2. **Complete US-China decoupling**: Full decoupling is neither feasible (mutual economic dependencies too large) nor politically likely (both sides benefit from managed trade in non-strategic goods). Markets price excessive decoupling risk in some Chinese consumer/tech names. Position: selective long in Chinese consumption plays with minimal geopolitical exposure.
3. **European energy re-crisis**: European gas infrastructure diversification (LNG terminals, demand destruction, efficiency) has substantially reduced Russia energy dependence. A re-run of 2022 energy crisis is overpriced. Position: underweight European gas storage-linked trades.

**Portfolio-level hedging framework:**
- Allocate 2-3% of portfolio to geopolitical tail risk hedges (gold, long vol structures, defense equities)
- Maintain 5-7% in critical mineral/rare earth exposure as asymmetric upside
- Structure EM allocation along the four-tier framework, overweighting Tier 1, market-weighting Tier 2 with hedges, underweighting Tier 3 and Tier 4
- Use tariff escalation scenario probabilities to calibrate US equity sector tilts (overweight domestic-revenue companies, underweight import-dependent margins)

## Open Questions

1. **How much of the reshoring capex premium will compress as learning curves develop?** The 30-50% semiconductor premium is based on early-stage TSMC Arizona data. Mature operations could narrow the gap to 15-25%, but this is unproven. Need: 2-3 years of operational data from reshored fabs.

2. **What is the actual probability-weighted timeline for a Taiwan contingency?** This is the most consequential geopolitical risk for deglobalization analysis but the hardest to calibrate. My 10-15% over 3 years is analytical judgment, not derived from base rates (no comparable historical case). Need: better frameworks for assessing state-level conflict probability under deterrence conditions.

3. **Does China's critical mineral dominance translate to actual escalation leverage, or does the threat of use have diminishing credibility?** Nuclear weapons analogy: possession of a capability does not equal willingness to use it if the costs of use are prohibitive. China's mineral exports generate revenue and create dependency — weaponizing them accelerates the diversification China wants to prevent. Need: game-theoretic analysis of China's escalation calculus under different contingencies.

4. **How should the political economy of tariff reversal be modeled?** The ratchet mechanism is theoretically sound, but what are the conditions under which tariffs actually do get reversed? Historical episodes (post-Smoot-Hawley GATT rounds, post-WWII trade liberalization) required catastrophic economic failure or systemic shock to create political space for liberalization. Is a severe recession sufficient, or does it require a geopolitical realignment? Need: political science analysis of conditions for trade liberalization.

5. **What is the net inflation impact when deglobalization cost increases are offset by AI-driven productivity gains and China overcapacity-driven deflation pressure?** The KB correctly identifies this as an open question (`geopolitical_supply_fragmentation_premium`). The three forces (deglobalization inflation, AI deflation, China overcapacity deflation) may partially offset, but they operate on different sectors and timescales. Need: sectoral decomposition of which forces dominate where.

6. **How durable is the EM geopolitical bifurcation?** The 2022-2026 sample is too short for structural conclusions. A US-China de-escalation could partially reverse the divergence, while escalation would widen it. The key uncertainty is whether geopolitical alignment is becoming a structural factor in EM equity pricing or whether it's a cyclical theme that will fade. Need: longer sample; comparison to Cold War-era EM (front-line states vs. non-aligned movement) performance data.

7. **What is the fiscal cost of deglobalization to the US government?** CHIPS Act ($52B), IRA energy provisions (~$369B estimated, likely higher in practice), defense spending increases, strategic petroleum reserve rebuilding, critical mineral stockpiling, tariff revenue losses from trade volume reduction. The net fiscal impact is ambiguous (tariff revenue partially offsets subsidy costs) but likely net-negative for the deficit over a 10-year horizon. Need: CBO-style scoring of comprehensive deglobalization fiscal impact.

8. **How do private markets (PE/VC) adjust to deglobalization?** The public market analysis is relatively straightforward (equity re-rating, credit spreads), but private markets — which have been significant vehicles for cross-border investment, particularly US-China tech flows — face acute regulatory pressure (outbound investment screening). The repricing mechanism is slower and less transparent. Need: analysis of PE/VC fund mandates and compliance adaptations.
