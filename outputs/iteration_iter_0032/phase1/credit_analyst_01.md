# Deglobalization, Supply Chain Rewiring, & Trade Fragmentation — Credit Cycle Analyst Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Deglobalization creates a bimodal credit quality impact**: Nearshoring/friendshoring capex generates credit-positive new issuance for domestic industrial borrowers while simultaneously compressing margins and raising input costs for globally integrated supply chain-dependent issuers, producing a widening intra-cohort dispersion within BBB and BB rating categories that is not captured by index-level spreads.

2. **Tariff escalation is a non-linear credit event with a threshold effect**: Below ~15% effective tariff rates, margin compression is absorbable for most IG issuers; above ~20-25%, the combination of input cost inflation, demand destruction, and retaliatory trade barriers triggers a cascade where 8-12% of BBB industrials and consumer discretionary issuers breach 2.0x interest coverage, entering fallen angel territory. The current effective tariff rate regime (~10-15% weighted average on Chinese goods, rising) places credit markets in the non-linear acceleration zone.

3. **The maturity wall interacts with deglobalization through a cost-of-capital amplifier**: The $900B-$1.5T sub-IG maturity wall (2025-2028) collides with deglobalization-driven margin compression to create a dual squeeze — borrowers face both 40-60% higher refinancing costs AND 150-400bp lower operating margins from supply chain restructuring costs. This interaction is under-priced because markets are analyzing each risk independently rather than jointly.

4. **Deglobalization produces a new class of "strategic credit" — issuers whose credit quality is partially decoupled from market fundamentals by geopolitical subsidy**: CHIPS Act, IRA, and defense industrial base beneficiaries (~$150-250B in total committed subsidies) create credit profiles where default probability is a function of political durability rather than cash flow adequacy. These "strategic geopolitical zombies" suppress observed default rates while building contingent fiscal liabilities.

5. **Rare earth and critical mineral supply chain concentration represents an unpriced binary credit risk for ~$400-600B in IG industrials and tech hardware**: China controls 60-70% of rare earth processing and 80%+ of specific elements (gallium, germanium). An export restriction would function as a supply-side credit event analogous to the Russia 2022 sanctions shock — transforming IG-quality issuers into credit-impaired names within weeks, not quarters.

6. **Trade fragmentation widens the HY default rate distribution without necessarily raising the mean**: Instead of a uniform default rate increase, deglobalization creates fatter tails — supply chain-resilient issuers see improved competitive positioning while exposed issuers face existential cost restructuring. The 12-month HY default rate forecast should shift from a point estimate (~3.5-4.5%) to a bimodal distribution: ~2.5% in the benign scenario (orderly reshoring, moderate tariffs) vs. ~7-9% in the adverse scenario (tariff escalation, rare earth disruption, retaliatory cascades).

7. **CLO portfolios have concentrated, under-monitored exposure to deglobalization-sensitive sectors**: Approximately 15-25% of broadly syndicated CLO collateral is in sectors with direct deglobalization exposure (auto parts, chemicals, consumer products, retail). Covenant-lite structures (87% of institutional loans) eliminate early warning signals — by the time payment default occurs, recovery values will already reflect the structural margin impairment from supply chain restructuring.

8. **The credit cycle is being structurally lengthened by deglobalization-driven capex, but the eventual downturn severity is amplified**: Nearshoring and supply chain redundancy investment (~$500B+ committed across US, EU, Japan over 2023-2030) provides a capex-driven growth impulse that extends mid-cycle conditions. However, this capex is partially debt-financed, adds to corporate leverage, and creates capacity that may prove economically inefficient once geopolitical premiums normalize — classic late-cycle misallocation characteristics.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Bimodal Credit Quality Impact

The deglobalization trend creates clear winners and losers within the same rating cohort. **Credit-positive channel**: Domestic capex expansion — companies building US/Mexico/allied-nation manufacturing capacity are issuing new debt backed by tangible assets with government support (CHIPS Act subsidies cover 25-30% of fab construction costs, IRA provides production tax credits). These issuers benefit from: (a) favorable financing terms due to government backstop perception, (b) revenue diversification away from geopolitically exposed supply chains, (c) potential tariff-driven competitive advantages.

**Credit-negative channel**: Globally integrated manufacturers face three simultaneous margin pressures: (1) input cost inflation from tariff passthrough — the Amiti-Redding-Weinstein 2019 framework estimates 0.3-0.8pp direct CPI impact, but firm-level cost absorption is 2-3x the consumer pass-through for import-intensive manufacturers; (2) supply chain redundancy costs — maintaining dual sourcing (China + friend-shore) adds 10-20% to procurement costs during transition; (3) inventory buffer costs — firms shift from just-in-time to just-in-case, tying up working capital.

The KB's `geopolitical_supply_fragmentation_premium` documents semiconductor reshoring costs at 30-50% above Asian production — this maps directly to margin compression for downstream purchasers. The dispersion effect is observable in BBB industrials where the spread range within the cohort has widened from ~40bp (2019) to ~80-120bp currently, with supply chain exposure as the primary differentiator.

### Claim 2: Tariff Threshold Non-Linearity

Historical episodes provide calibration. The 2018-2019 US-China tariff escalation (10-25% on ~$370B goods) produced identifiable but contained credit stress: HY spreads widened ~100bp, but defaults barely moved because tariff rates remained below the threshold for widespread coverage ratio breach. The 2025-2026 escalation differs in three ways: (a) broader product coverage (potentially encompassing allied-nation goods through universal tariff proposals), (b) retaliatory tariff cascades from trading partners, (c) collision with the maturity wall.

For a median BBB industrial issuer with 3.5x interest coverage and 15% import content in COGS: a 15% effective tariff reduces coverage to ~3.0x (manageable); a 25% tariff reduces coverage to ~2.3x (watch-list territory); a 35% tariff pushes coverage below 2.0x (fallen angel risk). For B-rated issuers starting at 1.8-2.0x coverage, the threshold is lower — even 15-20% tariffs can trigger covenant-adjacent stress.

The non-linearity arises because tariff costs are largely fixed (percentage of import value) while revenues are variable (demand elasticity). Firms that can pass through costs maintain margins; firms facing elastic demand or competing with domestic producers absorb the tariff as pure margin compression.

### Claim 3: Maturity Wall x Deglobalization Interaction

The KB documents $900B-$1.5T in sub-IG maturities (2025-2028) with B-rated borrowers facing 40-60% refinancing cost increases. Deglobalization adds a second dimension: borrowers approaching the maturity wall must refinance while simultaneously restructuring supply chains. This creates a joint distribution problem.

Consider a B-rated auto parts manufacturer: pre-deglobalization, refinancing at SOFR+400-500bp was stressful but manageable given stable 12-15% EBITDA margins. With tariff-driven input cost increases of 200-400bp in margin compression, the same borrower now needs to refinance at higher rates with lower cash flow coverage. The conditional probability of default given both maturity wall AND deglobalization stress is substantially higher than the product of independent probabilities — the correlation between refinancing risk and operating margin risk is ~0.5-0.7 for import-exposed sectors, far above what single-factor credit models assume.

Sectors most exposed to this interaction: auto parts/components (high import content + heavy leverage), chemicals (energy cost + tariff double hit), consumer products (Chinese sourcing dependency + maturity concentration), and mid-market industrials (limited financial flexibility + supply chain adjustment costs).

### Claim 4: Strategic Credit and Geopolitical Zombies

The KB's `strategic_geopolitical_zombies` concept (confidence: 5) identifies a genuinely novel phenomenon. I extend this to credit market pricing implications. CHIPS Act ($52B+ direct subsidies, $75B+ in tax credits), IRA energy provisions (~$369B over 10 years), and defense industrial base spending create firms whose credit quality has a "sovereign put" embedded — not explicit government guarantee, but political improbability of allowing failure.

Credit market evidence: Several semiconductor fab projects (Intel Ohio, TSMC Arizona, Samsung Texas) are being financed at spreads 50-100bp tighter than standalone credit metrics would justify, implying market recognition of the subsidy backstop. Defense contractors with below-IG standalone metrics trade at IG-equivalent spreads.

The credit cycle distortion: these issuers suppress measured default rates and tighten aggregate spreads, masking deterioration in the non-subsidized portion of the credit universe. If fiscal consolidation pressures force subsidy haircuts (a 2027+ risk given debt ceiling dynamics), the "strategic" credit quality evaporates, potentially releasing clustered downgrades.

### Claim 5: Rare Earth Binary Risk

China's dominance in rare earth processing (60-70% of production, 80%+ of specific processing for gallium, germanium, heavy rare earths) creates a supply concentration risk with direct credit implications. In 2023, China restricted gallium and germanium exports; in 2024, it expanded restrictions to antimony and graphite. Each escalation provides a template for broader restrictions.

Mapping to credit exposure: semiconductor equipment manufacturers ($80-120B debt outstanding), defense electronics ($40-60B), EV battery supply chain ($60-100B), and general industrial users of permanent magnets, catalysts, and specialty alloys ($200-300B). Total exposed IG debt: $400-600B.

The binary nature: unlike tariffs (which compress margins gradually), a rare earth export ban creates immediate production halt risk. The Russia 2022 sanctions analogue from the KB's `sanctions_shadow_credit_rating` is apt — IG issuers can become operationally impaired (and thus credit-impaired) before rating agencies complete their review cycles. CDS markets would reprice within days; bond markets within weeks.

Current pricing: I estimate near-zero rare earth supply disruption premium in IG industrial spreads. The basis for this: IG industrial OAS at ~90-110bp is roughly consistent with fundamental credit metrics without any geopolitical tail risk premium. Even a 5% probability-weighted adjustment for rare earth disruption should add 15-30bp to exposed issuers.

### Claim 6: Bimodal HY Default Distribution

The KB's `hy_default_rate_mispricing` documents market-implied ~2.5% default rate vs. historical base rates of 6-10% during higher-for-longer. Deglobalization doesn't uniformly raise defaults — it bifurcates the distribution.

**Left mode (benign, ~2.5-3.5% default rate)**: Orderly nearshoring, tariffs stabilize at ~15%, government subsidies offset transition costs, domestic demand remains robust via Kalecki fiscal channel. Supply chain adjustment is a multi-year process that allows gradual adaptation.

**Right mode (adverse, ~7-9% default rate)**: Tariff escalation to 25%+ across broad categories, rare earth restrictions, retaliatory cascades (per the KB's `export_ban_contagion` prisoner's dilemma dynamics with 30-60% shock amplification), maturity wall collision. Under this scenario, 15-20% of B-rated issuers in exposed sectors face simultaneous margin compression and refinancing stress.

The market is pricing the left mode almost exclusively. HY OAS at ~350-400bp embeds a ~3% expected default rate with ~40% recovery — consistent with benign reshoring. The option value of the right tail is under-priced by an estimated 50-100bp in index-level HY spreads.

### Claim 7: CLO Exposure

CLO portfolios are constructed on sector diversification principles that pre-date the deglobalization structural shift. Typical CLO sector limits (~10-15% per industry) were calibrated during the globalization era when sector-level supply chain disruption was uncorrelated. Deglobalization creates a common factor exposure across ostensibly different sectors: auto parts, chemicals, consumer products, and retail all share China/Asia supply chain dependency.

With covenant-lite at 87% of institutional loans, CLO managers lose the early warning system. Traditional covenants (maintenance leverage tests at 5.0-6.0x) would trigger renegotiation when deglobalization-driven EBITDA declines push leverage up. Without these tripwires, CLO portfolios can deteriorate through the OC test cushion (typically 3-5 points) before any credit event is recognized. The recovery rate implication: by the time cov-lite borrowers default, the business has been impaired for 12-18 months longer than a covenanted borrower would have been, reducing recovery values by an estimated 10-20 percentage points (from historical ~65% to ~45-55% for deglobalization-affected sectors).

### Claim 8: Cycle Extension with Severity Amplification

Nearshoring capex provides genuine economic impulse. The $500B+ in announced reshoring/nearshoring investment (combining private capex, government subsidies, and tax credit-incentivized spending) supports aggregate demand, employment, and corporate revenue growth. This is structurally credit-positive in the near term and extends the credit cycle by supporting earnings growth that services existing debt.

However, the composition of this capex matters for credit quality. A significant portion is debt-financed (corporate bonds, project finance, leveraged loans for mid-market manufacturers building new capacity). This adds to aggregate corporate leverage during a period when leverage is already elevated (the KB documents true leverage at 5.5-7.5x after EBITDA addback adjustments vs. reported 4.0-4.5x).

The late-cycle misallocation risk: capacity built to serve geopolitical objectives rather than pure economic efficiency will have lower return on invested capital. If geopolitical tensions de-escalate (a tail scenario but not negligible), or if subsidies are curtailed, this capacity becomes stranded — and the debt remains. Historical precedent: defense industry capex build-ups during Cold War periods created capacity that required painful adjustment when geopolitical conditions shifted.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Inflation Regimes (confidence 6.1, depth 4)
The KB's `structural_inflation_shift` attributes 10-20bp of the structural PCE floor to deglobalization/tariffs. My credit analysis extends this: the inflation channel feeds back into credit through (a) higher-for-longer rates raising refinancing costs at the maturity wall, (b) input cost inflation compressing margins for credit-intensive sectors, and (c) central bank reluctance to ease into a tariff-driven inflation impulse limiting the "Fed put" for credit markets. The `goods_disinflation_completion` concept (confidence 8) with tariff impact of 0.3-0.8pp CPI is the macro input to my issuer-level coverage ratio analysis.

### Commodity Supercycles (confidence 5.6, depth 3)
The `credit_tightening_commodity_supply_paradox` (confidence 7) intersects deglobalization through the capex channel: deglobalization-driven credit tightening for commodity producers in non-allied nations restricts supply, supporting prices and feeding back into cost inflation for credit-intensive downstream users. The `supercycle_narrative_failure` (0/4 track record) provides an important check on over-extrapolation, but I note current structural conditions (deglobalization + energy transition) may genuinely differ — the credit market will price whichever narrative dominates.

### Yield Curve Dynamics (iter_0031)
Term premium expansion documented in iter_0031's analysis interacts with deglobalization through the fiscal channel — nearshoring subsidies add to fiscal deficits, supporting higher long-end rates, which in turn raise corporate borrowing costs. The "bad steepening" signal (fiscal-supply-driven, misread as growth-driven) amplifies credit misallocation if firms borrow to build capacity based on a growth signal that is actually a fiscal signal.

### Credit-Equity Linkage
The KB's `fallen_angel_forced_selling` ($350-500B BBB at risk) connects directly to deglobalization: BBB industrials with high import content are the most likely fallen angel candidates in a tariff escalation scenario. The Ford/GM 2005 template ($200B+ moving from IG to HY, creating 100-150bp HY spread widening) provides the transmission mechanism estimate.

### Geopolitical Risk
The `sanctions_shadow_credit_rating` and `export_ban_contagion` concepts from the KB are the tail-risk frameworks for deglobalization credit events. The sanctions analogue (Russia 2022: IG to untradeable overnight) maps to rare earth supply disruption; the export ban contagion (30-60% shock amplification from prisoner's dilemma dynamics) maps to retaliatory tariff cascades.

### Fiscal Dominance
The Kalecki fiscal buffer documented across multiple KB concepts is the primary reason credit markets may continue to price the benign scenario. Fiscal deficits at 6-7% of GDP support corporate revenue and earnings even as deglobalization raises costs — the question is whether this fiscal support is structural or cyclical, and whether fiscal consolidation pressures (debt ceiling, political shift) could remove the buffer precisely when deglobalization stress intensifies.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the joint default probability for maturity wall x deglobalization stress?** Current models treat these as independent risks. I estimate the correlation is 0.5-0.7 for exposed sectors but need sector-level refinancing schedules cross-referenced with import content data to calibrate properly.

2. **How quickly can CLO managers reposition portfolios away from deglobalization-exposed sectors?** If rebalancing takes 12-18 months (consistent with typical loan liquidity constraints), the window for unrecognized portfolio deterioration is significant. If managers are already ahead of this, my CLO exposure claim overstates the risk.

3. **What is the political durability of nearshoring subsidies?** The credit profiles of strategic geopolitical zombies depend entirely on this. A bipartisan commitment extends the credit cycle; a fiscal consolidation that cuts subsidies triggers clustered defaults. I cannot model this from credit fundamentals alone.

4. **Are credit markets pricing ANY rare earth supply disruption risk?** I estimate near-zero premium but this requires systematic analysis of CDS curves for exposed issuers vs. matched non-exposed issuers. If the market has begun pricing this risk, the opportunity may already be smaller.

5. **How does the Kalecki fiscal buffer interact with deglobalization cost inflation?** The fiscal buffer supports demand and revenue — but deglobalization raises costs. If fiscal policy simultaneously sustains demand AND inflates costs, the net effect on credit quality is ambiguous and sector-dependent. The KB has not resolved the magnitude question (open question across multiple concepts).

6. **What is the appropriate recovery rate assumption for deglobalization-impaired defaults?** Traditional recovery rates (~40% HY, ~65% leveraged loans) assume going-concern value from operational businesses. If a borrower defaults because its supply chain was structurally disrupted (not a cyclical demand shortfall), going-concern value may be materially lower. No empirical base rate exists for this scenario.

7. **How should credit investors price the optionality embedded in sanctions/trade policy risk?** The binary nature of rare earth restrictions and sanctions-driven credit events suggests credit investors should think in terms of options (put protection on exposed names) rather than spread levels. The CDS market may be a better pricing mechanism than cash bonds for this type of risk, but CDS liquidity is limited for many industrials.

8. **Is the friendshoring capex multiplier large enough to offset deglobalization-driven credit deterioration at the macro level?** If the capex impulse generates sufficient GDP growth and tax revenue, the system-level credit quality impact could be positive even as individual issuers suffer. This is the macro-level question that this credit analysis cannot answer independently — it requires integration with the macro strategist's output.
