# Deglobalization, Supply Chain Rewiring, & Trade Fragmentation — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The current deglobalization episode most closely resembles a composite of the 1930s Smoot-Hawley cascade (similarity 5.5/10), the 1970s OPEC-era supply fragmentation (6.5/10), the post-2001 China-WTO integration reversal-image (7.0/10), the 1980s US-Japan trade war (6.0/10), and the post-1945 Bretton Woods trade architecture build-out (5.0/10, as a reverse analogue).** No single analogue captures more than 70% of the current dynamics; the current episode is a genuinely novel combination of technology-enabled selective decoupling with unprecedented financial interconnection.

2. **The base rate for "clean deglobalization" — where trade volumes contract significantly without triggering a recession in advanced economies — is 0/4 in the historical sample.** Every prior episode of meaningful trade contraction (1930-33, 1974-75, 1980-82, 2008-09) was accompanied by or caused significant GDP contractions. The current attempt to achieve "strategic decoupling" without recession is historically unprecedented.

3. **Friendshoring produces a measurable but bounded cost premium: the historical analogue of 1980s US-Japan Voluntary Export Restraints (VERs) imposed 15-25% cost premiums on consumers that persisted for 8-12 years, suggesting a nearshoring capex multiplier of 1.3-1.5x relative to offshored baseline costs, declining to 1.1-1.2x over a decade as new capacity matures.** The CHIPS Act and IRA represent the fiscal infrastructure to underwrite this premium, paralleling Japan's MITI-era industrial subsidies but at larger absolute scale.

4. **Rare earth and critical mineral supply chain concentration today (China 60-90% of processing across key minerals) exceeds the OPEC oil concentration of the early 1970s (OPEC ~55% of global crude output in 1973), making the geopolitical vulnerability comparable to or greater than the pre-1973 oil dependency.** The key analogue difference: unlike oil in 1973, there is no "spare capacity" outside the dominant supplier, extending the vulnerability window from months (oil) to 5-10 years (mineral processing infrastructure).

5. **Tariff escalation follows a historically documented ratchet pattern: in 5/5 major protectionist episodes (1890 McKinley, 1922 Fordney-McCumber, 1930 Smoot-Hawley, 1971 Nixon Shock surcharge, 2018-present), initial tariffs triggered retaliatory spirals that exceeded the original action by 1.5-3x within 24 months.** The base rate for tariff de-escalation without a major economic shock or regime change is approximately 1/5 (the 1934 RTAA partially reversed Smoot-Hawley, but only after GDP fell 30%).

6. **Trade fragmentation produces a bimodal inflation outcome, not a normal distribution: either the fragmentation premium is absorbed as a one-time price-level shift (base case ~60% probability, analogous to 1971-72 post-Nixon Shock) or it triggers a persistent inflationary regime shift (40% probability, analogous to 1973-74 when the oil embargo interacted with pre-existing inflationary conditions).** The current environment — with already-elevated services inflation and large fiscal deficits — tilts the conditional probability toward the persistent outcome relative to a clean starting point.

7. **The sequencing of deglobalization's market impact follows a documented pattern across analogues: (1) FX adjustment first (6-12 months), (2) goods prices and corporate margins second (12-24 months), (3) capex cycle in reshoring beneficiaries third (18-36 months), (4) structural productivity impact last (36-60+ months).** The current episode is in the transition from Phase 1 to Phase 2 as of early 2026, with USD strength having partially pre-adjusted and corporate margin compression beginning to appear in tariff-exposed sectors.

8. **The "weaponization of interdependence" — using financial and trade networks as coercive tools — has a historical parallel in the British Empire's use of the sterling-bloc clearing system and naval blockade economics (1914-1945), which ultimately accelerated the very fragmentation it sought to prevent.** The base rate for financial weaponization preserving the incumbent system's centrality is 0/2 (British sterling bloc, Soviet COMECON both ultimately fragmented); the base rate for the transition taking longer than proponents expect is 2/2.

9. **Historical analogues suggest that deglobalization is structurally positive for real assets (commodities, real estate in reshoring corridors, infrastructure) and negative for financial assets dependent on global arbitrage (EM hard currency debt, multinational margins, global banks).** Across the 1930s, 1970s, and 1980s trade war episodes, real assets outperformed financial assets by 200-500bp annualized during the active fragmentation phase.

10. **The "China overcapacity offset" — the argument that Chinese industrial overcapacity will suppress global goods prices even as trade fragments — has a partial historical analogue in Japan's 1990s-2000s deflation export, which suppressed global goods prices for approximately 15 years despite contemporaneous trade tensions.** However, the Japanese analogue required an open trading system to transmit the deflationary impulse; tariff walls would block this channel, creating the paradox of Chinese overcapacity and Western goods inflation simultaneously.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Analogue Identification and Composite Framework

**ANALOGUE IDENTIFICATION**

**Analogue A: Post-2001 China-WTO Integration (Reverse Image) — Similarity 7.0/10**
- **Why selected**: The current deglobalization is most directly the unwinding of the 1995-2015 hyper-globalization wave. Understanding what globalization created tells us what deglobalization destroys.
- **Initial conditions match**: Massive trade imbalances, domestic political backlash against job losses, bipartisan consensus for change (TPP withdrawal under Obama/Trump, continued under Biden).
- **Key difference**: The integration was gradual (20 years); the unwinding is being attempted in 5-10 years, creating adjustment asymmetries.

**Analogue B: 1970s OPEC Supply Fragmentation — Similarity 6.5/10**
- **Why selected**: The most relevant precedent for a critical input (then oil, now semiconductors and rare earths) being weaponized by concentrated suppliers.
- **Initial conditions match**: Concentrated supply, geopolitical motivation for weaponization, advanced economies dependent on imports, pre-existing inflationary backdrop.
- **Key differences**: Oil is fungible and consumed; semiconductors are differentiated and embedded. The 1970s had no equivalent to the US tech sector's dependence on TSMC.

**Analogue C: 1980s US-Japan Trade War — Similarity 6.0/10**
- **Why selected**: The most direct precedent for bilateral managed trade between the world's two largest economies, including VERs, semiconductor agreements, and Plaza Accord currency management.
- **Initial conditions match**: Trade deficit driving political pressure, technology competition framing, bilateral negotiations with both carrots and sticks.
- **Key differences**: Japan was a US security ally; China is a strategic competitor. Japan accepted constraints voluntarily; China is unlikely to.

**Analogue D: 1930s Smoot-Hawley Cascade — Similarity 5.5/10**
- **Why selected**: The canonical example of tariff escalation spirals and their macroeconomic consequences.
- **Initial conditions match**: Rising populist pressure, agricultural/industrial sector lobbying, retaliatory dynamics.
- **Key differences**: 1930 tariffs were broad-based and across all partners; current tariffs are targeted (primarily China). Gold standard constrained policy response; modern float provides a buffer. Financial system linkages were weaker in 1930.

**Analogue E: Post-1945 Bretton Woods Build-Out (Reverse Analogue) — Similarity 5.0/10**
- **Why selected**: As a reverse analogue, the construction of the liberal trading order shows what institutional infrastructure was required and how long it took (GATT 1947 → WTO 1995, nearly 50 years). The reverse process may take comparable time.
- **Initial conditions match**: Hegemonic power (then building institutions, now selectively dismantling them), technology enabling new trade patterns.
- **Key differences**: Building institutions required cooperation; dismantling them can be unilateral. The current global economy is far more interconnected than 1945.

### Claim 2: Base Rate for Clean Deglobalization

The historical sample of significant trade contractions:

| Episode | Trade Volume Change | GDP Impact (Advanced Economies) | Duration |
|---------|--------------------|---------------------------------|----------|
| 1930-33 | Global trade -65% | US GDP -30%, global recession | 3 years |
| 1974-75 | OECD imports -6.5% | OECD recession, -1.5% GDP | 18 months |
| 1980-82 | Global trade -2% | Double-dip recession, US -2.7% | 24 months |
| 2008-09 | Global trade -12% | Great Recession, -3.4% GDP | 18 months |

In every case, the trade contraction was accompanied by recession. The question is whether the current "managed decoupling" can avoid this base rate. Two factors argue for cautious optimism: (1) the decoupling is sector-specific, not broad-based, and (2) fiscal support (CHIPS, IRA) partially offsets the demand destruction. But the base rate strongly warns against assuming that trade fragmentation is economically costless.

The closest precedent for "partial" deglobalization is the 1980s US-Japan managed trade, which imposed measurable consumer costs (15-25% auto price premium from VERs, per Hufbauer & Elliott 1994) but avoided recession because: (a) it was bilateral not global, (b) Japan redirected production to US transplants, and (c) the broader trading system remained open. Conditions (a) and (c) are weaker today.

### Claim 3: Friendshoring Cost Premium and Nearshoring Capex Multiplier

**Historical calibration from VER episode:**
- The 1981 Japan VER on autos raised US car prices by approximately $1,000-$1,500 per unit (15-20% premium on affected models) per Dinopoulos & Kreinin (1988).
- The premium persisted until Japanese transplant factories reached full production (1986-1990), approximately 5-8 years.
- Total consumer cost: estimated $5.8B/year (1984 dollars) per FTC study, equivalent to ~$16B/year in 2025 dollars.

**Extrapolation to current reshoring:**
- Semiconductor fabrication: TSMC Arizona fab costs estimated $40B vs. $15-20B for equivalent Taiwan fab (2.0-2.7x multiplier), though part of this reflects greenfield vs. brownfield.
- Intel Ohio: $20B for capacity that could be built for $8-12B in Asia (1.7-2.5x multiplier).
- After adjusting for subsidies (CHIPS Act covering 25-35% of incremental cost), the net cost premium is approximately 1.3-1.5x.
- Learning curve and supply chain maturation historically reduce cost premiums by 40-60% over a decade (consistent across semiconductor, auto, and steel reshoring episodes).
- **Nearshoring capex multiplier estimate: 1.3-1.5x initially, declining to 1.1-1.2x by Year 10.**

**Cross-analogue validation from steel industry:**
- The US steel industry, protected by tariffs since the 1960s and subject to multiple rounds of VERs, achieved cost parity with imports only after ~25 years and massive consolidation (US Steel from 60% market share to 12%).
- This suggests that "nearshoring capex multiplier convergence" takes longer than optimists assume.

### Claim 4: Rare Earth and Critical Mineral Concentration Risk

**Comparison to 1973 OPEC concentration:**

| Metric | OPEC 1973 (Oil) | China 2025 (Critical Minerals) |
|--------|------------------|-------------------------------|
| Share of global production | ~55% crude | 60-70% mining, 80-90% processing |
| Spare capacity outside dominant | ~5-10% (non-OPEC) | Near-zero for processing |
| Substitutability | Medium (coal, nuclear) | Low-medium (depends on mineral) |
| Time to build alternative | 3-5 years (North Sea, Alaska) | 5-15 years (mining + processing) |
| Strategic reserves | SPR built post-crisis | Minimal for most minerals |
| Weaponization precedent | 1973 embargo | 2010 Japan rare earth cutoff, 2023 Ga/Ge export controls |

**Key insight from the OPEC analogue:** The 1973 embargo triggered a massive supply response (North Sea, Alaska, efficiency gains) that broke OPEC's pricing power within 12-15 years. However, the supply response required large capital investment and favorable geology. For critical minerals, the geological constraints are less binding (rare earths aren't actually rare), but the processing infrastructure bottleneck is severe — building a rare earth processing facility takes 7-10 years and faces environmental permitting challenges in Western economies.

**The vulnerability window (time between weaponization risk materializing and alternative supply coming online) is approximately:**
- Rare earths: 7-12 years (MP Materials, Lynas ramping, but processing still China-dependent)
- Lithium: 3-5 years (Australian, Chilean, Argentine capacity expanding)
- Cobalt: 5-8 years (diversification away from DRC/Chinese processing)
- Gallium/Germanium: 5-10 years (China controls 80%+ of processing)
- Advanced semiconductors: 3-5 years (TSMC Arizona, Intel Ohio, Samsung Texas ramping)

### Claim 5: Tariff Escalation Ratchet

**Historical pattern documentation:**

| Episode | Initial Action | Retaliation | Escalation Multiple | Resolution |
|---------|---------------|-------------|---------------------|------------|
| 1890 McKinley Tariff | US avg tariff 48% | European counter-tariffs | 1.5x | 1894 Wilson tariff reduction |
| 1922 Fordney-McCumber | US avg tariff 38% | 27 countries retaliated | 2.0x | Smoot-Hawley (further escalation) |
| 1930 Smoot-Hawley | US avg tariff 59% | 60+ countries retaliated | 3.0x | 1934 RTAA, but only after Depression |
| 1971 Nixon surcharge | 10% import surcharge | European threats, Smithsonian | 1.5x | Reversed within 4 months (Smithsonian Agreement) |
| 2018-present | 25% on Chinese steel/aluminum | China retaliatory tariffs | ~2.0x and still escalating | Unresolved |

**The ratchet mechanism operates through:**
1. **Domestic constituency lock-in**: Protected industries lobby against tariff removal (Olson's Logic of Collective Action).
2. **Retaliatory spiral**: Each retaliation creates new domestic constituencies demanding counter-retaliation.
3. **Sunk cost**: Firms that have begun reshoring lobby against tariff reversal that would make their investments uncompetitive.
4. **Geopolitical framing**: Once tariffs are justified on national security grounds (Section 232, Section 301), removal is politically coded as "weakening national security."

**The only reliable de-escalation mechanism in the historical sample is a macroeconomic crisis severe enough to overcome the political lock-in** (Depression for Smoot-Hawley, oil shock for the Nixon surcharge, which was part of a broader economic emergency). This creates a dangerous dynamic: the tariff escalation continues until it contributes to the very crisis that forces de-escalation.

### Claim 6: Bimodal Inflation Outcome

**Analogue decomposition for the two modes:**

**Mode A: One-time price-level shift (60% base case probability)**
- **Analogue: 1971-72 post-Nixon Shock.** The 10% import surcharge and dollar devaluation raised import prices by 15-20% cumulatively, adding approximately 1.5-2.0pp to CPI over 18 months. But this was a one-time adjustment; absent the subsequent oil embargo, inflation would have moderated.
- **Current mapping**: If tariff escalation stabilizes at current levels (effective tariff rate ~20-25% on Chinese goods, ~5-10% on others), the pass-through would add 0.5-1.5pp to core CPI over 12-24 months, then fade. This requires: (a) tariffs don't escalate further, (b) no secondary commodity shock, (c) inflation expectations remain anchored.

**Mode B: Persistent inflationary regime shift (40% probability)**
- **Analogue: 1973-75.** The oil embargo interacted with pre-existing inflationary conditions (monetary accommodation, fiscal expansion, wage-price dynamics) to produce a persistent inflation regime. The supply shock alone would have been transitory; the interaction with the macro backdrop made it structural.
- **Current mapping**: If tariff escalation coincides with (a) ongoing services inflation persistence, (b) large fiscal deficits, and (c) Fed easing prematurely (the first_wave_1966_69_analogue from the KB), the fragmentation premium could become embedded in expectations and wage dynamics. The combination of structural_inflation_shift (+30-80bp floor) with trade fragmentation premium creates a plausible path to 3.5-4.5% core PCE as a semi-permanent state.

**What tilts between the two modes:**
- Fed reaction function: Restrictive → Mode A (base rate 3/3 from KB's 1966-69 analogue analysis)
- Fiscal policy: Expansion during fragmentation → Mode B
- Labor market: Tight labor + import price increases → wage-price feedback → Mode B
- Expectations: 5y5y breakevens above 2.8% → Mode B

### Claim 7: Sequencing Template

**Cross-analogue sequencing evidence:**

**Phase 1 — FX Adjustment (Month 0-12)**
- 1971 Nixon Shock: Dollar devalued 7.9% vs. gold immediately, 20%+ trade-weighted over 18 months
- 1985 Plaza Accord: Dollar fell 50% vs. yen over 2 years
- 2018-19 tariffs: CNY depreciated 10-12% to partially offset tariff impact
- **Current**: USD/CNY has adjusted ~8% since 2022; broader DXY strong on rate differentials but weakening as growth concerns mount

**Phase 2 — Goods Prices & Corporate Margins (Month 12-24)**
- 1971-73: Import prices rose 15-20% cumulative with 12-18 month lag
- 1981-85 VERs: Auto prices rose 15-25% within 12-18 months
- 2018-19 tariffs: Amiti, Redding & Weinstein (2019) found ~100% pass-through to US importers within 12 months
- **Current**: Early evidence of margin compression in consumer discretionary and industrial sectors with China exposure. Goods disinflation has stalled (consistent with goods_disinflation_completion from KB).

**Phase 3 — Capex Cycle in Reshoring Beneficiaries (Month 18-36)**
- Post-OPEC: North Sea, Alaska pipeline investment peaked 3-5 years after embargo
- Post-VER: Japanese transplant construction 1982-1988 (Honda Marysville, Toyota Georgetown, etc.)
- Post-CHIPS Act: Intel, TSMC, Samsung fabs breaking ground 2023-2026, production 2027-2029
- **Current**: Construction spending in manufacturing structures up ~80% since 2022 (Census Bureau data), concentrated in semiconductor and battery facilities

**Phase 4 — Structural Productivity Impact (Month 36-60+)**
- Post-Smoot-Hawley: US industrial productivity stagnated 1930-33, recovered only with WWII mobilization
- Post-VER: US auto industry productivity improved with Japanese transplant competition, but took 15+ years
- Post-OPEC: Energy efficiency revolution (BTU/GDP fell 40% 1973-2000), but gains were slow and required sustained high prices
- **Current**: Too early to assess. AI productivity gains could partially offset fragmentation costs, but the historical base rate for technology offsetting trade costs within 5 years is 0/3 (none of the above examples saw rapid technology offset).

### Claim 8: Weaponization of Interdependence and System Centrality

**British sterling-bloc analogue (1931-1945):**
- Britain used the sterling clearing system and imperial preference system to maintain economic influence after losing industrial competitiveness.
- The system initially worked: the sterling bloc channeled trade flows through London and maintained demand for sterling assets.
- But the weaponization (blocking German/Japanese access, freezing assets) accelerated the development of alternative systems (German bilateral clearing, Japanese Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere).
- Post-WWII: Sterling's reserve currency share fell from ~60% to ~30% in 20 years — faster than proponents expected, slower than bears predicted.

**Soviet COMECON analogue (1949-1991):**
- A deliberate alternative trading system built to insulate allied economies from Western financial pressure.
- Worked for ~30 years as a closed system, then collapsed rapidly when member states defected (Poland, Hungary in 1989).
- Key lesson: Parallel systems can persist for decades if backed by sufficient coercive power, but collapse is nonlinear.

**Current parallels:**
- US financial sanctions (SWIFT exclusion of Russia, secondary sanctions) are the modern analogue of sterling-bloc clearing restrictions.
- China's CIPS, digital yuan, and bilateral currency swaps are the modern analogue of alternative clearing systems.
- The base rate for the incumbent system maintaining primacy: 0/2 (both sterling and COMECON ultimately lost centrality). But the transition timeline is 20-40 years, not 5-10.
- Dedollarization pace from KB: 0.5-0.8pp annual decline in USD reserve share. At this pace, USD falls from ~58% to ~45-50% of reserves by 2035. This is historically consistent with sterling's decline (similar annual pace in the 1950s-60s).

|--------|------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|--------|
| 1930-33 | Gold +69%, Farmland -25% | DJIA -89%, bonds mixed | Variable |

The pattern is consistent but the magnitude varies enormously depending on whether the fragmentation episode coincides with a broader macro downturn. The common thread: assets with local/domestic demand drivers outperform assets that depend on open global capital flows.

**Specific beneficiaries in the current analogue set:**
- Industrial CRE in "reshoring corridors" (Ohio, Texas, Arizona) — consistent with geopolitical_premium_safe_haven_cre from KB
- Copper, lithium, and other electrification metals — demand elevated by parallel green transition
- Defense industrial base companies — consistent with geopolitical_fiscal_constraints from KB
- US small-cap industrials (more domestic, less trade-exposed than mega-cap)

### Claim 10: China Overcapacity Offset and the Japanese Deflation Export Analogue

**Japanese deflation export (1995-2015):**
- Japan's persistent overcapacity and yen weakness exported deflation to trade partners via: (a) cheap manufactured goods, (b) yen carry trade capital flows, (c) competitive pressure on pricing in industries from autos to electronics.
- The deflationary impulse was estimated at -0.3 to -0.5pp on US core goods CPI by IMF (2003) and BoJ research.
- **Critical precondition**: The transmission required an open trading system. Japan's goods reached US consumers through integrated supply chains and low tariff barriers (avg US tariff on Japanese goods ~3% post-Uruguay Round).

**Application to China 2025-30:**
- China's industrial overcapacity is more severe than Japan's was: PPI deflation for 20+ months, capacity utilization in manufacturing at 73-75% (vs. Japan's ~78% trough in 2002).
- However, if tariff walls block the direct trade channel, the deflationary impulse cannot reach US consumers.
- The "leakage" channel: Chinese overcapacity flows to third countries (Vietnam, Mexico, India), which then export to the US. This is the "tariff circumvention" risk that motivates friendshoring requirements.
- **The paradox**: If tariffs are effective at blocking Chinese goods, they raise US domestic prices. If they're ineffective (circumvention), they don't achieve the strategic decoupling goal. There is no equilibrium where tariffs simultaneously block Chinese goods AND keep US prices low.
- Historical precedent: Japanese auto VERs raised US car prices while Japan redirected to transplant production and upmarket vehicles. The consumer cost was real and persistent until domestic competition increased.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Inflation Regimes (Direct, High Confidence)
- Deglobalization is one of 3-4 structural forces (alongside housing supply, healthcare costs, and fiscal expansion) driving the structural_inflation_shift identified in iter_0023. The deglobalization component (10-20bp of the 30-80bp total) may be conservative if tariff escalation continues.
- The bimodal inflation outcome (Claim 6) directly maps to the first_wave_1966_69_analogue: if the Fed treats fragmentation-driven goods inflation as "transitory" and eases prematurely, the probability of the persistent mode increases substantially.
- The goods_disinflation_completion concept is directly explained by the Phase 2 sequencing: supply-normalization disinflation is exhausted, and tariff pass-through is beginning.

### Commodity Supercycles
- The supercycle_narrative_failure concept (0/4 at 5-year horizons) needs to be tested against the deglobalization thesis. The current structural conditions (reshoring demand, critical mineral constraints) provide a stronger fundamental case than prior supercycle narratives, but the base rate remains cautionary.
- Critical mineral concentration (Claim 4) creates a commodity-specific risk that is qualitatively different from broad commodity supercycle claims. The risk is concentrated in specific minerals with processing bottlenecks, not a broad-based resource scarcity story.

### Rates-Equity Correlation
- The geopolitical_supply_fragmentation_premium and geopolitical_correlation_ratchet concepts from the KB are directly amplified by ongoing deglobalization. The ratchet mechanism (Claim 5's tariff escalation) creates new sunk costs that prevent correlation normalization even if geopolitical tensions ease.
- Trade fragmentation is a supply shock, which the KB identifies as producing positive stock-bond correlation. Persistent deglobalization could extend the positive correlation regime beyond what cyclical models predict.

### Credit Cycles
- Reshoring capex creates a bifurcated credit environment: industrial credits in reshoring corridors (Ohio, Arizona, Texas) benefit from government-backed demand; trade-dependent credits (importers, retailers with China supply chains) face margin compression and downgrade risk.
- The sequencing template (Claim 7) implies credit stress in trade-exposed sectors peaks in Phase 2 (12-24 months), while reshoring-beneficiary credits peak in Phase 3 (18-36 months).

### FX Regimes
- Dedollarization (Claim 8) connects directly to the fx_regimes topic. The 0.5-0.8pp annual pace of reserve diversification is consistent with sterling's historical decline, but the USD has structural advantages (depth, rule of law, network effects) that sterling lacked by 1945.
- The EM geopolitical bifurcation (em_geopolitical_bifurcation from KB) is a direct consequence of friendshoring: aligned EMs (India, Mexico, Vietnam) capture redirected trade flows and FDI; non-aligned EMs lose access.

### Equity Cycles
- Sector rotation implications: deglobalization is structurally positive for domestically-oriented industrials, defense, and infrastructure; negative for globally-optimized supply chains (consumer electronics, fast fashion, automotive).
- The PE multiple compression risk for trade-exposed mega-cap tech is potentially large but under-analyzed. Apple's China manufacturing exposure represents a risk with no direct analogue in the 1980s VER episode because the VERs targeted a commodity product (autos) not a platform ecosystem.

### Demographics
- The three_tier_geopolitical_divergence concept is reinforced by deglobalization dynamics: "aging-rich" nations are the ones implementing industrial policy and reshoring; "young-poor" nations are the potential beneficiaries of redirected manufacturing; "collapsing-middle" nations face the most uncertainty about which bloc they join.

### Real Estate Cycles
- Consistent with geopolitical_premium_safe_haven_cre from KB: industrial real estate in reshoring corridors is experiencing demand growth directly attributable to CHIPS Act/IRA spending. This is a structural shift, not a cyclical bounce.

### Commodity Inflation Transmission
- The critical mineral vulnerability (Claim 4) creates a novel transmission channel: not the traditional commodity-price-to-CPI pipeline, but a supply-availability-to-production-disruption channel. If gallium/germanium exports are restricted, the inflation impact comes through production bottlenecks and shortage premiums rather than commodity price movements.

## Open Questions

1. **Can selective decoupling avoid the 0/4 base rate for recession?** The current episode is genuinely different in being sector-specific rather than broad-based, and accompanied by fiscal support. But the base rate is strongly cautionary. What is the minimum threshold of trade disruption that triggers recession, and is the current trajectory approaching it?

2. **What is the actual tariff pass-through elasticity in 2025-26?** The Amiti-Redding-Weinstein (2019) finding of ~100% pass-through to US importers was based on the 2018-19 tariffs. Has the subsequent reshoring and supply chain adjustment changed the elasticity? Are firms absorbing more margin compression than in 2018-19?

3. **How does the interaction between deglobalization and AI/automation change the analogue applicability?** No prior deglobalization episode coincided with a transformative general-purpose technology. If AI automates 20-30% of the cost advantage that made offshoring attractive, does that fundamentally change the cost calculus of reshoring? This could reduce the nearshoring capex multiplier below the 1.3-1.5x historical estimate.

4. **What is the critical mineral "weaponization probability" over the next 5 years?** The 2010 Japan rare earth cutoff lasted only 2 months before China backed down. The 2023 Ga/Ge export controls are ongoing but limited in scope. What would trigger a full-scale critical mineral embargo, and what is the market pricing of this tail risk?

5. **Is the dedollarization pace (0.5-0.8pp/year) stable, accelerating, or decelerating?** The sterling analogue suggests the pace can remain steady for decades, but it can also accelerate sharply during crises (sterling's reserve share collapsed from 30% to 10% between 1967-1976 after the devaluation). What would trigger an acceleration in USD reserve diversification?

6. **How should investors calibrate the timing of the Phase 2 → Phase 3 transition?** The sequencing template suggests we are in late Phase 1/early Phase 2, but the timing depends on the pace of tariff escalation. A sharp acceleration (e.g., across-the-board 25%+ tariffs on all Chinese goods) would compress the timeline; a negotiated pause would extend it.

7. **What is the interaction between deglobalization and the energy transition?** Both require massive capital investment in physical infrastructure. Are they complementary (green reshoring = reshoring with clean energy) or competing (limited construction labor, permitting capacity, and fiscal space must be shared between IRA and CHIPS)?

8. **Does the "friendshoring premium" create a natural limit to deglobalization?** If reshoring costs stabilize at 1.3-1.5x, but the geopolitical risk premium on offshored supply chains rises above that (due to tariff uncertainty, embargo risk, sanctions risk), then friendshoring becomes economically rational regardless of policy mandates. At what risk premium does this tipping point occur, and are we past it?

9. **How do the analogues interact?** The current episode combines features of multiple analogues simultaneously (tariff escalation + critical mineral vulnerability + currency weaponization + industrial policy). No prior episode combined all of these. Does the interaction amplify or dampen the outcomes relative to any single analogue?

10. **What is the electoral cycle's impact on tariff trajectory?** The 1930 Smoot-Hawley, 1971 Nixon Shock, and 2018 Trump tariffs were all at least partially motivated by electoral dynamics. Does the current US political equilibrium (bipartisan China hawkishness) suggest that the tariff ratchet has fewer political off-ramps than prior episodes?
