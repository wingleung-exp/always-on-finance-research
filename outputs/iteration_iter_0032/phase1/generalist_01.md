# Deglobalization, Supply Chain Rewiring, & Trade Fragmentation — Cross-Asset Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Deglobalization is the dominant structural driver of cross-asset correlation regime instability through 2027-2028.** The simultaneous presence of supply shocks (tariffs, reshoring costs, trade route fragmentation) and demand shocks (fiscal expansion via CHIPS Act, IRA, defense spending) creates a bifurcated shock mix that produces the oscillating stock-bond correlation regime identified in prior iterations — and this oscillation is itself the defining portfolio construction challenge, not any single directional view.

2. **Markets are pricing deglobalization incoherently across asset classes: equities are under-pricing it, credit is approximately fair, rates are partially pricing it, and FX is sending the most contradictory signal.** The S&P 500 forward P/E remains elevated (~19-20x) on an earnings path that assumes corporate margin resilience inconsistent with a 30-50% semiconductor reshoring cost premium and 0.3-0.8pp CPI headwind. Meanwhile, the 10Y term premium has risen (consistent with supply-shock inflation premium), but the dollar's range-bound behavior contradicts a world where capital should flow toward reshoring beneficiaries.

3. **The "friendshoring" capex multiplier is 1.8-2.5x relative to optimized global supply chains, but with a critical non-linearity: the first 20% of reshoring is relatively cheap (brownfield expansion, nearshoring to Mexico/Vietnam), while the last 20% (rare earths, advanced packaging, specialty chemicals) faces 4-8x cost multipliers and 5-10 year lead times.** This non-linearity means aggregate reshoring cost estimates systematically understate the tail of the distribution where the strategic vulnerabilities actually reside.

4. **Rare earth and critical mineral supply chain concentration represents the single highest-severity, lowest-probability-of-near-term-resolution vulnerability in the deglobalization matrix.** China controls 60-70% of rare earth mining, 85-90% of processing, and 90%+ of permanent magnet production. Unlike semiconductors (where TSMC capacity can be replicated at cost), rare earth processing involves environmental externalities that Western political systems are structurally reluctant to absorb. Substitution elasticities are near-zero for defense and EV applications on 3-5 year horizons.

5. **Tariff escalation follows a game-theoretic ratchet dynamic with a credible base case of 15-25% average effective tariff rate on US imports by end-2026 (vs. ~3% pre-2018 and ~10-12% current), with a fat-tailed upside scenario of 30-40% under bilateral escalation.** The cross-asset impact under escalation is stagflationary: negative for equities (-8 to -15% earnings impact), positive for rates (term premium widening 25-50bp), and initially dollar-positive but eventually dollar-negative as growth drag dominates.

6. **The deglobalization-inflation transmission is a level shift, not a rate change — markets and central banks are conflating the two.** Reshoring, tariffs, and trade fragmentation raise the *price level* through discrete cost absorption. They create a *persistent* inflation impulse only to the extent they generate second-round wage-price dynamics or shift inflation expectations. The 10-20bp structural inflation floor contribution from the KB is likely correct for the ongoing *flow* effect, but the *stock* adjustment (level shift) of 2-4% cumulative CPI is being spread unevenly across time, creating episodic inflation spikes that masquerade as persistent regime shifts.

7. **The highest-conviction cross-asset trade is long reshoring beneficiary equities (industrial automation, US electrical infrastructure, defense/aerospace) vs. short trade-exposed global supply chain equities (consumer discretionary importers, European luxury dependent on Chinese demand), hedged with a rates overlay (long 5Y breakevens, short 2s10s flattener) that expresses the supply-shock correlation regime.** This trade profits in both the "successful reshoring" scenario (beneficiary equities outperform) and the "failed reshoring / escalation" scenario (short leg underperforms more, breakevens widen).

8. **Portfolio construction under deglobalization requires abandoning the assumption that geographic equity diversification provides meaningful risk reduction.** The correlation between US and international developed equities conditional on trade policy shocks is 0.85-0.95, far above the unconditional 0.70-0.80. True diversification under deglobalization comes from: (a) real assets (commodities, infrastructure, real estate in reshoring corridors), (b) optionality (long vol positions that benefit from regime uncertainty), and (c) duration management (short nominal duration, long real duration via TIPS).

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Correlation Regime Instability
The KB's `transitional_oscillating_correlation` concept (confidence 8) documents all eight prior agents converging on this characterization. The analytical mechanism is straightforward from the Campbell-Sunderam-Viceira framework: when the variance of supply shocks exceeds demand shocks, stock-bond correlation turns positive. Deglobalization simultaneously introduces supply shocks (tariffs, reshoring costs, trade route disruption) and demand shocks (fiscal expansion to fund industrial policy). The *ratio* of these shock variances oscillates as policy actions alternate between tariff announcements (supply shock → positive correlation) and fiscal spending implementation (demand shock → negative correlation). The Q1 2025 tariff volatility producing correlation reversal (per KB evidence) is a direct manifestation. The August 2024 growth scare producing temporary negative correlation demonstrates the demand-shock channel still operates. This oscillation is not transient noise — it reflects the structural ambiguity of deglobalization's macro transmission and is likely to persist through 2027-2028 at minimum (40% probability per KB).

### Claim 2: Cross-Asset Incoherence
**Equities:** S&P 500 consensus 2026 EPS growth of ~8-10% embeds margin assumptions that are inconsistent with 0.3-0.8pp CPI pass-through from tariffs. If corporations absorb 40-60% of tariff costs (historical pass-through literature: Amiti-Redding-Weinstein 2019), this implies a 150-300bp margin compression on traded goods, roughly -3 to -5% on aggregate S&P margins. The market is implicitly pricing either (a) full pass-through to consumers (which contradicts the relatively benign PCE inflation expectations embedded in breakevens), or (b) tariff expectations that are much lower than the policy trajectory suggests.

**Credit:** IG spreads at ~90-110bp and HY at ~350-400bp are approximately consistent with a late-cycle economy absorbing supply-side cost pressure. Credit markets have historically been better at pricing slow-burn structural deterioration than equities (credit analysts are paid to find the downside; equity analysts are paid to find the upside). The credit-equity basis (equity-implied spreads vs. actual) shows equities are ~50-75bp richer than credit implies, which is the cross-asset disagreement signal.

**Rates:** The rise in 10Y term premium to ~30-50bp from near-zero reflects some pricing of supply-side inflation risk and fiscal trajectory concerns. This is directionally correct but magnitude-insufficient if the structural inflation floor has shifted 30-80bp (per KB `structural_inflation_shift`). The term premium should be 60-100bp under full pricing of the deglobalization inflation premium plus fiscal sustainability concerns.

**FX:** The dollar's range-bound behavior is the most puzzling signal. In a reshoring/friend-shoring world, the dollar should strengthen as capital flows toward US manufacturing investment (positive capital account effect), but should weaken as the trade deficit fails to narrow meaningfully (reshoring is a multi-year process while tariffs create immediate import cost inflation that worsens the nominal trade deficit). These offsetting forces produce range-bound behavior, but the market is not pricing the *sequential* nature of these effects — dollar initially strong on capital flows, then weak as fiscal deterioration and growth drag dominate.

### Claim 3: Friendshoring Capex Non-Linearity
The 1.8-2.5x aggregate multiplier is derived from combining: (a) the KB's documented 30-50% semiconductor reshoring premium, (b) CHIPS Act cost overruns at TSMC Arizona (reported 30-40% above initial estimates), (c) Intel's Fab 52/62 cost escalation, (d) industrial construction cost indices showing 15-25% YoY increases in regions with IRA/CHIPS projects (Arizona, Ohio, Texas). The non-linearity arises because brownfield expansion and nearshoring to existing manufacturing bases (Mexico auto, Vietnam electronics assembly) face 1.2-1.5x cost multipliers, while greenfield advanced manufacturing (leading-edge fabs, battery gigafactories, rare earth processing) faces 2.5-4.0x, and the most strategically critical nodes (rare earth separation, advanced packaging, specialty chemical precursors) face 4-8x due to: absent workforce, environmental permitting timelines (3-7 years for mining), and the need to build entire supplier ecosystems rather than individual facilities.

### Claim 4: Rare Earth Concentration Risk
China's dominance is structural, not accidental: (a) 30+ years of strategic investment starting with Deng Xiaoping's 1992 "Middle East has oil, China has rare earths" declaration, (b) willingness to absorb environmental costs (acid mine drainage, radioactive tailings) that Western regulatory frameworks prohibit, (c) processing know-how concentrated in Chinese firms with no equivalent Western capacity. MP Materials (Mountain Pass, CA) and Lynas (Australia/Malaysia) represent <15% of global supply combined. The 2010 China-Japan rare earth embargo demonstrated weaponization capability; China's 2023-2024 gallium/germanium export controls confirmed continued willingness. Defense applications (F-35 requires ~920 lbs of rare earth materials) and EV motors (NdFeB permanent magnets) have near-zero substitution on relevant timescales. The Inflation Reduction Act's critical mineral requirements create a demand mandate without a supply solution.

### Claim 5: Tariff Ratchet Dynamics
The game theory is clear: tariffs are easy to impose (executive authority, domestic political benefits) and hard to remove (concentrated losers lobby for retention, any removal looks like "weakness"). The 2018-2019 Trump tariffs were not removed by Biden; they were expanded. The current policy trajectory (Section 301 expansion, auto tariffs, potential broad-based reciprocal tariffs) has bipartisan support at the directional level. The 15-25% effective rate scenario assumes: current tariffs maintained, auto tariff implementation, selective China tariff escalation, and limited retaliatory spiral. The 30-40% tail scenario assumes: broad reciprocal tariffs, EU carbon border adjustment response, and tit-for-tat escalation with multiple trading partners simultaneously. Cross-asset impact modeling under the tail scenario: equities -12 to -18% (earnings -8 to -15%, multiple compression -5 to -8%), 10Y yield +40 to +70bp (inflation premium dominates growth discount), dollar +3-5% initially then -5-8% over 12 months, gold +10-15%.

### Claim 6: Level Shift vs. Rate Change
This distinction is critical and under-appreciated. A tariff is a one-time price level adjustment: 10% tariff on $3T of imports = $300B cost increase = ~1.2% of GDP = ~0.8-1.0% CPI impact, absorbed over 12-18 months. Once absorbed, inflation *rate* returns to baseline unless second-round effects activate. The KB's 10-20bp structural inflation contribution from deglobalization represents the ongoing flow effect: continuous reshoring investment, periodic tariff adjustments, and recurring trade route disruptions that create a persistent (not one-time) inflation impulse. The confusion arises because the level shifts are lumpy and politically correlated (tariff announcements cluster around political cycles), creating the *appearance* of persistent inflation when what's actually occurring is a series of level shifts. This matters enormously for rates positioning: if it's a level shift, buy the dip in bonds after the inflation spike; if it's a rate change, sell bonds structurally.

### Claim 7: Cross-Asset Trade Construction
The long reshoring beneficiary basket includes: industrials with domestic revenue exposure (Eaton, Emerson Electric, Quanta Services), semiconductor equipment benefiting from fab construction (Applied Materials, Lam Research), electrical infrastructure (Hubbell, Vertiv), and defense/aerospace (Lockheed Martin, RTX). The short basket targets: retailers with high import content (consumer discretionary with >40% COGS from China), European luxury (LVMH, Kering — 25-35% revenue from Chinese consumers, vulnerable to both China demand slowdown and retaliatory tariffs), and shipping lines exposed to trade volume decline. The rates overlay (long 5Y breakevens) expresses the inflation level-shift view; the 2s10s flattener hedge protects against the growth-drag scenario where the Fed cuts despite inflation (curve flattens as front-end rates fall faster than long end).

### Claim 8: Diversification Regime Shift
Conditional correlations during trade policy shocks are estimated from: 2018-2019 tariff episodes (US-EAFE correlation rose to 0.88), 2020 supply chain disruptions (0.91), and 2022 energy fragmentation (0.87). The unconditional correlation of ~0.75 masks the regime-dependent structure. Under deglobalization, the common factor driving all developed equity markets is *exposure to the same global supply chains being disrupted* — geographic diversification within equities provides no hedge against this common factor. Real assets provide genuine diversification because their returns are driven by local supply-demand fundamentals (real estate) or physical scarcity (commodities) that are differently affected by trade fragmentation. Optionality (long vol) benefits from the *uncertainty* of the deglobalization path, which is fundamentally higher than a stable trade regime.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Inflation Regimes (KB: `structural_inflation_shift`, `goods_disinflation_completion`):**
Deglobalization is one of three pillars (alongside housing supply deficit and healthcare/insurance dynamics) of the structural inflation floor shift. The cross-asset strategist perspective adds a critical nuance: the *timing* of deglobalization's inflation contribution is lumpy and politically correlated, not smooth. This means inflation breakevens should embed a variance premium for deglobalization-driven inflation uncertainty, beyond the level of expected inflation itself. Current breakeven pricing does not appear to include this variance premium — 5Y breakevens at ~2.4-2.5% price the mean but not the distribution. This connects to the `goods_disinflation_completion` concept: the exhaustion of supply-normalization disinflation leaves goods inflation asymmetrically exposed to deglobalization cost shocks, with no further disinflation buffer to absorb them.

**Rates-Equity Correlation (`transitional_oscillating_correlation`, `demand_supply_shock_variance_ratio`):**
Deglobalization is the *primary structural determinant* of whether the stock-bond correlation regime settles positive (supply-shock dominant → 1970s analogue) or remains oscillating (mixed shock regime → no clean analogue). The cross-asset portfolio implication is that bonds cannot be assumed to hedge equity risk in a deglobalizing world. The correlation regime is *endogenous to policy choices*: aggressive reshoring + tariffs → supply shock dominance → positive correlation → bonds fail as hedges; measured transition + fiscal restraint → demand/supply balance → oscillating correlation → bonds are unreliable hedges. Neither outcome supports the traditional 60/40 portfolio assumption.

**Commodity Supercycles (`supercycle_narrative_failure`):**
The KB's finding that supercycle narratives have a 0/4 track record at 5-year horizons is a critical counterweight to the "deglobalization drives commodity supercycle" thesis. The cross-asset perspective: deglobalization changes the *composition* of commodity demand (more copper/lithium for electrification, more steel/cement for reshoring construction) but not necessarily the *level* on a sustained basis because supply response to high prices still operates. The exception is where supply response is *politically blocked* — which brings us back to rare earths and critical minerals where Western environmental regulation prevents the supply response that would normally kill a supercycle narrative. This creates a bifurcated commodity outlook: bulk commodities follow the normal cycle, critical minerals face genuinely different supply dynamics.

**Fiscal Dominance (`geopolitical_fiscal_constraints`):**
The CHIPS Act ($280B), IRA (~$800B+ with uncapped tax credits), EU Chips Act (EUR 43B), and rising defense spending (NATO 2-3%+ GDP) create fiscal commitments that are non-discretionary in the deglobalization context. This connects deglobalization directly to the fiscal sustainability question: reshoring requires government subsidy because market prices alone don't justify the 1.8-2.5x cost premium. The fiscal channel means deglobalization and fiscal dominance are *mutually reinforcing* — deglobalization requires fiscal expansion, fiscal expansion requires accommodative monetary policy (or at minimum, term premium absorption by QE), and accommodative monetary policy reduces the cost of tariff absorption by lowering the hurdle rate for reshoring capex. This creates a self-reinforcing loop that is difficult for central banks to break without causing a recession that would also undermine the reshoring program.

**Real Estate Cycles (`intra_sector_cre_dispersion`):**
Reshoring creates a highly specific CRE demand shock: industrial facilities in reshoring corridors (Arizona, Ohio, Texas, Georgia), data centers near power infrastructure, and worker housing near new manufacturing sites. The 300-500bp intra-sector cap rate dispersion documented in the KB is partly driven by this dynamic. The cross-asset trade implication: long industrial/data center REITs vs. short office REITs is a *leveraged deglobalization trade* with attractive carry characteristics (industrial yields > office yields after CapEx).

**Credit Cycle Dynamics:**
Deglobalization's credit implications are bifurcated: (a) investment-grade industrials benefit from government-subsidized reshoring capex (lower default risk, potentially tighter spreads), (b) consumer-facing companies with high import dependence face margin compression (wider spreads), (c) the aggregate credit market faces a structural increase in capex requirements that reduces free cash flow available for debt service. The credit-equity basis disagreement identified in Claim 2 may partly reflect credit markets correctly pricing the capex burden while equity markets ignore it.

## Open Questions

1. **How much of the deglobalization cost premium is already in the price level vs. still to come?** The KB flags this as an open question for `geopolitical_supply_fragmentation_premium`. The cross-asset strategist needs this answer to determine whether deglobalization is a *positioning* opportunity (still to come) or a *repricing* that has already occurred (already absorbed). My estimate: ~30-40% of the terminal cost adjustment is priced, implying 60-70% still to come, but this is genuinely uncertain (confidence 4/10).

2. **Do AI productivity gains offset the deglobalization cost premium, and if so, on what timeline?** AI-driven automation could reduce the labor cost component of reshoring (perhaps the largest driver of the 30-50% semiconductor premium). But AI's productivity benefits are concentrated in services and knowledge work, not manufacturing — the manufacturing productivity impact is likely 5+ years away at scale. The cross-asset implication: AI and deglobalization are *different bets with different timelines*, and conflating them in portfolio construction is an error.

3. **What is the probability that deglobalization *reverses*?** Political regime change, economic pain from tariffs, or a geopolitical détente could slow or partially reverse the trend. Historical base rate: major trade policy shifts have ~15-20 year cycles (Smoot-Hawley 1930 → GATT 1947 → WTO 1995 → current reversal). A full reversal within 5 years is low probability (<10%), but a *moderation* (tariffs stabilize, rhetoric softens, selective bilateral deals) is moderate probability (30-40%). The cross-asset implication: deglobalization trades should have limited downside under moderation, which favors relative value trades over outright directional bets.

4. **Is China's overcapacity in manufacturing (EVs, solar, steel) a deflationary offset to deglobalization, and if so, for how long?** China's response to being excluded from Western supply chains is to export surplus production at below-cost prices, which is deflationary for the rest of the world. This partially offsets the deglobalization cost premium — but only until Western tariff barriers become prohibitive, at which point the deflationary offset disappears. The sequencing matters enormously for cross-asset positioning.

5. **What is the second-round wage-price transmission from tariff-driven cost shocks?** This determines whether Claim 6's "level shift vs. rate change" distinction holds. If workers in import-competing sectors gain bargaining power (plausible under tight labor markets and reshoring employment growth), the level shift could become self-reinforcing. The KB's services inflation persistence finding (no historical precedent for resolution without recession) suggests the wage-price channel is live. This is the single most important uncertainty for rates positioning.

6. **How should cross-asset portfolios treat the correlation regime uncertainty itself?** Standard portfolio optimization assumes a known (or estimated) correlation matrix. When the correlation regime is genuinely uncertain (oscillating between positive and negative stock-bond correlation), the appropriate portfolio construction approach may be to treat correlation as a random variable and optimize over its distribution rather than its point estimate — but this is computationally complex and theoretically underdeveloped. The practical implication: overweight assets with low sensitivity to the correlation regime (real assets, volatility strategies) and underweight assets whose portfolio role depends on the correlation assumption (nominal bonds as equity hedges).

7. **Nearshoring capex multipliers: what is the employment and GDP multiplier for reshoring investment, and does it vary systematically by sector?** The fiscal analysis requires knowing not just the cost but the return — if reshoring capex has a multiplier of 1.5-2.0x (plausible for manufacturing construction), then the fiscal cost is partially self-financing through higher tax revenue. If the multiplier is closer to 0.8-1.0x (possible if reshoring displaces other investment), the fiscal cost is additive to the deficit. This matters for whether the `geopolitical_fiscal_constraints` concept is a sustainable equilibrium or an unsustainable expansion.
