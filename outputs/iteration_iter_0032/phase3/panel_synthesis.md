**Topic:** deglobalization | **Category:** structural_themes | **Iteration:** iter_0032

## HIGH_CONFIDENCE

**1. Rare earth and critical mineral processing concentration is the highest-severity deglobalization vulnerability, with a 5-10 year unclosable window.**

- **Confidence: 8/10**
- **Originating agents:** risk_analyst_01 (8/10), risk_analyst_02 (8/10), generalist_01 (8/10), generalist_02 (7/10), credit_analyst_01 (6/10), challenger_01 (6/10 — partially dissents on elasticity)
- **Surviving evidence:** China controls 60-70% of rare earth mining, 85-90% of processing, 80%+ of gallium, germanium, and graphite anode material (USGS 2025). Sequential weaponization demonstrated: Ga/Ge controls (Jul 2023), graphite (Oct 2023), antimony (Aug 2024), processing technology ban (2025). No spare capacity outside China. Alternative processing infrastructure requires 5-10 years and billions in capex (Lynas, MP Materials collectively <15% of Chinese capacity). The 2010 Japan embargo precedent — cited by challenger_01 as evidence of supply elasticity — was partially refuted in pair_3 debate: Molycorp went bankrupt in 2015 from Chinese price manipulation, current restrictions target processing *technology* not raw material quotas, and rare earth demand has grown 3-5x since 2010. The vulnerability window overlaps with the peak risk period for Taiwan contingencies. Near-zero substitutability for defense (F-35: ~920 lbs rare earth materials) and EV motors on 3-5 year horizons. This exceeds the 1973 OPEC concentration (~55% of crude) because OPEC had non-OPEC spare capacity; rare earth processing has none.

**2. Tariff escalation follows a ratchet dynamic with a strong historical base rate of non-reversal.**

- **Confidence: 8/10**
- **Originating agents:** generalist_02 (8/10 — 5/5 historical episodes), generalist_01 (7/10), risk_analyst_02 (7/10), macro_strategist_01 (7/10)
- **Surviving evidence:** In all 5 major protectionist episodes (1890 McKinley, 1922 Fordney-McCumber, 1930 Smoot-Hawley, 1971 Nixon surcharge, 2018-present), initial tariffs triggered retaliatory spirals exceeding the original action by 1.5-3x within 24 months. Only 1/5 de-escalated, and that required GDP falling 30% (Depression → 1934 RTAA). The mechanism is well-theorized (Olson's collective action: concentrated beneficiaries lobby for retention, diffuse consumers can't organize against it) and empirically confirmed (US steel tariffs imposed 2018 remain in 2026 under different administration, solar tariffs renewed and expanded). Bipartisan US protectionist convergence removes the political off-ramp. Pair_0 debate confirmed complementary mechanisms from generalist_01 (game theory) and generalist_02 (historical base rate) strengthen combined confidence.

**3. Deglobalization drives positive stock-bond correlation through supply-shock dominance, eroding 60/40 portfolio diversification.**

- **Confidence: 8/10**
- **Originating agents:** quant_researcher_02 (8/10), generalist_01 (8/10), macro_strategist_01 (6/10)
- **Surviving evidence:** Campbell-Sunderam-Viceira framework: supply-shock variance exceeding demand-shock variance flips correlation positive. Deglobalization is fundamentally a supply-side phenomenon (higher input costs, reduced productive capacity, commodity price floors). Empirically validated in 2022 (simultaneous -20% equities, -15% long bonds). KB already documents this at confidence 8 via `transitional_oscillating_correlation`. quant_researcher_02 estimates shift from ρ = -0.3 to ρ = +0.2, increasing 60/40 portfolio volatility by 15-20% and reducing Sharpe ratio by 0.10-0.15. The oscillation between positive and negative regimes (supply vs. demand shock dominance alternating with policy actions) is itself the central portfolio construction challenge. Pair_2 debate confirmed agreement from both sides.

**4. Tariff pass-through is non-linear with a critical threshold at ~15-25% effective rates.**

- **Confidence: 7.5/10**
- **Originating agents:** credit_analyst_01 (6/10), macro_strategist_01 (7/10)
- **Surviving evidence:** Convergence from entirely independent frameworks. credit_analyst_01 derives the threshold from coverage ratio arithmetic: a median BBB industrial with 3.5x interest coverage and 15% import COGS at 15% tariff maintains ~3.0x (manageable); at 25% drops to ~2.3x (watch-list); at 35% breaches 2.0x (fallen angel risk). macro_strategist_01 derives it from empirical pass-through literature: at 10% tariffs, 30-50% pass-through (firms absorb via margins); above 25%, 60-90% pass-through (margin compression exhausts). Both cite Amiti-Redding-Weinstein (2019) and Cavallo/Fajgelbaum et al. for calibration. Pair_1 debate called this "the single strongest consensus finding" due to convergence from bottom-up credit and top-down macro frameworks. Current effective tariff regime (~12-18% weighted average) sits at the cusp of the danger zone.

**5. Deglobalization structurally elevates term premium by 40-80bp above the 2010-2019 average, driven primarily by inflation *variance* rather than inflation level.**

- **Confidence: 7.5/10**
- **Originating agents:** quant_researcher_02 (7/10), risk_analyst_01, macro_strategist_01
- **Surviving evidence:** In affine term structure models, term premium compensates for inflation uncertainty. Deglobalization increases uncertainty through tariff policy discontinuity (binary shocks with 0.3-0.8pp CPI impact, unpredictable timing), supply-chain tail risk (rare earth disruption as option-like downside), and central bank reaction function ambiguity (supply-side inflation creates impossible trinity). Kim-Wright/ACM decompositions show 80-130bp term premium vs. near-zero in 2015-2019; deglobalization accounts for roughly one-third to one-half. Three reinforcing channels: (1) higher inflation volatility, (2) reduced foreign central bank Treasury purchases from trade surplus recycling decline, (3) increased fiscal costs of industrial policy (CHIPS, IRA). Pair_2 debate called this "probably the highest-confidence shared claim" between the quant and risk analysts.

**6. Markets are systemically underpricing deglobalization tail risks due to cross-silo opacity and backward-looking risk models.**

- **Confidence: 7.5/10**
- **Originating agents:** risk_analyst_01 (8/10), risk_analyst_02, quant_researcher_02, credit_analyst_01
- **Surviving evidence:** No single desk, fund, or risk model integrates trade policy + supply chain architecture + commodity pricing + FX regime shifts + sovereign credit. Standard VaR uses historical correlations calibrated to a globalized regime. Companies disclose revenue by geography but rarely supply chain dependency by geography. HY OAS at 350-400bp with compressed BB-CCC ratio (2.0-2.5x vs. historical 3.0-3.5x) shows PC1 explaining >65% of variance — markets are not discriminating. Analogous to pre-2008 CDO correlation risk: the correlation assumption is invisible until it materializes. Pair_2 confirmed convergence from different diagnostic lenses (quant: compressed dispersion; risk: structural opacity). The specific rare earth disruption premium in IG industrial spreads is estimated at near-zero — even a 5% probability-weighted adjustment should add 15-30bp to exposed issuers.

**7. The "deglobalization" label conflates distinct processes operating on different timescales.**

- **Confidence: 7.5/10**
- **Originating agents:** risk_analyst_02 (8/10), challenger_01 (7/10)
- **Surviving evidence:** Three separable processes: (a) strategic decoupling — deliberate state-directed technology/supply chain severance (5-15 year, security logic); (b) tariff-driven fragmentation — episodic, politically motivated (1-3 year, mercantilist logic); (c) resilience restructuring — private-sector risk repricing (3-7 year, corporate logic). These can contradict: US tariffs on Canadian aluminum (mercantilist) undermine Canada as friendshoring partner for critical minerals (security). challenger_01's framing as "re-globalization through different nodes" is compatible. WTO data confirms global goods trade at ~$25.3T in 2024, near record; global services trade at record highs. Pair_3 debate called this "the most analytically useful framework in either analysis." The aggregate "deglobalization" narrative masks structurally important compositional shifts — using total trade volume as evidence for or against deglobalization is misleading (refuted in pair_3).

## MODERATE_CONFIDENCE

**1. Friendshoring is currently ~70% cosmetic trade diversion, but trajectory is toward genuine relocation as enforcement tightens.**

- **Confidence: 6.5/10**
- **Originating agents:** challenger_01 (7/10), risk_analyst_02 (7/10 — friendshoring trilemma)
- **Evidence:** Vietnamese exports to US surged from $47B (2018) to $114B (2024), but Vietnamese imports from China surged in parallel from $65B to $130B+ — correlation >0.9. Pair_3 debate called this "the single most compelling data point in either analysis." Estimated 20-40% of Vietnam's incremental US-bound exports represent Chinese goods with minimal transformation (Freund, Maliszewska et al. 2024). Cost spectrum: cosmetic (3-8%), partial (8-15%), genuine (15-30%), full reshoring (30-50%+). Corporations overwhelmingly choosing options 1-2. However, rules-of-origin enforcement is tightening (SE Asian solar tariffs, proposed auto content requirements). risk_analyst_02's friendshoring trilemma (optimize for two of {cost, security, allied sourcing}) captures the structural constraint. Debate synthesis: currently cosmetic, moving toward genuine at uncertain speed.

**2. Structural inflation contribution from deglobalization is 10-25bp steady-state, with episodic volatility spikes creating fatter right tail.**

- **Confidence: 6.5/10**
- **Originating agents:** macro_strategist_01 (7/10 — 15-40bp), challenger_01 (7/10 — 5-10bp), risk_analyst_02 (6/10), generalist_01 (7/10)
- **Evidence:** Three transmission channels identified by macro_strategist_01: direct cost (10-20bp via Amiti-Redding-Weinstein), supply chain redundancy (5-15bp via McKinsey/Bonadio et al.), reduced competition / markup channel (5-10bp via Blanchard-Giavazzi 2003). Pair_1 debate found macro_strategist_01's decomposition stronger but noted partial double-counting in upper bound, narrowing to 15-30bp. challenger_01's downward adjustment for Chinese overcapacity absorption (Fajgelbaum et al.: 20-50% tariff absorption by Chinese exporters) and cosmetic friendshoring lowers the realized floor. Pair_3 synthesis: "5-12bp structural steady-state, with fatter tails on episodic supply disruptions." Reconciling all inputs: 10-25bp central range with the risk_analyst_02 volatility channel adding episodic spikes of variable magnitude. The tradeable/non-tradeable wedge reversal (goods from -0.5% to +0.5-1.0%, services sticky at 3.5-4.0%) makes 2% headline structurally harder.

**3. China's industrial overcapacity partially offsets tariff inflation in specific sectors, but not across the board.**

- **Confidence: 6/10**
- **Originating agents:** challenger_01 (7/10 — central thesis), generalist_02 (7/10 — Japan deflation export analogue)
- **Evidence:** Overcapacity is staggering: solar 1,200 GW capacity vs. 400-500 GW installations (2.5-3x), EV batteries 2,500 GWh vs. 1,000-1,200 GWh demand, steel 300M excess tonnes. Chinese EV export prices fell 20-30% since 2022 even without tariff increases. Fajgelbaum et al. (2020) and Cavallo et al. (2021) show Chinese exporters absorbed 20-50% of 2018-2019 tariffs. generalist_02's Japan 1990s-2000s deflation export analogue is apt — but required open trading system to transmit. The paradox (pair_3): tariffs that block Chinese goods raise US prices; tariffs that fail to block them fail strategically. **Critical nuance that survived debate:** The offset is sector-specific, not aggregate. Works for EVs, solar, batteries, steel. Does NOT work for semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, aerospace components, specialized chemicals — precisely the sectors where supply chain fragmentation is most acute. challenger_01's headline claim of "close to zero in aggregate" was refuted in pair_3 for obscuring this dispersion.

**4. Nearshoring capex announcements overstate actual execution by 30-50%, with realized completion rate of 50-70%.**

- **Confidence: 6/10**
- **Originating agents:** challenger_01 (6/10 — 25-45% completion), risk_analyst_01 (capex multiplier 1.3-1.8x)
- **Evidence:** Brookings analysis: 35-50% completion rate for $1B+ industrial policy projects within stated timelines. CHIPS Act: ~$30B in awards announced but only ~$8-10B disbursed as of Q1 2026. TSMC Arizona: originally 2024 production, revised to limited 2026 at N4. EU Court of Auditors: ~60% of EU Chips Act headline counts pre-existing commitments. IRA: ~55-65% take-up rate on manufacturing credits in first 2 years. Pair_3 debate found challenger_01's correction "essential" but the 25-45% estimate too pessimistic because national security framing creates stronger political persistence than typical industrial policy. Synthesis: 50-70% completion rate, not 100% (implicit in most other agents) or 25-45% (challenger_01's extreme).

**5. Dollar funding is the most likely amplification channel from deglobalization shock to financial crisis.**

- **Confidence: 6/10**
- **Originating agents:** risk_analyst_01 (8/10)
- **Evidence:** Mechanical linkage: trade surplus recycling decline → reserve diversification → offshore dollar funding thinning → crisis-time liquidity crunch. Validated by 2008 ($600B+ Fed swap lines) and March 2020 (FRA-OIS to 75bp, cross-currency basis to -150bp within days). ~$80-90T cross-currency basis swap market is the transmission mechanism. Novel and critical insight: bifurcated swap line architecture under geopolitical stress — Fed can backstop allies but not adversaries or non-aligned nations. Pair_2 debate called this "the strongest novel contribution in either analysis." Rated moderate rather than high confidence because it's primarily from one agent and the specific bifurcated swap line dynamic is untested.

**6. Bimodal inflation outcome: ~60% one-time price-level shift, ~40% persistent regime shift.**

- **Confidence: 6/10**
- **Originating agents:** generalist_02 (6/10), generalist_01 (7/10 — level shift framing), macro_strategist_01 (multiple claims)
- **Evidence:** Mode A analogue: 1971-72 post-Nixon Shock — import surcharge raised prices 1.5-2.0pp CPI over 18 months, then faded. Mode B analogue: 1973-75 — oil embargo interacted with pre-existing inflation, monetary accommodation, and fiscal expansion to become structural. Tilt factors identified: Fed reaction function (restrictive → Mode A), fiscal stance (expansion → Mode B), labor market (tight → Mode B), expectations (5y5y breakevens above 2.8% → Mode B). Pair_0 debate found bimodal framing "more analytically honest" than pure level-shift framing. The 60/40 split is necessarily imprecise but the bimodal structure is well-grounded in historical analogues. Current conditions (elevated services inflation, large fiscal deficits, near-full employment) tilt conditionally toward Mode B relative to a clean starting point.

**7. The 2024-2030 transition window represents maximum systemic vulnerability as supply chains are partially duplicated but neither old nor new configurations are fully operational.**

- **Confidence: 6/10**
- **Originating agents:** risk_analyst_01 (6/10)
- **Evidence:** Historical pattern: transitions between stable equilibria are highest-risk (1997 EM FX, 2008 banking model, 2020 Fed reserves). During transition: capital committed to incomplete projects (TSMC Arizona $40B+, Intel $20B+, Samsung $17B+), legacy relationships degraded but alternatives not ready, dual supply chain costs add 20-40% to total procurement. Workforce gap: US needs 500,000+ additional manufacturing workers. Capex multiplier of 1.5-2.0x (synthesis of agent estimates after debate corrections) during build-out phase, narrowing over time. Well-reasoned but timing uncertain — window could extend to 2035 or compress if policies accelerate.

**8. Deglobalization is not a standalone priced factor but a regime variable that shifts loadings on existing factors (value, quality, momentum, domestic-revenue).**

- **Confidence: 6/10**
- **Originating agents:** quant_researcher_02 (8/10)
- **Evidence:** Per HLZ (2016) framework, standalone "deglobalization factor" would fail t-stat > 3.0 threshold due to multicollinearity with value (capital-intensive firms), quality (pricing power survivors), size (domestic mid-caps), and momentum (policy tailwind). Spanning tests via Hou-Xue-Zhang q-factor model would show alpha statistically indistinguishable from zero. Historical precedent: 1930s, 1970s, 1980s trade episodes never produced distinct factors in academic studies. Reshoring-winner baskets show 60-80% of returns explained by known factors, with t-stat on intercept ~1.0-1.5. Pair_2 debate confirmed on return cross-section but noted tail risk structure is genuinely different from what standard factor models capture. Rated moderate because it's primarily one agent's framework, though theoretically well-grounded.

**9. The deglobalization consensus trade (long commodities / short duration / long inflation) is crowded, creating asymmetric unwind risk.**

- **Confidence: 6/10**
- **Originating agents:** challenger_01 (8/10), quant_researcher_02 (6/10 — crowding at 60-70th percentile)
- **Evidence:** BofA FMS: 72% of fund managers expect above-trend inflation (highest sustained reading). CFTC: managed money net long commodities at 70th-85th percentile of 5-year range. Breakeven inflation options show elevated demand for above-3% CPI outcomes. challenger_01 argues the "unwind risk exceeds the remaining return." However, quant_researcher_02 notes crowding is at 60-70th percentile, below the >90th percentile threshold where crowding becomes predictive per KB's tail-only signal framework. Pair_3 found the positioning argument "the most investable insight" from challenger_01. Synthesis: observable liquid-market positioning is elevated but not extreme; the deeper concern (per risk_analyst_01) is that much deglobalization exposure is embedded in corporate balance sheets and private markets where crowding metrics don't exist.

**10. Four-tier EM structure (strategic beneficiaries / trade diversion / crossfire / excluded) refines the binary aligned/non-aligned model.**

- **Confidence: 5.5/10**
- **Originating agents:** risk_analyst_02 (6/10)
- **Evidence:** Tier 1 (India, Vietnam, Poland): FDI inflows + favorable trade terms. Tier 2 (Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia): absorb rerouted goods, vulnerable to transshipment crackdowns. Tier 3 (South Korea, Germany, Taiwan): deep linkages to both blocs, forced into costly hedging. Tier 4 (Russia, potentially China): permanent investable universe shrinkage. Pair_3 debate confirmed the framework is "materially improved" over the binary model. challenger_01's connector economy analysis actually supports it — Vietnam is Tier 2 (vulnerable to crackdowns), not Tier 1. Classification boundaries are fuzzy for some countries and can shift with political events.

**11. Maturity wall × deglobalization interaction creates correlated stress with conditional default probability far exceeding the product of independent probabilities.**

- **Confidence: 5.5/10**
- **Originating agents:** credit_analyst_01 (7/10)
- **Evidence:** $900B-$1.5T sub-IG maturities (2025-2028) collide with deglobalization-driven margin compression. Borrowers face 40-60% higher refinancing costs AND 150-400bp lower operating margins simultaneously. Correlation between refinancing risk and operating margin risk estimated at 0.5-0.7 for import-exposed sectors (auto parts, chemicals, consumer products). Joint distribution problem: conditional probability of default given both stresses is substantially higher than single-factor models assume. Pair_1 confirmed both components individually well-documented, joint distribution analytically sound but empirically untested — no prior maturity wall has coincided with a deglobalization shock.

## REFUTED

**1. "$400-600B in IG facing binary credit risk from rare earth disruption" (credit_analyst_01, Claim 5)**
- **Killed in:** pair_1 debate
- **Reasoning:** Exposure estimate is too aggregated and inflated. "Binary" framing (IG to credit-impaired "within weeks") applies credibly only to a narrow subset (~$80-120B semiconductor equipment, ~$40-60B defense electronics). The broader $200-300B "general industrial users" category has multi-month inventory buffers and partial substitution pathways. Russia 2022 analogy is misleading because energy is a flow commodity consumed immediately while rare earths are stock inputs with buffers. Directional risk survives; quantified claim does not.

**2. NAIRU shift of 10-25bp from "15-25% reduction in effective labor supply" (macro_strategist_01, Claim 7)**
- **Killed in:** pair_1 debate
- **Reasoning:** Freeman's (2006) "1.5 billion workers" describes gross labor force in countries that opened to trade, not effective labor supply competing with advanced-economy workers. Most Chinese manufacturing workers were never substitutable for US service-sector workers. The domestic labor channel operates through product-market competition (Autor-Dorn-Hanson "China shock"), not direct labor supply. The 15-25% reduction figure is "not meaningfully calibratable." Directional claim (deglobalization tightens labor markets at margin) survives; quantification does not. Self-assessed at 4/10.

**3. r-star increase of 15-30bp from geopolitical fiscal ratchet (macro_strategist_01, Claim 5)**
- **Killed in:** pair_1 debate
- **Reasoning:** Laubach-Williams confidence intervals span ±100bp, rendering a 15-30bp point estimate meaningless. The claim that defense spending "violates Ricardian equivalence" because it responds to security externalities is a non-sequitur — the motivation for spending is irrelevant to equivalence. Fiscal multiplier estimates cited (Ramey, Nakamura-Steinsson) are for output, not r-star — the mapping requires structural assumptions not provided. Direction is trivially true; specific r-star attribution is not credible. Self-assessed at 5/10.

**4. CLO recovery rates 10-20pp lower under deglobalization (credit_analyst_01, Claim 7)**
- **Killed in:** pair_1 debate
- **Reasoning:** Conflates two effects: (a) covenant-lite recovery drag (general, well-documented) and (b) deglobalization-specific structural impairment (novel, undemonstrated). Supply chain restructuring costs impair EBITDA but don't necessarily destroy asset values — newly built reshored capacity *adds* tangible asset value supporting recovery in liquidation. The 10-20pp figure appears reverse-engineered without a causal mechanism specific to deglobalization. Directional concern about CLO monitoring gaps survives; quantified recovery haircut does not.

**5. Friendshoring cost premium of 1.3-1.5x declining to 1.1-1.2x by Year 10 (generalist_02, Claim 3)**
- **Killed in:** pair_0 debate
- **Reasoning:** generalist_02's primary analogue (1980s VERs) is a poor fit — VERs restricted quantity on existing products through existing supply chains, not building greenfield processing infrastructure. More critically, generalist_02's own evidence contradicts the convergence timeline: US steel achieved cost parity "only after ~25 years and massive consolidation." If steel — a commodity product — took 25 years, semiconductor fabrication and rare earth processing reaching 1.1-1.2x in 10 years is unsupported. The initial 1.3-1.5x after subsidies may also be too low given TSMC Arizona's 50%+ cost overruns.

**6. Tariff + overcapacity "net effect close to zero for traded goods in aggregate" (challenger_01, Claim 3)**
- **Killed in:** pair_3 debate
- **Reasoning:** Works for sectors with massive Chinese overcapacity (EVs, solar, batteries, steel) but fails for sectors where supply chain fragmentation is most acute and where there is no Chinese overcapacity buffer: semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, aerospace components, specialized chemicals, medical devices. The "in aggregate" framing obscures the dispersion that matters most for portfolio construction and credit risk. The sector-specific offset survives; the aggregate characterization does not.

**7. Specific probability assignments in geopolitical scenario trees (risk_analyst_02)**
- **Killed in:** pair_3 debate
- **Reasoning:** Probability weights (55%/30%/15% for tariff scenarios; 45%/35%/20% for US-China competition) are presented as analytical judgment without derivation from base rates or models. 8% assigned to military confrontation is unfalsifiable on any reasonable timeline. These "give a false sense of quantitative rigor to what is essentially a qualitative assessment." The scenario *structures* are useful; the probability assignments are decoration.

**8. 3-5x amplification estimate for systemic tariff scenarios (risk_analyst_01, Claim 5)**
- **Killed in:** pair_2 debate
- **Reasoning:** No quantitative evidence supports this specific multiplier. The 2018-2019 trade war never reached the "systemic" threshold defined (universal 25%+), so there's no empirical calibration. Smoot-Hawley operated in a fundamentally different financial architecture (no swap lines, no floating rates). The figure "appears to be an assertion dressed as an estimate." The qualitative point about non-linear amplification above a threshold survives.

## KEY_DISAGREEMENTS

**1. Nearshoring capex multiplier: What is the true cost premium?**
- generalist_01: 1.8-2.5x aggregate, 4-8x for critical tail nodes
- generalist_02: 1.3-1.5x after subsidies → 1.1-1.2x by Year 10 (refuted)
- risk_analyst_01: 1.3-1.8x, rising to 2.5x+ with full ecosystem costs
- Debate narrowed but did not resolve: ~1.5-2.0x gross for brownfield/nearshore, 2.5-4.0x for greenfield advanced manufacturing. After subsidies: ~1.2-1.5x for subsidized sectors, 1.8-2.5x for unsubsidized. Learning curve reduction timeline is critical and uncertain (10 years? 25 years?).
- **Future research:** Need 2-3 years of operational data from TSMC Arizona and Intel Ohio to calibrate.

**2. AI productivity gains vs. deglobalization cost premium: who wins?**
- Flagged as the #1 open question by macro_strategist_01 and quant_researcher_02
- All agents agree AI benefits concentrate in services/knowledge work, not manufacturing
- Manufacturing productivity impact likely 5+ years away at scale
- generalist_02's base rate: 0/3 for technology offsetting trade costs within 5 years
- But if AI delivers even 0.5pp of productivity growth, it materially weakens the inflation floor shift and the margin compression driving credit deterioration
- **Future research:** Sector-level decomposition of which force dominates where. The net effect is not a single number — it varies enormously by industry.

**3. Is the deglobalization consensus trade offering positive or negative asymmetry?**
- challenger_01 (8/10): Crowded, inverted asymmetry, unwind risk exceeds remaining return
- Most other agents: Tail risks remain underpriced, structural shift is real
- Both can be true simultaneously: liquid-market positioning may be crowded while structural tail risks in cross-silo exposures remain unpriced
- **Future research:** Better measurement of non-observable positioning (corporate balance sheets, PE/VC, pension allocations) where crowding metrics don't exist.

**4. Can deglobalization avoid recession? (Base rate: 0/4)**
- generalist_02: Every prior episode of meaningful trade contraction was accompanied by recession
- Other agents implicitly assume managed transition is feasible
- Two factors favor optimism: sector-specific (not broad-based) fragmentation, and fiscal support (CHIPS, IRA)
- Two factors favor pessimism: current tariffs are broadening, fiscal support has attrition
- **Future research:** What is the minimum threshold of trade disruption that triggers recession, and is the current trajectory approaching it?

**5. Corporate revealed preference: Does it invalidate the structural deglobalization thesis?**
