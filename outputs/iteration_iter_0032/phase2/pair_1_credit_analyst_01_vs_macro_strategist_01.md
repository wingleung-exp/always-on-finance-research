## AGREEMENTS

**1. Tariff non-linearity threshold at ~15-25%.**
Both agents converge on a remarkably similar threshold effect. Agent A places the non-linear zone at 15-25% effective tariff rates (Claim 2), calibrated via coverage ratio arithmetic for BBB issuers. Agent B independently identifies ~15-20% as the pass-through inflection point (Claim 6), calibrated via Cavallo et al. and Fajgelbaum et al. empirical work on 2018-19 tariffs. Both cite Amiti-Redding-Weinstein (2019) for the 0.3-0.8pp CPI estimate. The convergence from entirely different frameworks — one bottom-up credit, one top-down macro — strengthens confidence in the threshold range. Combined evidence base: coverage ratio breakpoints + empirical pass-through elasticity estimates + the shared observation that the current effective tariff regime (~12-18%) sits right at the cusp of the danger zone. This is the single strongest consensus finding across both analyses.

**2. Reshoring capex is near-term stimulative but creates long-run fragility.**
Agent A (Claim 8): capex extends the credit cycle but debt-finances potentially inefficient capacity — "classic late-cycle misallocation." Agent B (Claim 4): capex is demand in the near term, ambiguously disinflationary in the long run, creating a "temporal mismatch." Both cite the 30-50% cost premium for reshored semiconductor production from the same KB concept (`geopolitical_supply_fragmentation_premium`). The difference is emphasis — A focuses on the leverage buildup, B on the inflation profile — but the directional claim is identical. Both flag that announced investment may not fully materialize (Agent B explicitly cites a 30-50% completion shortfall historical base rate).

**3. Bimodal outcomes dominate point estimates.**
Agent A frames this as bimodal HY default distributions (Claim 6: 2.5% vs. 7-9%). Agent B frames it as scenario-weighted tariff outcomes (Claim 6: three discrete scenarios with 50%/30%/20% weights). Both reject single-point forecasting for deglobalization outcomes. This is analytically correct — the policy-driven nature of deglobalization (as opposed to market-driven processes) genuinely produces discrete scenarios rather than continuous distributions.

**4. The Kalecki fiscal buffer is load-bearing but of uncertain durability.**
Both agents identify fiscal deficits at 6-7% of GDP as the primary reason credit markets can price the benign scenario. Agent A raises this in Claim 6 evidence and the fiscal dominance connection. Agent B builds it into the geopolitical ratchet (Claim 5). Neither resolves the durability question, and both correctly flag it as the key uncertainty.

## DISAGREEMENTS

**1. Magnitude of structural inflation impact from deglobalization.**

- **Agent A**: Treats this as an input from the KB (10-20bp), does not independently re-estimate it, and focuses downstream on credit implications.
- **Agent B**: Independently re-estimates at 15-40bp (Claim 1), arguing the KB's 10-20bp estimate omits the markup channel (Blanchard-Giavazzi 2003) and supply-chain-redundancy cost channel.

**Agent B is stronger here.** Agent A implicitly accepts the KB's narrower range without interrogating it, then builds credit analysis on that foundation. Agent B's decomposition into three channels (direct cost: 10-20bp; redundancy: 5-15bp; markup: 5-10bp) is more rigorous and better sourced. The markup channel in particular — reduced import competition raising equilibrium firm markups — is a well-established mechanism that the KB apparently underweights. However, Agent B's upper bound of 40bp may overstate the case: the three channels are not fully additive because redundancy costs and markup expansion both operate through marginal cost, creating partial double-counting. A fair range is probably 15-30bp, not 15-40bp.

**2. What is the primary transmission mechanism to asset prices?**

- **Agent A**: Credit spreads and default rates. Deglobalization is fundamentally a story about dispersion, fallen angels, and CLO portfolio impairment.
- **Agent B**: Inflation expectations, r-star, and correlation regimes. Deglobalization is fundamentally a story about the macro policy reaction function and risk premia.

**Neither dominates; they are genuinely complementary.** But Agent B's framing is more architecturally important because it nests Agent A's concerns: if r-star rises 15-30bp and the Fed over-tightens relative to optimal partial accommodation, that *is the mechanism* that triggers Agent A's maturity wall stress and refinancing cascade. Agent A treats the rate environment as exogenous; Agent B endogenizes it. Agent A's analysis would be materially improved by incorporating Agent B's policy-error channel.

**3. Is the rare earth supply chain risk priced?**

- **Agent A** (Claim 5): "Near-zero" rare earth disruption premium in IG spreads, with $400-600B in exposed IG debt. Estimates 15-30bp should be added to exposed issuers even at 5% probability weighting.
- **Agent B**: Does not address rare earth risk at all.

**Agent A is stronger on this specific point** but overstates confidence. The "near-zero premium" claim is Agent A's own estimate ("not market-observable," per the confidence assessment), and the $400-600B exposure mapping is admittedly approximate. The China rare earth restriction precedents (gallium/germanium 2023, antimony/graphite 2024) are real, but Agent A's comparison to the Russia 2022 sanctions shock overstates the analogy: Russia sanctions affected a globally traded commodity (energy) with immediate macro transmission, while rare earth restrictions affect intermediate inputs with longer substitution timelines. The "IG to credit-impaired within weeks" framing is dramatic but plausible only for a narrow subset of issuers with zero inventory buffer and zero substitution options.

**4. Recovery rates under deglobalization-driven defaults.**

- **Agent A** (Claim 7): Recovery values for deglobalization-affected sectors will be 10-20pp lower than historical averages due to structural impairment of going-concern value.
- **Agent B**: Silent on recovery rates.

**Agent A raises a legitimate point but the 10-20pp haircut is weakly supported.** The reasoning — that covenant-lite delays default recognition, allowing business impairment to compound — is sound in general but not specific to deglobalization. The same argument applies to any slow-moving credit deterioration. Agent A conflates two effects: (a) covenant-lite recovery drag (general, well-documented) and (b) deglobalization-specific structural impairment of going-concern value (novel, speculative). The latter requires the assumption that supply chain disruption permanently impairs business models rather than forcing adaptation — an assumption that ignores firm-level restructuring capacity.

## NOVEL_INSIGHTS

**Agent A only:**

1. **"Strategic geopolitical zombies" (Claim 4)** — the concept that subsidy-dependent issuers create credit profiles where default probability is a function of political durability rather than cash flow. This is genuinely novel and analytically productive. The implication that these issuers suppress aggregate default statistics while building contingent fiscal liabilities is an important masked-risk observation. Confidence is appropriately low (5/10), but the concept deserves development.

2. **CLO common-factor exposure (Claim 7)** — the observation that CLO sector diversification limits were calibrated during the globalization era and that deglobalization creates cross-sector correlation through shared China/Asia supply chain dependency. This is a structural portfolio construction critique, not just a credit call. The 15-25% exposure estimate needs validation, but the conceptual point — that ostensibly diversified CLO portfolios may have concentrated deglobalization beta — is valuable.

3. **Joint distribution of maturity wall + deglobalization (Claim 3)** — the specific argument that conditional default probability is far higher than the product of independent probabilities, with estimated correlation of 0.5-0.7 for exposed sectors. This is the kind of interaction effect that single-factor models systematically miss.

**Agent B only:**

1. **Tradeable/non-tradeable wedge reversal (Claim 3)** — the arithmetic showing that the 1995-2019 "globalization subsidy" (goods deflation subsidizing above-target services inflation) is now reversing, making 2% headline inflation structurally harder to achieve. This is clean, well-quantified, and directly actionable for inflation-linked asset positioning. The wedge framing is more useful than the aggregate inflation-floor estimate because it identifies the *compositional* shift.

2. **Correlation regime shift and ERP implications (Claim 8)** — the argument that supply-shock dominance structurally shifts bond-equity correlation positive, raising the equity risk premium by 30-50bp. This connects deglobalization to portfolio construction and asset allocation in a way Agent A entirely ignores. The 2022 calibration (simultaneous -20% equities, -15% long bonds) is the right reference point.

3. **Optimal partial accommodation framework (Claim 2)** — the formal NK result that the welfare-optimal response tolerates 60% of the inflation impulse. This matters because it defines the policy-error risk: if the Fed treats deglobalization inflation as demand-driven and tightens fully, the sacrifice ratio is 2-3x higher. This is not just academic — it's the mechanism that could transform a manageable supply shock into a recessionary credit event.

4. **NAIRU shift from effective labor supply reduction (Claim 7)** — theoretically interesting but Agent B correctly self-assesses at 4/10 confidence. The "1.5 billion workers removed" framing dramatically overstates integration — most of those workers were never in direct competition with advanced-economy labor markets.

## REFUTED_CLAIMS

**1. Agent A, Claim 5: "$400-600B in IG industrials and tech hardware" facing "binary credit risk" from rare earth disruption.**

The exposure estimate is too aggregated to be actionable and likely inflated. Agent A includes "general industrial users of permanent magnets, catalysts, and specialty alloys ($200-300B)" — a catch-all category where most issuers have multi-month inventory buffers and partial substitution pathways. The "binary" framing (IG to credit-impaired "within weeks") applies credibly only to a narrow subset: pure-play semiconductor equipment manufacturers and specific defense electronics contractors. For the broader $400-600B, a rare earth restriction would compress margins over quarters, not trigger overnight credit events. The Russia 2022 analogy is misleading because energy is a flow commodity consumed immediately, while rare earths are stock inputs with inventory buffers. **Survives as a directional risk; fails as a quantified claim.**

**2. Agent B, Claim 7: NAIRU shift of 10-25bp from "15-25% reduction in effective labor supply."**

Agent B correctly self-assesses this at 4/10 confidence, and it deserves that score. The Freeman (2006) "1.5 billion workers" figure describes gross labor force in countries that opened to trade, not the effective labor supply competing with advanced-economy workers. Most Chinese manufacturing workers were never substitutable for US service-sector workers. The domestic labor market channel from trade is real but operates through product-market competition (the Autor-Dorn-Hanson "China shock"), not direct labor supply. A 15-25% reduction in "effective labor supply" is not meaningfully calibratable from available data. **The directional claim (deglobalization tightens labor markets) survives; the quantification does not.**

**3. Agent A, Claim 7: CLO recovery rates will be 10-20pp lower due to deglobalization.**

As noted in disagreements, this conflates covenant-lite recovery drag (general) with deglobalization-specific impairment (undemonstrated). The 10-20pp figure appears reverse-engineered from the difference between historical leveraged loan recovery (~65%) and a hypothetical ~45-55% range, without a causal mechanism specific to deglobalization. Supply chain restructuring costs impair EBITDA but don't necessarily destroy asset values — in fact, newly built reshored capacity *adds* tangible asset value that supports recovery in liquidation. **The directional concern about CLO monitoring gaps survives; the quantified recovery haircut does not.**

**4. Agent B, Claim 5: r-star increase of 15-30bp from the "geopolitical fiscal ratchet."**

Agent B acknowledges the Laubach-Williams confidence interval spans ±100bp, which alone should disqualify a 15-30bp point estimate as meaningful. More fundamentally, the claim that defense/industrial-policy spending "violates Ricardian equivalence" because it responds to security externalities is a non-sequitur — Ricardian equivalence fails for many reasons (liquidity constraints, finite horizons), but the *motivation* for government spending is irrelevant to the equivalence proposition. The fiscal multiplier estimates cited (Ramey 2011, Nakamura-Steinsson 2014) are for *output*, not r-star — mapping from fiscal multiplier to neutral rate requires additional structural assumptions that Agent B does not provide. **The direction (more fiscal spending puts upward pressure on rates) is trivially true; the specific r-star attribution is not credible.**

---

## BOTTOM LINE

Agent A is the stronger analysis for **actionable risk identification** — the maturity wall interaction, CLO exposure, and strategic zombie concepts are specific enough to inform portfolio positioning. Agent B is the stronger analysis for **structural framing** — the partial accommodation framework, wedge reversal arithmetic, and correlation regime logic provide the macro architecture within which Agent A's credit risks play out.

The critical gap in both: neither adequately addresses the **AI-productivity offset**. Agent B flags this as the "highest-priority open question" but doesn't attempt even a directional estimate. If AI delivers even 0.5pp of productivity growth, it materially weakens several claims from both agents — it offsets part of Agent B's inflation floor shift and relaxes the margin compression driving Agent A's coverage ratio breakdowns. This is the largest unresolved variable in the deglobalization thesis.
