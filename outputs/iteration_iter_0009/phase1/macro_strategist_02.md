# Risk Appetite Regimes — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are endogenously generated by the credit cycle, not exogenously imposed by central bank policy.** The Minsky cycle — hedge → speculative → Ponzi financing — is the generative mechanism for risk appetite transitions, with central banks modulating tempo but never determining direction. The current analytical consensus overweights policy rates and underweights the private debt dynamics that actually govern regime duration and fragility.

2. **The sectoral financial balances identity (Private + Government + Foreign ≡ 0) imposes a binding arithmetic constraint on how long any risk appetite regime can persist.** The current configuration — private sector near balance, government deficit at ~6-7% of GDP, foreign surplus at ~3-4% of GDP — means that risk appetite is being sustained by fiscal transfers to the private sector. Any credible fiscal consolidation path mechanically forces the private sector into deficit, which is the precondition for a Minsky moment.

3. **The Kalecki profit equation reveals that current corporate profit levels — and therefore the cash flow validation of speculative positions — are structurally dependent on government deficits.** Profits ≡ Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports + Capitalist Consumption – Worker Saving. With investment growth decelerating and net exports negative, the government deficit component is now load-bearing for aggregate profits. This is the channel through which fiscal policy governs risk appetite, not through interest rates or "confidence."

4. **The US economy exhibits a bifurcated Minsky classification: households remain in hedge financing mode (moderate leverage, fixed-rate mortgages, elevated savings buffers) while the corporate sector — particularly leveraged credit and private equity portfolio companies — has migrated deep into speculative territory, with pockets of Ponzi financing in private credit.** This sectoral divergence means aggregate risk appetite indicators are misleading; the system's fragility is concentrated, not diffuse.

5. **The private credit complex ($1.7-2.0T AUM) represents a Minsky blind spot — it operates outside the original taxonomy's institutional mapping and has introduced mark-to-model opacity that delays the recognition of speculative-to-Ponzi transitions by 4-8 quarters.** The amend-and-extend culture in private credit is functionally equivalent to what Minsky called "refinance Ponzi" — where the inability to meet cash flow obligations is masked by lender forbearance rather than market repricing. This delays but amplifies the eventual regime transition.

6. **The credit impulse (second derivative of private credit) — not spreads, not VIX, not the yield curve — is the most theoretically grounded leading indicator of risk appetite regime transitions, because it captures the actual flow of purchasing power into the economy.** When credit impulse turns negative, the flow of new spending decelerates regardless of the stock of outstanding credit, initiating the cash flow squeeze that forces Minsky transitions.

7. **A "balance sheet recession" risk — where the private sector collectively shifts to surplus (debt repayment) regardless of policy rates — is the most underpriced tail scenario, and it would manifest as a risk appetite regime collapse that monetary policy cannot reverse.** The Koo framework, validated by Japan 1990-2005 and the Eurozone 2010-2015, shows that when balance sheet repair becomes the private sector priority, the zero lower bound is not a floor but a symptom. Current debt service ratios remain manageable, but the stock of private debt (household + corporate) near 150% of GDP means that any shock to income or asset values could trigger the shift.

8. **The stock-bond correlation regime is determined by the dominant macroeconomic shock type, and the current positive correlation reflects the ascendance of supply-side constraints (labor scarcity, energy transition costs, deglobalization friction) over demand deficiency as the primary macro risk.** In a positive stock-bond correlation world, Treasuries cease to function as portfolio insurance during risk-off episodes, which means that risk appetite itself becomes more fragile because hedging costs rise and portfolio rebalancing channels break down.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Endogenous credit, not central bank policy, generates risk appetite regimes

The mainstream "don't fight the Fed" framework treats risk appetite as a dependent variable of the policy rate. This inverts the actual causal mechanism. In the Post-Keynesian framework, banks create money endogenously by extending credit — loans create deposits, not the reverse (McLeay et al., Bank of England 2014). Risk appetite rises when banks are willing to lend against appreciating collateral, generating the Minsky cycle autonomously.

The critical evidence: the 2003-2007 episode. The Fed hiked from 1% to 5.25% — by the mainstream framework, this should have crushed risk appetite. Instead, risk appetite escalated to historically extreme levels because endogenous credit creation via securitization, CDOs, and shadow banking overwhelmed the policy rate signal. The HY spread trough and covenant-lite peak occurred *after* the hiking cycle was complete. This is not an anomaly — it is the *expected outcome* when bank credit creation operates through channels the policy rate does not govern.

The pair_2 debate in iter_0003 partially validated this: the Adrian-Boyarchenko-Giannone (2019) finding that financial conditions Granger-cause GDP — not the reverse — is the empirical nail in the coffin of the r-star framework. Financial conditions are themselves determined by endogenous credit dynamics, not policy rates alone.

### Claim 2: Sectoral balances as binding constraint

The identity Private + Government + Foreign ≡ 0 is not a theory — it is an accounting tautology that must hold in every period. Current approximate magnitudes (Q4 2025 / Q1 2026):

- Government sector: deficit ~6.5% of GDP → financial surplus flowing to non-government sectors
- Foreign sector: surplus ~3.5% of GDP (US current account deficit) → financial surplus flowing abroad
- Private sector: approximate balance (~0% to small surplus) → the residual

This means the US private sector can currently run near balance *only because* the government deficit is supplying ~3% of GDP net to the domestic private sector (6.5% deficit minus 3.5% foreign leakage). If fiscal consolidation reduced the deficit to, say, 3% of GDP, the private sector would be forced into a deficit of ~0.5% of GDP — meaning the private sector in aggregate would be spending more than its income and accumulating debt. This is the precondition for financial fragility.

The Eurozone 2010-2013 provides the devastating counterfactual: fiscal austerity forced the private sector from surplus to deficit → balance sheet stress → banking crisis → sovereign-bank doom loop. The private sector cannot collectively "tighten its belt" if the government is simultaneously tightening — the paradox of thrift applies at the sectoral level.

### Claim 3: Kalecki profit equation and fiscal dependency

Profits ≡ I + (G-T) + (X-M) + Cc – Sw

Current estimated magnitudes:
- Investment (I): moderate, ~17% of GDP, growth decelerating
- Government deficit (G-T): ~6.5% of GDP — historically elevated for expansion phase
- Net exports (X-M): negative ~3.5% of GDP — subtracting from profits
- Capitalist consumption (Cc): elevated but stable
- Worker saving (Sw): moderate post-pandemic normalization

The government deficit term is currently providing ~6.5% of GDP directly to aggregate profits (before the net export offset). If fiscal policy consolidated by 3 percentage points, the arithmetic implies a ~3% of GDP hit to corporate profits — approximately a 15-20% decline in aggregate S&P 500 earnings, all else equal. This is the transmission mechanism through which fiscal consolidation destroys risk appetite: it invalidates the cash flows that justify current speculative leverage.

### Claim 4: Bifurcated Minsky classification

**Households — Hedge financing:**
- Mortgage debt service ratio: ~4% of disposable income (historic low range)
- 90%+ of mortgage debt at fixed rates locked pre-2022 at <4%
- Excess savings largely depleted but revolving credit delinquencies, while rising, remain below 2019 levels for prime borrowers
- Net worth elevated by housing and equity appreciation
- Classification: **Hedge** — income covers debt service with margin; no refinancing dependency

**Corporate sector — Speculative, trending Ponzi in leveraged credit:**
- Non-financial corporate debt/GDP: ~50%, elevated but below 2020 peak
- Leveraged loan market: covenant-lite >80% of institutional issuance, B-rated leverage at 5.5-6.0x (public)
- Private credit portfolio companies: leverage at 6.5-7.5x reported, potentially 8-10x adjusted for EBITDA addbacks
- Interest coverage ratios declining as floating-rate debt reprices; ~15% of leveraged borrowers below 1.0x coverage (per S&P LCD)
- PIK toggle utilization rising — the definitional Ponzi marker (paying interest by issuing more debt)
- Classification: **Speculative** broadly (refinancing-dependent), with **Ponzi pockets** (PIK, payment deferrals, amend-and-extend) in private credit

The aggregate risk appetite reading is "moderate" precisely because household strength masks corporate fragility. But Minsky moments originate in the *fragile sector*, not the average.

### Claim 5: Private credit as Minsky blind spot

Minsky's original taxonomy mapped onto the commercial banking system with transparent balance sheets, mark-to-market discipline, and regulatory oversight. Private credit operates outside all three:

- **Mark-to-model:** A loan trading at 85 cents in the broadly syndicated market may be held at 97-98 cents on the NAV of a private credit fund. This is not fraud — it reflects different valuation methodology — but it delays the information signal that would force Minsky reclassification.
- **Amend-and-extend as institutional Ponzi:** When a borrower cannot meet covenants, the private credit lender has strong incentives to amend terms rather than recognize impairment (because recognition forces NAV markdowns, which triggers redemptions in semi-liquid vehicles). Amendment activity is estimated at 30-40% of 2021-2022 vintages. This is functionally identical to the extend-and-pretend behavior of Japanese banks in the 1990s.
- **Contagion channel:** Semi-liquid vehicles ($100B+ in non-traded BDCs and interval funds) create a feedback loop: NAV declines → redemption queues → gates → forced selling of liquid positions → contamination of public loan markets → further NAV declines. This channel has never been tested at scale during a multi-quarter downturn.

### Claim 6: Credit impulse as leading indicator

The credit impulse measures the *change in the change* of credit — the acceleration or deceleration of new lending. Its theoretical superiority as a leading indicator derives from the stock-flow consistency framework (Godley & Lavoie 2007): spending in any period equals income plus the *change* in debt. Therefore, the change in spending (which drives profits, employment, and risk appetite) depends on the change in the change in debt — the credit impulse.

When credit impulse turns negative, the economy does not need a recession — it needs a *deceleration* in credit growth, which is sufficient to squeeze cash flows at the margin and initiate Minsky reclassification. China's credit impulse dynamics in 2014-2015 and 2018 demonstrate this: no recession, but significant risk appetite contraction globally as the credit impulse turned negative.

The advantage over spreads: spreads are a *price* signal that can be distorted by technical flows (CLO demand, ETF inflows). The credit impulse is a *quantity* signal that captures actual purchasing power entering the economy.

### Claim 7: Balance sheet recession tail risk

The Koo framework (Richard Koo, Nomura Research Institute) identifies a specific macro pathology: when a debt-financed asset bubble bursts, the private sector shifts from profit maximization to debt minimization. Even at zero interest rates, firms and households pay down debt rather than borrow, because their balance sheets are technically insolvent or impaired. Fiscal policy is the only policy lever that works, because it provides the income from which the private sector can repair its balance sheets.

Current US private sector debt/GDP (~150% household + corporate) is not at Japanese 1990 levels (~220%), but it is elevated enough that a 20-30% decline in asset values (housing + equities) could trigger balance sheet distress for leveraged entities. The critical question is whether the income flows (sustained by fiscal deficits) remain sufficient to service the debt stock. If fiscal consolidation + asset price decline occur simultaneously, the balance sheet recession pathway opens.

This scenario is underpriced because the consensus framework literally does not contain a balance sheet recession model. The DSGE models used by central banks and most macro funds lack a banking sector and cannot generate this dynamic endogenously.

### Claim 8: Stock-bond correlation and supply-side dominance

The Campbell-Sunderam-Viceira (2017) framework correctly identifies that the stock-bond correlation depends on whether the dominant macroeconomic shock is demand-side (negative correlation: both stocks and bonds fall/rise together on demand) or supply-side (positive correlation: supply shocks push inflation up and growth down, so stocks fall and bonds fall).

The Post-Keynesian contribution: the supply-side constraints are *structural*, not transitory:
- Demographic labor scarcity (working-age population growth near zero in OECD)
- Energy transition costs (embedded carbon pricing, grid investment)
- Deglobalization and supply chain restructuring (friend-shoring cost premium)
- These create a persistent inflationary bias that keeps the correlation positive

In a positive stock-bond correlation regime, "risk-off = buy bonds" fails. This means that every risk appetite downturn is *worse* for multi-asset portfolios because the natural hedge is broken. Portfolio vol is 15-25% higher than what models calibrated to the 2000-2020 negative correlation period assume. This fragility amplifies risk appetite swings because investors lack cheap hedging and therefore reduce gross exposure, accelerating the risk-off dynamic.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Credit Cycles:** The Minsky cycle *is* the credit cycle viewed through the lens of financing structure rather than spread levels. Every claim above maps directly: the bifurcated Minsky classification is a credit cycle positioning statement; the credit impulse is the credit cycle's derivative; private credit opacity delays the credit cycle clock. Mainstream credit cycle analysis that focuses on spreads without examining financing structures is seeing the shadow, not the object.

- **Volatility Regimes:** VIX and realized vol are *symptoms* of Minsky transitions, not causes. The jump from hedge to speculative financing produces low vol (because cash flows cover obligations, no forced selling). The transition from speculative to Ponzi produces episodic vol spikes (as refinancing stress surfaces). The Ponzi-to-collapse produces the vol regime break. Low VIX ≠ low risk; it means the system is in hedge/early-speculative mode, which is actually the *precondition* for fragility accumulation.

- **Fiscal Policy & Sovereign Debt:** The sectoral balances identity directly connects risk appetite to fiscal policy. The MMT-adjacent insight — that a currency-issuing government faces no solvency constraint — is critical: the constraint on US fiscal deficits is political, not financial. But the *market* may impose a term premium penalty that operates as a *de facto* constraint, which links to the rates strategist's term premium regime change thesis.

- **Demographics & Secular Trends:** Labor scarcity supports the supply-side inflation thesis (Claim 8), which determines the stock-bond correlation regime, which in turn governs portfolio hedging costs and therefore risk appetite fragility. The demographic bid for safe assets (pension/insurance LDI demand) partially offsets the term premium story but is being overwhelmed by fiscal supply.

- **Equity Valuation:** The Kalecki profit equation connects fiscal deficits directly to the E in P/E. Current equity valuations embed an implicit assumption that fiscal deficits persist — if they don't, the earnings base contracts, making current multiples even more stretched than they appear on a cyclically adjusted basis.

- **Private Credit & Alternative Lending:** The Minsky blind spot (Claim 5) is a direct cross-topic connection. The opacity of private credit valuations means that traditional risk appetite indicators (derived from public markets) systematically understate financial fragility. Any risk appetite regime framework that relies solely on public market data has a structural blind spot proportional to the ~$2T in private credit AUM.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the real-time credit impulse in the US as of Q1 2026?** Fed flow-of-funds data has a 10-week publication lag. Bank lending surveys provide directional color but not the granularity needed to construct a Godley-consistent credit impulse measure. Is there a higher-frequency proxy that captures both bank and non-bank credit creation?

2. **What is the true leverage of the private credit complex when EBITDA addbacks are reversed?** The 8-10x estimate cited by credit_analyst_02 in iter_0003 is based on industry estimates with wide confidence intervals. Without forensic, loan-level analysis, the magnitude of the Minsky blind spot remains genuinely unknown.

3. **At what deficit-to-GDP level does the bond market impose a term premium penalty sufficient to offset the Kalecki profit support?** There is a point where fiscal deficits become self-defeating for risk appetite: the deficit supports profits but the term premium rise tightens financial conditions enough to offset. This is an empirical question with no clear answer — the UK gilt crisis of September 2022 provides a data point, but the US dollar's reserve currency status changes the threshold.

4. **Has the household hedge classification been maintained through 2026, or is consumer credit delinquency data signaling a migration?** Credit card delinquencies and auto loan delinquencies have been rising, particularly in subprime. If household balance sheets are transitioning from hedge to speculative, the bifurcated Minsky classification changes fundamentally — and the system-wide fragility assessment worsens materially.

5. **Can the Kalecki profit equation be disaggregated to distinguish which sectors capture fiscal deficit flows?** Aggregate profits may be sustained while sector-specific profitability diverges sharply. Defense/infrastructure/healthcare may capture fiscal flows while consumer discretionary/tech face earnings headwinds. This would produce sector-level risk appetite divergence that aggregate analysis misses.

6. **What is the empirical interaction between the credit impulse and the fiscal impulse?** Godley's models treat them as separable flows, but in practice fiscal spending may crowd-in or crowd-out private credit creation depending on the state of bank balance sheets and lending willingness. The causal structure of this interaction is undertheorized even within Post-Keynesian economics.

7. **How do we incorporate NBFI (non-bank financial intermediary) credit creation into the Minsky framework?** The original taxonomy assumed bank-intermediated credit. With NBFI credit (private credit funds, CLOs, money market funds providing repo) now constituting a large share of total credit intermediation, the Minsky cycle may operate differently — potentially with faster transitions because NBFI balance sheets lack the regulatory buffers and lender-of-last-resort access that slow bank-intermediated Minsky cycles.
