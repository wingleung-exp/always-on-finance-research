# Risk Appetite Regimes — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Term premium regime shifts are necessary but not sufficient for identifying risk appetite regimes; the interaction between term premium dynamics and the slope's rate-expectations component is the operative signal.** My prior iteration overstated term premium as a standalone identifier. The panel correctly noted that ACM and Kim-Wright diverge by 40-80 bp, rendering the level noisy in the middle of its range. The refinement: what matters is whether curve movements are *concordant* (term premium and rate expectations moving in the same direction, as in reflation or recession-fear regimes) or *discordant* (term premium expanding while rate expectations fall, as in fiscal dominance). Discordant moves are the highest-information states because they signal that different parts of the investor base are pricing different macro outcomes—the classic precondition for regime fragility.

2. **The four-regime taxonomy must be expanded to at least six regimes to account for post-2020 structural developments.** The prior taxonomy missed two regimes surfaced by the panel debate: (a) **Decorrelated Fragmentation**, where different asset classes inhabit different risk appetite states simultaneously (e.g., SVB March 2023: banking stress + credit resilience + equity vol spike + curve bull-steepening, all contradicting a unitary regime), and (b) **Microstructure-Amplified Momentum**, where 0DTE gamma concentration and passive flow dominance produce a steep curve with moderate term premium alongside extreme intraday fragility—a regime that looks benign on daily data but is structurally unstable at higher frequencies. These are not edge cases; they reflect the new market structure.

3. **The "fiscal dominance" term premium regime now has a quantifiable entry threshold: 10-year ACM term premium exceeding +40 bp while the 2s10s slope is steepening, *and* Treasury auction tail frequency exceeds 50% over a rolling 3-month window.** This triple-trigger framework addresses the panel critique that a single term premium level is insufficient. October 2023 fits: ACM TP rose above +50 bp, the curve steepened despite no change in Fed expectations, and 10y and 30y auctions tailed in 4 of 6 events in the Q3-Q4 window. The bill-coupon issuance mix shift in early 2024 (moving supply toward bills) reversed all three indicators, confirming the framework's sensitivity to the actual driver.

4. **Correlation volatility between bonds and equities—not the correlation level—is the rates market's most actionable contribution to cross-asset risk appetite assessment, and it is driven primarily by inflation uncertainty.** The stock-bond correlation has been regime-dependent throughout history: negative during the 2000-2020 disinflationary era, positive during the 1970-2000 inflationary era, and now oscillating between -0.4 and +0.6 on 60-day rolling windows. This volatility—standard deviation of rolling correlation roughly 0.25 vs. 0.10 in 2012-2019—destroys the diversification assumption underpinning 60/40 portfolios. The causal driver is inflation uncertainty: when the market is unsure whether the next shock is demand-driven (stocks and bonds move in opposite directions) or supply-driven (they move together), correlation becomes unstable. The MOVE/VIX ratio signals which regime is dominant: MOVE/VIX above 7 signals a rates-led, supply-shock-driven environment where correlation is positive; below 5 signals a demand-driven environment where the traditional hedge holds.

5. **The structural elevation of equilibrium term premium—driven by declining foreign official holdings, basis trade fragility, and reduced dealer intermediation capacity—means that risk appetite regimes now oscillate around a higher baseline, making historical comparisons misleading without level adjustment.** The panel validated the foreign demand claim (share declining from 33% to ~22%) and dealer capacity decline (~75% below pre-GFC peaks). Combining these with the basis trade overhang (+$800B notional), I estimate the structural shift in equilibrium 10-year term premium at +50 to +80 bp relative to the 2010-2019 average. This means that an ACM term premium reading of +30 bp today is analogously "loose" as -30 bp in 2017—what appears as modest compensation is actually an accommodative financial conditions posture once the structural shift is accounted for.

6. **The repo market has replaced the federal funds rate as the binding constraint on the persistence of risk-on regimes.** With Fed reserves at ~$3.2T (estimated early 2026) approaching the $2.5-3.0T "ample reserves" floor, the marginal determinant of whether leveraged positioning can sustain a risk-on regime is repo market functioning, not the policy rate. The SOFR-IORB spread is the early warning indicator: when it is stable and near zero, the plumbing supports risk-taking; when it spikes above +10 bp persistently or exhibits intraday volatility above historical norms, reserve scarcity is becoming binding. The September 2019 episode demonstrated that repo stress can emerge discontinuously—the system appears fine until it suddenly isn't, with the transition measured in hours, not weeks.

7. **Private credit's opacity creates a term premium externality in public markets that existing models do not capture.** The $1.8T private credit market, operating on quarterly mark-to-model NAVs, suppresses observable credit volatility and therefore observable risk appetite signals. But the debt servicing burden on private credit borrowers (floating-rate loans at SOFR + 500-650 bp, or roughly 9-11% all-in) generates real-economy fragility that eventually surfaces in public credit and rates markets. When private credit stress materializes—through rising amendment rates (15-20% in 2024-25 per panel findings), fund-level gate activations, or semi-liquid vehicle redemption queues—it will arrive as a *surprise* term premium event in public markets because the standard indicators (public HY spreads, default rates) will have provided minimal warning. This is the single largest unpriced fragility channel for term premium.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Concordance/discordance framework:**
The October 2023 episode is the clearest illustration. 10-year yields rose ~120 bp from July to October. Using ACM decomposition: ~80-85 bp was term premium expansion, ~35-40 bp was rate expectations (modest upward revision to terminal rate). These moved in the *same direction* (concordant) but from *different sources*—the term premium was driven by Treasury supply concerns (post-refunding), while rate expectations reflected resilient data. This concordance masked the fact that the drivers implied *contradictory* economic outcomes: fiscal supply pressure is bearish for sustainability, while strong data is bullish for growth. The concordance was unstable—it resolved in November when Bessent/Treasury shifted to bill issuance and term premium collapsed while rate expectations remained steady. By contrast, the 2022 inversion was also concordant (both term premium compression and rate expectations for cuts drove the long end lower), but from *aligned* sources (both reflecting recession pricing). Concordant-aligned moves are stable; concordant-misaligned moves are fragile. Discordant moves (term premium rising while rate expectations fall, or vice versa) are the loudest signal of regime transition in progress.

**Claim 2 — Expanded taxonomy:**
The prior four regimes (Reflation, Recession Fear, Complacency, Fiscal Dominance) mapped cleanly to 2020-2023. But the SVB episode (March 2023) produced a term structure signature that fit none of them: the curve bull-steepened dramatically (2s10s went from -107 bp to -40 bp in days), term premium was roughly flat (the move was entirely rate expectations as the market priced emergency cuts), yet equities fell, credit was resilient, and bank CDS blew out. This was Decorrelated Fragmentation—different asset classes reflecting different regime states. Similarly, early 2024 through mid-2024 featured moderate curve steepness and low term premium alongside VIX sub-13, but 0DTE volumes exceeded 50% of SPX options activity, creating a surface calm with structural intraday fragility. A taxonomy must accommodate these or it fails the most interesting recent episodes.

**Claim 3 — Fiscal dominance threshold:**
Beyond October 2023, I test the triple trigger retroactively: (a) The 2018 February selloff: ACM TP rose to ~+30 bp, but auction performance was normal (tails <1 bp) and the curve flattened. Two of three triggers absent—correctly excluded from fiscal dominance. (b) Late 2025 steepening (estimated): if term premium rose above +40 bp with auction tails widening on 20y and 30y, this would constitute a fiscal dominance episode. The three-trigger framework reduces false positives relative to term premium alone. The 50% auction tail frequency threshold is calibrated to be 2x the normal ~25% tail rate, signaling demand is persistently insufficient to absorb supply at prevailing yields.

**Claim 4 — Correlation volatility as the actionable signal:**
From 2000 to 2020, 60-day rolling stock-bond correlation averaged roughly -0.30 with standard deviation ~0.10. Since 2022, the average has been approximately -0.05 to +0.10 (near zero) but with standard deviation ~0.25. This means a portfolio manager calibrating a hedge to -0.30 correlation is wrong by more than 30 percentage points roughly 40% of the time—rendering the hedge worse than useless on an expected basis. The MOVE/VIX ratio captures this because rates volatility (MOVE) reflects inflation/supply uncertainty while equity volatility (VIX) reflects growth uncertainty. When MOVE dominates, supply shocks are the marginal risk and stocks and bonds sell off together. When VIX dominates, demand shocks are marginal and the traditional hedge works. The ratio hit 8.5 in October 2023 (rates-led stress, positive correlation) and fell to 4.2 in August 2024 (equity-led growth scare, negative correlation). Both levels correctly predicted the contemporaneous correlation regime.

**Claim 5 — Structural term premium elevation:**
I aggregate the contributions: (a) Foreign official demand decline: Warnock and Warnock (2009) estimated 80-100 bp impact from foreign official purchases. A 33% to 22% share decline, if linearly scaled, removes roughly 25-35 bp of the effect. However, the elasticity is likely non-linear (removal of price-insensitive buyers at the margin has outsized impact), so the estimate is 30-50 bp. (b) Dealer capacity decline: reduced intermediation raises the bid-ask spread and the risk premium demanded by the marginal buyer. This is harder to quantify but plausibly adds 10-20 bp to term premium in steady state, with episodic spikes of 30-50 bp during stress. (c) Basis trade fragility overhang: the option value of a potential forced unwind demands compensation from cash Treasury holders—estimated at 5-15 bp in steady state. Total structural shift: +45 to +85 bp, centered around +60 bp. This is consistent with the observable shift in ACM term premium from an average of roughly -30 bp in 2015-2019 to roughly +20-30 bp in 2024-2025—a ~50-60 bp upward shift.

**Claim 6 — Repo as binding constraint:**
The Fed's RRP facility peaked at $2.5T in late 2022 and has been drawn to near-zero by mid-2025, removing a shock absorber for money market stress. Bank reserves at ~$3.2T are approaching the estimated $2.5-3.0T "ample" floor, though the true threshold is uncertain and only revealed in stress. The SOFR-IORB spread has been a reliable early warning: it spiked during September 2019 (to +300 bp intraday), was stable throughout the RRP drawdown period (suggesting reserves remained ample), and is the single highest-frequency observable for plumbing stress. The key insight is that federal funds rate policy is second-order once repo functioning becomes the binding constraint—a 25 bp cut is irrelevant if repo financing costs spike 100 bp during a basis trade unwind.

**Claim 7 — Private credit term premium externality:**
The mechanism: $1.8T in private credit assets are floating-rate, mostly SOFR + 500-650 bp. At current SOFR levels (~4.3-4.5%), all-in costs are 9-11%. Borrowers are overwhelmingly mid-market companies with limited pricing power. Rising amendment rates (15-20%) signal stress is already occurring but is being managed through bilateral negotiation rather than public market repricing. When this stress exceeds the capacity for bilateral workout—through a maturity wall, a recession, or a rate environment that persists longer than the borrowers' cash runway—the credit losses will surface in public markets via (a) BDC stock price declines (observable, but small ~$100B market cap), (b) insurance company portfolio markdowns (larger, slower), and (c) CLO subordination erosion if leveraged loan defaults rise in sympathy. The term premium externality occurs because the *surprise* nature of private credit stress arriving in public markets creates uncertainty about the total quantum of losses, which is exactly the type of ambiguity that term premium is supposed to compensate for—but existing models have no way to price private credit opacity.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Volatility regimes:** The MOVE/VIX ratio framework directly connects yield curve regime identification to the volatility regime topic. The claim that VIX has degraded as a risk appetite barometer (0DTE microstructure, per the panel's challenger_01) reinforces the argument that MOVE—and rates volatility more broadly—is now the *primary* volatility-based risk appetite signal, inverting the traditional hierarchy. The correlation volatility claim also connects to vol surface dynamics: straddle prices on the stock-bond correlation (if they existed) would be at multi-decade highs.

- **Credit cycles:** The private credit externality claim (Claim 7) is a direct bridge to credit cycle analysis. The maturity wall ($300-350B sub-IG in 2026-27 per the panel) interacts with the term premium regime: if the maturity wall arrives during a fiscal dominance regime, refinancing costs compound because both credit spreads and the risk-free rate carry elevated term premium. The concordance framework also applies—credit spreads widening alongside term premium expansion (concordant) signals systemic stress; credit tight while term premium rises (discordant) signals a technical/supply-driven rates event that credit markets are ignoring, potentially creating a buying opportunity in duration.

- **Equity cycles:** The claim that term premium is more relevant for equity duration than rate expectations has direct portfolio implications. In a fiscal dominance regime, the equity risk premium should be measured against the term-premium-adjusted risk-free rate, not the yield level. This potentially explains the "puzzle" of tight equity risk premiums (by historical standards) coexisting with elevated macro uncertainty—if the structural term premium shift of +50-80 bp is not properly attributed, the equity risk premium appears lower than it actually is.

- **FX regimes:** The foreign official demand decline (Claim 5) is simultaneously a term premium and FX phenomenon. The recycling of current account surpluses into Treasuries suppressed both term premium and dollar depreciation pressure. The unwinding of this channel raises term premium *and* introduces structural dollar support questions. The correlation between EM risk appetite and the DXY is mediated through dollar-denominated debt (~$13T offshore per BIS), making US term premium dynamics a direct input to EM risk appetite.

- **Monetary policy transmission:** The repo-as-binding-constraint claim (Claim 6) implies that monetary policy transmission has bifurcated: the overnight rate affects the economy through the traditional bank lending channel, but the risk appetite regime is determined by the plumbing infrastructure (reserves, repo, collateral). The two can diverge—a rate cut could be stimulative for the real economy while failing to ease financial conditions if repo stress is simultaneously tightening financing for leveraged positions.

- **Demographics:** The existing knowledge base concept of "fiscal_demographic_term_premium" connects directly: aging populations drive higher fiscal spending (healthcare, pensions), which drives higher Treasury issuance, which raises term premium, which shifts the baseline around which risk appetite regimes oscillate. This is the slowest-moving but most powerful structural force in term premium determination.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the concordance/discordance framework empirically robust across enough historical episodes to be used for regime identification, or is it an elegant post-hoc rationalization of a small sample?** I need to backtest this against at least 20 major yield curve episodes going back to the 1990s, decomposing each into term premium and expectations components and classifying concordance versus discordance.

2. **How should the two new regimes (Decorrelated Fragmentation, Microstructure-Amplified Momentum) be integrated into a trading framework?** The original four regimes had clear curve trade implications (steepener/flattener/butterfly). What are the term structure trades for regimes that are either asset-class-specific or frequency-specific?

3. **At what level of reserves does repo stress become discontinuous rather than gradual, and can this be inferred from SOFR futures positioning or money market fund flow data before it manifests in spot SOFR?** This is the single most important real-time question for risk appetite persistence—and it is fundamentally unknowable until it happens.

4. **Does the private credit stress channel ultimately manifest as a term premium event, a credit spread event, or both—and with what lead/lag structure?** If private credit losses first appear in BDC equity prices (small, fast) before surfacing in insurance company portfolios (large, slow) before hitting public HY spreads (observable, medium speed), the term premium may only react at the third stage, by which time the opportunity has passed.

5. **How should the structural term premium shift (+50-80 bp estimate) be incorporated into term premium models themselves?** Current ACM and Kim-Wright estimates already embed the foreign demand and dealer capacity shifts implicitly through their fitted parameters—am I double-counting by adding a structural adjustment on top? Or are the models slow to capture structural breaks, meaning the adjustment is necessary in real time but will eventually be absorbed into the model estimates ex post?

6. **Is the 1965-69 analogue (identified by the panel as the most underappreciated parallel) consistent with the term premium dynamics of that era?** If so, what was the term premium trajectory during the transition from "sideways nominal" to "grinding real depreciation," and does it map to the current structural configuration?

7. **Can the MOVE/VIX ratio framework be extended to incorporate the 0DTE-driven degradation of VIX?** If VIX is structurally suppressed by systematic vol-selling, the MOVE/VIX ratio is biased upward, which would systematically over-signal rates-led stress. A "VIX-adjusted" version—perhaps using the put-call skew or VIX term structure slope as a VIX quality check—might improve the signal.
