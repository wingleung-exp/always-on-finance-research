## Open Questions

1. **Can the commodity ToT factor pass the HLZ threshold in commodity-exporter subsamples?** This requires a formal spanning test: regress commodity-exporter carry residuals (after dollar + momentum) on a commodity ToT factor and test t ≥ 3.0. If it passes, the FX factor model expands from 3 to 4 factors. If it fails, the non-linear interaction documented in M4 is real but not a distinct factor — it's a conditional amplification of carry.

2. **What is the true implementable capacity of FX carry strategies?** AUM in systematic carry has grown from ~$50B (2005) to an estimated $200B+ (2025). At what AUM level does the carry premium compress below the combined cost of implementation frictions (~15-30bp annualized for G10) plus the opportunity cost of capital? Market impact models for FX are less developed than for equities, and capacity estimates range from $100B (pessimistic, Garleanu and Pedersen 2013 calibration) to $500B+ (optimistic, assuming FX market depth scales with daily turnover of ~$7.5T).

3. **Does the agricultural inflation factor exposure in EM carry create a natural hedge or a hidden risk for investors with existing commodity exposure?** If the investor's portfolio includes commodity longs (direct or through equity exposure to commodity producers), the food-inflation channel in EM carry may be partially hedged. But if the investor treats EM carry as pure interest-rate exposure and sizes accordingly, the hidden commodity beta creates unintended concentration.

5. **How should the BoJ normalization scenario be incorporated into factor models?** Standard factor models treat central bank policy as a regime variable affecting all currencies symmetrically. BoJ normalization would represent a currency-specific structural break (USD/JPY carry collapsing by 100-200bp) that does not fit the systematic factor framework. Options: (a) treat JPY carry as a separate "Japan factor" with its own regime process; (b) model BoJ normalization as a jump process overlaid on the systematic carry factor; (c) simply reduce JPY carry allocation until BoJ uncertainty resolves. 6. **Is the "new equilibrium" Sharpe of 0.25-0.35 itself subject to further compression as systematic strategy AUM continues to grow?** The logic of factor crowding implies that as more capital pursues the carry premium, the premium compresses toward the cost of implementation. If the current 0.25-0.35 Sharpe already reflects partial crowding, continued AUM growth could push the equilibrium Sharpe below the threshold where carry is viable as a standalone allocation — leaving it useful only as a diversifying tilt within multi-factor portfolios.

If the correlation is 0.8+, they should be treated as a single risk factor for portfolio construction. If it's 0.4-0.6, they provide partial diversification within the "funding stress" category. The answer determines whether an investor with basis-trade-aware risk limits needs separate carry-trade limits or can treat them as overlapping.
