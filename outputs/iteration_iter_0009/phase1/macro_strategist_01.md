# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The Iran war oil shock has triggered a supply-side risk-off regime — the most analytically challenging configuration for risk appetite frameworks, and the one most likely to produce policy errors.** Unlike demand-driven risk-off events (2008, 2020), where the central bank can unambiguously ease, a supply shock pushes inflation up and output down simultaneously, paralyzing the policy reaction function. The March 18 FOMC hold at 3.50-3.75% — despite a softening labor market and VIX at 27 — is the revealed preference of a central bank trapped by supply-shock dynamics. This distinction between supply-driven and demand-driven risk-off is the single most important analytical lens for the current environment.

2. **The Phillips Curve constraint is now binding in real time, validating the claim from prior iterations that a steepened Phillips Curve shortens risk-on regimes and complicates risk-off responses.** With PCE inflation projected at 2.7% *before* the full pass-through of oil at $106/bbl, and the Fed's own projections revised upward on inflation, the central bank faces a binding tradeoff: easing to support growth would validate inflation expectations, while holding or tightening to anchor inflation deepens the growth hit. Benigno and Eggertsson's (2023) non-linear Phillips Curve — where inflation is more sensitive to positive supply shocks at already-above-target inflation — is being tested in real time.

3. **The Taylor Rule now prescribes *tightening* even as risk appetite deteriorates, creating a dangerous divergence between what markets need (easing) and what the inflation mandate demands.** With π ≈ 2.7% (and likely to rise toward 3.0-3.5% as oil passes through), π* = 2.0%, plausible r* ≈ 1.0-1.5%, and y ≈ 0 (output gap near zero but closing downward), a standard Taylor Rule (i = r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*)) prescribes a rate of approximately 4.4-5.2% — meaningfully above the current 3.50-3.75%. The Fed is already running a negative Taylor residual of 65-145bp, but unlike in prior cycles, this reflects an inflation-constrained choice not to tighten further, not an accommodation-driven risk-on impulse. The Taylor residual's *sign* misleads in a supply-shock context — this is a critical refinement to my prior claim about the Taylor residual as a regime identifier.

4. **The current regime is best classified as "stagflationary fragility" — a distinct state from both the "late-cycle risk-on" of iter_0008 and from classical demand-driven recession risk.** Asset allocation implications diverge sharply from demand-driven risk-off: nominal government bonds *fail* as safe havens in a supply-shock environment (because inflation expectations rise, increasing term premium), equities face margin compression from both the cost side (energy inputs) and the demand side (consumer spending diverted to gasoline), and credit faces the dual squeeze of rising input costs and a central bank unable to backstop. The 1973-74 oil embargo and 1979-80 Iranian Revolution are the closest analogues — both produced 30-40% equity drawdowns with bonds offering no offset.

5. **The central bank put strike price has moved discontinuously lower due to the supply-shock constraint, confirming and sharpening the iter_0008 consensus that the put is "structurally shallower and more conditional."** The Fed's March 18 hold demonstrates that the put is *unavailable* when risk-off is supply-driven. The put activates only when financial plumbing breaks (March 2020 SMCCF) or when inflation is sufficiently below target to permit easing. With PCE at 2.7% and oil at $106, neither condition holds. The implied put strike — the level of equity/credit deterioration at which the Fed would ease despite above-target inflation — is significantly lower than consensus estimates, probably requiring HY spreads above 600-700bp or a systemic financial plumbing event rather than the current 470bp.

6. **The credit-spread reflexivity mechanism identified in prior iterations (tight spreads → lower refinancing costs → delayed defaults → validation of risk-taking) is now operating in reverse, and the maturity wall ($300-350B sub-IG in 2026-27) converts this from a gradual adjustment to a forcing function.** With HY spreads at 470bp and widening, issuers approaching maturity face refinancing costs that have roughly doubled. Companies with 2026-27 maturities that assumed refinancing at sub-300bp spreads now face a viability threshold: can operating cashflows cover the incremental interest burden from repricing? For leveraged issuers with 5-6x debt/EBITDA facing simultaneous input cost inflation from oil, the answer is increasingly no. The "dual squeeze" (maturity wall + cost inflation) is the binding interaction that could transform gradual spread widening into non-linear credit deterioration.

7. **Dollar liquidity tightening via the oil-price channel creates a secondary, correlated risk-off impulse for emerging markets that amplifies the global risk appetite downturn.** The oil price surge mechanically tightens dollar liquidity for oil-importing EMs (India, Turkey, much of Southeast Asia) through current account deterioration, while simultaneously strengthening the dollar as petrodollar recycling flows shift. This activates the Miranda-Agrippino and Rey (2020) Global Financial Cycle mechanism: EM borrowers with $13T in offshore dollar debt face margin compression on both sides (local currency income down, dollar obligation costs up). The Minskyan framing — EM borrowers as structural Ponzi units in a strong-dollar environment — is directly applicable and implies EM risk-off that compounds the developed-market supply-shock dynamics.

8. **The demand-versus-supply shock identification problem — which I flagged as an open question in iter_0008 — is now the first-order determinant of correct asset allocation, and real-time identification remains extremely difficult.** The Iran war is unambiguously a supply shock *today*, but second-round effects (consumer confidence, business investment decisions, wealth effects from equity declines) create demand-side drag with a 2-4 quarter lag. The risk is a *compound shock*: supply-driven inflation prevents easing → demand deteriorates → unemployment rises → but inflation remains above target → the Fed is trapped. This compound scenario maps onto the 1973-75 experience (oil supply shock → stagflation → delayed and inadequate policy response → deep recession) and is the highest-probability path to a severe, sustained risk-off regime.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Supply-side risk-off as distinct regime:**
The IS-LM-AS framework makes the distinction clear. A negative demand shock shifts IS left: output falls, inflation falls, the interest rate falls — the central bank can accommodate unambiguously. A negative supply shock shifts AS left: output falls, inflation rises, and the interest rate effect depends on relative elasticities. The optimal Taylor Rule response differs by sign: ease for demand shocks, tighten (or hold) for supply shocks. Blanchard and Galí (2007, "Real Wage Rigidities and the New Keynesian Model," JMCB) formalized the "divine coincidence" breakdown: when supply shocks hit, stabilizing inflation and stabilizing the output gap become contradictory objectives. The Fed's March 18 statement explicitly acknowledges "elevated uncertainty" about the "implications of developments in the Middle East" — the language of a central bank recognizing it faces a supply shock without a clean policy response.

**Claim 2 — Phillips Curve binding:**
Pre-pandemic, the flat Phillips Curve (Del Negro et al. 2020, Hazell et al. 2022 QJE) allowed the Fed to tolerate positive output gaps without inflation consequences, extending risk-on regimes. Post-pandemic evidence (Benigno and Eggertsson 2023) suggests steepening, particularly at positive output gaps. The current configuration tests this further: with oil at $106/bbl (up 47% from pre-shock), the supply-side inflation impulse hits an economy where core PCE was already 2.7%. If pass-through is consistent with historical oil-price elasticities (Hamilton 2003, 2011: each 10% oil price increase raises headline CPI by 0.2-0.4pp), the Iran shock alone adds 0.8-1.5pp to headline inflation over 2-3 quarters, pushing PCE toward 3.5-4.2%. At that level, the Phillips Curve constraint becomes acutely binding — the Fed cannot ease without risking a 1970s-style inflation expectations unanchoring.

**Claim 3 — Taylor Rule divergence:**
Using the canonical Taylor (1993) specification with r* = 1.25% (midpoint of HLW estimates), π = 2.7% (Fed's own March projection, pre-full-oil-pass-through), π* = 2.0%, and y = 0 (CBO output gap near zero): i = 1.25 + 2.7 + 0.5(0.7) + 0.5(0) = 4.30%. With oil pass-through raising π to 3.5%, i = 1.25 + 3.5 + 0.5(1.5) + 0.5(-0.5) = 5.25%. The current rate of 3.50-3.75% implies a negative Taylor residual of 55-175bp. But critically, this negative residual does *not* signal accommodation in the way prior iterations' framework assumed — it signals a central bank *choosing not to follow the rule* because the supply-shock context makes strict Taylor adherence counterproductive. This is the exact weakness identified in the iter_0008 debate (pair_2): the Taylor residual is "undefined" or misleading during shocks that break the divine coincidence. I now accept this critique and incorporate it: the Taylor residual is informative for demand-driven regimes but unreliable during supply shocks.

**Claim 4 — Stagflationary fragility regime:**
The 1973-74 analogue is the most directly relevant historical template. Oil prices quadrupled; the Fed initially hesitated, then tightened; equities fell 48% (S&P 500, Jan 1973 to Oct 1974); 10-year yields rose from 6.4% to 8.0% (bonds failed as diversifier); gold rose 70%. The key mechanism: supply-driven inflation prevented the monetary policy easing that would have cushioned the equity decline, while simultaneously eroding real earnings. The current configuration shares the critical structural features: oil supply shock, inflation already above target, central bank credibility partially restored but fragile. The differences (larger fiscal deficit today, different labor market structure, passive/algorithmic flows) affect the velocity and amplitude but not the directional logic.

**Claim 5 — Central bank put conditionality:**
The iter_0008 synthesis correctly identified the "bifurcated put" (operational for plumbing breakdowns, dormant for asset price declines). The Iran shock sharpens this further: the put is not merely dormant but *structurally unavailable* because the inflation constraint is binding. The Fed's revealed preference on March 18 — holding rates despite VIX at ~27, HY spreads at 470bp, and equity markets under pressure — demonstrates the put strike is well below current market conditions. By comparison, in December 2018 (the last "pure demand" risk-off), the Fed pivoted at VIX ~36 and HY spreads ~540bp with inflation *below* target. The inflation differential (2.7% and rising vs. ~1.9% in Dec 2018) accounts for the put asymmetry.

**Claim 6 — Reverse credit reflexivity and maturity wall:**
HY OAS at 470bp represents roughly a 200bp widening from the tights of ~270bp in late 2025. For a typical B-rated issuer with 5.5x leverage and $500M in outstanding notes maturing in 2026-27, this widening translates to approximately 200bp higher coupon on refinanced debt, or ~$10M in additional annual interest expense — a 15-20% increase in interest burden. Simultaneously, oil at $106 raises input costs for energy-intensive issuers (transportation, chemicals, manufacturing) by an estimated 3-8% of COGS. The interaction is multiplicative: higher interest burden + compressed margins = accelerating covenant stress. The credit_analyst reports from this iteration of BDC stress in mid-market borrowers corroborate this mechanism operating in real time.

**Claim 7 — Dollar-EM contagion channel:**
Oil-importing EMs face a mechanical current account deterioration: India's oil import bill at $106/bbl is approximately $90B higher annually than at $72/bbl, equivalent to ~2.5% of GDP. This drains FX reserves, weakens the rupee, and tightens domestic financial conditions independently of any portfolio flow effects. The BIS's $13T in offshore dollar debt amplifies this: dollar strength forces EM corporates and sovereigns to defend currency pegs or absorb FX losses. The taper tantrum (2013) and 2018 EM crisis both demonstrated this channel. The current episode combines the oil-specific current account hit with the general dollar strength from safe-haven flows — a compound stress not seen since 1979-80.

**Claim 8 — Compound shock risk:**
The 1973-75 sequence provides the clearest historical template: oil shock (Oct 1973) → inflation surge (CPI from 3.6% to 12.3% by Dec 1974) → consumer demand deterioration (real PCE fell 2.4% in 1974) → corporate profit compression (S&P earnings fell 15% in 1974) → prolonged recession (16 months) → equity bear market (48% peak-to-trough). The critical feature was the *lag* between the supply shock and the demand destruction: the economy appeared resilient for 2-3 quarters before second-round effects overwhelmed. If the current shock follows a similar timeline, the demand-side damage from consumer spending diversion ($740/household annual gas cost increase = approximately $95B aggregate demand drag) and confidence effects would materialize in Q3-Q4 2026 — precisely when the maturity wall refinancing peaks.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Credit Cycles:** The reverse credit reflexivity (Claim 6) is the *credit cycle* manifestation of the risk appetite regime shift. The maturity wall converts a macro regime shift into forced credit events. The distressed ratio (credit_analyst_01's 5%/10% threshold framework) is the relevant real-time monitor: if the HY distressed ratio crosses 10%, the correction-to-downturn transition is in progress. The CLO arbitrage shutdown mechanism (iter_0008, HC Claim 3) likely activates if spreads widen further — with HY at 470bp, AAA CLO liabilities are repricing and arbitrage economics are deteriorating.

**Volatility Regimes:** The supply-shock regime produces a specific volatility structure: elevated equity vol (VIX ~27), elevated rates vol (MOVE likely elevated given term premium uncertainty), and elevated commodity vol simultaneously. The 0DTE microstructure degradation thesis (challenger_01) is being tested — VIX at 27 appears responsive to genuine stress, suggesting the VIX is "noisier" rather than "broken." The correlation volatility thesis (generalist_01) is directly relevant: stock-bond correlation is likely flipping positive (both selling off), destroying 60/40 diversification.

**FX Regimes:** Dollar strength is both a consequence of the supply-shock risk-off (safe haven flows) and a transmission mechanism that globalizes it (Claim 7). The EM contagion channel connects directly to the Global Financial Cycle (Miranda-Agrippino and Rey 2020). Oil-exporting EM currencies (ruble, real, MXN) may diverge from oil-importing EM currencies (INR, TRY, THB), creating within-EM dispersion not captured by broad EM indices.

**Fiscal Policy and Sectoral Balances:** The Post-Keynesian insight (macro_strategist_02) about fiscal deficits as the structural floor for risk appetite faces a critical test. The 6% US fiscal deficit should provide a cushion against demand deterioration, but if oil-driven inflation forces fiscal restraint (spending cuts to offset energy subsidies, or political pressure to reduce deficits), this floor erodes. The Kalecki profit equation channel (government deficit → corporate profits) is being offset by the energy cost channel (oil price → margin compression). Net effect is ambiguous and depends on the fiscal response.

**Private Credit:** The BDC stress reports corroborate the iter_0008 thesis that private credit "masks observable risk appetite through mark-to-model suppression." The current supply shock tests this directly: mid-market borrowers face simultaneous cost inflation (energy) and tightening credit conditions — the "dual squeeze." If private credit NAVs do not adjust to reflect this stress, the mark-to-model suppression thesis is strengthened; if they adjust promptly, the "genuine resilience" counter-thesis gains evidence.

**1965-69 Analogue:** The generalist_02's 1965-69 analogue (sideways nominal, declining real) now faces a more severe analogue competitor: 1973-74. The key differentiator is the oil shock. If the Iran conflict produces a prolonged energy supply disruption, the 1973-74 template (sharp stagflationary risk-off) dominates; if it resolves quickly, the 1965-69 template (grinding real erosion) remains operative. The current evidence strongly favors 1973-74 as the binding analogue for the next 2-3 quarters.

## Open Questions

1. **Duration of the Iran conflict and oil supply disruption:** This is the single largest source of uncertainty. A resolution that brings oil back below $85/bbl within 1-2 months would collapse the supply shock thesis and shift the regime toward demand-driven dynamics. A prolonged disruption (6+ months with Strait of Hormuz constrained) would validate the 1973-74 compound shock template. No macro framework can endogenize geopolitical outcomes — this is an irreducible uncertainty that dominates all other model parameters.

2. **What is the Fed's actual loss function weight on financial stability vs. inflation?** The March 18 hold reveals that at VIX ~27 and HY ~470bp, financial stability concerns are *insufficient* to override the inflation mandate. What level of financial stress would cause the Fed to ease despite above-target inflation? The 2022 UK gilt crisis (BoE eased despite 10%+ inflation) suggests the threshold is plumbing dysfunction, not asset price declines — but the Fed has not been tested at this scale since March 2020. Identifying this threshold is essential for estimating the put strike.

3. **Will second-round inflation effects from oil pass-through unanchor inflation expectations?** The University of Michigan 5-year inflation expectations and the Cleveland Fed's inflation expectations model are the key real-time monitors. If 5-year expectations break materially above 3%, the Fed's credibility restoration (inflation from 9% → 2.7%) is imperiled, and the Taylor Rule prescription shifts dramatically hawkish. This would convert a 1-2 quarter supply shock into a multi-year stagflationary regime, the worst-case outcome for risk appetite.

4. **Can the 6% fiscal deficit absorb the demand-side drag without becoming inflationary?** Fiscal policy is the only unambiguously available stabilization tool when monetary policy is inflation-constrained. But fiscal expansion into a supply shock risks amplifying inflation (Sargent 1982 on the fiscal theory of the price level). The optimal fiscal response — targeted energy subsidies, investment in alternative supply — differs sharply from the aggregate demand stimulus that was appropriate in 2020. Whether the fiscal authorities can execute this distinction is a political economy question, not an economic one.

5. **How does QT interact with the supply-shock risk-off?** The Fed is still conducting quantitative tightening (balance sheet runoff). In a demand-driven risk-off, QT would be paused immediately (as in March 2020). In a supply-driven risk-off, QT pause sends an ambiguous signal — is the Fed easing (inflationary) or merely preventing plumbing dysfunction (neutral)? The repo market stress indicators (SOFR-IORB spread) identified by rates_strategist_01 are the early warning system. If reserve scarcity tightens independently of the policy rate, QT becomes a shadow tightening channel that amplifies the risk-off regime transition.

6. **Is the current episode the "second leg" of the generalist_02's two-leg instability pattern?** If we date the first leg to the credit stress of late 2025 / early 2026, the Iran shock could be the exogenous catalyst for the second decisive leg. The pattern (4 of 5 historical instability episodes featured initial shock → partial recovery → second decisive leg) would place us at the inflection point. Alternatively, if the Iran conflict is genuinely exogenous and unrelated to prior financial fragilities, it represents a new first leg rather than a continuation. The answer depends on whether the pre-existing financial vulnerabilities (maturity wall, CLO arbitrage deterioration, private credit stress) amplify the geopolitical shock into a broader regime transition.
