# Central Bank Balance Sheet Normalization — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Central bank balance sheet policy is best understood within the NK framework as a non-standard extension of the Taylor Rule operating through the term premium channel, not as a separate policy instrument with independent objectives.** QE and QT alter the quantity of duration risk absorbed by the private sector, shifting the term premium component of long-term rates. In the Woodford (2012) irrelevance benchmark, balance sheet composition is neutral when markets are frictionless and the policy rate follows the optimal Taylor path. Balance sheet policy therefore only matters to the extent that it addresses market imperfections — specifically, preferred habitat segmentation (Vayanos and Vila 2021), collateral scarcity, and the zero lower bound constraint. The corollary is that balance sheet normalization should be evaluated by whether it restores the conditions under which the standard interest rate instrument is sufficient for macroeconomic stabilization — not by whether it reaches some arbitrary size target.

2. **The "optimal" terminal balance sheet size is determined by the reserve demand curve, which is a structural object that has shifted rightward and become steeper since the GFC due to post-crisis liquidity regulation (LCR, NSFR) and changes in payment system architecture.** Pre-GFC, the Fed operated in a "scarce reserves" regime with approximately $15-45 billion in aggregate reserves; post-GFC, the floor system requires reserves substantially above the kink in the demand curve. Copeland, Duffie, and Yang (2021) estimated that the reserve demand curve is approximately flat above a threshold (the "ample" region) and becomes near-vertical below it (the "scarce" region). The critical policy challenge is that the threshold is unobservable in real time, is likely time-varying as the financial system evolves, and can only be revealed by crossing it — as the September 2019 repo spike demonstrated when reserves fell to approximately $1.39 trillion. As of early 2026, with reserves near $3.2-3.4 trillion and the Fed continuing to run off its balance sheet, the question is whether the kink has shifted upward due to bank regulatory demand, Treasury General Account volatility, and standing repo facility (SRF) availability.

3. **Quantitative tightening is not the symmetric reverse of quantitative easing — the asymmetry arises from the nonlinearity of the reserve demand curve and the irreversibility of portfolio rebalancing effects.** QE compresses term premia smoothly because adding reserves to an already-ample system moves along the flat portion of the demand curve, while the duration extraction operates through the well-behaved preferred habitat mechanism. QT, by contrast, introduces discontinuous risk because draining reserves moves the system toward the steep portion of the demand curve, and the threshold is unknown. Furthermore, the portfolio rebalancing effect of QE (Krishnamurthy and Vissing-Jorgensen 2011) — whereby investors displaced from Treasuries move into riskier assets — does not simply reverse under QT because the capital structures, leverage positions, and risk appetites built during QE create path-dependent fragilities. The Bernanke (2020) observation that "QT is like watching paint dry" was premised on reserves remaining in the ample zone; the September 2019 episode falsified this within a year.

4. **The Reverse Repo Facility (RRP) functions as a liquidity buffer that masks the true proximity to reserve scarcity, and its depletion is a necessary — though not sufficient — condition for observing binding reserve constraints.** The RRP allows money market funds and GSEs to park cash at the Fed overnight, effectively absorbing excess liquidity that would otherwise remain in the banking system as reserves. When the RRP balance is large (it peaked at approximately $2.55 trillion in December 2022), QT drains liquidity from the RRP rather than from bank reserves, creating the illusion that the system can tolerate substantial balance sheet reduction without stress. As the RRP approaches zero (it fell below $200 billion by early 2025 and has fluctuated near $100-200 billion since), further QT will drain reserves dollar-for-dollar from the banking system, accelerating the approach toward the scarcity threshold. The Fed's decision to slow the pace of QT in June 2024 — reducing the monthly Treasury cap from $60 billion to $25 billion — was an implicit acknowledgment that the RRP buffer was being exhausted and reserve distribution was becoming uneven.

5. **Cross-central-bank balance sheet normalization introduces a global monetary tightening impulse that is greater than the sum of its parts, because reserve drainage in one jurisdiction can tighten dollar funding conditions globally through the FX swap basis channel.** The Fed, ECB, and BOJ collectively expanded their balance sheets by approximately $12-13 trillion during 2020-2022. The Fed has been running QT since June 2022 and has reduced its balance sheet by roughly $1.8-2.0 trillion. The ECB began passive QT (non-reinvestment of PEPP and APP maturities) and has reduced its balance sheet by approximately €1.8-2.0 trillion from its peak. The BOJ remains the outlier, having only begun tentative normalization in 2024 by modifying YCC and allowing some JGB roll-off. The global dimension matters because the USD is the dominant funding currency: when Fed QT tightens dollar liquidity, it can widen the cross-currency basis, making dollar funding more expensive for non-US banks, which tightens global financial conditions beyond what any domestic Taylor Rule would prescribe. Avdjiev, Du, Koch, and Shin (2019) documented this "dollar shortage" channel as a key transmission mechanism of US monetary policy to the global economy.

6. **The interaction between QT and Treasury supply dynamics creates a compound duration absorption problem that is unprecedented in scale.** The US Treasury is simultaneously issuing large volumes of new debt (financing 6-7% of GDP deficits) while the Fed is allowing existing holdings to mature without reinvestment. The private sector must absorb both new issuance and the Fed's roll-off — a combined net supply that is substantially larger than anything pre-GFC. Greenwood, Hanson, Rudolph, and Summers (2014) showed that the maturity composition of government debt affects term premia: heavy issuance at the long end requires higher term premia to attract buyers. The Fed's QT compounds this by removing the price-insensitive marginal buyer precisely when supply is elevated. The implication is that the equilibrium term premium during QT-plus-fiscal-expansion is materially higher than during QT alone or fiscal expansion alone — the effects are likely super-additive rather than merely additive because marginal buyer capacity is finite and convex in the risk premium required.

7. **Within the New Keynesian framework, balance sheet normalization alters the transmission mechanism of conventional monetary policy by changing the slope of the IS curve and the sensitivity of financial conditions to the policy rate.** When the central bank holds a large portfolio of long-duration assets, changes in the policy rate are partially offset by the portfolio's anchoring effect on long rates — the "stock effect" of QE holdings acts as a persistent compression of term premia that dulls the transmission of rate hikes to the real economy. As QT reduces the portfolio, this anchoring effect diminishes, and the IS curve steepens — meaning a given change in the policy rate produces a larger change in financial conditions and output. This has direct implications for the Taylor Rule calibration: the optimal policy rate path during QT should be less aggressive (smaller rate moves) than the Taylor Rule would prescribe in the absence of QT, because QT itself is providing additional tightening through the term premium channel. Failure to account for this interaction — running the Taylor-prescribed rate path concurrently with QT — risks over-tightening, as arguably occurred during 2022-2023 when the combined effect of 525bp of rate hikes and $95 billion/month QT produced financial stress (SVB, regional banks) that a standard Taylor Rule analysis would not have predicted.

8. **The endgame of balance sheet normalization is not a return to the pre-GFC operating framework but a "new normal" floor system with a permanently larger balance sheet, and the key policy question is whether this new steady state introduces structural inflation bias or financial stability risks through the fiscal-monetary nexus.** The Fed has explicitly stated it will continue operating a floor system (ample reserves), which requires a balance sheet substantially larger than pre-GFC levels. As of early 2026, estimates of the "minimum comfortable" reserve level range from $2.5-3.5 trillion (compared to $15-45 billion pre-GFC). This means the Fed will hold a permanent portfolio of $5-6+ trillion in securities (reserves plus currency in circulation plus other liabilities). The NK framework raises a concern: a central bank that permanently holds a large portfolio of government debt is effectively monetizing a portion of the fiscal deficit on a structural basis. Under Ricardian equivalence this is neutral, but if agents perceive the central bank's portfolio as reducing the fiscal cost of deficits (by returning interest income to the Treasury), it may weaken fiscal discipline — a "fiscal dominance" risk that Sargent and Wallace (1981) formalized as "unpleasant monetarist arithmetic." The question is whether the permanently larger balance sheet represents a benign operational choice or the thin end of a wedge toward fiscal-monetary entanglement.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Balance sheet policy as Taylor Rule extension:**
The theoretical foundation is Woodford (2012), who demonstrated that in a representative-agent NK model with complete markets, the composition of the central bank's balance sheet is irrelevant for macroeconomic outcomes — the "Wallace neutrality" result extended to the modern context. Balance sheet policy only gains traction through market segmentation (Vayanos and Vila 2021 preferred habitat model), limits to arbitrage (He and Krishnamurthy 2013), or the zero lower bound. The key empirical finding from the QE literature is that QE operates primarily through the term premium channel: Gagnon, Raskin, Remache, and Sack (2011) estimated that QE1 reduced 10-year term premia by 30-100 basis points, and D'Amico and King (2013) found localized supply effects in specific maturities consistent with preferred habitat. The implication for normalization is clear: QT should be expected to raise term premia by reversing these supply effects, and the magnitude should depend on the same preferred habitat parameters — the elasticity of demand across maturities and the degree of market segmentation. Treating balance sheet normalization as a term premium adjustment within the broader policy stance — rather than as a separate, parallel instrument — is essential for policy coherence. The Fed's attempts to run QT on "autopilot" while actively managing the fed funds rate represent an implicit claim that the two instruments can be separated, which the NK framework suggests is only approximately true in the ample-reserves region.

**Claim 2 — Reserve demand curve and optimal terminal size:**
The empirical challenge is that the reserve demand curve is a structural relationship that has shifted substantially and can only be partially identified from observed data. Pre-GFC, the Fed operated with minimal reserves and relied on open market operations to hit the fed funds target; the reserve demand curve was well-understood because it was regularly probed. Post-GFC, the floor system means the Fed operates in the flat region of the demand curve, and the kink is never tested under normal conditions — it is only revealed in stress. Copeland, Duffie, and Yang (2021) used the September 2019 episode to estimate the kink: reserves fell to approximately $1.39 trillion, and the repo rate spiked 300+ basis points intraday, indicating the system had crossed from ample to scarce. However, this threshold is not fixed: Lopez-Salido and Vissing-Jorgensen (2024) argue that the LCR and NSFR requirements have created a "regulatory demand" for reserves that shifts the demand curve rightward by $500 billion to $1 trillion relative to the 2019 level. The Fed's own Senior Financial Officer Survey (2024-2025) suggests banks' stated "lowest comfortable level of reserves" (LCLOR) aggregates to approximately $2.8-3.2 trillion, though individual bank responses are highly heterogeneous and likely upwardly biased by strategic incentives. The TGA (Treasury General Account) adds further complication: TGA balances of $500-900 billion represent reserves that are temporarily withdrawn from the banking system but can be released rapidly when the Treasury spends, creating reserve volatility that interacts with the proximity to the scarcity threshold.

**Claim 3 — QE/QT asymmetry:**
The theoretical asymmetry was anticipated by Stein (2012), who argued that the financial stability implications of large-scale asset purchases are asymmetric: QE encourages risk-taking and leverage-building that cannot be smoothly unwound. Empirically, Greenwood, Hanson, and Stein (2016) showed that the maturity transformation undertaken by the financial sector during QE — shadow banks issuing short-term liabilities to fund long-duration assets — creates fragility that is exposed during QT when term premia rise and duration losses accumulate. The SVB episode in March 2023 is a partial illustration: the bank held a large portfolio of long-duration MBS and Treasuries acquired during QE, and the combined effect of rate hikes and QT-induced term premium increases generated unrealized losses that triggered a deposit run. The broader point is that QE creates a portfolio allocation and leverage state across the financial system that is path-dependent — unwinding it is not simply "QE in reverse" because the financial system has endogenously restructured around the QE regime. The Brunnermeier and Sannikov (2014) model of the "paradox of volatility" captures this: prolonged low-vol environments (QE) encourage leverage and risk-taking, creating the conditions for a violent reversal when the regime shifts (QT).

**Claim 4 — RRP as liquidity buffer:**
The RRP facility was introduced to provide a floor for money market rates by offering an alternative investment for non-bank institutions that cannot hold reserves directly. During QE, the RRP absorbed excess liquidity: money market funds preferred the guaranteed RRP rate to near-zero repo rates, parking trillions at the facility. This created a layered liquidity structure: RRP → reserves → bank lending. During QT, the drain initially came from the RRP layer: as the Fed reduced its Treasury holdings, the corresponding reduction in money market liquidity was absorbed by money funds reducing their RRP balances rather than by banks reducing reserves. This sequence was stabilizing — it allowed QT to proceed without reserve scarcity. The data confirm: between January 2023 and early 2025, aggregate reserves remained relatively stable near $3.2-3.5 trillion while RRP balances fell from $2.2 trillion to under $200 billion, indicating that QT was draining the "RRP buffer" rather than bank reserves. The policy-relevant inference is that with RRP near exhaustion, the character of QT has fundamentally changed: further balance sheet reduction now drains reserves directly, and the approach toward the scarcity threshold accelerates. The Fed's June 2024 taper of the QT pace was a recognition of this phase transition.

**Claim 5 — Global QT and the dollar funding channel:**
The dollar-centric global financial system means that Fed balance sheet policy has extraterritorial effects. Shin (2012) documented the role of the dollar in global banking: non-US banks hold approximately $10-12 trillion in dollar-denominated assets funded through wholesale dollar markets. When Fed QT drains dollar reserves, it tightens conditions in the FX swap market, where non-US banks borrow dollars. The cross-currency basis — the premium paid for dollar funding through FX swaps — is a direct measure of this strain. During 2022-2023 QT, the EUR/USD 3-month cross-currency basis widened from near zero to -25 to -40 basis points, indicating dollar funding stress. The ECB's concurrent balance sheet reduction amplified this because it reduced euro-area bank reserves that could be swapped into dollars. The BOJ's unique position — maintaining massive balance sheet while the Fed and ECB tighten — creates a carry trade incentive structure (borrow yen, buy dollars) that partly alleviates dollar funding stress but introduces FX volatility risk (the yen carry unwind vulnerability). The global coordination problem is that no central bank internalizes the cross-border spillovers of its QT program, and the aggregate effect on global dollar liquidity is unmonitored and potentially nonlinear.

**Claim 6 — QT-plus-Treasury-supply compound effect:**
Estimates of the combined private-sector duration absorption requirement are striking. The CBO projects net Treasury issuance of approximately $1.8-2.2 trillion per annum for fiscal years 2025-2027. The Fed's QT, even at the tapered pace, removes approximately $300-400 billion in annual demand. Net private absorption therefore needs to run at $2.1-2.6 trillion per year — compared to a pre-pandemic norm of $500-800 billion. Greenwood, Hanson, Rudolph, and Summers (2014) estimated that each 1 percentage point increase in the weighted-average maturity of outstanding Treasuries raises the 10-year term premium by approximately 5-8 basis points. The Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee (TBAC) has responded by shifting issuance toward the front end (T-bills), which reduces the duration absorption requirement but increases rollover risk and short-term funding pressure. The equilibrium term premium should reflect the supply-demand balance: more duration to absorb → higher term premia → steeper yield curve. The interaction with QT is super-additive because the removal of the price-insensitive central bank buyer increases the volatility of demand at Treasury auctions, raising the "auction risk premium" that primary dealers require to underwrite large offerings. Weak Treasury auction tails in late 2023 and periodically through 2025 provide anecdotal evidence for this channel.

**Claim 7 — QT altering the monetary transmission mechanism:**
This claim follows from the NK model with a term structure (Rudebusch and Swanson 2012). In the standard 3-equation NK model, the policy rate maps directly to financial conditions through the IS curve, and the term premium is either constant or treated as exogenous. When QT makes the term premium endogenous to the balance sheet size, the effective monetary stance becomes a function of both the policy rate and the balance sheet — a two-instrument problem. The "stock effect" of QE (the persistent term premium compression from the outstanding portfolio) has been estimated at 50-100 basis points for the Fed's holdings as of 2024 (Christensen and Rudebusch 2012; Bonis, Ihrig, and Wei 2017). As QT reduces the portfolio, this stock effect diminishes, and term premia rise, tightening financial conditions independent of the fed funds rate. The implication for the Taylor Rule is that the appropriate "neutral" fed funds rate is lower during QT than otherwise — the term premium increase is doing some of the tightening work. If the FOMC fails to account for this and sets the fed funds rate according to a Taylor Rule that treats the term premium as exogenous, it will systematically over-tighten. The difficulty is calibration: the term premium is not directly observable, and estimates (Adrian, Crump, and Moench 2013; Kim and Wright 2005) diverge substantially, making it hard to adjust the Taylor Rule prescription in real time.

**Claim 8 — The new normal and fiscal-monetary nexus:**
The permanently larger balance sheet has implications that extend beyond monetary operations. When the Fed holds $5-6 trillion in Treasuries and MBS, it earns interest on those holdings and remits profits (net of operating expenses and the interest paid on reserves) to the Treasury. During 2010-2021, these remittances averaged $80-100 billion annually — a meaningful fiscal transfer. During 2022-2024, the Fed operated at a net loss ("deferred asset") as the interest paid on reserves exceeded the income earned on lower-yielding legacy holdings. As rates normalize and the portfolio turns over, remittances will resume, but at a level that depends on the spread between the portfolio yield and the IOER rate. The fiscal-monetary entanglement is this: a larger Fed balance sheet means larger remittances (when positive), which reduces the fiscal deficit and the net issuance of Treasuries — creating a feedback loop where the central bank's operational framework affects fiscal sustainability. Sargent and Wallace (1981) showed that if fiscal policy is "dominant" — if the government sets the primary deficit without regard to debt sustainability — then the central bank must eventually monetize the debt, regardless of its inflation target. The permanently larger balance sheet may lower the political barrier to fiscal dominance by making central bank money creation a less visible mechanism. Bianchi and Melosi (2022) formalized a modern version: fiscal inflation can arise even with a credibly inflation-targeting central bank if fiscal policy creates expectations that the central bank will eventually accommodate.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary Policy (monetary_policy):** The balance sheet is inseparable from the overall monetary policy stance. The Taylor Rule must be augmented to account for the term premium channel of QT — the "shadow policy rate" that includes both the fed funds rate and the financial conditions tightening from balance sheet reduction is the operationally relevant measure of stance. The existing KB concept `central_bank_reaction_function_uncertainty` (from iter_0010) is directly relevant: uncertainty about how the Fed weighs the balance sheet instrument against the rate instrument is a distinct source of policy reaction function ambiguity. Forward guidance about QT pace and terminal size is as important as rate guidance, yet has historically received less structured communication — the Fed's "background QT" approach compounds reaction function uncertainty.

**Yield Curve Dynamics (yield_curve_dynamics):** Balance sheet normalization is a first-order driver of the term premium, which is the primary determinant of yield curve shape at maturities beyond the policy-rate-sensitive front end. The compound effect of QT plus Treasury supply (Claim 6) predicts structurally steeper yield curves — higher term premia at the long end — than would prevail under QT alone. The existing KB depth on yield curve dynamics (confidence 6.5, depth level 3) should incorporate the balance sheet channel explicitly: any term premium decomposition that does not account for the central bank's portfolio is mis-specified. The convexity of the reserve demand curve (Claim 2) also connects to the curvature of the yield curve through the money market pass-through — reserve scarcity events produce front-end volatility spikes that can propagate to curve inversions or dislocations.

**Volatility Regimes (volatility_regimes):** The existing KB concept `qt_dual_vol_channels` (confidence 7, iter_0010) identified reserve scarcity and duration extraction as QT's volatility transmission channels. This analysis deepens that concept by specifying the RRP depletion mechanism as the trigger that shifts QT from the benign "RRP drainage" phase to the volatile "reserve drainage" phase. The implication for volatility regime analysis is that the current environment (RRP near exhaustion, reserves approaching uncertain thresholds) represents an elevated probability of a volatility regime transition catalyzed by reserve scarcity — similar to the pre-September-2019 configuration but at a different scale and with the SRF as a partial mitigant.

**Fiscal Policy & Fiscal Dominance (fiscal_policy, fiscal_dominance):** The permanently larger balance sheet and the remittance channel create a direct fiscal-monetary linkage that is relevant to the fiscal policy divergence analysis from iter_0011. The existing concepts on US fiscal deficits at near-full employment (6-7% of GDP) take on additional significance in the context of balance sheet normalization: the central bank's ability to normalize its balance sheet is constrained by the fiscal authority's borrowing needs, because the combined absorption of QT roll-off and new issuance may exceed private sector capacity, forcing the central bank to slow or pause QT regardless of its monetary policy objectives. This is a mechanism through which fiscal dominance can manifest even without explicit monetary financing — the balance sheet becomes the transmission channel.

**Credit Cycles (credit_cycles):** QT tightens credit conditions through multiple channels: higher term premia increase corporate borrowing costs, reserve drainage tightens bank funding and potentially constrains lending, and the removal of the price-insensitive marginal buyer for Treasuries and MBS increases the credit risk premium across the investment-grade curve (since corporate spreads are priced relative to Treasuries). The financial accelerator mechanism (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist 1999) predicts that these effects are amplified when corporate leverage is elevated — and US corporate leverage as of 2025-2026 is near post-GFC highs in certain segments (leveraged loans, private credit). The credit cycle sensitivity to QT is therefore state-dependent and potentially nonlinear.

**FX Regimes (fx_regimes):** The cross-central-bank balance sheet normalization (Claim 5) directly connects to the FX regime analysis from iter_0012. The BOJ's delayed normalization relative to the Fed and ECB supports yen carry trades, while the dollar funding channel of Fed QT introduces a structural bid for dollars that is independent of rate differentials. The existing KB concept `boj_normalization_catalyst` (confidence 6.75) should be updated to incorporate the balance sheet dimension: BOJ normalization that reduces JGB holdings would release duration to the private sector and drain yen reserves, potentially triggering the carry unwind through a liquidity channel rather than a rate channel.

## Open Questions

1. **Where is the reserve scarcity threshold as of 2026, and how has it shifted since 2019?** The September 2019 episode placed the threshold near $1.39 trillion, but regulatory changes (more stringent LCR interpretation, intraday liquidity requirements), the introduction of the SRF, and changes in reserve distribution across banks have likely shifted it. The Fed's surveys suggest banks' aggregate "lowest comfortable" reserve level is $2.8-3.2 trillion, but survey responses may be strategically inflated. A structural estimate of the current demand curve kink — accounting for regulatory demand, TGA volatility, and SRF take-up capacity — is the most important quantitative gap in this analysis.

2. **Does the Standing Repo Facility (SRF) fundamentally alter the reserve scarcity dynamics, or does it merely shift the stress from repo markets to the SRF itself?** If the SRF is effective and unlimited in practice, it functions as an elastic supply of reserves that prevents the system from ever truly entering the scarce-reserves region — effectively making the reserve demand curve flat everywhere. But SRF access is limited to primary dealers and eligible depository institutions, its pricing may carry stigma, and its capacity under stress is untested. Whether the SRF is a genuine structural change to the operating framework or a backstop that will prove inadequate when needed is an open empirical question.

3. **How should the Taylor Rule be modified to incorporate the balance sheet instrument — is an additive "shadow rate" approach sufficient, or is a non-linear specification required?** The Wu-Xia shadow rate framework (Wu and Xia 2016) provides a linear mapping from the balance sheet to an equivalent policy rate, but this assumes constant elasticity. If the term premium response to balance sheet changes is nonlinear — steeper when reserves are near the scarcity threshold — then a linear shadow rate underestimates the tightening from QT in the current environment. A state-dependent Taylor Rule that conditions the balance sheet adjustment on the proximity to reserve scarcity has not been developed in the literature.

4. **What is the interaction between QT and bank capital regulation — does QT mechanically reduce the banking system's capacity to intermediate Treasury markets?** When reserves decline, banks' high-quality liquid assets (HQLA) for LCR purposes also decline. If banks respond by reducing Treasury inventory (to conserve balance sheet capacity), this reduces dealer intermediation capacity in Treasury markets, which widens bid-ask spreads and increases auction risk — a "Treasury market functioning" channel of QT that operates independently of the term premium channel. The October 2023 Treasury market stress and periodic episodes of poor market depth suggest this channel may be active.

5. **How do the ECB and BOJ balance sheet normalization paths interact with the Fed's, and is there a Nash equilibrium in global QT pacing?** Each central bank's QT pace affects global dollar liquidity, cross-currency bases, and the term premium in its own sovereign bond market. The ECB's passive QT (non-reinvestment) is slower and more predictable than the Fed's, while the BOJ is only beginning. If all three normalize simultaneously, the aggregate duration supply to the private sector could overwhelm absorption capacity. If they coordinate (formally or informally), the pace is slower but the duration is longer. The game-theoretic structure of this problem — each central bank optimizing for domestic conditions while generating cross-border externalities — has not been analyzed in the academic literature.

6. **Does the permanently larger balance sheet introduce a structural inflation bias by lowering the perceived fiscal cost of deficits?** The remittance channel means that a portion of the interest on government debt is effectively rebated through the central bank. If politicians internalize this as reducing the net cost of borrowing, the political equilibrium shifts toward larger deficits — a fiscal-monetary feedback loop that the NK model would classify as a shift toward the "fiscal dominance" regime of Sargent-Wallace (1981). Whether this channel is quantitatively important depends on political economy parameters that are outside the NK model's specification but critical for long-run equilibrium selection.
