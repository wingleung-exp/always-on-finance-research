### Claim 8: Limits of the Analogue Approach

**Unprecedented feature (a) — Passive market share:**

This likely means contagion velocity is structurally faster than any historical precedent — but by how much is unknowable.

**Unprecedented feature (b) — Layered latent illiquidity:**
No prior episode contained the simultaneous presence of: (1) $1.7T in private credit with mark-to-model pricing, (2) $800B+ in leveraged Treasury basis trades, and (3) $200B+ in semi-liquid retail credit vehicles with gate provisions. Historical analogues can illuminate the mechanics of each in isolation (private credit → early 2000s CLO illiquidity; basis trades → 1998 LTCM; retail gating → 2008 money market fund gating) but the combination is unprecedented.

**Unprecedented feature (c) — AI economic displacement dynamic:**
Prior technology analogues (1960s computing, 1990s internet, 2000s mobile) created new jobs roughly in pace with displacement. The AI cycle may displace cognitive labor faster than new tasks emerge (a hypothesis, not a certainty). If true, the supply-side dynamics are qualitatively different: deflationary in goods/services prices (productivity gain), but inflationary in fiscal costs (retraining, safety nets) and potentially deflationary in demand (income displacement). No prior analogue captures this combination.

**Honest assessment of analogue reliability:**
Given these unprecedented features, I estimate the analogue approach provides a useful prior for the *direction* and *sequencing* of regime transitions (confidence: 7/10 — these are mechanism-driven and likely to persist) but a less reliable guide for *magnitude* and *velocity* (confidence: 5/10 — market microstructure has changed enough that the calibration from historical episodes requires a substantial uncertainty buffer).
