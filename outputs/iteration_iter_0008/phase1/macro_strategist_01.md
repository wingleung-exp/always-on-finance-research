# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are fundamentally driven by the position of real interest rates relative to the natural rate (r-star), the output gap, and the expected trajectory of monetary policy.** Risk-on regimes emerge when policy rates sit below the neutral rate, the output gap is closing but not yet inflationary, and forward guidance credibly signals accommodation. Risk-off regimes are triggered when this configuration breaks — either through tightening beyond neutral or through shocks that abruptly revise growth expectations downward.

2. **A two-state Markov-switching framework anchored to the Taylor Rule residual — the gap between the actual policy rate and the Taylor-prescribed rate — is the most internally consistent method for identifying risk appetite regimes from a New Keynesian perspective.** When the Taylor residual is negative (policy is looser than prescribed), risk appetite is structurally supported. When it turns positive or reverses sharply, regime transitions follow with a lag of roughly 2–6 months, mediated by financial conditions indices.

3. **Contagion velocity in modern markets is primarily a function of the monetary policy transmission mechanism operating in reverse.** The same channels that transmit accommodation — portfolio rebalancing, credit creation, carry-trade leverage, and wealth effects — become vectors for rapid risk appetite withdrawal when the policy stance shifts. The speed of contagion has increased structurally because (a) forward guidance has compressed the information lag between policy expectations and asset pricing, and (b) the proliferation of risk-parity and volatility-targeting strategies mechanically amplifies the transmission.

4. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades are endogenous to the New Keynesian framework when we incorporate financial frictions à la Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist (1999) and Brunnermeier-Sannikov (2014).** The financial accelerator mechanism implies that adverse shocks to net worth tighten borrowing constraints, reduce investment, lower asset prices further, and generate a non-linear feedback loop. This is not a structural break — it is the standard model operating in its non-linear region.

5. **The current macro regime (Q1 2026) presents a transitional risk appetite configuration.** With the Fed funds rate likely in the 3.75–4.25% range and plausible r-star estimates of 1.0–1.5% (Laubach-Williams, Holston-Laubach-Williams), the real policy rate remains modestly above neutral. The output gap has narrowed to approximately zero or slightly positive. This configuration historically corresponds to a "late-cycle risk-on with increasing fragility" regime — risk assets can still perform, but the margin of safety against regime shift is thin.

6. **The Phillips Curve slope matters for regime persistence.** A flat Phillips Curve (empirically supported over the 2010–2019 period, per Del Negro et al. 2020) permits longer risk-on regimes because inflation remains contained even as output gaps close, allowing central banks to maintain accommodation. A steepening Phillips Curve — which there is some evidence for post-pandemic — would shorten risk-on regimes by forcing earlier tightening.

7. **Flight-to-quality episodes are most accurately modeled as jumps in the stochastic discount factor driven by revisions to expected marginal utility growth.** In the consumption-CAPM extended with habit formation (Campbell-Cochrane 1999) or long-run risks (Bansal-Yaron 2004), risk appetite collapses when agents revise downward their expectations of future consumption growth, increasing the price of risk discontinuously. This connects the macro fundamentals (output gap, policy) to asset price dynamics through a coherent pricing kernel.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — r-star and the Output Gap as Regime Determinants:**
The IS curve in the New Keynesian three-equation model (IS, Phillips Curve, Taylor Rule) implies that the output gap is a function of the deviation of the real rate from r-star: x_t = E_t[x_{t+1}] - σ(i_t - E_t[π_{t+1}] - r*). When i - Eπ < r*, the output gap is positive and expanding — the macro environment structurally supports risk-taking. Empirically, Adrian, Boyarchenko, and Giannone (2019, "Vulnerable Growth," AER) show that financial conditions — which proxy for risk appetite — are strongly predicted by the stance of monetary policy relative to neutral. The IMF's Global Financial Stability Reports (2018–2024) have repeatedly documented the correlation between accommodative monetary policy and the buildup of risk appetite as measured by compressed credit spreads, elevated leverage, and equity valuation expansion.

**Claim 2 — Taylor Residual as Regime Identifier:**
The Taylor Rule (1993) prescribes i_t = r* + π_t + 0.5(π_t - π*) + 0.5(y_t - y*). The residual (actual rate minus Taylor-prescribed rate) provides a single, theoretically grounded metric for the stance of policy relative to what fundamentals warrant. Hofmann and Bogdanova (BIS Working Papers, 2012) documented that sustained negative Taylor residuals in the 2000s preceded the global credit boom — a prolonged risk-on regime. Conversely, the Volcker tightening produced the sharpest positive Taylor residual of the post-war era and coincided with the most severe risk-off episode. The 2–6 month lag for transmission is consistent with Romer and Romer (2004) monetary shock estimates and the Chicago Fed's National Financial Conditions Index dynamics.

**Claim 3 — Reverse Transmission as Contagion Channel:**
The portfolio balance channel, formalized by Vayanos and Vila (2021, JPE) in the context of QE, works in both directions. When central banks tighten or signal tightening, the term premium rises, long-duration assets reprice, and leveraged carry positions unwind. Miranda-Agrippino and Rey (2020, RES) demonstrate the existence of a "Global Financial Cycle" driven primarily by US monetary policy — a single common factor explains a large share of cross-border capital flows, credit growth, and asset price comovement. This factor is, in essence, global risk appetite, and it is dominated by the Federal Reserve's policy stance. The mechanism has accelerated because: (i) forward guidance means markets price expected rate paths, not just current rates, compressing information transmission; (ii) risk-parity strategies (Asness et al., 2012) and volatility-targeting allocations mechanically sell risk assets when realized volatility rises, creating a self-reinforcing feedback.

**Claim 4 — Financial Accelerator and Cascades:**
Bernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist (1999) showed that the external finance premium is counter-cyclical and amplifies shocks through balance sheet effects. Brunnermeier and Sannikov (2014, AER) extended this to show that the economy has a "volatility paradox" — periods of low volatility encourage leverage buildup, which creates the preconditions for sharp non-linear corrections. Adrian and Shin (2010, 2014) provided empirical evidence that broker-dealer leverage is pro-cyclical and that balance sheet contractions transmit into market liquidity withdrawal. These are standard mechanisms within the augmented New Keynesian framework, not exogenous structural breaks.

**Claim 5 — Current Regime Assessment:**
The Laubach-Williams (2003) and Holston-Laubach-Williams (2017) models estimate r-star for the US in the 1.0–1.5% range as of late 2025 estimates. With headline PCE inflation likely running near 2.3–2.8% and the nominal fed funds rate in the 3.75–4.25% range, the real rate is approximately 1.0–1.5% — roughly at or slightly above r-star. The CBO output gap estimate has narrowed to near zero. This configuration — neutral-to-tight policy, closed output gap, inflation near but slightly above target — historically corresponds to late-expansion fragility. Risk assets can grind higher on earnings growth, but the cushion against adverse shocks (which would push the economy into negative output gap territory while policy is already near or above neutral) is minimal.

**Claim 6 — Phillips Curve Slope and Regime Duration:**
Del Negro, Giannoni, and Schorfheide (2020) and Hazell, Herreno, Nakamura, and Steinsson (2022, QJE) document that the slope of the Phillips Curve flattened significantly from 1990–2019. A flat Phillips Curve means inflation is less responsive to the output gap, allowing central banks to tolerate positive output gaps without tightening — extending risk-on regimes. Post-pandemic evidence (Benigno and Eggertsson, 2023) suggests the slope may have steepened, particularly at positive output gaps, consistent with a non-linear Phillips Curve. If this steepening persists, the window for risk-on regimes narrows because inflation pressure triggers tightening sooner.

**Claim 7 — Stochastic Discount Factor and Flight-to-Quality:**
Campbell and Cochrane (1999) show that habit formation generates time-varying risk aversion that rises sharply in recessions. Bansal and Yaron (2004) show that small persistent revisions to long-run consumption growth generate large asset price movements through the stochastic discount factor. Both frameworks predict that risk appetite collapses are not arbitrary events but reflect rational (or near-rational) responses to deteriorating macro fundamentals processed through investor preferences. Lettau and Ludvigson (2001) provide empirical support, showing that the consumption-wealth ratio (cay) predicts equity returns because it captures shifts in expected future returns — i.e., shifts in the price of risk.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence (1–10) | Justification |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| 1. r-star and output gap drive regimes | **8** | Core NK model prediction with strong empirical backing (Adrian et al. 2019). Minor uncertainty around r-star measurement. |
| 2. Taylor residual as regime identifier | **7** | Theoretically clean; good in-sample fit historically. Out-of-sample reliability weakened by QE-era unconventional tools that blur the relevant "rate." |
| 3. Reverse transmission as contagion | **8** | Miranda-Agrippino and Rey's Global Financial Cycle evidence is compelling. Mechanistic logic is tight. Some uncertainty about whether passive/algorithmic flows are truly the marginal driver. |
| 4. Financial accelerator cascades | **7** | Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist is canonical. However, the degree of non-linearity and the thresholds that trigger cascade behavior are difficult to calibrate ex ante — the model tells you cascades can happen, not precisely when. |
| 5. Current regime: late-cycle fragility | **6** | Conditional on r-star estimates which carry wide confidence intervals (~±1%). If r-star is higher than estimated (as some argue post-pandemic), the current stance may be less restrictive than assessed. |
| 6. Phillips Curve slope and regime duration | **7** | Strong pre-pandemic evidence for flatness. Post-pandemic steepening evidence is suggestive but not yet settled — only ~4 years of data in the new regime. |
| 7. SDF jumps and flight-to-quality | **7** | Theoretically elegant and empirically supported in broad strokes. However, the specific asset-pricing framework chosen (habit vs. long-run risk vs. disaster risk) matters for quantitative predictions and none fully resolves the equity premium puzzle. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**Volatility Regimes (confidence: 3.0, depth: 0):** Risk appetite and volatility are two sides of the same coin in the NK framework. The VIX is interpretable as a measure of the risk-neutral variance of the stochastic discount factor. Regime shifts in risk appetite produce regime shifts in volatility. The Brunnermeier-Sannikov "volatility paradox" provides the direct link — low-volatility regimes foster leverage accumulation that creates the preconditions for high-volatility transitions. A volatility regime framework that does not account for the underlying monetary policy stance and output gap is descriptively useful but structurally incomplete.

**Credit Cycles (confidence: 6.0, depth: 3):** The credit cycle is the primary transmission mechanism through which risk appetite regimes affect the real economy. In the financial accelerator, credit conditions endogenously amplify the business cycle. Risk-on regimes correspond to credit expansion (compression of the external finance premium), and risk-off regimes correspond to credit contraction. The Minsky moment — when risk appetite abruptly reverses — maps onto the financial accelerator's non-linear region. Credit spread movements are leading indicators of regime transitions because they incorporate both default expectations and risk premia.

**Equity Cycles (confidence: 5.0, depth: 2):** Equity valuations are a function of the discount rate (driven by the stochastic discount factor and hence by risk appetite) and expected earnings (driven by the output gap and growth trajectory). Risk-on regimes support P/E expansion through discount rate compression; risk-off regimes produce P/E contraction. The correlation between equity returns and bond returns flips sign across risk appetite regimes — negative correlation (diversification) in standard risk-on environments, positive correlation (contagion) during severe risk-off episodes when both growth and inflation expectations collapse simultaneously.

**FX Regimes (confidence: 4.0, depth: 1):** The global financial cycle (Miranda-Agrippino and Rey) implies that risk appetite regimes are transmitted internationally through the dollar. Risk-off regimes correspond to dollar strength (flight to the reserve currency and unwinding of dollar-funded carry trades), EM capital outflows, and tightening of global financial conditions. The Mundell-Fleming framework predicts that in a world of high capital mobility and floating rates, monetary policy in the dominant currency area (the US) effectively exports its risk appetite regime to the rest of the world.

**Monetary Policy Transmission (implicit connection):** Risk appetite is, in substantial part, the transmission mechanism of monetary policy to financial conditions and ultimately to the real economy. The Financial Conditions Index (Goldman Sachs, Chicago Fed) operationalizes this — it measures how accommodative or restrictive the composite effect of rates, credit spreads, equity prices, and the dollar is on future economic activity. Changes in risk appetite regimes are, in this view, changes in the efficacy and direction of monetary transmission.

## Open Questions

1. **Has r-star shifted post-pandemic, and by how much?** If the natural rate is structurally higher (due to fiscal deficits, reshoring investment, AI-driven capex), then current policy may be less restrictive than the standard NK model implies, and the risk-on regime may be more durable than Claim 5 suggests. The Laubach-Williams model has wide confidence intervals (~±1%), making real-time assessment difficult.

2. **Do algorithmic and passive flows constitute a new structural feature of risk appetite transmission, or are they simply faster-acting versions of the same portfolio rebalancing channels?** If risk-parity, CTA trend-following, and vol-targeting strategies have fundamentally altered the transmission dynamics (rather than merely accelerating them), then the NK framework may need to incorporate these as endogenous financial frictions rather than treating them as exogenous noise.

3. **Is the Phillips Curve non-linearity durable?** If inflation is materially more sensitive to positive output gaps than to negative ones (a convex Phillips Curve), the duration of risk-on regimes will be structurally shorter going forward because central banks will tighten sooner. This is an empirical question that requires more post-pandemic data to resolve.

4. **What is the interaction between QT (quantitative tightening) and risk appetite independent of the policy rate?** The NK three-equation model does not natively distinguish between rate-based and balance-sheet-based tightening. If QT affects risk appetite through a reserve-scarcity channel (Copeland, Duffie, Yang 2021) that operates independently of the short rate, then the Taylor residual is an incomplete regime identifier. I would need bank reserve data and repo market stress indicators to assess whether this channel is quantitatively important in the current cycle.

5. **What are the welfare-optimal central bank responses to risk appetite regime shifts?** The standard NK loss function (minimize deviations of inflation from target and output from potential) does not explicitly include financial stability. If we extend the loss function to include a financial stability mandate (as Woodford 2012 and others have explored), the optimal Taylor Rule coefficients change, and the model's predictions for risk appetite regimes shift. This is unresolved in the academic literature and has direct bearing on how central banks will actually behave at the margin.

6. **Can we distinguish demand-driven risk-off from supply-driven risk-off in real time?** A demand shock (e.g., a financial crisis) pushes both growth and inflation down; a supply shock (e.g., an energy price spike) pushes growth down and inflation up. The optimal policy response differs sharply. Risk appetite may collapse in both cases, but the asset allocation implications diverge (nominal bonds hedge demand shocks but exacerbate losses in supply shocks). Real-time identification of shock type remains a first-order unsolved problem in applied macroeconomics.
