# Risk Appetite Regimes — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are endogenous to the credit cycle, not exogenous shocks to be modeled as stochastic switches.** What mainstream finance labels "risk-on" and "risk-off" regimes are the observable surface of Minsky's hedge-speculative-Ponzi continuum. Risk appetite does not fluctuate randomly; it is generated by the internal dynamics of credit creation, asset price validation, and the progressive erosion of margins of safety during expansions.

2. **The sectoral financial balances identity determines the sustainability of any given risk appetite regime.** A prolonged risk-on regime requires some sector to be running persistent deficits (i.e., borrowing). In practice, sustained risk-on regimes since the 1980s have been underwritten by private sector deficit spending (households in the 2000s, corporates via buyback-leveraging in the 2010s–2020s). The risk-on regime persists only as long as that deficit spending accelerates — and collapses when it cannot.

3. **Regime transitions ("risk-off events") are credit events, not sentiment events.** The proximate trigger is typically a liquidity withdrawal cascade rooted in the inability of Ponzi-finance units to roll over positions. Sentiment is the effect, not the cause. The 2007–08 crisis, the 2020 March dislocation, and the 2022 UK gilt crisis all featured credit channel transmission, not pure sentiment contagion.

4. **The credit impulse (second derivative of credit growth) is the single best leading indicator for regime transitions.** A decelerating credit impulse, even when credit levels are still growing, signals the transition from Minsky's euphoric phase toward the precipice. This is because the flow of new borrowing, not the stock of debt, drives marginal asset demand.

5. **Modern market microstructure accelerates regime transitions but does not cause them.** Passive flows, algorithmic market-making, and the concentration of risk in non-bank financial intermediaries (NBFIs) compress the temporal gap between Minsky's "displacement" phase and the "revulsion" phase. The gate-contagion risk in semi-liquid private credit vehicles (the $200B+ retailization channel identified in prior research iterations) is an example of how microstructure can amplify an endogenous credit deterioration.

6. **Government sector deficits provide the structural floor for risk appetite in a monetary sovereignty regime.** By the sectoral balances identity, if the foreign sector drains domestic income (current account deficit), the private sector can only net save if the government runs a deficit of sufficient size. Austerity in this context mechanically forces the private sector into deficit spending or contraction — both of which destabilize risk appetite. The post-2020 fiscal expansion structurally supported the prolonged risk-on regime; any fiscal consolidation mechanically pressures it.

7. **Cross-border capital flows create "imported" risk appetite regimes that are more fragile for currency users than currency issuers.** Emerging market risk-on regimes funded by dollar-denominated credit are structurally Ponzi in the Minskyan sense: borrowers earn in local currency but owe in foreign currency, creating an inherent mismatch that unwinds violently when dollar liquidity tightens. This is the channel through which Fed policy propagates risk-off contagion globally.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Endogenous risk appetite:** Minsky's core insight (*Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*, 1986) is that stability itself is destabilizing. During tranquil periods, validated expectations lead to thinner safety margins, lower risk premia, and expanded leverage. The "Great Moderation" of 2003–2007 was not a structural change but the euphoric phase of a Minsky cycle, during which the VIX compressed to historic lows precisely because fragility was building underneath. Risk appetite is the behavioral manifestation of where the economy sits on the hedge-speculative-Ponzi spectrum. Mainstream regime-switching models (Hamilton-type Markov models) capture the statistical pattern but miss the causal mechanism: it is not a hidden state that switches; it is the evolving structure of balance sheets.

**Claim 2 — Sectoral balances as regime constraint:** Godley's sectoral balances identity is an accounting tautology: (S – I) + (T – G) + (M – X) ≡ 0. A risk-on regime characterized by rising asset prices requires expanding credit somewhere. US household saving rates fell from ~10% in the early 1990s to near-zero by 2005–2007, indicating massive private sector deficit spending that underwrote the risk-on regime. The corporate sector similarly moved into net deficit via leveraged buybacks post-2012, with non-financial corporate debt rising from ~65% to ~80% of GDP by 2019. When private deficit spending hit its limit (subprime borrowers in 2007, corporate cashflow coverage deteriorating by late 2019), the risk-on regime could not sustain itself.

**Claim 3 — Credit events, not sentiment:** The March 2020 "dash for cash" was triggered not by fear of COVID per se, but by the margin call cascade in levered Treasury basis trades and corporate credit deterioration. The September 2022 UK gilt crisis was a collateral margin spiral in LDI pension funds — a pure Minsky Ponzi-finance unwind. In every case, the sequence was: leveraged positions → margin/collateral pressure → forced liquidation → price decline → contagion. Sentiment indices tracked the aftermath, not the cause.

**Claim 4 — Credit impulse as leading indicator:** Biggs, Mayer, and Pick (2010) formalized the credit impulse and showed its strong correlation with GDP growth and asset prices. The mechanism is Kaleckian: aggregate profits are a function of investment and capitalist consumption, both of which are credit-driven. When the flow of new credit decelerates, profits decelerate, which undermines the ability to service existing debt — the Minsky moment arrives not when credit stops growing, but when it stops *accelerating*. China's credit impulse turns in 2015–16 and 2021–22 preceded global risk-off episodes with remarkable consistency.

**Claim 5 — Microstructure amplification:** The rise of NBFIs (now holding roughly 50% of global financial assets per FSB estimates) means that Minsky cycle dynamics operate outside the regulated banking system where lender-of-last-resort facilities are designed to intervene. The $200B+ in semi-liquid private credit vehicles identified in the prior research iteration (retailization_liquidity_risk concept) represents a particularly dangerous node: quarterly redemption windows against illiquid 5–7 year WAL assets create the classic Minsky mismatch between liability structure and asset structure, concentrated among 55+ HNW investors with demographic draw-down incentives.

**Claim 6 — Fiscal deficit as risk appetite floor:** Post-COVID US fiscal deficits of 6–8% of GDP provided approximately $1.5–2T annually in net financial assets to the private sector (by the sectoral balances identity, adjusted for the current account deficit). This is the structural foundation of the risk-on regime. Kalecki's profit equation shows that government deficits directly sustain corporate profits. Any move toward fiscal consolidation — whether through DOGE-style spending cuts or tax-driven deficit reduction — mechanically withdraws this support. The 2010–2013 European austerity experiment provides the counterfactual: fiscal tightening forced the private sector into deeper surplus (deleveraging), collapsing risk appetite across European periphery assets.

**Claim 7 — Dollar-denominated Ponzi finance:** Emerging market borrowers who issue in USD are Ponzi units by definition when the dollar is strengthening: their income (local currency) systematically falls relative to their obligations (USD-denominated). The BIS estimates approximately $13T in offshore dollar-denominated debt, representing a structural fragility that transmits Fed tightening into global risk-off cascades. The "taper tantrum" of 2013, the EM crisis of 2018, and the 2022 strong-dollar squeeze all followed this Minskyan channel.
