# Risk Appetite Regimes — Structured & Private Credit Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite in credit markets is best measured not by spread levels but by the terms borrowers can extract**: covenant-lite share, EBITDA addback multiples, PIK toggle availability, and maturity extension ease are the true thermometer of credit risk appetite — and these structural concessions persist long after spreads have repriced.

2. **CLO arbitrage economics function as a regime amplifier**: when liability spreads tighten relative to asset spreads (positive arbitrage), the CLO machine floods the leveraged loan market with demand, compressing pricing and degrading documentation in a self-reinforcing cycle. When arbitrage turns negative, the demand withdrawal is abrupt and non-linear, triggering a regime shift.

3. **Private credit has created a parallel risk appetite regime that operates on a different cycle than public markets**: because private credit NAVs are mark-to-model, fundraising is commitment-based, and deployment pressure is structural, private credit risk appetite can remain elevated even as public credit markets reprice — creating a dangerous divergence that delays but amplifies eventual stress recognition.

4. **The current risk appetite regime (as of early 2026) is bifurcated**: public leveraged credit has normalized to a mid-cycle posture following 2025's spread volatility, while private credit remains in a late-cycle, aggressive deployment posture driven by $400B+ of dry powder and GP pressure to put committed capital to work before investment period expirations.

5. **Risk appetite regime transitions in credit are driven more by the maturity wall calendar than by macro sentiment**: the binding constraint is not whether investors "feel" risk-on but whether borrowers with near-term maturities can access refinancing. A maturity wall that concentrates $300B+ of sub-investment-grade debt in a 12-month window can force a regime shift regardless of macro conditions.

6. **The shift from bank-intermediated to market-based and private credit has made risk appetite regimes more binary**: banks could warehouse risk and smooth credit provision through cycles; CLOs and private credit funds face structural constraints (reinvestment period windows, NAV triggers, LP redemption queues) that create cliff effects in risk appetite.

7. **Rating agency lag is a regime-masking mechanism**: in high-risk-appetite periods, rating agencies tolerate aggressive EBITDA addbacks and elevated leverage through new issuance ratings, implicitly validating the regime. In transitions, mass downgrade waves (the "fallen angel" cascade) force selling from constrained investors and accelerate regime collapse.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 — Terms over spreads as risk appetite measure

Spread levels are noisy and subject to technical factors (ETF flows, index rebalancing, hedging demand). But the share of covenant-lite issuance in the leveraged loan market — which climbed from ~25% pre-GFC to 90%+ in peak risk appetite periods — reveals what lenders are actually willing to accept. Similarly, EBITDA addbacks averaging 25-40% of reported EBITDA on new LBO financings indicate that the "real" leverage on new deals is 1-2x higher than headline figures suggest. These structural concessions are sticky: once a borrower has a cov-lite facility, it doesn't revert to covenanted on refinancing unless the borrower is distressed. This means each risk appetite cycle leaves a permanent residue of weaker documentation in the outstanding stock of credit.

### Claim 2 — CLO arbitrage as regime amplifier

CLO issuance has historically tracked the spread differential between AAA CLO liabilities and the weighted-average spread on underlying leveraged loan collateral. When this arbitrage is positive (asset spreads > liability costs + management fees + equity target return), CLO creation accelerates. In 2021 and into early 2024, US CLO issuance ran at $130-180B annually, creating an almost insatiable bid for leveraged loans. This demand compression drove new-issue loan spreads to S+325-375bps even on heavily leveraged transactions. The feedback loop: tighter loan spreads → marginally less attractive arbitrage → but still positive → more CLOs → more demand → even tighter spreads, until external shock or liability repricing breaks the loop. The reversal is rapid: when AAA CLO spreads gap wider (as in late 2022), new issuance effectively pauses, removing the marginal buyer from a market where CLOs hold ~70% of outstanding leveraged loans.

### Claim 3 — Private credit's parallel regime

Private credit AUM crossed $2T globally, with direct lending being the largest strategy. Unlike public credit, which marks to market daily, private credit funds report NAVs quarterly using model-based valuations. This creates three regime-masking dynamics: (a) NAVs appear stable during periods of public market volatility, attracting retail and institutional capital seeking "lower volatility"; (b) deployment pressure from LP commitments with use-it-or-lose-it investment periods means GPs must lend even when pricing and terms deteriorate; (c) the competitive dynamic between private credit mega-funds drives a race to offer more leverage, looser terms, and higher hold sizes to win mandates. The result: private credit risk appetite can remain at peak levels even when public leveraged loan and high yield markets are repricing — a divergence that either resolves by private credit eventually marking down (lagged recognition) or public markets recovering (soft landing).

### Claim 4 — Current bifurcation

Public leveraged loan spreads widened during mid-2025 tariff-related volatility and have partially retraced but remain above 2024 tights. New CLO issuance has moderated from 2024 peaks. Covenant terms on public market deals have tightened marginally — more maintenance covenant "springing" tests, slightly lower addback caps. Meanwhile, private credit continues deploying at a pace driven by 2021-2023 vintage fund investment periods approaching expiration. Anecdotal evidence from placement agents and fund administrators suggests private credit leverage multiples on new platform deals remain at 5.5-6.5x (before addbacks), with limited movement despite public market repricing. This bifurcation is historically unusual and unstable.

### Claim 5 — Maturity wall as regime driver

The maturity wall facing leveraged borrowers is the structural determinant of when risk appetite must translate into actual credit provision or default. Approximately $350B of leveraged loans and high-yield bonds rated B/B- or below mature in 2026-2027. In a high-risk-appetite regime, these borrowers refinance easily — extend maturities, maintain leverage. In a low-risk-appetite regime, refinancing becomes impossible for the weakest credits, forcing restructuring or default. The maturity wall is a forcing function: regardless of macro sentiment, borrowers whose debt matures must refinance or default. This makes the maturity schedule a more reliable predictor of regime transitions than surveys, flow data, or spread levels.

### Claim 6 — Binary regime dynamics from structural shift

Banks historically acted as credit shock absorbers — they could hold loans on balance sheet, extend and pretend, and smooth credit provision through cycles. CLOs cannot do this after their reinvestment periods expire (typically 4-5 years post-closing). A CLO past its reinvestment date becomes a runoff vehicle that can only reinance, not deploy new capital. This creates a "reinvestment cliff" where a cohort of CLOs simultaneously loses the ability to buy loans, removing demand in a concentrated period. Private credit funds face analogous constraints: LP advisory committee pressure when NAVs decline, leverage facility margin calls, and (for semi-liquid vehicles like interval funds and BDCs) redemption queues that force asset sales at the worst time.

### Claim 7 — Rating agency procyclicality

Rating agencies assign new-issue ratings that incorporate (and thus validate) the EBITDA addbacks and synergy assumptions in sponsor models. A B2/B-rated new LBO financing effectively represents the rating agency's imprimatur on the adjusted leverage calculation. In risk-on regimes, this validation enables more aggressive structures. The lag cuts both ways: in regime transitions, rating agencies typically allow 2-4 quarters of underperformance before downgrading, creating a period where market prices have already repriced but ratings haven't moved. When downgrades eventually arrive in waves, they trigger forced selling from CLOs (which have CCC bucket limits of typically 7.5%) and insurance companies (with risk-based capital charges that jump at sub-investment-grade thresholds).

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary policy transmission**: Rate regime directly sets the cost side of CLO arbitrage economics (AAA CLO liability spreads price off SOFR). Higher-for-longer compresses arbitrage and reduces the CLO bid, shifting credit risk appetite. The maturity wall becomes more threatening when base rates are elevated because refinancing costs are higher.

- **Liquidity regimes**: The semi-liquid private credit vehicle structure (interval funds, non-traded BDCs) creates a potential liquidity mismatch that can force procyclical selling. Redemption queues in these vehicles function like a slow-motion bank run and connect private credit stress to retail investor behavior.

- **Equity market valuations**: LBO leverage is sized off enterprise value, which is a multiple of (adjusted) EBITDA. If equity market multiple compression reduces comparable valuations, new LBO financing becomes harder to structure at the leverage levels borrowers need — a direct channel from equity risk appetite to credit risk appetite.

- **Geopolitical risk and trade policy**: Tariff uncertainty directly impacts leveraged borrower cash flows (many are in manufacturing, industrials, consumer goods), potentially accelerating maturity wall stress for affected sectors. Supply chain restructuring may simultaneously create new deal flow for private credit (capex financing) while stressing existing portfolio companies.

- **Shadow banking and financial plumbing**: Private credit's growth is itself a manifestation of the post-GFC shift from bank to non-bank credit intermediation. Understanding risk appetite regimes requires mapping the full credit creation chain: LP commitment → fund vehicle → warehouse leverage → deployed capital → borrower.

- **Volatility regimes**: Credit spread vol regimes interact with CLO warehouse economics. A warehouse lender providing leverage for loan accumulation during CLO ramp-up faces mark-to-market risk. Elevated spread vol increases warehouse margin calls, which can abort CLO formation mid-ramp and create forced selling of partially accumulated portfolios.

## Open Questions

1. **How large is the true maturity wall after accounting for private credit refinancing capacity?** Private credit can refinance public market borrowers (the "take-private" of credit), but at what scale? If private credit absorbs $50-100B of maturing public market debt, the public market maturity wall shrinks but risk concentrates in less transparent vehicles.

2. **What is the real leverage on private credit portfolios after adjusting for addbacks?** Without regulatory disclosure requirements comparable to public markets, the gap between reported and economic leverage in private credit remains opaque. Is the private credit system operating at 5.5x or 7.5x true leverage? The answer matters enormously for loss-given-default in a downturn.

3. **How will semi-liquid private credit vehicles behave in a sustained drawdown?** Interval funds and non-traded BDCs have not been tested by a prolonged risk-off regime. Gate provisions and redemption queues are designed to prevent runs, but if NAVs decline 15-20%, will retail LPs accept gates or will pressure build for forced liquidation?

4. **Is there a "CLO refinancing wall" analogous to the corporate maturity wall?** As CLOs from the 2021-2022 vintage approach reinvestment period expiration, the loan market loses a cohort of active buyers. Does this structural demand reduction interact with the corporate maturity wall to create a double squeeze?

5. **What role do insurance company allocations to private credit play in regime stability?** Life insurers have dramatically increased private credit allocations, often via affiliated platforms. If insurance regulators increase capital charges or insurers face policyholder outflows, this could remove a large and supposedly stable capital base from private credit — a channel that is poorly understood and monitored.

6. **Can we empirically identify the "tipping point" in CLO arbitrage economics where issuance goes from positive to effectively zero?** The relationship appears non-linear — slight changes in AAA spreads can halt the entire issuance machine — but the exact threshold shifts with equity return expectations and manager willingness to subsidize deals.
