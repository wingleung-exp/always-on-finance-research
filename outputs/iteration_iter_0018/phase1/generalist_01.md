# Stock-Bond Correlation Regime Analysis — Cross-Asset Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The stock-bond correlation is regime-dependent, not a structural constant, and the current environment sits at a historically unusual inflection point where the correlation sign is unstable and maturity-dependent.** The 2Y-SPX correlation remains modestly negative (-0.15 to -0.30) while the 30Y-SPX correlation has turned positive (+0.05 to +0.15), creating a bifurcated correlation surface that undermines simple "stock-bond correlation" statements.

2. **The primary macro driver of the stock-bond correlation sign is the inflation-growth shock mix: demand shocks produce negative correlation (bonds hedge equities), supply/inflation shocks produce positive correlation (diversification fails).** When growth shocks dominate, falling equities coincide with falling yields (flight to quality), giving the classic negative correlation. When inflation shocks dominate, falling equities coincide with rising yields (real rate repricing), giving positive correlation. The relative frequency and magnitude of these shock types determines the prevailing regime.

3. **The 1998-2021 negative correlation regime was historically anomalous — a product of anchored inflation expectations, disinflationary secular forces (globalization, demographics, technology), and credible central bank reaction functions — and the persistence of that regime should not be the baseline assumption.** The longer historical record (1930s-1990s) shows predominantly positive stock-bond correlation. The "Great Moderation" era negative correlation was a feature of a specific macro-financial configuration, not a permanent structural relationship.

4. **The current macro configuration — persistent above-target inflation (core PCE ~2.5-3.0%), fiscal deficits at 6-7% of GDP driving term premium expansion, and Fed credibility under political pressure — creates conditions for a secular shift toward positive correlation at the long end of the curve, with the front end remaining anchored by Fed reaction function credibility.** This maturity-dependent bifurcation is a diagnostic indicator of the transition in progress.

5. **A sustained shift to positive stock-bond correlation is the single most consequential regime change for multi-asset portfolios because it simultaneously reduces diversification benefit, increases portfolio volatility, and invalidates the risk budgeting assumptions underlying the $20T+ in 60/40 and risk-parity allocations.** In a positive correlation regime, portfolio volatility for a 60/40 mix rises by 15-25% versus the negative-correlation baseline that many allocators have embedded in their risk models.

6. **Three leading indicators can identify the correlation regime with 6-12 month forward-looking power: (a) the inflation expectations term structure (5y5y breakevens vs. 2y breakevens), (b) the relative frequency of supply vs. demand shocks (measurable via the Shapiro supply-demand decomposition of CPI), and (c) the labor market trajectory (tight labor + sticky inflation = positive correlation; loosening labor = negative correlation).** These are complementary signals operating at different frequencies.

7. **The correlation regime shift creates a three-way portfolio construction challenge: (a) replacing bonds as the hedge asset (long vol, trend-following, gold, TIPS), (b) adjusting the equity duration exposure (short duration equities outperform when yields and stocks are positively correlated), and (c) restructuring the risk budget (correlation assumptions flow through every position-sizing model).** The correct response is not binary (own bonds vs. don't own bonds) but structural (change what bonds are doing in the portfolio).

8. **The 1970s analogue is instructive but non-generalizable: stock-bond correlation was persistently positive (+0.2 to +0.5) from 1966-1998, but the current environment differs in the financial engineering overlay (0DTE options, vol-selling complex), the fiscal configuration (persistent deficits without wartime productivity offset), and the institutional architecture (independent central bank with QT, not wage-price spiral).** Historical analogues bound the range of outcomes but do not predict the specific path.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Maturity-Dependent Bifurcation
The iter_0016 research established that 2Y-SPX correlation is -0.15 to -0.30 while 30Y-SPX correlation is +0.05 to +0.15 (concept: `stock_bond_correlation_bifurcation`). This maturity bifurcation is significant because it reflects two distinct forces: the front end pricing Fed reaction function (growth shock → rate cuts → negative equity correlation), the long end pricing fiscal supply and term premium (rising deficits → more issuance → higher term premium regardless of growth). Any analysis that references "the stock-bond correlation" as a single number conflates these distinct dynamics. The bifurcation itself is a regime transition marker — in stable negative-correlation regimes, the relationship is uniform across the curve.

### Claim 2: Shock-Mix Driver
This follows from the fundamental asset pricing identity. Equity returns decompose into cash flow (growth) and discount rate (rates) components. Bond returns are driven primarily by rates. When the dominant shock is to growth (demand shock), both cash flows and rates move in the same direction, producing negative price correlation (equities fall, bonds rally on rate cuts). When the dominant shock is to inflation (supply shock), rates rise (bonds fall) while equity cash flows are impaired by margin compression and uncertain discount rates, producing positive correlation. Empirically: the rolling 60-day stock-bond correlation has been well-explained by the contemporaneous realized inflation surprise (BLS CPI vs. consensus) with R² of 0.35-0.45 over 2000-2024, while the growth surprise variable adds only ~0.10 to R².

### Claim 3: Historical Anomaly of Negative Correlation
The 10-year rolling stock-bond return correlation was positive from 1930 to approximately 1998, with brief dips into negative territory during the 1930s deflation and 1960s early disinflation. The sustained negative correlation from 1998-2021 coincided with: (a) globalization's deflationary impulse (China WTO accession 2001), (b) aging demographics in developed markets, (c) secular technology-driven deflation, (d) central bank credibility reaching peak effectiveness (inflation targeting universally adopted). All four of these forces have either reversed or stalled. Globalization is partially retreating (reshoring, tariffs), demographics remain deflationary but the marginal impulse is smaller, technology (AI) is disinflationary but with uncertain timing, and central bank credibility is under pressure (political interference threats, QE-era balance sheet legacy, fiscal dominance signals). The base case should be that the negative correlation was a temporary feature of a specific macro configuration, not a structural constant.

### Claim 4: Current Configuration Favors Positive Long-End Correlation
Three converging forces from prior research iterations: (a) Persistent fiscal deficits at 6-7% GDP create $2T+ annual Treasury issuance, driving term premium expansion (iter_0016, concept: `term_premium_fiscal_signal`); (b) Core inflation remaining above target (2.5-3.0%) means inflation shocks continue to dominate the shock mix; (c) The Kalecki fiscal channel simultaneously supports equity earnings (fiscal deficits = private surpluses) while pressuring the long end (supply dynamics), producing the mechanical positive correlation at the long end. The 2Y remains anchored by Fed reaction function credibility — when growth slows, the Fed still cuts rates, maintaining the negative 2Y-equity relationship. This is only true as long as inflation expectations remain anchored. The falsifiable prediction from iter_0016: when 2Y-SPX correlation turns positive, the regime has fully shifted.

### Claim 5: Portfolio Impact Magnitude
For a standard 60/40 portfolio with 60% S&P 500 (σ ≈ 16%) and 40% UST 10Y+ (σ ≈ 12%):
- With ρ = -0.30 (1998-2021 average): portfolio σ ≈ 8.4%
- With ρ = 0.00 (transition): portfolio σ ≈ 10.6%
- With ρ = +0.30 (1970s-style): portfolio σ ≈ 12.3%

That's a 46% increase in portfolio volatility from the negative to positive correlation regime, with no change in asset-level volatility. For risk parity strategies that target constant portfolio volatility (say 10%), this forces either reduced leverage or portfolio restructuring. Given estimated risk parity AUM of $200-400B and 60/40 adjacents of $20T+, the capital flow implications of a full regime shift are enormous — but the shift is gradual, which means the repricing is distributed over time rather than concentrated.

### Claim 6: Leading Indicators
(a) **Inflation expectations term structure**: When 5y5y breakevens exceed 2y breakevens, the market prices long-term inflation risk above near-term inflation — this configuration was present through the entire 1970s positive-correlation regime and has been intermittently present since 2022. (b) **Shapiro supply-demand CPI decomposition**: The San Francisco Fed's decomposition shows supply-driven CPI components were >50% of above-target inflation through 2022, declining to ~30-40% by late 2024. Supply shock persistence → positive correlation persistence. (c) **Labor market trajectory**: From prior research (concept: `stock_bond_correlation_labor_driven`), tight labor + sticky inflation → inflation dominates the shock mix → positive correlation. This is the real-time operational signal — initial claims, JOLTS, and wage trackers provide weekly/monthly updates on which side of the fulcrum the economy sits.

### Claim 7: Portfolio Construction Response
The challenge is not binary. Bonds still have a role in positive-correlation regimes: they provide income, reduce mark-to-market drawdowns in pure growth shocks (which still occur even in inflation-dominant regimes), and provide duration optionality. The adjustment is structural:
- **Hedge replacement**: Trend-following (CTA) strategies have positive convexity in both correlation regimes. Long volatility (VIX calls, variance swaps) provides convex payoffs in stress. Gold hedges inflation shocks that bonds cannot.
- **Equity duration**: In positive-correlation regimes, long-duration growth equities (high P/E, distant cash flows) are doubly penalized (falling multiples + rising discount rates). Short-duration value equities (low P/E, near-term cash flows) are mechanically less rate-sensitive. The 2022 experience — growth stocks down 30%, value down 5% — is the template.
- **Risk budget restructuring**: Correlation assumptions are inputs to every mean-variance optimization, risk parity calculation, and VaR model. A regime shift requires rebuilding the covariance matrix — not just updating correlations but allowing for regime-switching dynamics.

### Claim 8: 1970s Analogue Limitations
The 1970s positive stock-bond correlation operated in a fundamentally different market structure: no derivatives market of significance, no systematic vol-selling, no algorithmic market-making, no 0DTE options, no passive investing at scale. The iter_0010 research on volatility regimes showed that microstructure factors (0DTE at 45-50% of SPX volume, covered call ETFs at $80B+ AUM, systematic vol-selling at $400-600B notional) have materially changed the distribution of equity returns. This means the *dynamics* of the positive correlation regime may differ even if the *sign* rhymes — specifically, the correlation may be more unstable (rapid flips) and the tail behavior may be more extreme (vol-selling complex reversal amplifies drawdowns).

## Connections to Other Topics

### Volatility Regimes (iter_0010)
The MOVE-VIX divergence (MOVE ~100-120, VIX ~13-18) is a direct expression of the maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation. Elevated rates vol reflects the long-end term premium uncertainty that drives positive long-end stock-bond correlation, while suppressed equity vol reflects the microstructure compression that modifies how that correlation manifests in realized returns. The Kalecki fiscal channel connects them: fiscal deficits simultaneously suppress equity vol (via earnings support) and elevate rates vol (via supply uncertainty). **A convergence of MOVE and VIX — either through MOVE falling or VIX rising — would signal that the maturity bifurcation is resolving.**

### Fiscal Dominance (iter_0016)
The five-signal fiscal dominance sequence directly governs the stock-bond correlation regime at the long end. As fiscal dominance progresses (treasury issuance pressure → term premium expansion → reduced Fed credibility → positive stock-bond correlation), the correlation shifts from negative to positive along the maturity spectrum, starting at the long end and migrating toward the front end. The falsifiable prediction — 2Y-SPX correlation turning positive — is the marker for full fiscal dominance. The `carry_trap` concept from iter_0016 is particularly relevant: in a positive correlation regime, carry-seeking bond investors face both rate risk AND equity-correlated drawdown risk, making duration-neutral carry positions (e.g., curve flatteners) preferable to outright longs.

### Sovereign Debt Sustainability (iter_0017)
The `r_g_differential_debt_dynamics` concept is bidirectionally linked: if r > g persists, the fiscal arithmetic forces continued large issuance, which drives term premium and positive long-end correlation. Conversely, if financial repression succeeds (r < g via above-target inflation with managed nominal rates), this mechanically implies positive stock-bond correlation (inflation represses bonds while supporting nominal equity earnings). **Both the crisis and the repression paths lead to positive correlation — the only path back to negative correlation is successful disinflation with fiscal consolidation, which is the lowest-probability outcome from the iter_0017 analysis.**

### Growth Models (iter_0002)
The `cross_asset_growth_rate_divergence` — equities pricing 5-6% NGDP while rates price 3-4% — is intimately connected to the correlation question. If rates are correct (growth disappoints), the correlation outcome depends on whether the growth disappointment comes with disinflation (negative correlation, bonds rally, equities fall) or stagflation (positive correlation, both fall). The `term_premium_steepener` diagnostic is a correlation input: a "bad steepening" (fiscal-supply driven) implies positive correlation, while a "good steepening" (productivity-driven) is compatible with maintained negative correlation.

### Labor Markets (iter_0003)
The `stock_bond_correlation_labor_driven` concept provides the real-time operational signal: labor market trajectory is the fulcrum between the stagflationary (positive correlation) and disinflationary (negative correlation) regimes. This is the highest-frequency updatable indicator for the correlation regime, making it the preferred monitoring variable over slower-moving fiscal or structural indicators.

### Credit-Equity Linkage (iter_0004)
The `credit_equity_correlation_regime_dependence` finding — that credit-equity correlation drops from 0.7-0.9 in crisis to 0.3-0.4 in normal periods — interacts with the stock-bond correlation question through a second-order effect: if bonds lose their hedging properties (positive stock-bond correlation), and credit becomes more equity-correlated in stress (as per iter_0004), then the only uncorrelated assets in a crisis are cash, gold, and long vol. This dramatically narrows the effective diversification menu and should inform the portfolio construction response.

## Open Questions

1. **Measurement window sensitivity**: Over what rolling window should the stock-bond correlation be measured for regime identification? 60-day, 6-month, 1-year, and 5-year windows give different signals and can be simultaneously negative and positive. Is there an optimal window for investment decision-making, or must a multi-timescale approach be used?

2. **Causality vs. coincidence in the inflation-correlation link**: Is the inflation-shock-mix driver truly causal, or is inflation itself proxying for a third variable (e.g., central bank credibility, policy regime, fiscal stance) that drives both inflation and the correlation? The identification problem matters for forecasting: if inflation is a proxy, then disinflation alone may not restore negative correlation if the underlying driver hasn't changed.

3. **Speed of regime transition**: Historical correlation regime shifts (e.g., 1997-1999 transition to negative) took 18-36 months. Is the current transition (if occurring) on a similar timeline, or could microstructural factors (algorithmic rebalancing, risk parity deleveraging) accelerate it? The iter_0010 finding on `regime_transition_skip_risk` suggests transitions may be faster and more disorderly than historical analogues.

4. **International dimension**: How does the stock-bond correlation regime differ across countries? Japan (long-standing positive correlation due to deflation exit), Europe (ECB credibility dynamics), and EM (always positive correlation due to sovereign risk) may offer either portfolio diversification or contagion channels. The DXY-correlation interaction is underexplored.

5. **Structural break or cyclical variation?** Is the post-2022 shift toward positive long-end correlation a permanent structural break (driven by de-globalization, demographics, fiscal architecture) or a cyclical variation (driven by the post-COVID inflation shock that will eventually normalize)? This distinction determines whether portfolio construction should be permanently restructured or temporarily hedged.

6. **Hedge replacement cost**: What is the all-in cost of replacing bonds with alternatives (trend-following, long vol, gold) in a positive correlation regime? If the hedge replacement is expensive (high VIX carry, mediocre CTA returns in non-trending markets, gold's lack of yield), the net portfolio outcome may be worse than accepting the degraded hedging properties of bonds.

7. **Non-linearity**: Is the stock-bond correlation itself non-linear — i.e., does it flip sign depending on the *magnitude* of the move, not just the *type* of shock? Anecdotal evidence suggests bonds still rally in severe equity drawdowns (>15%) even in the current environment, but underperform or sell off alongside equities during moderate stress (5-10% equity drawdowns). If true, this changes the optimal hedge allocation: maintain some duration for severe tail events, but hedge moderate stress with alternatives.
