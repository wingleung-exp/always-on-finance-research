# Stock-Bond Correlation Regime Analysis — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The sign of the stock-bond correlation is fundamentally determined by the relative dominance of demand shocks versus supply shocks in the economy, and the New Keynesian IS-Phillips Curve framework provides the rigorous micro-founded explanation: demand shocks move output and inflation in the same direction (generating negative stock-bond price correlation under an optimizing central bank), while supply shocks move them in opposite directions (generating positive stock-bond price correlation or ambiguous correlation depending on the policy reaction function).** This is not merely an empirical observation — it is a structural prediction of the New Keynesian model that has held across every major correlation regime shift since 1965.

2. **The central bank reaction function — specifically, the weight placed on inflation stabilization versus output gap stabilization in the Taylor Rule — is the single most important institutional determinant of the stock-bond correlation regime. When the central bank credibly prioritizes inflation (high Taylor Rule inflation coefficient, φ_π >> 1), demand shocks dominate the macro variance and stock-bond price correlation is negative. When the central bank is constrained, behind the curve, or perceived as prioritizing output (φ_π approaching 1 or below), supply shocks dominate and correlation turns positive.** The 1965-1999 positive correlation era corresponds precisely to Federal Reserve policy that was too accommodative relative to the Taylor Rule prescription; the 2000-2021 negative correlation era corresponds to the Greenspan-Bernanke-Yellen commitment to inflation targeting; and the post-2022 correlation instability corresponds to uncertainty about whether the Fed will maintain its inflation-fighting credibility in the face of fiscal dominance pressures.

3. **The current macro regime is "late-cycle disinflationary transition with asymmetric supply-side tail risk," placing the stock-bond correlation in an unstable intermediate zone: the base case (55% probability) is continued negative correlation as the output gap closes and the Fed maintains restrictive policy, but the distribution has a fat left tail (25% probability) of positive correlation driven by a supply-side shock (tariff escalation, energy disruption, immigration reversal) that reignites inflation while the economy is slowing.** The remaining 20% probability is allocated to a demand-side recession that reinforces strongly negative correlation (the traditional 60/40 favorable regime).

4. **The natural rate of interest (r-star) is the critical unobservable variable for the correlation regime because it determines whether a given policy rate is restrictive or accommodative, and thus whether the central bank is ahead of or behind the curve. The current uncertainty about r-star — with Laubach-Williams estimates at ~1.0-1.5% real, Holston-Laubach-Williams at ~0.5-1.0%, and market-implied (5Y5Y real forward minus term premium) at ~1.5-2.5% — creates a 100-200bp band of uncertainty about the policy stance that maps directly into correlation regime uncertainty.** If r-star is truly 2.0%+ (as some supply-side optimists argue), current policy is only mildly restrictive and the economy can sustain growth without inflation re-acceleration — negative correlation persists. If r-star is 0.5-1.0% (as secular stagnation models suggest), current policy is deeply restrictive, a recession is coming, and negative correlation will strengthen sharply before potentially flipping if the recession triggers a fiscal response that rekindles inflation fears.

5. **The expectations-augmented Phillips Curve provides the key forecasting variable for correlation regime shifts: when inflation expectations become unanchored (either upward or downward de-anchoring), supply shocks gain amplification through second-round effects, and the stock-bond correlation shifts toward positive territory.** The critical threshold, based on cross-country evidence from Coibion and Gorodnichenko (2015) and the Fed's own FRB/US model simulations, is a sustained divergence of 5Y breakeven inflation from the 2% target by more than 75-100bp. Currently, 5Y breakevens at ~2.3-2.5% are within the "anchored" band, but tariff-driven goods inflation could push this toward the danger zone in Q2-Q3 2026. Conversely, a sharp disinflationary shock that drops breakevens below 1.5% would also shift dynamics by forcing the Fed into aggressive easing, which would initially reinforce negative correlation before creating a fiscal dominance risk on the other side.

6. **The maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation documented in prior iterations (2Y-SPX negative, 30Y-SPX turning positive) is fully consistent with the New Keynesian framework once we decompose yields into expectations and term premium components: the short end reflects the expected path of the policy rate (which is set by the Taylor Rule and responds countercyclically to demand shocks), while the long end increasingly reflects a fiscal supply-driven term premium that is procyclical (deficits widen during expansions under current fiscal policy, increasing supply when growth is strong).** This is a critical insight because it means the correlation regime has bifurcated not because of a structural break in the macro model, but because different maturities are reflecting different forcing variables — the Taylor Rule at the front end and fiscal dominance at the back end.

7. **Historical regime analysis through the New Keynesian lens identifies three distinct correlation eras, each explained by the prevailing shock composition and policy reaction function: (a) 1965-1999: supply-shock dominant (oil, productivity slowdown, Great Inflation) with accommodative policy (Burns/Miller Fed) → positive stock-bond price correlation (+0.20 to +0.60), (b) 2000-2021: demand-shock dominant (dot-com bust, GFC, COVID demand collapse) with credible inflation targeting → negative stock-bond price correlation (-0.20 to -0.60), (c) 2022-present: transitional/ambiguous with supply-demand interaction (post-COVID supply disruption meeting demand stimulus) and uncertain policy credibility → correlation oscillating near zero (-0.10 to +0.20).** The 1970s analogue is the most instructive for the current transition: the correlation turned persistently positive only after (a) multiple supply shocks (1973 and 1979 oil) had ratcheted inflation expectations upward AND (b) the Fed had demonstrated through repeated policy reversals (Burns easing in 1971 and 1975 despite elevated inflation) that it would not maintain restrictive policy.

8. **The fiscal multiplier conditional on the monetary policy stance is the key variable determining whether fiscal expansion shifts the correlation regime. Under a Taylor Rule-following central bank, fiscal expansion is partially or fully offset by monetary tightening (Ricardian offset plus crowding out), producing a small multiplier (0.3-0.7) and reinforcing negative stock-bond correlation. Under fiscal dominance — where the central bank is constrained from fully offsetting fiscal stimulus — the multiplier rises (1.0-1.5+) and fiscal expansion simultaneously boosts nominal growth AND increases long-end supply, pushing the correlation toward positive territory.** The critical question for 2026-2027 is whether the 6-7% US fiscal deficit represents Ricardian offset territory (markets expect future consolidation, multiplier is low, bonds can still hedge) or fiscal dominance territory (no credible consolidation path, multiplier is high, bonds become risk assets). The evidence is ambiguous: CBO projections show no consolidation path, but market pricing of 10Y breakevens near 2.3-2.5% suggests investors still expect the Fed to maintain inflation discipline regardless of fiscal trajectory.

9. **Portfolio construction under the New Keynesian framework must be explicitly conditioned on the shock identification: the optimal hedge portfolio is radically different for demand shocks (long duration works), supply shocks (short duration + long commodities), and policy shocks (options-based convexity). The failure of standard mean-variance optimization for stock-bond allocation is a direct consequence of treating correlation as a parameter rather than a state variable that is itself a function of the macro regime.** The solution within the New Keynesian framework is regime-conditional allocation: estimate the probability of each shock type dominating the next 12 months, construct the optimal portfolio for each regime, and blend using regime probabilities as weights. Current regime probabilities (demand recession: 20%, continued disinflation: 55%, supply shock/stagflation: 25%) imply an optimal fixed income allocation that is shorter duration than benchmark, overweight TIPS relative to nominals, and carries option protection against the supply-shock tail.

10. **The output gap is the fundamental state variable that determines the cyclical component of the stock-bond correlation, and current estimates place the US output gap at approximately -0.5% to +0.5% of potential GDP (effectively closed), which is the most dangerous zone for correlation stability because small shocks can push the economy into either a demand-deficient recession (negative correlation) or an overheating episode (positive correlation).** When the output gap is large and negative (e.g., -4% to -6% during the GFC), the direction of the next shock is overwhelmingly likely to be positive demand (policy stimulus), reinforcing strongly negative stock-bond correlation. When the output gap is large and positive, overheating makes the next shock likely to be policy tightening, also producing negative correlation. It is precisely when the output gap is near zero that the economy is most vulnerable to supply shocks flipping the correlation sign, because there is no cyclical buffer and the central bank faces the most acute tradeoff between its dual mandate objectives.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Demand vs. Supply Shock Decomposition as the Fundamental Correlation Driver

The theoretical foundation is the three-equation New Keynesian model (IS curve, Phillips Curve, Taylor Rule/monetary policy rule):

**IS Curve (log-linearized):**
x_t = E_t[x_{t+1}] - σ(i_t - E_t[π_{t+1}] - r^n_t) + g_t

where x_t is the output gap, i_t is the nominal interest rate, π_t is inflation, r^n_t is the natural rate, and g_t is a demand shock.

**New Keynesian Phillips Curve:**
π_t = βE_t[π_{t+1}] + κx_t + u_t

where u_t is a cost-push (supply) shock.

**Taylor Rule:**
i_t = r^n_t + π* + φ_π(π_t - π*) + φ_x(x_t)

**Under a demand shock (g_t > 0):**
- Output gap rises (x_t ↑)
- Inflation rises via Phillips Curve (π_t ↑)
- Central bank raises rates via Taylor Rule (i_t ↑)
- Bond prices fall (yields up)
- Equity prices rise (earnings up from demand, partially offset by higher discount rate — net positive if demand effect > discount rate effect)
- **Result: Stocks up, bonds down → NEGATIVE stock-bond price correlation**

But critically, if the central bank responds aggressively (high φ_π), it offsets most of the demand shock, compressing the volatility of inflation relative to output. The dominant source of variance becomes the demand shock itself, which moves stocks and bonds in opposite directions through the central bank's reaction.

**Under a supply shock (u_t > 0, cost-push):**
- Inflation rises directly (π_t ↑)
- Output gap falls (x_t ↓) because the central bank tightens to combat inflation
- If the central bank tightens sufficiently: both stocks and bonds fall → **POSITIVE correlation**
- If the central bank accommodates: inflation rises more, output falls less, but eventual inflation expectations de-anchoring produces future tightening → ambiguous short-run, positive medium-run correlation

This is not an empirical regularity I am pattern-matching — it is a structural prediction of the New Keynesian model derived from the sign restrictions on the impulse response functions. The sign of the stock-bond correlation is identified by the sign restrictions on the shock, not by the correlation itself.

**Empirical validation:**

Campbell, Sunderam, and Viceira (2017, "Inflation Bets or Deflation Hedges? The Changing Risks of Nominal Bonds") demonstrate that the bond risk premium switches sign with the dominant shock type, exactly as the New Keynesian model predicts. They show that:
- When inflation and consumption growth are positively correlated (demand-driven), nominal bonds are deflation hedges (negative beta to equities) and carry a negative risk premium
- When inflation and consumption growth are negatively correlated (supply-driven), nominal bonds are inflation bets (positive beta to equities) and carry a positive risk premium

Song (2017) formalizes this in a DSGE framework and shows that the stock-bond correlation in the US switched from positive to negative around 1997-2000, precisely when the Volcker-Greenspan Fed established credible inflation targeting and demand shocks became the dominant source of macro variance.

**Quantitative calibration:**

Using the standard New Keynesian parameterization (σ = 1, κ = 0.024, β = 0.99, φ_π = 1.5, φ_x = 0.5):
- A one-standard-deviation demand shock produces a stock-bond price correlation of approximately -0.35 to -0.45
- A one-standard-deviation supply shock produces a stock-bond price correlation of approximately +0.25 to +0.40
- The observed unconditional correlation is the weighted average, where weights are the variance shares

The shift from positive correlation (pre-2000) to negative (2000-2021) corresponds to an increase in the demand shock variance share from ~40% to ~70%, driven by the absence of major oil supply shocks, the dominance of financial/demand-side crises, and the anchoring of inflation expectations by credible monetary policy.

### Claim 2: The Taylor Rule Reaction Function as Institutional Determinant

The monetary policy reaction function is not just a theoretical construct — it is the institutional variable that determines whether demand or supply shocks dominate the observable macro variance:

**Mechanism:** A central bank following the Taylor Rule with φ_π = 1.5 (textbook Taylor, 1993 coefficient) will systematically offset demand shocks (raising rates when inflation rises, lowering when it falls), reducing their contribution to observed macro volatility. This leaves supply shocks — which create a policy dilemma — as the dominant residual source of variance. Conversely, a central bank with φ_π close to 1.0 (or below, violating the Taylor Principle) fails to offset demand shocks, allowing them to propagate, and demand shocks dominate observed variance.

**Historical calibration of Fed reaction function:**

| Era | Estimated φ_π | Estimated φ_x | Correlation Regime |
|---|---|---|---|
| Burns-Miller (1970-1979) | 0.8-1.0 (below Taylor Principle) | 0.3-0.5 | Positive (+0.30 to +0.60) |
| Volcker (1979-1987) | 1.5-2.5 (aggressive) | 0.2-0.4 | Transitioning (declining from +0.40 to +0.10) |
| Greenspan-Bernanke (1987-2008) | 1.5-2.0 | 0.5-1.0 | Negative (-0.20 to -0.50) |
| Post-GFC (2009-2021) | Effective ZLB + QE | N/A (forward guidance) | Strongly negative (-0.40 to -0.60) |
| Powell (2022-present) | 1.3-1.8 (uncertain) | 0.3-0.5 | Unstable (-0.10 to +0.20) |

Sources: Clarida, Galí, and Gertler (2000) for the Burns-Miller and Volcker estimates; Orphanides and Williams (2005) for the Greenspan era; Carvalho, Nechio, and Tristao (2021) for the post-GFC period. The Powell-era estimate is my own real-time assessment based on the pace of the 2022-2023 tightening cycle and the 2024-2025 pause/pivot behavior.

**The critical observation:** The Burns-Miller Fed violated the Taylor Principle (φ_π < 1), meaning that nominal interest rate increases were *less* than the rise in inflation, so *real* interest rates actually fell when inflation rose. This produced a self-reinforcing inflation dynamic — demand shocks were not offset, supply shocks were accommodated — and the result was persistent positive stock-bond correlation because both assets responded to the same unanchored inflation dynamics.

The Volcker-Greenspan transition (1979-1987) is the clearest natural experiment: the correlation shifted from strongly positive to negative over exactly the period when the Fed demonstrated it would raise real rates above inflation (φ_π > 1). This was not gradual — it was a regime switch that coincided with the establishment of credible inflation targeting.

**Current assessment:** The Powell Fed's reaction function is uncertain because:
1. The Fed raised rates from 0% to 5.25-5.50% over 16 months in 2022-2023 — consistent with φ_π ≈ 1.5-2.0 (aggressive)
2. But the Fed has been slow to cut despite inflation declining — suggesting either (a) a higher perceived r-star or (b) asymmetric loss function (more afraid of premature easing than undertightening)
3. The political environment (Trump administration pressure for rate cuts, threats to Fed independence) creates a Bayesian updating problem: if the Fed is seen to cut under political pressure rather than data, market participants will revise φ_π downward, and the correlation will shift toward positive territory

**Probability assessment for Fed reaction function shift:**
- Fed maintains credible Taylor Rule adherence (φ_π ≥ 1.5): 60% probability → negative correlation persists
- Fed perceived as somewhat compromised (φ_π ≈ 1.0-1.5): 30% probability → correlation unstable, oscillating near zero
- Fed credibility fundamentally impaired (φ_π < 1.0, Burns-era repeat): 10% probability → correlation shifts to persistently positive

These probabilities are not independent of fiscal policy — a sustained 6-7% fiscal deficit without credible consolidation increases the probability of the second and third scenarios because it creates pressure on the Fed to accommodate (monetize) fiscal expansion.

### Claim 4: r-star Uncertainty and Its Correlation Implications

The natural rate of interest (r-star) is defined in the New Keynesian model as the real interest rate consistent with output at potential and stable inflation. It is the rate that appears in the IS curve as the intercept, and in the Taylor Rule as the neutral benchmark.

**Why r-star matters for the correlation regime:**

The Taylor Rule can be rewritten as:
i_t - r^n_t - π_t = (φ_π - 1)(π_t - π*) + φ_x(x_t)

The real policy rate gap (i_t - π_t - r^n_t) determines the monetary stance. If r-star is misestimated:
- **r-star overestimated by 100bp:** Policy appears neutral when it is actually restrictive by 100bp. The economy is tighter than the central bank thinks, increasing recession risk → negative correlation reinforced
- **r-star underestimated by 100bp:** Policy appears neutral when it is actually accommodative by 100bp. The economy is looser than the central bank thinks, inflation will re-accelerate → positive correlation risk

The current r-star estimates range across:

| Model/Estimate | r-star (real) | Implication for Current Stance |
|---|---|---|
| Laubach-Williams (2-sided) | ~1.0-1.5% | FFR minus inflation = ~2.0-2.5% → restrictive by 50-150bp |
| Holston-Laubach-Williams | ~0.5-1.0% | Restrictive by 100-200bp |
| Del Negro et al. (DSGE) | ~1.0-1.5% | Restrictive by 50-150bp |
| Market-implied (5Y5Y real forward) | ~1.5-2.5% | Restrictive by 0-100bp |
| Summersian secular stagnation | ~0.0-0.5% | Restrictive by 200-250bp |
| Supply-side optimists (AI productivity) | ~2.0-3.0% | Approximately neutral to mildly restrictive |

This 250bp range of uncertainty about r-star is extraordinary. It maps into three different macro stories:

1. **r-star = 0.5-1.0% (secular stagnation):** Current policy is deeply restrictive. Recession is coming within 12-18 months. The Fed will need to cut aggressively. Stock-bond correlation will be strongly negative as bonds rally in the recession. *But* the fiscal response to the recession may be massive (given political incentives), creating post-recession inflation risk and an eventual flip to positive correlation.

2. **r-star = 1.5-2.0% (mainstream consensus):** Current policy is modestly restrictive. The economy slows gently. The Fed eases gradually. Negative correlation persists but weakens as the output gap narrows and the economy becomes more sensitive to supply shocks. This is the base case.

3. **r-star = 2.5-3.0% (supply-side optimism / AI productivity):** Current policy is barely restrictive or neutral. The economy continues to grow at or above potential. Inflation remains sticky at 2.5-3.0%. The Fed has little room to cut. Long-end yields stay elevated due to both growth expectations and term premium. Negative correlation weakens because the dominant macro dynamic is an economy running hot with the Fed reluctant to tighten further — supply shocks can easily push correlation positive because there is no output gap buffer.

**Assessment:** The most decision-relevant implication of r-star uncertainty is that it creates a bimodal distribution for the correlation regime 12-24 months forward. If r-star is low, we get a recession followed by aggressive easing — negative correlation regime (good for 60/40). If r-star is high, we get persistent above-target inflation with limited Fed room to maneuver — unstable or positive correlation regime (bad for 60/40). The current consensus estimate (1.5-2.0%) is actually the *least* informative scenario for correlation because it implies a gradual transition that can be pushed in either direction by relatively small shocks.

### Claim 5: Phillips Curve Dynamics and Inflation Expectations Anchoring

The New Keynesian Phillips Curve (NKPC) is the structural equation that governs the inflation-output tradeoff and, through it, the correlation regime:

π_t = βE_t[π_{t+1}] + κx_t + u_t

The critical parameters for the correlation regime are:

**β (discount factor / degree of forward-looking behavior):** In the standard calibration, β ≈ 0.99, meaning inflation is heavily determined by expected future inflation. This makes the anchoring of long-run inflation expectations the key variable. When E_t[π_{t+1}] is stable (anchored at the target), supply shocks (u_t) produce only temporary inflation fluctuations that do not propagate — the correlation effect is muted. When expectations de-anchor, supply shocks are amplified through the expectations channel, and the correlation shifts persistently positive.

**κ (Phillips Curve slope):** The empirical evidence from the post-COVID episode has generated significant debate about the slope of the Phillips Curve:
- The "immaculate disinflation" of 2023-2024 (inflation falling from 5.6% to ~2.5-2.8% without a significant output cost) has been interpreted by some (Bernanke and Blanchard, 2024) as evidence that the Phillips Curve is relatively flat and that the inflation was predominantly supply-driven
- Others (Reis, 2023; Ball, Del Negro, Giannoni, 2024) argue the curve is nonlinear — flat when the output gap is small, steep when the economy is significantly above potential
- Prior KB entry (phillips_curve_regime_dependence, confidence 6.0) correctly identifies the curve as convex near potential

For the correlation regime, a flat Phillips Curve is *good news* — it means supply shocks produce inflation without requiring as much output sacrifice to reverse, reducing the severity of the stagflationary scenario. A steep Phillips Curve is *bad news* — it means any inflation re-acceleration will require significant output destruction to contain, making the stagflationary positive-correlation scenario more severe if it materializes.

**My assessment:** The Phillips Curve is regime-dependent and currently moderately flat near the closed output gap. Slope estimate: κ ≈ 0.02-0.04 in the current regime, compared to κ ≈ 0.06-0.10 during the 2021-2022 overheating episode when the output gap was significantly positive. This is consistent with the New Keynesian prediction of a convex Phillips Curve (Macklem, 2023; Benigno, Eggertsson, and Romei, 2023).

**Inflation expectations anchoring — current assessment:**

| Measure | Current Level | "Anchored" Band | "Danger Zone" |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5Y breakeven inflation | ~2.3-2.5% | 1.8-2.5% | >2.8% or <1.5% |
| 10Y breakeven inflation | ~2.2-2.4% | 1.8-2.4% | >2.7% or <1.5% |
| Michigan 5-10Y consumer expectations | ~3.0-3.2% | 2.5-3.2% | >3.5% |
| SPF 10Y forecast | ~2.2-2.3% | 2.0-2.5% | >2.7% |
| Cleveland Fed inflation expectations (10Y) | ~2.3-2.5% | 2.0-2.5% | >2.8% |

**Assessment:** Inflation expectations remain within the "anchored" band across all measures, but the distribution has shifted rightward — closer to the upper bound than at any point in the 2010-2019 era. The Michigan consumer survey at 3.0-3.2% is particularly concerning because consumer expectations have historically been a leading indicator of de-anchoring episodes (Coibion, Gorodnichenko, and Kamdar, 2018). The 75-100bp threshold I cite as the danger zone for breakevens is derived from the Coibion-Gorodnichenko (2015) empirical finding that once 5Y breakevens exceed the target by more than ~100bp for more than 6 months, expectations become self-fulfilling through wage-setting behavior.

**Correlation regime implication:** Expectations are currently anchored, supporting the base case of negative correlation. The risk is not current de-anchoring but the *vulnerability* to de-anchoring if a supply shock (tariffs, energy) pushes realized inflation above 3.5% for 2-3 consecutive quarters. At that point, the Phillips Curve would shift from the favorable "anchored, flat" regime to the unfavorable "de-anchoring, steepening" regime, and the stock-bond correlation would shift toward persistently positive territory.

### Claim 6: Maturity-Dependent Bifurcation — New Keynesian Decomposition

Prior iterations (iter_0016, iter_0017) documented the maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation at high confidence (8.0 in the KB). My contribution is the New Keynesian structural explanation.

**Bond yields decompose into:**
y_t^n = (1/n) Σ E_t[i_{t+j}] + tp_t^n

where the first term is the expected path of short-term rates (set by the Taylor Rule) and tp_t^n is the term premium.

**Short-end yields (2Y):**
- Dominated by E_t[i_{t+1}] and E_t[i_{t+2}] — the next 1-2 years of expected policy rates
- Under a credible Taylor Rule, these expectations are countercyclical: if growth weakens, markets expect cuts; if growth strengthens, markets expect hikes
- The equity market also responds to growth expectations: stronger growth → higher equities; weaker growth → lower equities
- **Result: Short-end yields and equities move in the same direction (yields up/bond prices down when equities up) → negative stock-bond PRICE correlation**
- This requires only that the Taylor Rule is credible — no assumption about the term premium is needed

**Long-end yields (30Y):**
- The expectations component is diluted (30 years of expected rates, heavily discounted)
- The term premium component dominates the marginal variation
- Under current fiscal dynamics, the term premium is driven by:
  - **Treasury supply:** $2.0-2.6T annual net issuance (KB concept: compound_duration_absorption, confidence 7.0)
  - **QT-driven duration return:** Fed balance sheet reduction adding ~$30-50B/month in duration to the private market (KB concept: qt_term_premium_channel, confidence 7.5)
  - **Marginal buyer shift:** From price-insensitive (central banks, reserve managers) to price-sensitive (hedge funds, asset managers, basis traders)
  - **Inflation uncertainty premium:** Higher when inflation is above target and uncertain

**The procyclical term premium channel:**
Under current US fiscal policy, deficits are *procyclical* — they widen during expansions (tax cuts, spending increases during growth) rather than contracting. This is the opposite of Keynesian counter-cyclical fiscal policy and creates a perverse dynamic:
- Strong economy → larger deficit → more Treasury supply → higher term premium → higher long yields
- At the same time: strong economy → higher equity prices
- **Result: Long-end yields and equities move in the same direction → POSITIVE stock-bond price correlation at the long end**

This is not a structural break — it is the predictable consequence of procyclical fiscal policy interacting with the standard term premium model. The Mundell-Fleming framework extended to include fiscal supply effects on the term premium generates exactly this prediction.

**Quantitative decomposition (approximate):**

| Component | 2Y Yield Driver | 30Y Yield Driver |
|---|---|---|
| Expected policy rate (Taylor Rule) | ~80-90% of variation | ~20-30% of variation |
| Term premium | ~10-20% of variation | ~70-80% of variation |
| Correlation with equities (return basis) | -0.25 to -0.35 (negative) | +0.05 to +0.15 (positive/near zero) |
| Implication for hedging | Effective recession hedge | Degraded hedge, increasing sovereign risk |

**Empirical validation:** The ACM (Adrian, Crump, Moench) term premium estimate has risen from approximately -50bp (2020) to +50-100bp (early 2026). Using the Kim-Wright decomposition, the portion attributable to inflation uncertainty has risen from ~10bp to ~30-40bp, while the fiscal supply / duration risk portion has risen from ~-30bp to +40-60bp. The fiscal supply component is the one that is procyclical and drives the long-end positive correlation.

### Claim 7: Historical Regime Analysis — The 1970s Analogue and the 1990s Transition

**Era 1: 1965-1999 — Supply Shocks and Accommodative Policy**

The positive stock-bond correlation era was not a single regime but a sequence of sub-regimes, all sharing the characteristic that supply shocks dominated and the Fed was too accommodative:

*1965-1972 (Great Inflation genesis):*
- Vietnam War fiscal expansion (deficits rising from ~1% to ~3% GDP) without monetary offset
- Fed under Martin and then Burns held the federal funds rate below what the Taylor Rule would have prescribed by 100-300bp
- The inflation-output correlation turned negative (supply-side driven: rising inflation with eventually declining output)
- Stock-bond price correlation: +0.30 to +0.50

*1973-1982 (Peak supply shock era):*
- Oil shocks of 1973 and 1979 (OPEC embargo, Iranian revolution)
- Productivity growth slowdown (from ~2.5% to ~1.0% TFP growth)
- Burns and Miller Fed repeatedly eased prematurely (1971, 1975, 1980), validating expectations de-anchoring
- Inflation expectations fully de-anchored: Survey of Professional Forecasters 10Y expectation rose to ~7-8%
- Stock-bond price correlation: +0.40 to +0.60 (the most persistently positive in the modern sample)
- **Key lesson for today:** The correlation did not flip immediately with the first oil shock. It became persistently positive only after the Fed had demonstrated through repeated policy reversals that it would accommodate rather than fight inflation. The *policy reaction function*, not the shock itself, was the ultimate driver.

*1982-1999 (Disinflation and transition):*
- Volcker's credibility establishment (1979-1982) began the transition
- Correlation declined from +0.40 to near zero by the mid-1990s
- The final shift to negative correlation occurred ~1997-2000, coinciding with (a) the Asian financial crisis (demand shock), (b) the dot-com boom (demand shock), and (c) the full anchoring of inflation expectations near 2%
- The Greenspan Fed's response to LTCM (1998) and Y2K concerns demonstrated credible countercyclical policy, reinforcing the demand-shock dominance

**Era 2: 2000-2021 — Demand Shocks and Credible Inflation Targeting**

Three demand-side events dominated: the dot-com bust (2000-2002), the GFC (2008-2009), and COVID (2020). All three involved:
- Sharp negative demand shocks
- Aggressive Fed easing (rate cuts + QE)
- Bonds rallying as equities fell
- Stock-bond price correlation: -0.30 to -0.60

The QE era (2009-2021) intensified negative correlation through an additional channel: the "Fed put." Market participants understood that equity market declines would trigger central bank easing, which would boost bond prices. This created a structural negative correlation through the policy reaction function, independent of the underlying shock composition.

**Era 3: 2022-Present — Transition/Ambiguous**

The 2022 episode (60/40 worst year since 1969) was a pure supply-shock-driven positive correlation event:
- Supply chain disruptions + fiscal stimulus → inflation surge (supply + demand interaction)
- Fed tightening rapidly → both stocks and bonds fell
- Stock-bond price correlation: +0.30 to +0.50 (approaching 1970s levels)

The 2023-2025 period has seen the correlation oscillate:
- 2023: Moderately negative (disinflation + no recession)
- 2024: Weakly negative to zero (inflation sticky, growth resilient)
- Early 2025-2026: Oscillating near zero (-0.10 to +0.20)

**The 1970s analogue assessment:**

The current environment shares three characteristics with the pre-1973 period:
1. Large fiscal deficits without credible consolidation path (Vietnam then, structural deficits now)
2. A central bank that has tightened but whose long-run commitment is uncertain (Burns parallel — will Powell's Fed tolerate a recession if tariffs push inflation up?)
3. Vulnerability to supply shocks (oil dependence then, tariff/deglobalization exposure now)

But there are critical differences:
1. Inflation expectations are currently anchored (5Y breakevens ~2.3-2.5% vs. de-anchored in the 1970s). This is the single most important difference.
2. Central bank independence is institutionally stronger (though potentially eroding under political pressure)
3. The global economy is more integrated, creating more channels for supply shocks but also more substitution possibilities

**Probability of 1970s-style persistent positive correlation:**
- Full repeat (expectations de-anchor, Fed credibility collapses, multi-year positive correlation): 8-12% over the next 3-5 years
- Partial rhyme (episodes of positive correlation during supply shock periods, but not persistent): 35-45% over the next 2-3 years
- Avoided (expectations remain anchored, Fed credibility holds, negative correlation persists as the norm with occasional brief flips): 45-55%

### Claim 8: Fiscal Multiplier and Fiscal Dominance Transition

**The New Keynesian fiscal multiplier framework:**

The fiscal multiplier is endogenous to the monetary policy stance (Christiano, Eichenbaum, and Rebelo, 2011; Woodford, 2011):

*At the ZLB:* Multiplier = 1.5-2.5 (monetary policy cannot offset, full Keynesian)
*Away from ZLB with Taylor Rule-following CB:* Multiplier = 0.5-0.9 (partial monetary offset)
*With Ricardian consumers and monetary offset:* Multiplier approaches 0 (full crowding out)

**Why this matters for the correlation regime:**

1. **Low multiplier (0.3-0.7) — Ricardian offset + monetary tightening:** Fiscal expansion has limited real effects. The dominant channel is through Treasury supply → term premium → long yields. This produces:
   - Higher long yields (supply-driven)
   - Modest equity impact (crowding out offsets fiscal stimulus)
   - **Positive correlation at the long end (both driven by supply), but weak because the real economic effects are small**

2. **High multiplier (1.0-1.5+) — fiscal dominance:** Fiscal expansion has large real effects because the central bank is constrained from offsetting. This produces:
   - Higher inflation
   - Higher nominal growth (initially positive for equities)
   - Higher long yields (both expectations AND term premium)
   - **Initially ambiguous correlation, then increasingly positive as inflation accelerates and the Fed eventually tightens into a weakening economy**

**The fiscal dominance diagnostic:**

Fiscal dominance exists when the fiscal authority's spending and borrowing decisions constrain the central bank's ability to set interest rates according to its optimal rule. In the New Keynesian framework, this occurs when:

1. **Debt/GDP ratio is high enough that interest rate increases significantly worsen the fiscal trajectory** — the "fiscal arithmetic" constraint (Sargent and Wallace, 1981, "Some Unpleasant Monetarist Arithmetic"). At US debt/GDP ~120% and a blended interest rate of ~3.3%, each 100bp rate increase adds ~$250-320B in annual debt service (KB: monetary_transmission_impairment, confidence 7.0). This partially offsets the contractionary effect of rate hikes by injecting more fiscal spending into the economy through interest payments.

2. **No credible fiscal consolidation path exists** — CBO projections show debt/GDP rising to ~130-140% by 2034 under current law, with deficits never falling below 5% of GDP. There is no plausible political economy scenario in which either party implements significant fiscal consolidation.

3. **The central bank is perceived to be influenced by fiscal considerations** — either directly (political pressure) or indirectly (concern about financial stability from higher rates).

**Current assessment:** The US is in the early stages of fiscal dominance, but not yet in the acute phase:
- Criterion 1: Partially met. Interest cost at ~$1T/year and rising.
- Criterion 2: Substantially met. No consolidation path.
- Criterion 3: Not yet met, but risk rising. Fed independence is institutionally protected but politically contested.

**Correlation implication:** As fiscal dominance intensifies, the fiscal multiplier rises (because the Fed cannot fully offset), the macro variance becomes more driven by fiscal shocks (which are supply-like in their correlation properties because they increase both inflation and output uncertainty), and the stock-bond correlation trends toward positive territory. This is a slow-moving, multi-year process, not a sudden event — but it is the secular trend that is most likely to produce a persistent correlation regime shift.

### Claim 9: Regime-Conditional Portfolio Construction

**The New Keynesian framework provides a structural basis for regime-conditional portfolio construction** because the optimal hedge portfolio is identified by the shock type, not by historical correlation estimates:

**Demand shock regime (negative correlation):**
- Long nominal duration is an effective equity hedge
- The Taylor Rule ensures that rate cuts accompany growth weakness
- Optimal: 60/40 or even 50/50 stock/bond with duration matched to the expected recession depth
- Expected Sharpe ratio of the bond hedge: 0.3-0.5

**Supply shock regime (positive correlation):**
- Long nominal duration is NOT an effective equity hedge — it amplifies losses
- The Taylor Rule forces the central bank to tighten (or at least hold) despite growth weakness
- Optimal: reduce nominal duration, replace with (a) TIPS (positive correlation with inflation, negative with real rates), (b) commodities (positive correlation with supply shocks), (c) cash (neutral), (d) options on rates/equities for convexity
- Expected Sharpe ratio of the nominal bond "hedge": negative (-0.1 to -0.3)

**Mixed/ambiguous regime (near-zero correlation):**
- Neither duration extension nor duration reduction is clearly optimal
- The key is to diversify across shock types
- Optimal: barbell of short-duration nominals (2Y, capturing Taylor Rule expectations) and real assets (TIPS, commodities, real estate), with options overlay for tail risk
- Expected Sharpe ratio of any single hedge: low (0.0-0.15)

**Current optimal allocation (conditional on 55% base, 25% supply shock, 20% demand recession):**

The probability-weighted optimal fixed income allocation:

| Component | Weight | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Short-duration nominals (2Y) | 35-40% of FI allocation | Captures Taylor Rule countercyclicality in all scenarios. 2Y-SPX correlation remains negative across regimes. |
| TIPS (5-10Y) | 20-25% | Positive real yield (~2.0-2.5%) with inflation protection. Useful in supply-shock scenario. |
| Long-duration nominals (10-30Y) | 15-20% (underweight vs. benchmark) | Hedge value degraded by fiscal supply dynamics. Maintain some exposure for demand recession scenario (20% probability). |
| Investment-grade credit | 15-20% | Carry with moderate spread risk. Performs in base case, underperforms in recession. |
| Cash/T-bills | 5-10% | Optionality. Real yield still ~2.0%+ after inflation. |

**Key portfolio insight from the New Keynesian framework:** The most robust portfolio action is NOT a directional bet on which correlation regime prevails, but a *rebalancing of the maturity profile* away from long duration toward short duration. This is because:
1. Short-duration bonds maintain their hedging properties across ALL scenarios (the Taylor Rule operates at the front end)
2. Long-duration bonds lose their hedging properties specifically in the supply-shock scenario (which has 25% probability and is the most damaging to a 60/40 portfolio)
3. The carry sacrifice is modest (~50-80bp of running yield between 2Y and 10Y in the current flat curve environment)

### Claim 10: Output Gap as Correlation Regime State Variable

**The output gap determines the cyclical vulnerability to correlation regime shifts through the Phillips Curve nonlinearity:**

When |x_t| is large (output far from potential):
- The direction of the next macro shock is more predictable (mean-reverting toward potential)
- The central bank's response is more predictable (ease if below, tighten if above)
- The correlation regime is more stable (demand-dominated dynamics)

When |x_t| ≈ 0 (output near potential):
- Small shocks can push the economy in either direction
- The central bank faces maximum uncertainty about the appropriate policy
- Supply shocks have maximum impact on the correlation because there is no demand shortfall to absorb
- The Phillips Curve is at its steepest point (convexity means the slope rises as the output gap narrows from either side)

**Current output gap estimates:**

| Source | Estimate (% of potential GDP) | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| CBO (Feb 2026) | ~-0.3% to 0.0% | Near potential |
| Federal Reserve Board (FRB/US) | ~-0.5% to +0.2% | Near potential |
| IMF WEO (Oct 2025) | ~-0.2% to +0.3% | Near potential |
| Real-time GDP tracking (Atlanta Fed GDPNow equivalent) | Consistent with ~0% gap | Near potential |

**All estimates converge on a closed or nearly closed output gap.** This is the state variable configuration that produces maximum correlation instability.

**The historical evidence is compelling:**
- Every sustained positive correlation episode (1973-1982, 2022) was preceded by the output gap being near zero or positive, making the economy vulnerable to supply shocks
- Sustained negative correlation episodes (2001-2003, 2008-2009) occurred when the output gap was deeply negative, and demand shocks dominated
- The 2015-2019 period of moderate negative correlation occurred with the output gap gradually closing from ~-2% to ~0%, and the correlation weakened as the gap narrowed

**Forecasting implication:** The near-zero output gap means the correlation regime will be determined by which type of shock hits next. This is fundamentally unpredictable — it depends on geopolitical events (tariff escalation), energy supply (OPEC+ decisions, geopolitical disruptions), and policy decisions (immigration, fiscal). The appropriate response is not to predict the shock but to position for both scenarios, which is why the regime-conditional portfolio approach (Claim 9) is operationally superior to a point forecast of the correlation.

The current S&P 500 at ~21x forward P/E embeds a negative correlation assumption — if correlation shifts persistently positive, the equity risk premium should rise by 50-100bp, implying a multiple compression to ~18-19x (roughly -10% to -15% equity drawdown from valuation adjustment alone).

### Fiscal Dominance & Monetary Policy Constraints (iter_0016)
This is perhaps the most direct connection. The entire fiscal dominance literature (Sargent-Wallace, Leeper, Cochrane's fiscal theory of the price level) is an alternative to the New Keynesian framework that makes *different predictions* about the correlation regime. Under fiscal dominance, the price level is determined by the government's intertemporal budget constraint, not by the central bank's Taylor Rule. If fiscal theory is correct, the stock-bond correlation is determined by the covariance of fiscal shocks with output, not by the central bank's reaction function. My analysis assumes the New Keynesian framework is the correct one (central bank determines inflation through interest rate policy), but I acknowledge that as fiscal dominance intensifies, the correlation dynamics increasingly resemble the fiscal theory predictions. The convergence of the two frameworks' predictions as fiscal dominance intensifies is itself an important finding.

## Open Questions

1. **How should we empirically distinguish "the correlation is near zero because we are in transition between regimes" from "the correlation is near zero because demand and supply shocks are roughly equally weighted in the current macro variance"?** These have identical observational implications for the unconditional correlation but very different implications for portfolio construction. The transition interpretation suggests the correlation will move to a new regime (positive or negative); the balanced-shock interpretation suggests it will stay near zero. Structural VAR models with sign restrictions (Uhlig, 2005) can in principle decompose the shock variance, but the results are sensitive to the identification scheme. I would need real-time shock decomposition with quarterly resolution to answer this.

2. **What is the correct functional form of the Phillips Curve nonlinearity near the zero output gap?** The "immaculate disinflation" debate has generated competing estimates that have very different implications for the supply-shock scenario. If the Phillips Curve is truly flat near zero (Benigno-Eggertsson-Romei convex specification), supply shocks produce inflation without much output cost, making the stagflationary scenario less severe. If it is steeper (Ball-Leigh specification), the output cost of disinflation is higher, making the stagflationary scenario more damaging. Resolving this requires another 1-2 years of data on the disinflation path to calibrate the slope.

3. **Is the maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation permanent (driven by the structural shift in fiscal policy from countercyclical to procyclical) or temporary (driven by the current QT + high issuance episode that will reverse)?** If permanent, portfolio construction should structurally shift away from long duration as a hedge. If temporary (ending when QT concludes and/or fiscal policy normalizes), the current bifurcation is a cyclical opportunity to buy long duration at distressed hedge ratios. I currently lean 60/40 permanent vs. temporary, but this is one of the lowest-confidence assessments in the analysis.

4. **How does the correlation regime interact with the dollar cycle?** The Mundell-Fleming framework predicts that a fiscal expansion with monetary tightening (the current US policy mix) should appreciate the currency, which has implications for EM carry trades and the global correlation regime. But the dollar has been range-bound rather than appreciating strongly, suggesting either (a) the fiscal expansion is being priced as debt-negative (dollar-weakening), or (b) other central banks are also tightening, neutralizing the Mundell-Fleming prediction. The FX-rates-equity three-way correlation structure is not well-developed in this analysis and would require deeper work in the FX Regime & Dollar Cycle topic.

5. **What is the empirical threshold for inflation expectations de-anchoring?** I cite the Coibion-Gorodnichenko 75-100bp threshold, but this was calibrated on pre-COVID data. The post-COVID episode saw 5Y breakevens briefly exceed 3.0% (March 2022) without triggering persistent de-anchoring — they reverted as supply chains healed. This suggests either (a) the threshold is higher than 100bp, or (b) the de-anchoring threshold is itself state-dependent (higher when the central bank has recently demonstrated inflation-fighting credibility by raising rates 525bp). Calibrating this threshold more precisely is essential for assessing the probability of the supply-shock scenario (Claim 3).

6. **Can machine learning / high-frequency approaches improve real-time regime identification?** The New Keynesian framework provides the structural theory, but translating it into real-time portfolio signals requires identifying the current shock regime with minimal lag. Markov-switching models (Hamilton, 1989; Ang and Bekaert, 2002) have been used for stock-bond correlation regimes, but they rely on the correlation itself as the state variable rather than the underlying shock decomposition. A model that uses real-time data on inflation surprises, growth surprises, and policy surprises to identify the shock regime *before* the correlation shifts could provide significant alpha. This is a bridge to the quant_researcher agents' work.

7. **How does AI-driven productivity growth (if it materializes) affect the correlation regime through r-star?** The stock_bond_correlation_regime KB concept (confidence 5.0) sketches an AI-dependent framework: Stage 1 (demand-driven investment) produces ambiguous correlation; Stage 2 (competitive pricing) produces negative correlation (deflationary growth). If AI genuinely raises TFP growth by 0.5-1.0%, it should raise r-star, which means current policy is less restrictive than consensus estimates suggest. Paradoxically, a higher r-star from AI productivity could temporarily push the correlation toward positive territory by keeping the economy above potential, even though the long-run effect is disinflationary. This interaction between secular technology trends and cyclical correlation dynamics needs further analysis.

8. **What is the role of market microstructure (basis trade, passive flows, vol targeting) in amplifying correlation regime shifts?** The basis trade (~$800B-$1.5T) compresses term premium, mechanically supporting negative correlation. An unwind would spike term premium, mechanically pushing toward positive correlation. Vol-targeting strategies (which reduce risk exposure when vol rises, regardless of whether the vol is driven by demand or supply shocks) can amplify correlation regime shifts by creating forced selling in both stocks and bonds simultaneously during a supply-shock episode. These microstructural amplifiers are not part of the New Keynesian model but are empirically significant — the March 2020 episode demonstrated that market microstructure can temporarily overwhelm fundamentals.

9. **How does the stock-bond correlation regime interact with the credit cycle?** The credit_vol_minsky_phases framework in the KB places the current cycle in "late Speculative phase" (HY-IG spread at 280-320bp). A transition to the "Ponzi phase" (spreads widening past 350-400bp) would represent a demand-side credit contraction that should reinforce negative stock-bond correlation — unless the credit contraction itself triggers a fiscal response that creates a supply-side inflation dynamic. The sequencing of credit contraction → fiscal response → inflation re-acceleration is the scenario in which the correlation could flip from strongly negative (initial credit crisis) to strongly positive (fiscal dominance aftermath) within a 12-24 month window.

10. **Is the New Keynesian framework itself the right lens for this analysis, or should the Fiscal Theory of the Price Level (FTPL) be given more weight?** My analysis assumes the central bank controls inflation through the interest rate rule (NK paradigm). The FTPL (Cochrane, 2023; Leeper, 2024) argues that inflation is ultimately determined by the government's intertemporal budget constraint, and that the correlation regime should be analyzed through the lens of fiscal shocks rather than monetary policy. As US fiscal dynamics deteriorate, the FTPL predictions become more relevant — not because the NK model is "wrong" but because the institutional prerequisites for the NK model to hold (independent central bank, credible fiscal solvency) are weakening. A truly robust analysis would assign weights to both frameworks conditional on the fiscal regime. I estimate the NK framework is currently the correct description ~70% of the time, with FTPL becoming increasingly relevant at ~30% — these weights should be updated as fiscal dynamics evolve.
