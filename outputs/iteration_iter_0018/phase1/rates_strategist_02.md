# Stock-Bond Correlation Regime Analysis — Central Bank Policy Transmission Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The stock-bond correlation regime is fundamentally determined by whether the dominant macro shock is a demand shock (negative correlation) or a supply/inflation shock (positive correlation), and central bank credibility is the mechanism that governs which type dominates market pricing.**

2. **The post-2020 flip to persistent positive stock-bond correlation is a direct consequence of impaired monetary policy transmission: central banks lost the ability to anchor inflation expectations, making inflation the marginal pricing factor for both asset classes simultaneously.**

3. **Financial conditions indices (FCIs) systematically understate the tightening effect of a positive correlation regime because they fail to account for the destruction of portfolio diversification, which mechanically raises effective portfolio volatility and risk premia even at unchanged asset price levels.**

4. **Central bank forward guidance loses potency in a positive correlation regime because the expectations channel — which relies on compressing term premia and supporting risk assets — cannot function when rate and equity moves are co-directional on inflation news.**

5. **The correlation regime is asymmetric around the policy rate: during hiking cycles the correlation tends to be more persistently positive than during cutting cycles, because tightening simultaneously pressures bond prices (higher rates) and equity valuations (higher discount rates), while easing benefits bonds directly but only helps equities with a lag through the credit channel.**

6. **The 1998-2020 negative correlation regime was an anomaly sustained by three conditions that are now partially or fully reversed: anchored inflation expectations below target, central bank credibility as an automatic stabilizer (the "Fed put"), and a demand-shock-dominated business cycle.**

7. **The PBOC and BOJ represent opposite extremes of how policy transmission shapes correlation: BOJ's yield curve control mechanically suppressed bond volatility and forced all macro adjustment into equities and FX, while PBOC's administered rate system impairs the interest rate channel so severely that correlation dynamics are driven by credit quantity rather than price.**

8. **The current (2025-2026) environment of sticky services inflation, fiscal expansion, and central bank balance sheet normalization creates structural support for positive correlation, and a return to the negative correlation regime requires either a significant deflationary shock or a multi-year rebuilding of central bank inflation-fighting credibility.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Demand vs. Supply Shock Dominance

The theoretical framework is straightforward but often misapplied. In a demand shock (e.g., recession fear), equities fall on weaker earnings expectations while bonds rally on expected rate cuts — producing negative correlation. In a supply/inflation shock, equities fall on margin compression and higher discount rates while bonds also fall on rising inflation expectations and term premia — producing positive correlation. The central bank's role is decisive: a *credible* inflation-targeting central bank converts supply shocks into demand shocks in market pricing, because markets trust the central bank will tighten sufficiently to kill the inflation, meaning the dominant second-round effect is the demand impact of that tightening. This is why the Volcker restoration of credibility in the early 1980s eventually led to a secular decline in positive correlation, reaching consistently negative territory by the late 1990s. The Campbell-Shiller-Viceira decomposition of stock-bond covariance confirms that the discount rate component (driven by monetary policy expectations) and the cash flow component (driven by real economy outcomes) shift in relative importance depending on the inflation regime.

### Claim 2: Post-2020 Correlation Flip and Impaired Transmission

The 2021-2023 inflation episode demonstrated that the transmission mechanism from policy rates to inflation expectations was severely impaired. Despite the fastest hiking cycle in four decades (0-5.25% in 16 months for the Fed), breakeven inflation expectations remained elevated and volatile well above the pre-COVID range. The stock-bond correlation flipped positive in Q1 2022 and has remained predominantly positive since. The key mechanism: when markets cannot confidently forecast the terminal rate because inflation persistence is uncertain, every data release simultaneously reprices both the bond curve (rate expectations) and equities (discount rate and growth expectations) in the same direction. CPI prints became the dominant single-day mover of both asset classes — upside surprises hit both stocks and bonds, downside surprises lifted both. This is the signature of a supply-shock-dominated regime where the central bank's reaction function is uncertain.

### Claim 3: FCI Mismeasurement Under Positive Correlation

Standard financial conditions indices (Goldman Sachs FCI, Chicago Fed NFCI, Bloomberg FCI) aggregate moves in rates, credit spreads, equities, and the dollar into a single measure of how tight or easy conditions are. But they treat these components as independent. In a negative correlation regime, a bond rally offsets an equity selloff, and the FCI captures the net effect reasonably well. In a positive correlation regime, coincident declines in both stocks and bonds amplify portfolio losses, raising realized volatility, triggering VaR-based deleveraging, and reducing risk appetite — none of which the FCI directly measures. A portfolio running 60/40 stocks/bonds with a historical assumption of -0.2 correlation suddenly faces portfolio volatility roughly 15-20% higher when correlation flips to +0.3. This is a tightening of *effective* financial conditions that the standard FCI misses. Risk parity funds, pension ALM frameworks, and bank capital models all embed negative correlation assumptions, meaning the true tightening from a regime flip is systematically larger than headline FCI readings suggest.

### Claim 4: Forward Guidance Impotence

Forward guidance operates through the expectations channel: by committing to a future path of rates (or indicating willingness to tolerate certain outcomes), central banks aim to compress term premia and support asset prices, thereby easing financial conditions beyond what the current policy rate alone would achieve. The classic example is the Fed's 2012-2013 "threshold guidance" (unemployment below 6.5% or inflation above 2.5% before considering hikes). This worked because in a negative correlation regime, dovish guidance simultaneously rallied bonds (lower expected rates) and equities (lower discount rates, implicit "put"). In a positive correlation regime, dovish guidance rallies bonds but may *not* rally equities if the reason for guidance is deteriorating growth — or hawkish guidance sells off both. The information content of guidance is contaminated by the correlation regime. Empirically, the R² of forward guidance surprises (measured as changes in 2-year yields on FOMC days) on equity returns dropped significantly during 2022-2023 vs. 2015-2019, consistent with the positive correlation undermining the asset price channel.

### Claim 5: Asymmetry Between Hiking and Cutting Cycles

During hiking cycles, both the direct rate effect (bond prices fall) and the discount rate effect on equities (valuations compress) push in the same direction. The credit channel reinforces this with a lag as tighter lending standards reduce credit availability. During cutting cycles, bonds rally immediately but equities may not follow if the cuts are a response to genuine economic deterioration — the "bad news cuts" problem. The 2001 and 2008 cutting cycles saw negative stock-bond correlation persist because cuts were clearly responding to demand shocks. But the early stages of cutting cycles initiated from a positive correlation regime are ambiguous: are cuts a response to mission-accomplished disinflation (bullish for equities) or deteriorating growth (bearish)? This ambiguity keeps correlation elevated until the market resolves the question. The practical implication is that the traditional "buy bonds as recession hedge" trade has a lower Sharpe ratio in positive correlation environments, precisely when the hedge is most needed.

### Claim 6: The 1998-2020 Anomaly

The negative correlation regime that dominated 1998-2020 depended on three structural conditions: (a) inflation expectations were firmly anchored at or below 2%, meaning supply shocks were not the marginal driver of macro pricing; (b) central banks had demonstrated willingness and ability to cut aggressively in downturns (Greenspan put → Bernanke put → Yellen/Powell put), making monetary easing the expected response to equity declines; (c) the dominant shock type was demand-driven — the dot-com bust, GFC, European debt crisis, and COVID were all demand/financial shocks. Prior to 1998 — specifically from the late 1960s through the late 1990s — the correlation was predominantly positive, driven by inflation volatility and less credible monetary policy. The 2020s environment echoes the pre-1998 period in key respects: inflation has become volatile and uncertain, central bank credibility is damaged (the "transitory" error), and supply shocks (pandemic dislocations, energy transition, geopolitical fragmentation) are at least as important as demand shocks.

### Claim 7: BOJ and PBOC as Extreme Cases

BOJ's yield curve control (YCC, 2016-2024) was the most extreme form of interest rate channel manipulation in modern central banking. By capping 10-year JGB yields, the BOJ mechanically eliminated bond volatility and forced all adjustment into other channels — primarily the exchange rate and equity prices. The stock-bond correlation in Japan was suppressed near zero during YCC because bonds simply couldn't move. When YCC was widened (Dec 2022) and eventually abandoned (Mar 2024), JGB volatility surged and Japanese stock-bond correlation repriced. The PBOC operates through a fundamentally different transmission mechanism: administered deposit/lending rates, reserve requirements, window guidance, and direct credit allocation. The interest rate channel is intentionally muted, meaning Chinese stock-bond correlation is driven primarily by credit impulse and liquidity conditions rather than rate expectations. This makes Chinese stock-bond correlation behavior largely orthogonal to the framework that governs DM correlations.

### Claim 8: Structural Support for Positive Correlation in 2025-2026

Multiple factors support the persistence of positive stock-bond correlation in the current environment. First, services inflation remains sticky in the 3-4% range across most DM economies, keeping inflation uncertainty elevated. Second, fiscal policy is expansionary in the US (deficit >6% of GDP) and not significantly consolidating in Europe, adding demand-side inflation pressure that monetary policy must offset — a direct echo of the fiscal dominance dynamics explored in iter_0016. Third, central bank balance sheet normalization (QT) is ongoing, which mechanically adds term premium to bonds without directly tightening equity valuations, but the net effect is ambiguous and keeps rate volatility elevated. Fourth, the supply-side structural shifts (onshoring, energy transition, demographics) are inherently inflationary and create persistent supply-shock characteristics. A return to negative correlation would require either a sharp deflationary shock (severe recession, financial crisis) that overwhelms these structural factors, or a sustained period of below-target inflation that rebuilds central bank credibility — neither of which appears likely in the base case through 2026.

## Confidence Assessment

1. **Demand vs. supply shock framework for correlation** — **9/10**. This is the most robust theoretical framework, supported by decades of empirical work (Campbell, Sunderam & Viceira 2017; Baele, Bekaert & Inghelbrecht 2010). The only uncertainty is in edge cases where both shock types are present simultaneously.

2. **Post-2020 flip due to impaired transmission** — **8/10**. The timing and mechanism are well-supported. Slight uncertainty because pandemic-era fiscal policy (direct transfers) may have been as important as monetary policy impairment in driving the inflation that flipped the regime.

3. **FCI mismeasurement under positive correlation** — **7/10**. The logic is sound and the mechanical portfolio effects are quantifiable. Lower confidence because some newer FCI constructions (e.g., Fed's FCI-G) attempt to account for interaction effects, and the magnitude of the mismeasurement is model-dependent.

4. **Forward guidance impotence in positive correlation** — **7/10**. Theoretically clear but the empirical evidence is limited to one full cycle (2022-2024). The counterfactual — what would have happened without guidance — is inherently unobservable.

5. **Hiking/cutting cycle asymmetry** — **6/10**. This is partially supported by historical data but the sample of clean regime transitions is small. The 2024-2025 cutting cycle provides a live test.

6. **1998-2020 as anomaly** — **8/10**. Well-supported by the longer historical record (pre-1998 was predominantly positive correlation). The characterization of the three enabling conditions is somewhat stylized but captures the key dynamics.

7. **BOJ/PBOC as extreme cases** — **7/10**. The BOJ YCC case is well-documented. The PBOC characterization is more qualitative and the data on Chinese stock-bond correlation is noisier due to capital controls and market microstructure differences.

8. **Structural support for positive correlation 2025-2026** — **6/10**. This is inherently a forward-looking assessment. The structural factors are real but the timing and magnitude of any correlation shift depend on shock realizations that are by definition unpredictable. A severe recession could flip the regime quickly.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary Policy Transmission (monetary_policy):** This is the core connection. The correlation regime is arguably the single best summary statistic of how monetary policy transmission is functioning. Negative correlation = transmission intact (central bank is the marginal stabilizer). Positive correlation = transmission impaired (inflation/supply shocks dominate central bank response).

**Inflation Regimes (inflation_regimes):** The correlation regime is almost perfectly mapped to the inflation regime. Low and stable inflation → negative correlation. High, volatile, or uncertain inflation → positive correlation. The transition dynamics between inflation regimes are the most important driver of correlation regime transitions.

**Fiscal Dominance (iter_0016, fiscal_dominance):** Fiscal dominance directly promotes positive stock-bond correlation because expansionary fiscal policy in an inflationary environment forces central banks to tighten more (or accept higher inflation), creating coincident pressure on both stocks and bonds. The bond vigilante mechanism — where fiscal concerns drive term premia higher — hits both asset classes when equities are also repricing the growth drag from higher rates.

**Sovereign Debt Sustainability (iter_0017, sovereign_debt):** Rising debt/GDP ratios increase the term premium embedded in government bonds, which mechanically raises the correlation between rate moves and equity discount rate moves. Debt sustainability concerns can trigger coincident selloffs in both government bonds (credit risk) and equities (growth risk from potential austerity or financial instability).

**Volatility Regimes (volatility_regimes):** The correlation regime and volatility regime interact powerfully. Positive stock-bond correlation raises portfolio-level volatility for any given level of single-asset volatility, potentially triggering volatility-targeting and risk-parity rebalancing flows that amplify drawdowns. The "correlation crisis" is a distinct type of volatility event not well captured by single-asset vol measures.

**Equity Cycles (equity_cycles):** The correlation regime determines the discount rate sensitivity of equity valuations. In a negative correlation regime, lower rates buffer equity valuations during growth scares. In a positive correlation regime, rate increases compound equity decompression, making multiples more fragile and mean-reversion dynamics more violent.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the threshold level of inflation uncertainty (measured, say, by the dispersion of inflation forecasts or the width of inflation option-implied distributions) at which the correlation regime flips?** Is there a sharp threshold or a gradual transition? Identifying this threshold would be the single most valuable input for portfolio construction.

2. **How does the correlation regime interact with the neutral rate (r*) debate?** If r* has genuinely risen (due to fiscal expansion, AI investment, etc.), does the higher equilibrium rate level itself promote positive correlation even if inflation is stable, because rate volatility around a higher mean generates larger discount rate effects?

3. **Can central banks deliberately engineer a return to negative correlation, and if so, through what channel?** Would an explicit shift to average inflation targeting with credible commitment (i.e., accepting below-target inflation to rebuild credibility) be sufficient? Or is the correlation regime determined by structural factors (supply shocks, fiscal policy) that are outside central bank control?

4. **What is the empirical half-life of a correlation regime?** The negative regime lasted roughly 22 years (1998-2020). The preceding positive regime lasted roughly 30 years (late 1960s - late 1990s). Is the current positive regime likely to persist for decades, or could structural differences (faster information processing, more active central bank communication) shorten the cycle?

5. **How should central banks adjust their reaction function when they observe that the correlation regime has flipped?** Standard Taylor rule prescriptions are correlation-regime-agnostic, but the welfare implications of tightening are different when financial conditions tighten through both the rate and asset price channels simultaneously vs. when the asset price channel partially offsets rate tightening.

6. **What role does central bank balance sheet policy (QE/QT) play in the correlation regime, independent of the policy rate?** QE mechanically compresses bond volatility (similar to a weak form of YCC), which should suppress the positive correlation channel. Does QT, by restoring bond supply and duration risk to the private sector, structurally promote positive correlation?

7. **Is the intraday vs. monthly vs. annual correlation meaningfully different, and if so, which horizon matters most for policy transmission?** High-frequency correlation may be driven by algorithmic co-trading, while low-frequency correlation reflects fundamental macro regime. Central bank transmission operates at the medium-frequency (weeks to months) level. If these diverge, which one determines the effective transmission mechanism?
