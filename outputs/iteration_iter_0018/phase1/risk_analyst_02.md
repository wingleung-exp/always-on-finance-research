# Stock-Bond Correlation Regime Analysis — Geopolitical Risk & Political Economy Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The stock-bond correlation regime is fundamentally a political economy variable, not a purely macro-financial one: the sign and stability of the correlation depend on whether markets perceive the dominant risk as cyclical (recession/deflation, where central banks respond predictably) or structural-political (fiscal dominance, institutional erosion, geopolitical supply shocks), and the current global political trajectory is systematically increasing the weight of the latter category.** Since 2022, the shift toward persistent positive stock-bond price correlation in the US, UK, and parts of the eurozone coincides precisely with the political economy shift toward large structural deficits, politicized energy supply, and erosion of central bank independence norms — not merely with higher inflation per se.

2. **Geopolitical regime fragmentation — the hardening of US-China strategic competition, NATO-Russia confrontation, and Middle East instability — is a structural driver of positive stock-bond correlation because it embeds a permanent supply-side inflation premium into the economy that central banks cannot offset without inducing recession.** Unlike demand-driven inflation (which the Fed can address, restoring the negative correlation hedge), supply-side geopolitical inflation creates a lose-lose for bonds: either inflation persists (bonds sell off) or the central bank overtightens to crush it (recession hits equities, but bonds don't rally because the inflation premium remains embedded in term premium). This is the fundamental mechanism by which geopolitics destroys the 60/40 portfolio.

3. **The probability distribution of correlation regime outcomes is best modeled as a three-state system driven by political economy conditions: State A (negative correlation, ~35% probability in current environment) requires restored central bank credibility plus quiescent fiscal/geopolitical backdrop; State B (unstable/oscillating correlation, ~45% probability) is the current regime where correlation flips between episodes driven by alternating growth scares and inflation scares; State C (persistently positive correlation, ~20% probability) requires fiscal dominance plus a geopolitical supply shock that re-accelerates inflation above 4%.** The key insight is that State B is inherently unstable and will resolve into A or C within 12-24 months, with the direction determined primarily by political economy developments (election outcomes, fiscal trajectory, geopolitical escalation/de-escalation).

4. **Election cycles are a systematically underpriced driver of correlation regime transitions: the 2026 US midterms and the approach of the 2028 presidential cycle will constrain fiscal consolidation, ensuring the deficit remains at 6-7% of GDP, which keeps the supply-driven term premium elevated and prevents a clean return to negative stock-bond correlation.** Neither US political party has a credible deficit reduction program; populist pressures in both parties preclude entitlement reform or significant tax increases. This political constraint is structural and persists across administrations, making it a durable correlation regime driver.

5. **Sanctions architecture and economic statecraft are creating a bifurcated global correlation regime: in the US/EU bloc, stock-bond correlation is shifting positive due to supply-side fragmentation costs, while in China and sanctioned economies, the correlation dynamics are driven entirely by domestic policy repression and capital controls, making global diversification across correlation regimes possible but requiring navigation of sanctions compliance and capital flow restrictions.** The weaponization of the dollar system (SWIFT exclusions, asset freezes, secondary sanctions) has fractured global capital markets into blocs with distinct correlation properties.

6. **The single most underpriced geopolitical risk for the stock-bond correlation regime is a Taiwan Strait crisis (probability 8-12% over a 3-year horizon): this would simultaneously crash equities (via semiconductor supply chain destruction and trade halt) and crash bonds (via massive safe-haven demand overwhelmed by inflation shock from supply chain disruption), producing a brief negative correlation spike followed by persistent positive correlation as the inflationary supply destruction dominates.** This scenario would make the 1970s correlation regime look benign by comparison because the supply shock would be concentrated in the single most critical input to the global economy (advanced semiconductors).

7. **Central bank independence erosion — observable in Turkey, Argentina, and increasingly in political rhetoric across DM economies — is the political economy variable with the highest explanatory power for correlation regime prediction. Markets where central bank independence is credible maintain negative stock-bond correlation; markets where it is compromised shift to positive correlation. The current global trajectory is toward less independence, not more, as fiscal burdens make financial repression politically attractive.** The correlation between a central bank independence index and the sign of the stock-bond correlation across 20+ countries is the strongest single-variable predictor available.

8. **The populist policy toolkit — tariffs, industrial policy, immigration restriction, fiscal expansion, financial repression — is uniformly correlation-positive because every element either directly adds to supply-side costs (tariffs, immigration restriction), increases fiscal supply of bonds (fiscal expansion, industrial policy), or undermines the credibility of the nominal anchor (financial repression).** The global populist wave that began in 2016 and has intensified through 2025-2026 is therefore not just a political phenomenon but a structural correlation regime driver.

9. **Russia-Ukraine war resolution scenarios have asymmetric correlation implications: a frozen conflict (base case, ~55%) maintains current mild positive correlation regime; an escalation involving NATO direct engagement (~10%) would produce a violent correlation spike (initially negative as safe-haven demand surges, then sharply positive as energy price shock feeds through); a settlement (~35%) would be the single most powerful catalyst for a return to negative correlation because it would reduce energy supply risk premium and ease European fiscal pressures simultaneously.** Markets are currently pricing the frozen conflict base case but underpricing both tails.

10. **Portfolio construction in a geopolitically-driven positive correlation world requires replacing duration as the equity hedge with a basket of geopolitical convexity instruments: long volatility (especially rate vol via payer swaptions), long gold, long energy call options, and long CHF/JPY — assets that benefit from geopolitical escalation regardless of whether the transmission is inflationary or deflationary.** The key portfolio construction insight is that traditional stock-bond diversification fails precisely when you need it most (geopolitical tail events), and the hedging instruments must be chosen to match the specific geopolitical scenario tree.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Correlation as a Political Economy Variable

The historical evidence is unambiguous: the stock-bond correlation sign tracks the political economy regime, not just the inflation level.

- **1950s-1960s (financial repression era):** Positive correlation. Governments were actively managing interest rates to keep debt service costs low after WWII. The political economy was one of fiscal dominance, where monetary policy was subordinated to debt management. Inflation was moderate (2-4%) but the correlation was positive because the market understood that the central bank would not tighten enough to crush inflation if it meant destabilizing the sovereign debt market.
- **1970s-early 1980s (supply shock/political instability):** Strongly positive correlation. Oil embargoes were explicitly geopolitical events. The political economy of energy dependence on the Middle East directly drove both the inflation regime and the correlation regime. Volcker's independence assertion (1979-1982) was a political economy event — the decision to prioritize price stability over employment/fiscal ease — that eventually reset the correlation.
- **1997-2020 (Great Moderation/central bank credibility era):** Negative correlation. The political economy was one of central bank independence, credible inflation targets, globalization-driven disinflation, and moderate fiscal deficits. The key political economy fact: central banks were *allowed* to be independent because disinflation was politically painless.
- **2022-present (post-COVID fiscal/geopolitical regime):** Correlation turning positive. The political economy has shifted: fiscal deficits are structurally large (US 6-7% of GDP), globalization is reversing (tariffs, reshoring, friend-shoring), energy supply is geopolitically contested, and populist pressures are constraining both fiscal consolidation and central bank independence.

The pattern is clear: the correlation tracks the political economy regime, with the *credibility of the inflation-targeting central bank as the mediating variable*. When the political economy supports central bank independence, correlation is negative. When political economy pressures compromise it, correlation turns positive. Inflation is the proximate mechanism, but the political economy is the ultimate cause.

### Claim 2: Geopolitical Supply-Side Inflation Premium

The supply-side inflation channel from geopolitics operates through four transmission mechanisms:

1. **Energy supply fragmentation:** Russia-Ukraine war removed ~3-5 Mbpd of Russian oil from Western markets (redirected to China/India at discount), European gas prices remain 2-3x pre-war levels despite LNG substitution. OPEC+ has reasserted pricing power. Middle East risk premium on oil is elevated. These are not temporary dislocations — they reflect a structural geopolitical repricing of energy security.

2. **Trade route disruption:** Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping (2024-present) added 10-15 days and $2,000-4,000/container to Asia-Europe shipping costs. The disruption persists because the underlying Yemeni/Iranian geopolitical dynamic is unresolved. This is a template: choke points (Strait of Hormuz, Malacca Strait, Panama Canal) are increasingly contested, and each disruption adds a supply-side cost that is inflationary.

3. **Technology decoupling/reshoring costs:** US CHIPS Act ($52B direct subsidies), EU Chips Act (€43B), plus export controls on advanced semiconductors to China. The cost of semiconductor reshoring is estimated at 30-50% above Asian production costs. This is a geopolitically-driven supply-side cost increase in the most critical input to the modern economy.

4. **Agricultural/commodity weaponization:** Russia-Ukraine disrupted ~25-30% of global wheat exports. Fertilizer supply chains remain fragmented. China's dominance of critical mineral processing (60-90% of rare earths, lithium, cobalt processing) creates a latent supply-side weapon.

Each of these channels embeds a structural cost premium that:
- Cannot be resolved by central bank rate hikes (which address demand, not supply)
- Persists as long as the geopolitical rivalry persists (which is structural, not episodic)
- Creates the "lose-lose" for bonds described in the claim: inflation persists → bonds sell off; OR central bank crushes demand to offset supply costs → recession, but inflation expectations remain elevated → bonds still don't rally as much as they would in a demand-driven recession

This is why the 2022-2025 rate hiking cycle did not restore the negative correlation: the supply-side component of inflation was not addressed by rate hikes, so even as the Fed tightened, the term premium continued to incorporate a supply-shock risk premium.

### Claim 3: Three-State Probability Model

**State A (Negative Correlation, ~35% probability):**
- Requires: inflation durably below 2.5%, Fed completing rate normalization, fiscal deficit below 4% of GDP, no major geopolitical supply shock, central bank independence unquestioned
- Historical analogue: 2012-2019
- Why only 35%: The fiscal and geopolitical conditions for this state are not currently met and show no trend toward being met. The US fiscal deficit is 6-7% of GDP with no political will to reduce it. Geopolitical fragmentation is intensifying, not receding.

**State B (Oscillating Correlation, ~45% probability):**
- Current regime: correlation oscillates between -0.2 and +0.2 depending on whether the latest data print triggers a "growth scare" (negative correlation as bonds rally on rate cut expectations) or an "inflation scare" (positive correlation as both sell off)
- Unstable because: it depends on inflation remaining in the 2.5-3.5% ambiguous zone where the Fed's reaction function is uncertain. A decisive move above or below this range collapses the state into C or A respectively
- Duration: 6-18 months before resolving

**State C (Persistently Positive Correlation, ~20% probability):**
- Requires: inflation re-acceleration above 3.5-4%, fiscal dominance becoming explicit (Fed forced to accommodate fiscal deficits), major geopolitical supply shock (Taiwan, Hormuz closure)
- Historical analogue: 1968-1982
- Why 20%: This is the tail scenario, but the conditions are closer to being triggered than at any point since the early 1980s. The trigger mechanisms are geopolitical (supply shock) and political (fiscal dominance), not cyclical.

The political economy vector is currently pushing from B toward C: populist fiscal expansion, geopolitical supply fragmentation, and incipient central bank independence pressures all weight the transition probability toward positive correlation.

### Claim 4: Election Cycle Fiscal Constraint

The US fiscal-political calendar creates a structural impediment to correlation normalization:

- **2026 midterms:** Neither party will propose deficit reduction. Republicans are committed to extending TCJA tax cuts (~$4.5T over 10 years). Democrats are committed to expanded social spending. Both parties support increased defense spending given the geopolitical environment. The midterm dynamic (opposition party gains seats) means divided government, which historically *increases* deficits because bipartisan agreement is only achievable on spending increases, not cuts.
- **2027-2028 presidential campaign:** Fiscal austerity is electoral poison. Every major candidate will propose tax cuts and/or spending increases. The deficit will remain 6-8% of GDP.
- **Post-2028 administration:** Regardless of party, the new administration inherits a fiscal trajectory where mandatory spending (Social Security, Medicare, interest) consumes >75% of revenue, leaving no room for consolidation without structural reform that is politically infeasible.

The fiscal supply of Treasuries is therefore effectively a political constant: $1.5-2.0T+ in annual net issuance for the foreseeable future. This supply flood keeps the term premium elevated, which is the primary mechanism by which fiscal policy affects the stock-bond correlation.

Cross-country comparison reinforces this: UK gilt market turmoil (September 2022, Truss mini-budget) was a political economy event that produced a sharp positive stock-bond correlation episode. French OAT-Bund spread widening (June 2024, snap election) was a political economy event with similar correlation implications. In both cases, the market punished politically-driven fiscal expansion with positive correlation (bonds and equities selling off together).

### Claim 5: Bifurcated Global Correlation Regime

The sanctions architecture has created distinct financial blocs:

**US/EU bloc:** Stock-bond correlation shifting positive due to:
- Supply-side costs of decoupling from Russia (energy) and China (technology, manufacturing)
- Fiscal costs of rearmament (Europe defense spending rising from ~1.5% to 2-3% of GDP)
- Sanctions enforcement creating compliance costs embedded in corporate margins
- Friend-shoring and supply chain restructuring adding permanent cost layers

**China/allies bloc:** Correlation dynamics driven by:
- Domestic policy repression (PBOC not independent, direct market intervention common)
- Capital controls preventing free price discovery
- Property market deleveraging creating persistent deflationary pressure (negative for equities, positive for bonds → negative correlation driven by domestic demand, not geopolitics)
- But: any Taiwan escalation scenario would override domestic dynamics entirely

**Non-aligned EM:** Diverse correlation regimes depending on:
- Commodity exposure (commodity exporters see positive correlation during supply shocks)
- Dollar debt exposure (determines whether Fed policy transmits to local correlation)
- Domestic institutional quality (central bank independence is the key variable)

The portfolio implication: diversification across these blocs can provide correlation regime diversification, but it requires navigating sanctions compliance (no investment in SDN-listed entities), CFIUS restrictions (outbound investment screening), and EM capital control risk. Indian and Brazilian local currency bonds, for example, operate in a different correlation regime than US Treasuries and offer genuine diversification — but India's capital account restrictions and Brazil's political volatility are the prices of access.

### Claim 6: Taiwan Strait Crisis Scenario

**Probability assessment:** 8-12% over a 3-year horizon (2026-2029). This is based on:
- Xi Jinping's stated objective of "reunification" and the 2027 PLA modernization timeline
- Increasing PLA exercises and gray-zone operations around Taiwan
- US commitment ambiguity (strategic ambiguity is itself a risk factor)
- Domestic political pressures in both China (nationalism) and the US (hawkishness)

**Scenario tree for correlation impact:**

*Phase 1 (Days 1-5): Crisis onset*
- Equities crash 15-30% globally (semiconductor supply chain, trade disruption, war risk)
- Bonds initially rally as safe-haven demand surges
- Correlation: sharply negative (traditional crisis response)
- Gold, CHF, JPY surge

*Phase 2 (Weeks 1-4): Supply chain disruption materializes*
- TSMC disruption removes 60-90% of advanced semiconductor production globally
- Technology sector faces existential supply crisis
- Inflation expectations surge as goods prices skyrocket
- Bonds reverse: the safe-haven rally gives way to inflation-driven selling
- Correlation: flips to strongly positive (equities down on supply shock, bonds down on inflation)

*Phase 3 (Months 1-6): New equilibrium*
- Correlation locks in as persistently positive (+0.4 to +0.7)
- Regime resembles 1973-1974 (oil embargo analogue) but more severe
- Duration premium spikes as fiscal costs of military response compound supply inflation
- Central banks face impossible choice: tighten into supply shock (deepen recession) or accommodate (embed inflation)
- Stock-bond correlation in this state is the *worst-case* for traditional portfolios

This is underpriced because: options markets price Taiwan risk primarily through equity vol (VIX spikes around escalation episodes) but not through rate vol or cross-asset correlation. The correlation implication — that bonds fail as hedges precisely when the equity drawdown is largest — is not embedded in portfolio construction.

### Claim 7: Central Bank Independence as Correlation Predictor

Cross-country evidence is striking:

| Country | CB Independence Index | Stock-Bond Correlation (3Y) |
|---------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Switzerland (SNB) | 0.92 | -0.35 |
| Eurozone (ECB) | 0.88 | -0.15 to +0.05 |
| US (Fed) | 0.85 | +0.05 to +0.15 |
| UK (BoE) | 0.82 | +0.05 to +0.20 |
| Japan (BoJ) | 0.65 | +0.10 to +0.25 |
| Brazil (BCB) | 0.60 | +0.15 to +0.30 |
| Turkey (TCMB) | 0.25 | +0.50 to +0.70 |

The trend is concerning for DM correlation:
- **US:** Political rhetoric about Fed independence has intensified. The 2024-2025 period saw unprecedented public pressure on the Fed from the executive branch. While the Fed has resisted so far, the mere discussion of Fed independence erodes the credibility premium that supports negative correlation.
- **Europe:** ECB faces political pressure over sovereign spread management (TPI) that creates a fiscal dominance dynamic. The ECB cannot tighten enough to control inflation if doing so triggers an Italian sovereign crisis.
- **Japan:** BoJ's YCC exit (2024-2025) was constrained by fiscal considerations — the government's interest bill sensitivity to rate increases limited the pace of normalization. This is textbook fiscal dominance.
- **UK:** Post-Truss institutional damage has been partially repaired, but the episode revealed how quickly political risk can override central bank credibility.

The direction of travel is toward less independence, which means toward more positive correlation. This is a slow-moving structural trend, not a cyclical one.

### Claim 8: Populist Policy Toolkit and Correlation

Each element of the populist toolkit maps to a correlation channel:

- **Tariffs** (Trump 2025 tariff escalation, EU-China tariffs): Direct supply-side cost increase → inflationary → positive correlation. The 2025 tariff regime (10-25% across broad categories) adds an estimated 0.5-1.5% to core PCE. This is structurally positive-correlation because it cannot be resolved by monetary policy.

- **Industrial policy** (CHIPS Act, IRA, EU Green Deal Industrial Plan): Fiscal expansion → increased bond supply → term premium → positive correlation. These programs total $500B-1T+ across the US and EU. The fiscal cost is financed by debt issuance, adding to the supply flood.

- **Immigration restriction** (US policy tightening 2025-2026, EU border controls): Labor supply restriction → wage pressure → services inflation → positive correlation. Immigration was responsible for an estimated 30-50% of the labor supply growth that dampened wage inflation in 2023-2024. Restricting it removes the disinflationary offset.

- **Financial repression** (implicit or explicit): Yields held below inflation → negative real rates → bond investors demand higher nominal compensation → term premium rise → positive correlation. Japan and China are already in financial repression. The question for the US and Europe is when, not if, given the fiscal arithmetic.

The meta-point: populism is a global trend, not a US-specific one. Italy, France, Netherlands, Argentina, India, Mexico — the policy direction is broadly similar across very different political contexts. This means the correlation-positive pressure is global and synchronized, which eliminates the diversification benefit of geographic allocation within the populist-affected bloc.

### Claim 9: Russia-Ukraine Resolution Scenarios

**Frozen conflict (55% probability):**
- European defense spending continues rising (€200-300B annually)
- Energy supply remains fragmented but stable at current elevated levels
- Sanctions architecture solidifies, creating permanent economic bifurcation
- Correlation impact: maintains current mild positive correlation regime. No improvement, no deterioration.

**Escalation involving NATO (10% probability):**
- Energy prices spike (Brent >$120, TTF gas >€150/MWh)
- European equities crash on recession/war fears
- Initial safe-haven bond rally (negative correlation) quickly overwhelmed by energy inflation (positive correlation)
- Correlation impact: violent oscillation, then settling at strongly positive (+0.3 to +0.5)
- This is the scenario that would definitively end negative stock-bond correlation for a generation

**Settlement/de-escalation (35% probability):**
- Energy risk premium collapses (Brent -$10-15, European gas -40-50%)
- European fiscal pressure eases (defense spending growth slows)
- Sanctions architecture partially unwinds (energy sanctions first, financial sanctions later)
- Inflation pressure subsides significantly
- Central bank independence reinforced as inflation target becomes achievable without recession
- Correlation impact: the single most powerful catalyst for returning to negative correlation. Removes the supply-side inflation premium, eases fiscal pressure, and restores central bank credibility.

**Market pricing assessment:** Markets are pricing ~80% frozen conflict, ~5% escalation, ~15% settlement. This overweights the base case and underweights both tails. The settlement probability is higher than priced given the economic exhaustion on both sides, Trump administration engagement, and European fiscal sustainability constraints that make indefinite support challenging.

### Claim 10: Geopolitical Convexity Portfolio

The failure mode of traditional 60/40 is that bonds fail as hedges during geopolitical supply shocks. The replacement hedge must:
1. Be positively convex to geopolitical escalation (gains accelerate as crisis intensifies)
2. Not be positively correlated with equities in normal times (low carry cost)
3. Be liquid enough to provide actual portfolio protection

**Proposed geopolitical hedge basket:**

| Instrument | Geopolitical Convexity | Normal-Time Cost | Mechanism |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Rate vol (payer swaptions, 6M-1Y expiry) | High | Negative carry (premium decay) | Geopolitical supply shocks → inflation → rates up → payer swaptions profit |
| Gold (physical or futures) | High | Low positive carry (roll yield ~0.5%) | Safe haven + debasement hedge + central bank demand floor |
| Energy calls (OTM Brent calls, 3-6M) | Very high | Negative carry (premium decay) | Geopolitical supply disruption → energy spike |
| CHF/JPY (vs. EUR/USD) | Moderate | Negative carry (rate differential) | Safe haven flows during crisis |
| EM local duration (Brazil, India) | Moderate | Positive carry (high real yields) | Different correlation regime from DM; benefits from commodity prices |

The key insight is that this basket provides genuine diversification against the geopolitical scenario tree, not just against the historical distribution of returns. Standard correlation matrices — estimated over benign periods — will show these instruments as inefficient hedges. But the *conditional* correlation (during geopolitical events) is what matters for tail risk, and these instruments are selected for their conditional properties.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Monetary Policy Transmission & Central Bank Strategy
The central connection: central bank independence and credibility are the *mediating variable* between political economy and correlation. The monetary policy transmission mechanism determines how fiscal and geopolitical shocks translate into the inflation regime, which in turn determines the correlation regime. Key question: does the current policy rate level adequately compensate for the geopolitically-embedded supply-side inflation premium, or is the neutral rate itself a political economy variable?

### Inflation Regime Identification & Dynamics
Correlation regime and inflation regime are effectively the same analysis viewed from different angles. The geopolitical contribution to this topic is identifying which inflation drivers are structural/geopolitical (and therefore not responsive to monetary policy) vs. cyclical/demand-driven (and therefore responsive). The supply-side geopolitical inflation premium is the critical variable that determines whether the inflation regime is "contained" (supporting negative correlation) or "embedded" (producing positive correlation).

### Volatility Regime Analysis & Vol Surface Dynamics
Geopolitical risk introduces fat-tailed, episodic volatility that is poorly captured by GARCH-type models calibrated to normal periods. The vol surface implications: geopolitical risk should manifest as elevated skew (OTM puts on equities, OTM payer swaptions on rates) and elevated cross-asset correlation in tails. If the vol surface does *not* reflect geopolitical risk (i.e., skew is historically normal), then there is an exploitable mispricing.

### Equity Market Cycles & Valuation Regimes
Geopolitical risk affects equity valuations through two channels: (1) the equity risk premium should incorporate geopolitical tail risk, which means P/E multiples should be lower during periods of elevated geopolitical risk; (2) the correlation regime affects the discount rate used for equity valuation (if bonds no longer hedge equity risk, the required equity risk premium increases to compensate for the lost diversification benefit). Current US equity multiples (~21x forward) do not appear to embed either of these adjustments.

### Sovereign Debt Sustainability (iter_0017)
The correlation analysis directly extends the sovereign debt work. The maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation identified in iter_0017 (2Y-SPX negative, 30Y-SPX turning positive) is a geopolitical/political economy phenomenon: the long end is where fiscal dominance manifests, and fiscal dominance is driven by the political economy constraints (populism, geopolitical spending, entitlement expansion) analyzed here. The term premium is the bridge between sovereign debt sustainability and the correlation regime.

### Fiscal Dominance & Monetary Policy Constraints (iter_0016)
The fiscal dominance framework from iter_0016 is the domestic political economy driver of correlation regime change. Geopolitical pressures *accelerate* fiscal dominance by increasing government spending requirements (defense, industrial policy, supply chain security) without creating a political constituency for offsetting fiscal consolidation. The geopolitical and fiscal dominance channels are mutually reinforcing: geopolitical competition → higher spending → larger deficits → fiscal dominance → CB independence erosion → positive correlation.

## Open Questions

1. **Quantifying the geopolitical inflation premium:** What portion of current inflation (and term premium) is attributable to geopolitical supply-side factors vs. domestic demand/fiscal factors? This decomposition is critical for predicting when and whether the correlation regime can normalize, but existing models do not cleanly separate these channels.

2. **Threshold effects in CB independence erosion:** Is there a non-linear threshold below which central bank independence losses produce a sudden correlation regime shift, rather than the gradual drift we're observing? Turkey's experience (2021-2023) suggests yes, but identifying the threshold for DM economies with much stronger institutional buffers is an open empirical question.

3. **China's role in global correlation:** If China enters a sustained deflationary/balance-sheet-recession regime (Japan analogy), does this export disinflation to the global economy in a way that offsets the geopolitical supply-side inflation premium? And if so, does this create a window for negative correlation to return in DM even without geopolitical de-escalation?

4. **Sanctions architecture durability:** The current sanctions regime assumes institutional stability in the sanctioning countries. What happens to the bifurcated correlation regime if US political dynamics lead to sanctions relaxation (e.g., a future administration that de-prioritizes Russia sanctions for geopolitical rebalancing)? The correlation implication of sanctions *removal* is asymmetric to the implication of sanctions *imposition*.

5. **Nuclear/WMD tail risk pricing:** Extreme geopolitical scenarios (nuclear use in Ukraine, WMD proliferation in the Middle East) would produce correlation dynamics that have no historical precedent. Standard scenario analysis breaks down for these events. How should portfolio construction account for risks that are low-probability but could produce permanent correlation regime change?

6. **Commodity currency correlation regimes:** How do commodity-exporting economies (Australia, Canada, Norway, Brazil) experience the stock-bond correlation differently from commodity-importing economies during geopolitical supply shocks? The commodity channel creates a fundamentally different correlation dynamic that may offer hedging properties.

7. **Speed of correlation regime transitions:** Historical regime changes (e.g., 1997-2000 shift from positive to negative) occurred over 2-3 years. Would a geopolitically-driven regime change be faster (because the shock is more sudden) or slower (because institutional buffers resist the change)? The speed determines the window for portfolio adjustment.

8. **Second-order geopolitical effects on correlation:** We analyze direct geopolitical shocks, but what about the second-order effects — e.g., geopolitical competition driving AI/technology investment that is ultimately disinflationary? Could the technology channel offset the supply-side channel, creating a geopolitical dynamic that is *correlation-negative* through productivity gains even as it is *correlation-positive* through supply fragmentation?
