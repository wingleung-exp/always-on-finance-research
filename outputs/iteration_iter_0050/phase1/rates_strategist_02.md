# Credit Cycles — Central Bank Policy Transmission Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The monetary policy transmission mechanism is structurally impaired across at least three of five channels, making the policy rate an unreliable measure of actual policy stance.** Financial conditions indices have repeatedly diverged from the fed funds rate — at points during 2023-24, FCI loosened by 100-150bp equivalent even as the policy rate held at 5.25-5.50%, primarily through equity market appreciation and credit spread compression. The policy rate is not the policy.

2. **Transmission to leveraged credit is nonlinear and convex in rate duration, not rate level.** The maturity wall structure means each additional quarter of "higher for longer" brings a discrete tranche of borrowers into refinancing windows at 300-400bp higher coupons. The damage function is step-function, not continuous — 12 months at 5.5% is not twice as restrictive as 6 months at 5.5%. It can be 3-4x as restrictive because entirely different vintage cohorts enter the refinancing window. This interacts directly with the KB's sub_ig_maturity_wall concept but reframes it as a transmission timing problem rather than a pure credit problem.

3. **The Fed's demonstrated willingness to backstop credit markets has permanently embedded a put option in credit pricing, compressing the risk premium by an estimated 30-50bp structurally.** The 2020 SMCCF (corporate bond buying) established credible backstop expectations. This is not a temporary distortion but a regime change in the credit transmission channel — the Fed now functions as a non-price-sensitive buyer of last resort. This directly contributes to the KB's credit_premium_compression finding and partially explains the term_premium_credit_premium_divergence.

4. **The term premium/credit premium divergence is diagnostic of asymmetric transmission failure: the fiscal supply channel is working (repricing term premium) while the credit tightening channel is impaired (spreads compressed).** This is not simply a "mispricing" to be arbitraged — it reflects the fact that different transmission channels are operating on fundamentally different timelines. Term premium responds to flow (Treasury issuance is immediate and observable), while credit spreads respond to stock (accumulated default risk reveals slowly through the maturity wall).

5. **The SOFR transition has made monetary policy transmission more repo-dependent and therefore more vulnerable to plumbing disruptions than under LIBOR.** SOFR-indexed leveraged loans (~$1.4T) now transmit rate changes mechanically through a secured rate tied to Treasury repo, rather than an unsecured bank rate. This means: (a) transmission to floating-rate borrowers is immediate and mechanical — no bank intermediation lag, (b) repo market stress transmits directly to borrowing costs without the bank credit risk buffer that LIBOR provided, (c) the SEC clearing mandate (Q2 2026) represents a transmission infrastructure risk, not just a basis trade risk.

6. **The Fed's reaction function is now structurally asymmetric: the bar for cutting is higher than for holding, and the bar for holding is higher than for hiking.** Post-2021 credibility damage means the Fed treats inflation surprises as more costly than growth surprises by roughly 2:1. Markets consistently misprice this asymmetry by projecting a symmetric loss function. The forward curve has been wrong directionally on cuts in 6 of the last 8 quarters.

7. **Private credit's exclusion from financial conditions indices means the Fed is flying partially blind — the transmission mechanism to ~25-30% of leveraged credit is unobservable by the central bank itself.** The Fed's own FCI (based on public market observables) systematically overstates the looseness of financial conditions because private credit mark-to-model practices mask the actual restrictiveness of policy. The Fed may believe conditions are looser than they actually are for the most leveraged borrowers.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — FCI/Policy Rate Divergence:**
The Goldman Sachs FCI loosened by approximately 100bp equivalent between October 2023 and March 2024 while the fed funds rate was unchanged at 5.25-5.50%. The S&P 500 rallied ~25%, IG spreads compressed 30bp, HY spreads compressed 100bp+. By any reasonable financial conditions metric, policy eased materially without any rate change. This is the fundamental argument against treating the policy rate as the policy stance. The KB's identification of credit premium compression (HY OAS 350-420bp) and the 30-40% reduction in compensation per unit of default risk is itself evidence of transmission failure — the rate channel says "tight" while the asset price and credit channels say "easy."

**Claim 2 — Convex Transmission via Maturity Wall:**
The KB documents $900B-$1.5T sub-IG maturing 2025-2028 with B-rated borrowers facing 40-60% higher refinancing costs. From a transmission perspective, the critical insight is that this creates a step-function in policy impact. In Q1 2025, only the 2025 maturities face refinancing. By Q4 2026, the cumulative exposure includes 2025 and 2026 vintages. The marginal restrictiveness of an additional quarter at current rates is increasing over time because the maturity wall is front-loaded. This is compounded by the CLO reinvestment expiration ($250-350B over 18 months) removing the marginal bid precisely when supply increases. The transmission amplifies through time even without any policy rate change.

**Claim 3 — Central Bank Put in Credit Pricing:**
The SMCCF purchased $14B in corporate bonds and ETFs in 2020 — a tiny fraction of the $10T+ corporate bond market — yet IG spreads compressed from 373bp to 130bp and HY from 1100bp to 500bp during the program. The market impact was 10-50x the direct purchase volume, demonstrating that the signaling channel dominates the portfolio balance channel. This backstop expectation is now embedded in pricing. The KB's structural_improvement_vulnerability_asymmetry concept identifies central bank backstops as a risk-dampening factor that is underweighted in analysis — from a transmission perspective, it is specifically the backstop's effect on ex ante risk pricing that matters, not just its ex post crisis management function.

**Claim 4 — Asymmetric Channel Operation:**
Term premium has risen to 80-130bp (per KB) because Treasury issuance is immediately observable, large, and directly priced in the rates market. Credit spreads remain compressed because default risk transmits slowly through the maturity wall, EBITDA addbacks mask true leverage, and private credit absorbs the weakest borrowers invisibly. These are operating on different clock speeds. The rates market prices fiscal risk in real-time; the credit market prices default risk with a 12-24 month lag. The divergence is not irrational — it reflects the fact that these channels have structurally different transmission lags.

**Claim 5 — SOFR Transmission Infrastructure:**
Under LIBOR, rate changes transmitted to leveraged borrowers through bank credit decisions — banks could absorb rate changes in their margin, slow transmission, or tighten non-price terms instead. SOFR eliminates this intermediation buffer. A 25bp SOFR change transmits mechanically and immediately to $1.4T+ in floating-rate leveraged loans with zero discretionary buffering. This makes transmission faster but also more brittle. The basis trade ($800B-$1T per KB) and the SEC clearing mandate interact with this infrastructure. A repo market disruption under SOFR doesn't just disrupt the sovereign curve — it disrupts the entire leveraged loan transmission mechanism.

**Claim 6 — Asymmetric Reaction Function:**
The dot plot has consistently signaled more cuts than delivered. December 2023 dots projected 75bp of cuts in 2024; realized was 100bp but delivered much later than projected. The pattern is systematic: the Fed talks dovish to manage financial conditions but acts hawkish relative to guidance. This asymmetry is rational given the credibility cost of the "transitory" error — the Fed's loss function now assigns ~2x weight to inflation surprises vs. growth surprises. The market's persistent over-pricing of cuts (the forward curve has been above realized policy in most recent quarters) represents a systematic misunderstanding of this asymmetry.

**Claim 7 — Observability Gap:**
The Fed's FCI constructions (Liberty Street, Chicago Fed NFCI, Goldman Sachs FCI) are all based on publicly observable markets: Treasury yields, equity prices, IG/HY spreads, bank lending surveys. Private credit ($1.7-1.8T per KB) with 0.5-0.7 NAV autocorrelation and rising PIK usage (2% to 5-8%) is entirely invisible to these metrics. If private credit borrowers are experiencing tighter conditions (higher PIK, stricter terms, declining coverage) while public markets show loose conditions, the Fed's dashboard is misleading. The Fed may be holding policy tighter than intended for public market borrowers and not tight enough for the conditions it observes.

## Confidence Assessment

|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. FCI/Policy Rate Divergence | **9/10** | Directly observable in data. Multiple FCI measures confirm. The only uncertainty is magnitude, not direction. |
| 2. Convex Transmission via Maturity Wall | **7/10** | Arithmetic is certain (per KB consensus). The convexity claim requires the assumption that amend-and-extend doesn't fully smooth the step function — the KB's amend_extend_forbearance concept shows $200-400B pushed out, which is material but doesn't eliminate the wall. |
| 3. Central Bank Put Embedded | **7/10** | SMCCF evidence is strong. The 30-50bp structural compression estimate is calibrated against the pre/post-2020 spread regime, but disentangling the backstop effect from other structural changes (composition improvement, index BB share increase) is imprecise. |
| 4. Asymmetric Channel Operation | **8/10** | The different clock speeds of fiscal transmission (immediate) vs. credit transmission (12-24 month lag through maturity wall) are structurally grounded. The interpretation of the term premium/credit premium divergence as a transmission timing issue rather than a mispricing is novel but logically consistent. |
| 5. SOFR Transmission Vulnerability | **6/10** | The mechanical transmission argument is sound. The fragility claim depends on the probability of a repo market disruption significant enough to dislocate SOFR — this has happened (September 2019 repo spike) but the Fed established the SRF specifically to backstop this. The SRF may limit the vulnerability. |
| 6. Asymmetric Reaction Function | **7/10** | The pattern is observable in dot-plot vs. realized policy divergence. The 2:1 asymmetry estimate is a characterization rather than a precise calibration. The risk is that the reaction function shifts again — if unemployment rises sharply, the asymmetry could invert rapidly. |
| 7. Observability Gap | **8/10** | The exclusion of private credit from FCIs is factual. ## Connections to Other Topics

**To Kalecki Fiscal Buffer:** The fiscal buffer is the single largest source of transmission impairment. The 6-7% GDP deficit transmits directly to corporate profits via the Kalecki identity, offsetting the credit tightening that higher rates are supposed to produce. From a transmission perspective, fiscal policy is running 300-400bp of easing against monetary policy's 525bp of tightening, producing a net stance that is far less restrictive than the policy rate implies. This is not the Fed failing — it is fiscal policy overwhelming monetary transmission. The key question is whether DOGE-driven fiscal consolidation could remove this buffer faster than the market or the Fed expects, creating a sudden transmission acceleration.

**To Private Credit Structural Vulnerability:** Private credit is the single largest blind spot in the transmission mechanism. If the $1.7-1.8T in private credit is experiencing genuinely different financial conditions than public markets (plausible given rising PIK, mark-to-model delays), then the entire policy calibration is off. The transmission strategist's version of this concern: the Fed cannot calibrate what it cannot observe.

**To Basis Trade / Repo Infrastructure:** The $800B-$1T basis trade is not just a financial stability risk — it is a monetary policy transmission infrastructure risk. SOFR-based transmission runs through the same repo plumbing. A basis trade unwind that disrupts repo markets doesn't just create mark-to-market losses; it disrupts the mechanism by which the policy rate reaches the real economy.

**To Credit Cycle Stage Assessment:** The transmission perspective reframes the mid-cycle vs. late-cycle debate. If the transmission mechanism is impaired, then the standard mapping from "policy rate level" to "cycle stage" is unreliable. You can have a policy rate that implies late-cycle restrictiveness while actual financial conditions are mid-cycle easy. The KB's credit_cycle_stage_assessment concept should incorporate financial conditions, not just the policy rate, as the relevant input to cycle dating.

**To ROIC-WACC Bimodality:** The transmission mechanism's impact is itself bimodal. Rate-immune mega-cap tech (ROIC 25-40%) absorbs rate changes without behavioral response — transmission to this 30% of market cap is effectively zero. The S&P 493 with 2-3pp ROIC-WACC spreads is where transmission actually operates, and where 100bp matters disproportionately. The Fed's aggregate view of "economy responding to policy" is averaged across a population where transmission varies by an order of magnitude.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the elasticity of the Kalecki fiscal buffer to fiscal consolidation, and is there a threshold below which transmission suddenly accelerates?** The KB suggests ~2pp GDP fiscal consolidation as a trigger, but the transmission dynamics of fiscal withdrawal are likely nonlinear — a gradual 1pp reduction may be absorbed, while a sudden 2pp cut (plausible under DOGE scenarios) could produce a transmission acceleration that overshoots.

2. **Has the Standing Repo Facility (SRF) effectively backstopped the SOFR transmission vulnerability, or is it untested at scale?** The SRF was established post-September 2019 specifically to prevent repo market dislocations. If it works as designed, the SOFR fragility concern is largely mitigated. But it has never been tested during a genuine stress event with the basis trade at current size. The question is whether the SRF's $500B+ capacity is sufficient relative to combined basis trade + maturity wall repo demand.

3. **How does the Fed's observability gap in private credit affect its willingness to cut?** If the Fed cannot observe the true restrictiveness of its policy on the most leveraged borrowers, it may hold policy tighter than intended for longer than optimal. Conversely, if private credit stress eventually surfaces through contagion channels (BDC redemptions, insurance RBC), the Fed may be forced to cut more aggressively than it otherwise would. The timing and magnitude of this information revelation is the key unknown.

4. **At what point does the central bank put become self-defeating?** The backstop compresses credit risk premia, which loosens financial conditions, which requires the Fed to hold rates higher, which increases the severity of the eventual stress that triggers the backstop. There may be a level of backstop credibility beyond which the Fed is trapped — unable to tighten conditions through rates because the backstop expectation offsets the tightening. This is analogous to the fiscal self-referential instability loop but operating through the asset price channel rather than the term premium channel.

5. **Is the forward curve's persistent over-pricing of cuts an exploitable mispricing, or does it serve a financial conditions management function that the Fed tacitly endorses?** If the market prices more cuts than the Fed intends to deliver, this loosens financial conditions, which partially offsets the need for actual cuts. The Fed may prefer an "optimistically priced" forward curve as a financial conditions management tool — correcting the mispricing would itself tighten conditions. This creates a recursive problem for rate positioning: the mispricing may persist because correcting it is not in the Fed's interest.

6. **What is the correct financial conditions index construction that incorporates private credit, SOFR transmission lags, and the maturity wall's step-function dynamics?** Existing FCIs are point-in-time snapshots of observable market prices. An FCI that incorporated forward-looking maturity wall exposure, private credit conditions (even estimated), and SOFR transmission dynamics would likely show conditions 50-100bp tighter than current indices — but constructing this requires data that is partially unobservable.
