# Fiscal Dominance — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The current US fiscal configuration most closely resembles the US 1965-1968 "pre-ignition" period** (similarity: 7.5/10), not the commonly cited 1942-1951 Accord era. The 1960s analogue captures the *transition process* from partial to full fiscal dominance — the phase the US is actually in — whereas the Accord era describes an already-established regime. Markets priced zero fiscal dominance premium in 1965-67 and repriced violently in 1968-70.

2. **A "quiet period" of 3-7 years precedes market repricing in 4 of 5 historical fiscal dominance episodes.** During the quiet period, fiscal fundamentals deteriorate but markets show no stress because institutional absorption capacity (domestic savings, regulatory captive demand, reserve currency status) masks the buildup. The current quiet period began approximately 2020 and has lasted ~6 years — placing the US at or near the historical upper bound for the quiet phase.

3. **The resolution sequencing across analogues follows a remarkably consistent 5-stage pattern:** (i) fiscal expansion with no market signal, (ii) inflation persistence above expectations, (iii) bond market "tantrum" event, (iv) policy accommodation or attempted tightening, (v) regime crystallization (either repression or crisis). The US is currently between stages (ii) and (iii).

4. **The base rate for "gradual repression without crisis" among reserve-currency issuers is 2/3 (US 1942-51, UK 1945-70), while the base rate for crisis-first resolution among non-reserve-currency issuers is 3/4 (Italy 2011, France 1958, UK 2022 post-reserve-currency era, Argentina 2018).** Reserve currency status is the single most important classifier for which resolution pathway materializes, but the sample is too small (n=7) for reliable point estimates.

5. **The 1965-1982 US episode reveals that the transition from partial to full fiscal dominance took 5-7 years, but the transition from full fiscal dominance to resolution took an additional 10-14 years.** Total episode duration was 17 years. No analogue shows resolution in under 5 years absent external anchor (Bretton Woods collapse, EU/ECB backstop, wartime mobilization ending).

6. **The French Fourth Republic (1945-1958) is the most underappreciated analogue** because it demonstrates how political fragmentation prevents fiscal consolidation, leading to serial currency crises and eventual regime change. The political economy — coalition governments unable to cut spending, a central bank pressured to accommodate, and recurrent devaluations — rhymes more closely with the current US political configuration than the commonly cited Japan or UK cases.

7. **Cross-asset return patterns across fiscal dominance episodes produce a distinctive "signature":** equities initially outperform (Kalecki channel operational, 3-5 years), then underperform in real terms as inflation erodes earnings quality; gold outperforms in all episodes (+150-400% cumulative real); long-duration bonds are the worst-performing asset class in every episode without exception (-25 to -65% real cumulative); short-duration bills preserve capital but deliver negative real returns of -1 to -3% annually.

8. **The "Greenspan Conundrum" of 2004-2006 is the closest analogue for the current broken monetary transmission phenomenon** (similarity: 7.5/10), and its resolution — not gradual normalization but a discontinuous financial crisis — carries a warning: when transmission mechanisms break, they do not heal smoothly but reconnect violently. IG spreads went from 50bp to 600bp+ in 18 months.

### ANALOGUE DECOMPOSITION

#### Analogue 1: US 1965-1968 → 1982 (The Great Inflation Fiscal-Monetary Conflict)

**Similarity Scoring (10 dimensions):**

| Dimension | 1965-68 | Current US | Score |
|-----------|---------|------------|-------|
| Deficit/GDP at full employment | 1.5-3.0% (Vietnam + Great Society) | 6.0-7.0% | 6/10 (directionally similar, magnitude 2-3x larger) |
| Unemployment rate | 3.5-4.0% | 3.7-4.0% | 9/10 |
| Inflation trajectory | Rising from 1.5% to 4.5% | Falling from 9% but sticky at 2.7-3.0% | 6/10 (different starting point, similar stickiness) |
| Fed credibility | High but eroding (Martin under LBJ pressure) | High but questioned (effective phi_pi ~1.0-1.2) | 7/10 |
| Political constraint on Fed | LBJ directly pressured Martin | Implicit via debt service channel ($1T interest) | 8/10 (mechanism differs, constraint similar) |
| Debt/GDP level | 40-45% (low) | 120-125% (high) | 3/10 (fundamentally different) |
| External position | Bretton Woods, current account surplus | Floating rate, twin deficits | 4/10 |
| Market structure | Regulated banking, limited leverage | Basis trade ($1T at 50-100x), passive flows | 4/10 |
| Fiscal spending composition | Defense (Vietnam) + social (Great Society) | Entitlements + interest + defense + industrial policy | 7/10 |
| Political capacity to consolidate | Low (war + social programs) | Low (polarization + entitlements + interest) | 8/10 |

**Composite similarity: 6.2/10** — but the *transition dynamics* score ~7.5/10 because the key mechanism is identical: fiscal expansion at full employment with a constrained central bank producing gradual inflation persistence that markets initially ignore.

**Why this matters more than the 1942-51 Accord:** The Accord era began with explicit, formal fiscal dominance (rate caps). The US is not there yet. The question is: what does the *transition* look like? The 1965-68 period is the answer.

**Key sequence:**
- 1964-65: Tax cuts + Vietnam escalation + Great Society. Deficit rises. Markets complacent. 10Y yield ~4.2%.
- 1966: First "credit crunch." Fed tightened, but LBJ pressured Martin to ease. Bond tantrum. S&P fell 22% in 8 months.
- 1967: Fed eased. Inflation accelerated 2.9% to 4.2%. Gold outflows intensified.
- 1968: Surcharge tax passed (too little, too late). Inflation embedded. Sterling devalued (Nov 1967). Dollar gold pool collapsed (March 1968).
- 1969-71: Nixon pressure on Burns. Wage-price controls. Bretton Woods collapse. Full fiscal dominance established.
- 1971-82: Inflation regime. Three attempts at tightening (1969, 1974, 1979). Only Volcker succeeded by accepting a severe recession.

**The current US is approximately at the "1966-67" position** in this sequence: fiscal expansion established, inflation sticky above target, Fed constrained, one bond market tantrum already occurred (Oct 2023, 10Y to 5%), and markets have partially re-complacified.

**Cross-asset returns during the transition (1965-1970):**
- S&P 500: +30% (1964-68), then -36% peak to trough (1968-70)
- 10Y Treasury: yield from 4.2% to 8%+ (price decline ~25%)
- Gold: Fixed at $35/oz until 1971, then +400% by 1974
- Dollar: Stable until 1971 (Bretton Woods), then -30% by 1978

#### Analogue 2: US 1942-1951 (Treasury-Fed Accord Era)

Already well-covered in KB. Key additions from the analogue framework:

**What the KB underemphasizes:** The Accord era is relevant for the *resolution mechanism*, not the transition. The transition was fiat (wartime). The resolution was institutional negotiation — the Accord of March 1951 restored Fed independence. This is relevant because it shows that fiscal dominance can be *reversed* through institutional reform, not only through crisis.

**Asset class sequencing during the Accord transition:**
- Pre-Accord (1942-51): Long bonds pinned at 2.5%, real returns deeply negative (-3 to -5% annually)
- Inflation spike during Korean War (1950-51): CPI hit 9.4% in Feb 1951
- Post-Accord (1951-53): Long rates rose 50bp (2.5% to 3.0%), orderly transition
- Deleveraging (1951-80): Debt/GDP from 120% to 30% over 29 years

**Key lesson:** Resolution through institutional reform (Accord) produced a mild, orderly bond repricing (50bp). Crisis-first resolution (1966, 1973-74, 2022) produces violent repricing (100bp+ in weeks/months). The pathway to resolution matters enormously for asset class returns.

#### Analogue 3: Japan 2001-Present (The Gradual Archetype)

**Why Japan is both the most cited and least applicable analogue:**

Japan similarity scoring:
- Reserve-currency issuer: 7/10 (yen is reserve, but distant third after dollar and euro)
- Domestic absorption: 3/10 (Japan 90%+ domestic ownership vs. US ~70%)
- Current account: 2/10 (Japan surplus vs. US deficit — fundamentally different external constraint)
- Inflation regime: 2/10 (Japan fought deflation; US fighting inflation)
- Political capacity: 5/10 (LDP one-party dominance enabled gradual repression; US polarization prevents it)
- Debt/GDP level: 7/10 (Japan 250%+ vs. US 125%, but trajectory similar)

**Japan is the outlier that proves the rule:** Japan achieved indefinite extension of the quiet period because of three features the US lacks: (a) 90%+ domestic ownership eliminating external funding risk, (b) persistent current account surplus providing FX stability, and (c) deflation that made negative real returns less visible to savers. None of these obtain for the US.

**The Japan analogue's real utility:** It establishes the *theoretical maximum* for the quiet period (~25 years) and demonstrates that fiscal dominance need not produce crisis if domestic absorption capacity is sufficient. It sets the probability ceiling, not the point estimate.

#### Analogue 4: UK 1945-1976 (Reserve Currency in Managed Decline)

**This is the most relevant analogue for the *long-run trajectory* of a reserve-currency issuer under fiscal dominance:**

| Dimension | UK 1945-76 | Current US | Score |
|-----------|------------|------------|-------|
| Reserve currency status | Declining (sterling area shrinking) | Dominant but questioned | 6/10 |
| Welfare state expansion | NHS (1948), comprehensive welfare state | ACA, Social Security, Medicare expansion | 7/10 |
| Defense burden | Declining imperial commitments, Korean War | Sustained global presence, AUKUS, NATO | 6/10 |
| Inflation trajectory | Chronic 3-8%, spikes to 15%+ | Currently 2.7-3.0%, risk of re-acceleration | 5/10 |
| Financial repression tools | Regulated banking, capital controls | Limited (modern capital mobility) | 4/10 |
| External anchor | Bretton Woods (initially), IMF (eventually) | None | 3/10 |

**Key sequence:**
- 1945-49: Debt/GDP 250%+. Financial repression via rate caps. Inflation 5-10%.
- 1949: First sterling devaluation (30%). Balance of payments crisis.
- 1950-67: Gradual deleveraging via repression + growth. Serial "stop-go" crises.
- 1967: Second sterling devaluation (14%). Reserve currency status effectively lost.
- 1972-76: Barber boom, oil crisis, secondary banking crisis, IMF bailout (1976).

**Asset class returns (1945-1976):**
- Gilts: -3% real annually (cumulative: -60% real over 30 years)
- UK equities: roughly flat in real terms (nominal gains offset by inflation)
- Gold: restricted until 1971, then +400% by 1976
- Sterling: -60% vs. dollar over the full period

**Key lesson for the US:** The UK maintained fiscal dominance for 30 years with reserve-currency status, but at the cost of gradual currency erosion and serial crises. Reserve currency status delayed but did not prevent the ultimate adjustment. The 1976 IMF bailout was the final crystallizing event. For the US, the equivalent would be a loss of confidence in the dollar's reserve role — which the KB's dollar_smile_bifurcation concept addresses.

#### Analogue 5: France 1945-1958 (Political Fragmentation Analogue)

**Why this analogue is underappreciated:**

| Dimension | France 1946-58 | Current US | Score |
|-----------|----------------|------------|-------|
| Government stability | 25 governments in 12 years | Divided government, polarization | 7/10 |
| Fiscal consolidation capacity | Coalition politics prevented cuts | Entitlements + interest + defense ~75% of spending on autopilot | 8/10 |
| Central bank independence | Banque de France pressured to monetize | Fed constrained by debt service channel | 7/10 |
| Inflation trajectory | Chronic 5-15%, periodic spikes to 40%+ | Sticky 2.7-3.0%, risk of re-acceleration | 4/10 |
| External anchor | Marshall Plan (temporary), then none | None | 5/10 |
| Currency regime | Serial devaluations (1948, 1949, 1957, 1958) | Floating, reserve currency buffer | 3/10 |
| Resolution mechanism | De Gaulle's authoritarian reform + "heavy franc" | Unknown | 3/10 |

**Key lesson from France:** Political systems that cannot achieve fiscal consolidation through normal legislative processes eventually produce either (a) a crisis severe enough to force extraordinary measures, or (b) regime change. France tried 25 governments' worth of half-measures. The current US — with neither party able to achieve the 60-vote Senate threshold for major fiscal reform, and entitlement spending constitutionally difficult to cut — resembles the French stalemate more than commonly recognized.

**Asset class behavior in France 1945-58:**
- Equities (Paris Bourse): +300% nominal, roughly flat in real terms over 12 years
- Government bonds: -60 to -70% real cumulative
- Gold: +350% nominal, +150% real
- Real estate: +200-400% nominal, significant real appreciation
- French franc: -75% vs. dollar cumulative (serial devaluations)

### CURRENT SITUATION COMPARISON

**Where today is similar to all analogues:**
1. Fiscal expansion at full employment with no credible consolidation path
2. Central bank constrained — by LBJ pressure (1960s), by Bretton Woods (1940s), by debt service arithmetic (today)
3. Inflation sticky above institutional target
4. Markets initially complacent during fiscal deterioration
5. Political incapacity to consolidate spending

**Where today is fundamentally different from all analogues:**
1. **Debt/GDP starting point:** 125% is higher than the starting point of any analogue except the post-WWII cases (which had wartime acceleration, not structural deficits)
2. **Basis trade amplification:** $1T at 50-100x leverage has no historical parallel. LTCM ($125B notional) is the closest, but 8-10x smaller.
3. **Passive investing:** 50% of AUM in passive vehicles creates mechanical flows with no parallel in any analogue
4. **AI productivity potential:** If 0.5-1.0pp additional real GDP materializes, it invalidates the r-g framework entirely. No analogue includes this variable.
5. **Modern capital mobility:** All pre-1980 analogues operated under various capital controls. Financial repression tools that worked under Bretton Woods may not work under modern free capital flows.
6. **Digital capital flight:** Crypto, stablecoins, and instant cross-border transfers create escape valves from financial repression that did not exist in any analogue.

**How differences might alter outcomes:**
- Higher debt/GDP starting point → faster doom loop activation → compresses timeline
- Basis trade amplification → higher probability of acute path → increases crisis risk
- Passive investing → unknown (could extend quiet period OR amplify repricing)
- AI productivity → could invalidate thesis entirely → widens outcome distribution
- Modern capital mobility → makes financial repression harder to sustain → pushes toward shorter episodes
- Digital capital flight → creates new escape valves → weakens repression effectiveness

### RANGE OF OUTCOMES

Synthesizing across all 5 primary analogues, the distribution of outcomes for the US over the next 5-10 years:

**Scenario A: "Japan Path" — Indefinite Extension (15% probability)**
- Conditions: Domestic absorption capacity holds, inflation returns to 2%, no external shock
- Asset implications: Low real returns across all asset classes, gradual gold appreciation, compressed yields
- Duration: 10-25+ additional years
- Why unlikely: US lacks Japan's domestic absorption (70% vs. 90% domestic ownership), runs twin deficits, and has above-target inflation

**Scenario B: "1945-70 Repression Path" — Gradual Financial Repression (40% probability)**
- Conditions: Inflation sticky at 3-5%, real rates negative, regulatory captive buying increases, no acute crisis
- Asset implications: Long bonds -3 to -5% real annually, equities roughly flat in real terms, gold +8-15% annually, real estate outperforms
- Duration: 15-25 years to full deleveraging
- Why most likely: Matches the modal outcome across analogues (4/7 episodes) and aligns with reserve-currency base rate
- Signpost: 5Y5Y breakevens drifting to 2.8-3.5% without Fed response; term premium rising <50bp/quarter

**Scenario C: "1965-82 Inflation Regime" — Extended Inflation Episode (25% probability)**
- Conditions: Inflation re-accelerates above 4%, Fed accommodates partially, multiple tightening-easing cycles
- Asset implications: Equities -2 to -4% real annually (P/E compression from ~20x to ~10x despite nominal earnings growth), bonds -4 to -6% real annually, gold +10-20% annually, commodities outperform
- Duration: 10-17 years
- Why significant: The 1965-82 analogue is the closest match for transition dynamics, and the current US is at a similar point in the sequence
- Signpost: Core PCE re-accelerating above 3.5%; ULC persistently above 3.5%; 2Y-SPX correlation turning positive

**Scenario D: "Acute Crisis Path" — Basis Trade Unwind / Bond Market Crisis (15% probability)**
- Conditions: Term premium spike >100bp/quarter, basis trade deleveraging, Treasury auction failure or near-failure
- Asset implications: Long bonds -15 to -25% in repricing event, equities -25 to -40%, gold initial sell then +30-50%, credit spreads +300-500bp
- Duration: 3-12 months for acute phase, followed by Scenario B or C
- Why possible: Basis trade amplification mechanism has no historical parallel, SEC clearing mandate as calendar catalyst
- Signpost: SOFR-FF spread >50bp, bid-to-cover <2.0x, 30Y-SPX correlation >+0.25

**Scenario E: "AI Productivity Escape" — Structural Break Invalidates Thesis (5% probability)**
- Conditions: AI delivers 0.5-1.0pp additional real GDP growth within 3-5 years, deficits become self-funding
- Asset implications: Equities +15-25% annually, bonds stabilize, gold underperforms, dollar strengthens
- Duration: Permanent regime change
- Why unlikely: No historical precedent for technology-driven fiscal resolution at this speed. Post-war productivity boom took 15-20 years to fully manifest.
- Signpost: BLS productivity data showing sustained >2.5% annual growth for 4+ consecutive quarters

**Note on probability assignments:** These are ordinal rankings derived from n=7 analogues, not calibrated probabilities. The 95% confidence interval for any of these estimates spans at least ±20 percentage points. The ranking (B > C > A ≈ D > E) is more informative than the point estimates.

### SIGNPOSTS AND SEQUENCING

**Indicators that the US is at Stage 2 → Stage 3 transition (approaching bond tantrum):**
- Core PCE remains sticky at 2.5-3.5% for 12+ months without re-acceleration ✓ (currently occurring)
- Term premium rising gradually but persistently ✓ (ACM at 50-100bp, up from -50bp in 2020)
- 2Y-SPX correlation remains negative ✓ (Fed credibility intact at front end)
- Gold making new highs ✓ ($3,000+)
- Fiscal deficit persisting at 6-7% without recession ✓

**Indicators that Stage 3 (bond tantrum) is imminent (watch within 6-18 months):**
- Term premium velocity accelerating (>25bp/quarter sustained)
- Auction bid-to-cover declining below 2.2x on 10Y and 30Y
- Indirect bidder share declining below 60% (currently ~65%, down from 72%)
- SOFR quarter-end spikes exceeding 50bp above ON RRP
- SEC clearing mandate implementation creating basis trade margin calls (Q2 2026)

**Indicators that Stage 3 has occurred and Stage 4 (policy response) is underway:**
- 10Y yield moves >75bp in <30 days without recession catalyst
- Fed announces emergency liquidity facility for Treasury market
- Treasury shifts issuance further toward bills (T-bill share exceeding 25%, currently ~22%)
- Political rhetoric shifts from deficit spending to fiscal responsibility

**Most critical single signpost:** The 30Y-2Y correlation differential with SPX. Per the KB's maturity_dependent_correlation_bifurcation concept:
- Current: 30Y-SPX at +0.05 to +0.15, 2Y-SPX at -0.25 to -0.35
- Warning: 30Y-SPX exceeds +0.20 while 2Y-SPX remains below -0.15 (widening bifurcation)
- Critical: 2Y-SPX turns positive → transition to full fiscal dominance underway
- This is the single most falsifiable prediction from the analogue framework

**Historical sequencing from analogue synthesis:**
1. First 1-3 years: Quiet period continues. Markets ignore fiscal deterioration. Equities outperform (Kalecki channel). Credit spreads compress.
2. Years 3-5: Bond tantrum event. Term premium reprices 100-200bp. Equities correct 15-25%. Credit widens but recovers. Fed intervenes.
3. Years 5-10: Regime crystallization. Either repression (gradual, more likely for reserve-currency issuer) or inflation regime (if Fed accommodates). Gold outperforms. Long bonds underperform persistently.
4. Years 10-25: Resolution. Either through institutional reform (Accord-type), productivity growth, or forced austerity (UK 1976-type).

**What the analogue framework CANNOT tell us:**
- Precise timing of Stage 3 tantrum (base rate: 1-5 years from current position, but variance is extreme)
- Whether the basis trade converts gradual to acute path (no analogue, Knightian uncertainty)
- Impact of passive investing on repricing dynamics (genuinely novel)
- Whether AI productivity invalidates the thesis (no analogue for technology-driven fiscal resolution)
- Exact asset class return magnitudes (cross-analogue variance is 2-3x)

## Connections to Other Topics

### Rates-Equity Correlation (Direct)
The maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation (2Y-SPX negative, 30Y-SPX positive) has a precise parallel in 1965-69. During 1965-67, correlation was negative across the curve (monetary credibility intact). By 1969-71, the long end flipped positive as fiscal dynamics dominated. The current bifurcation is the analogue equivalent of the 1967-68 "splitting point." The KB's fiscal_stalemate_transition_duration of 5-7 years is well-calibrated against this.

### Monetary Policy Transmission (Direct)
The 2004-06 Conundrum analogue informs the broken_monetary_transmission concept. Base rate for FCI loosening during tightening: 1/7 (14%). Both episodes featured leverage-amplified carry trades suppressing spreads. The Conundrum resolved through GFC — the current episode's resolution mechanism remains unknown but the discontinuous reconnection pattern is the key warning.

### FX Regimes (Moderate)
The French Fourth Republic shows how serial devaluations accompany fiscal dominance for non-reserve currencies. The UK 1945-76 shows how even a declining reserve currency sustains fiscal dominance for decades, but with gradual currency erosion. Sterling lost ~60% vs. dollar over 30 years. The dollar_smile_bifurcation question — does reserve status hold during US-origin fiscal stress? — has a base rate of 1/1 (UK maintained reserve-adjacent status through 1976, though severely diminished).

### Sovereign Debt Sustainability (Direct)
When r > g persisted >3 years without fiscal adjustment, financial repression or crisis followed in 5/5 analogue cases. The US is approaching but has not crossed r > g (effective rate ~3.3% vs. nominal GDP ~5.5%). The r_g_convergence concept's 35-40% stress probability at r ≈ g is derived from a panel dominated by non-reserve issuers and should be adjusted downward for the US.

### Commodity/Inflation Regimes (Moderate)
In the 1965-82 analogue, commodity prices were a *lagging* indicator of fiscal dominance — oil did not spike until 1973 (8 years in), food prices not until 1972-73. The KB's agricultural_supply_shock_trilemma and OPEC breakeven amplifier describe amplification mechanisms that compound an already-active regime, not independent triggers.

### Credit Markets (Moderate)
Credit spreads compress during early-phase fiscal dominance (Kalecki channel) then widen violently during repricing. 1965-68 → 1969-70: IG ~50bp to ~200bp. 2004-06 → 2008-09: IG ~50bp to ~600bp. Current IG at ~85-100bp is consistent with early-phase positioning. The carry_trap concept correctly identifies this as negatively skewed payoff.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the quiet period genuinely ending, or has US institutional absorption capacity permanently extended it?** Japan's 25-year experience suggests domestic absorption can forestall repricing indefinitely. The US has ~70% domestic ownership vs. Japan's ~90%, so the buffer is smaller but still substantial. No model resolves this — monitoring the signposts above is the only approach.

2. **Does the 3x higher debt/GDP starting point (125% vs. 40% in 1965) compress or extend the transition timeline?** The arithmetic favors compression (higher interest expense creates faster self-reinforcing dynamics), but the reserve currency buffer favors extension. The net effect is unresolvable because no reserve-currency issuer has entered fiscal dominance at 125% debt/GDP with r ≈ g.

3. **Can AI productivity (0.5-1.0pp additional real GDP) invalidate the analogue framework entirely?** The post-WWII deleveraging was partly driven by a productivity boom (1.5-2.0% annual TFP growth). If AI delivers comparable gains, the r-g arithmetic improves. But the post-war boom coincided with favorable demographics, pent-up consumer demand, and massive infrastructure investment — none of which obtain today. Confidence in AI replication: 3/10.

4. **What is the probability the basis trade converts the gradual path to the acute path?** No analogue exists. LTCM ($125B notional) is closest but 8-10x smaller. Knightian uncertainty framing more appropriate than base rate.

5. **Does the SEC Treasury clearing mandate (CMESC Q2 2026) function as a regulatory trigger analogous to Regulation Q in 1966?** In 1966, Reg Q ceilings triggered disintermediation when market rates exceeded caps — a known-in-advance regulatory trigger whose market impact was underestimated. Base rate for regulatory triggers causing significant financial stress when targeting large leveraged positions: 2/3.

6. **How does passive investing (50% of AUM) alter fiscal dominance repricing dynamics?** No analogue includes passive as a dominant force. Could extend quiet period (mechanical absorption) or amplify repricing (crowded exit). Genuinely novel — the analogue approach cannot resolve this.

7. **Does the DM-EM asymmetry bias systematically overstate the gradual path probability?** The analogue sample inherently favors gradual resolution for reserve-currency issuers because *that is what happened*. The true distribution of outcomes for a reserve-currency issuer at 125% debt/GDP is unknown (n=0).
