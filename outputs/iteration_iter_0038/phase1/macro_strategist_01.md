# Fiscal Dominance — New Keynesian Macro Strategist (macro_strategist_01) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The US is operating in a Leeper (1991) "passive monetary / active fiscal" regime for the first time since the Treasury-Fed Accord era, and the transition was not a discrete event but a continuous drift observable through the effective Taylor Rule coefficient declining from ~1.5 (2017-2019) to ~1.0-1.2 (2022-present).** The NK fiscal theory of the price level (FTPL), formalized by Leeper (1991) and extended by Bianchi-Melosi (2017), distinguishes "active monetary / passive fiscal" (AM/PF) — where the central bank sets rates to satisfy the Taylor Principle and fiscal authority adjusts surpluses to stabilize debt — from "passive monetary / active fiscal" (PM/AF), where the price level must adjust to satisfy the government's intertemporal budget constraint because the fiscal authority refuses to adjust. The revealed-preference evidence — Fed pausing at 5.25-5.50% with core PCE at 4.3%, then cutting with core PCE at 2.7% — yields an effective inflation coefficient φ_π of approximately 1.0-1.2, barely satisfying the Taylor Principle. This coefficient is endogenous to fiscal conditions: with $36T+ debt, every 100bp of FFR increases adds ~$250-320B in annual interest expense, creating a fiscal feedback loop that structurally prevents φ_π from returning above 1.5. The US has drifted from AM/PF toward PM/AF without a formal announcement — the defining feature of a Bianchi-Melosi "occasionally binding" fiscal constraint.

2. **The output gap is effectively closed or slightly positive (~+0.3-0.8% of potential GDP), yet the fiscal stance is calibrated for a deep recession: the structural primary deficit of ~5.5-6.0% of GDP at sub-4% unemployment represents a fiscal impulse approximately 3-4 standard deviations above what the IS curve and historical multiplier estimates would prescribe.** Using CBO potential GDP estimates and standard Okun's Law coefficients (β ≈ 2.0), the unemployment gap of approximately -0.2 to -0.5pp (actual ~3.7-3.9% vs. estimated NAIRU ~4.0-4.2%) implies a near-zero or modestly positive output gap. In the textbook NK IS-LM framework, fiscal policy should be approximately neutral at full employment — running structural primary surpluses of 0.5-1.0% of GDP to stabilize the debt ratio. Instead, the US is running ~5.5-6.0% structural primary deficits (CBO estimate of ~6.5% total deficit minus ~0.5-1.0% cyclical component). The post-WWII mean structural deficit at comparable unemployment rates is ~1.5-2.0% of GDP. The deviation of 3.5-4.0pp represents fiscal policy that is maximally procyclical — the precise opposite of NK optimal policy prescriptions. This is the defining structural anomaly: not that deficits are large per se, but that they are large at full employment with no credible consolidation mechanism.

3. **The New Keynesian Phillips Curve (NKPC) dynamics create an asymmetric inflation trap under fiscal dominance: the standard "divine coincidence" — where stabilizing the output gap simultaneously stabilizes inflation — breaks down because fiscal policy injects persistent demand-side pressure that cannot be offset by monetary policy without violating the fiscal constraint.** In the canonical NKPC (π_t = β·E_t[π_{t+1}] + κ·x_t + u_t), inflation depends on expected future inflation, the output gap (x_t), and cost-push shocks (u_t). Under AM/PF, the central bank can credibly commit to reducing E_t[π_{t+1}] by tightening rates, anchoring expectations. Under PM/AF, the fiscal authority's refusal to adjust means the government budget constraint requires a higher price level path, which enters expectations as persistently elevated E_t[π_{t+1}]. The divine coincidence fails because the output gap term (x_t) includes a fiscal wedge: the Kalecki channel recycles ~10-25% of rate-hike contractionary intent back as fiscal stimulus via interest payments on $36T+ debt. This means the Fed cannot close x_t = 0 without either fiscal cooperation or rates high enough to trigger a financial crisis. The testable prediction: core PCE will exhibit a persistent positive bias of 0.3-0.7pp above the 2% target, not because of supply shocks, but because the fiscal wedge prevents the output gap from closing at the inflation-consistent level. The current 2.6-2.8% core PCE trajectory is consistent with this framework.

4. **The natural rate of interest (r*) is no longer identifiable in the standard Laubach-Williams or Holston-Laubach-Williams frameworks because fiscal dominance collapses the distinction between r* and the fiscal-sustainability-consistent rate, creating a zone of fundamental estimation uncertainty of ±150bp.** In the NK model, r* is the real rate consistent with output at potential and stable inflation in the absence of shocks. The HLW model estimates r* at approximately 0.5-1.5% real (equivalently, ~3.0-4.0% nominal at 2.5% inflation). But this estimate assumes AM/PF — i.e., that the fiscal authority adjusts to any r* the central bank identifies. Under PM/AF, the "natural rate" must also be consistent with fiscal solvency. With debt/GDP at ~125% and structural deficits of 5.5-6.0%, the fiscal-sustainability-consistent real rate (r_fiscal) is approximately g - (primary deficit / debt ratio) ≈ 2.5% - (5.5% / 125%) ≈ -1.9%. The gap between r* (~0.5-1.5%) and r_fiscal (~-1.9%) of approximately 2.4-3.4pp represents the "fiscal dominance premium" — the degree to which fiscal conditions distort the natural rate concept. The Fed's SEP longer-run dot dispersion widening from ~50bp to ~125bp+ reflects this identification crisis in real-time. The 2025-2026 framework review is likely to confront this problem but cannot resolve it because the resolution is fiscal, not monetary.

5. **The Mundell-Fleming trilemma under fiscal dominance produces a regime where fiscal expansion simultaneously supports the dollar through capital inflows AND undermines it through twin deficit deterioration — but the resolution timing is non-linear, with the supportive channel dominating for potentially years before an abrupt reversal.** In the IS-LM-BP framework, a fiscal expansion under floating rates and high capital mobility causes: (a) IS curve shifts right → higher output and interest rates → capital inflows → exchange rate appreciation → net export crowding-out (partially offsetting the fiscal stimulus). This is the standard channel that has supported the dollar through 2023-2025 despite widening fiscal deficits. However, the NK extension adds: (b) accumulating current account deficits and net international investment position deterioration create a stock-flow inconsistency that eventually requires dollar depreciation to restore external balance. The US current account deficit at ~3.5-4.0% of GDP with net IIP at -$20T+ is on an unsustainable trajectory. Historical Mundell-Fleming dynamics suggest the tipping point comes when foreign creditors' portfolio share of USD assets reaches saturation — proxied by the declining share of foreign official holdings (from ~33% to ~25% of outstanding Treasuries over 5 years) and the shift from price-insensitive to price-sensitive marginal buyers. The testable prediction: the dollar maintains range-bound strength (DXY 100-108) for 12-24 months before a structural decline of 10-20% over 3-5 years as the twin deficit channel dominates.

6. **Fiscal multipliers under current conditions are in a "worst-case configuration" from the NK perspective: high (1.2-1.8x) because of composition effects, but entirely demand-side with no supply-side offset, meaning every dollar of deficit spending adds approximately $1.2-1.8 to aggregate demand while increasing the output gap's fiscal wedge and raising term premium through the supply channel.** The NK literature (Christiano, Eichenbaum, Rebelo 2011; Woodford 2011) establishes that fiscal multipliers are state-dependent: highest at the zero lower bound (~1.5-2.5x), moderate during monetary accommodation (~1.0-1.5x), and lowest during monetary tightening (~0.5-0.8x) when crowding-out is active. Current conditions represent an unusual configuration: the Fed is no longer at the ZLB but is constrained from offsetting fiscal expansion by the fiscal dominance dynamics in Claim 1. Effective monetary offset is partial (~60-80% of textbook), yielding multipliers of approximately 1.2-1.8x rather than the sub-1.0 you'd expect with unconstrained monetary offset. The composition matters critically: transfer-heavy deficit spending (Social Security, Medicare, interest payments = ~80%+ of mandatory spending) has higher marginal propensity to consume than investment-oriented spending. The Kalecki identity channels ~$400-600B of the ~$2T deficit into corporate profits, sustaining demand even at elevated price levels. Ricardian equivalence — the theoretical benchmark where consumers save fiscal transfers anticipating future taxes — holds at approximately 20-30% offset in empirical estimates (Ramey 2019), far below the 100% of pure theory, meaning 70-80% of deficit spending translates to aggregate demand.

7. **The conjunction of Claims 1-6 produces a specific, testable macro forecast: the US economy will exhibit a "fiscal dominance plateau" characterized by above-trend nominal GDP growth (5.0-6.5%), above-target inflation (2.5-3.5% core PCE), below-NAIRU unemployment (3.5-4.2%), and a Fed funds rate that is insufficiently restrictive by Taylor Rule standards but constrained from tightening further — a configuration that can persist for 3-7 years before either fiscal consolidation or financial crisis forces resolution.** This is the formal NK characterization of what the KB calls "financial repression modal outcome" — but with explicit quantitative parameters derived from model-consistent estimation rather than historical analogy. The NK model predicts this plateau is a stable equilibrium under PM/AF (Bianchi-Melosi 2017 show such regimes can persist for extended periods), but it is NOT a steady state: the debt/GDP ratio continues to rise (~3-4pp per year under current dynamics), progressively narrowing the fiscal space and raising the probability of a transition to either Channel B (acute crisis) or Channel A (formal financial repression). The probability distribution: fiscal dominance plateau persists 3-7 years (55-65%), acute crisis within 3 years (15-20%), meaningful fiscal consolidation within 3 years (10-15%), AI productivity escape (10-15%).

8. **The maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation (2Y-SPX negative, 30Y-SPX positive) is the most reliable market-observable diagnostic of partial fiscal dominance, and the NK model provides a structural explanation: the front end reflects the Fed's cyclical reaction function (still AM within the business cycle) while the long end reflects the fiscal authority's intertemporal budget constraint (AF over the secular horizon).** Formally, in a Bianchi-Melosi regime-switching model, the economy can be locally AM/PF (Fed responds to cyclical inflation) while globally PM/AF (the fiscal trajectory dominates the long-run price level path). This produces a maturity-dependent response: short-dated bonds respond to the cyclical Taylor Rule (negative correlation with equities — Fed cuts when stocks crash), while long-dated bonds respond to the fiscal trajectory (positive correlation with equities — fiscal deterioration pushes up term premium and depresses equity valuations jointly). The critical threshold: when 2Y-SPX correlation turns positive (currently -0.25 to -0.35), the economy has transitioned from partial to full fiscal dominance — the Fed's cyclical credibility has collapsed. I estimate this threshold is approximately 2-5 years away under current fiscal trajectory, but could be triggered earlier by a financial crisis (basis trade unwind, SEC clearing mandate catalyst) that forces the Fed to choose between financial stability and inflation credibility.

9. **The NAIRU is shifting upward by approximately 0.3-0.5pp due to fiscal dominance dynamics, from a pre-2020 estimate of ~3.8-4.0% to a current ~4.2-4.5%, because the fiscal wedge creates persistent above-equilibrium demand that manifests as structural labor market tightness in fiscal-transfer-sensitive sectors (healthcare, government, construction).** The expectations-augmented Phillips Curve posits that inflation accelerates when unemployment is below NAIRU. Under fiscal dominance, the Kalecki profit channel and transfer spending shift the sectoral composition of demand toward labor-intensive services (healthcare employment +600K since 2022, government +400K, construction +200K — together explaining most of the "excess" employment above pre-pandemic trends). These sectors face inelastic labor supply curves, meaning additional demand produces wage pressure rather than output. Unit labor costs in services at ~4.5-5.0% (above the ~3.5% threshold identified in the KB's ULC regime analysis) confirm the NAIRU shift. The implication: the current ~3.7-3.9% unemployment rate is approximately 0.5-0.8pp below the fiscal-dominance-adjusted NAIRU, generating persistent services inflation pressure of ~0.3-0.5pp above what standard output gap models would predict.

10. **The 1942-1951 Treasury-Fed Accord period, while the closest US analogue, substantially OVERSTATES the ease of resolution because the modern structural environment lacks three critical features that made post-WWII deleveraging successful: (a) labor force growth (~0.3% vs ~1.5%), (b) pent-up consumer demand from wartime rationing, and (c) Bretton Woods capital controls that prevented capital flight during financial repression.** In the Solow-Swan growth model, potential output growth = labor force growth + labor productivity growth + capital deepening. Post-WWII: labor force growth of ~1.5% (demobilization + baby boom) + productivity growth of ~2.5-3.0% (technology diffusion, education expansion) + capital deepening = potential GDP growth of ~5.0-5.5% real. Current: labor force growth of ~0.3% (demographics) + productivity growth of ~1.0-1.5% (pre-AI) + capital deepening = potential GDP growth of ~2.0-2.5% real. This 2.5-3.0pp gap in potential growth directly worsens the r-g arithmetic: if g was 5.5% real post-WWII and r was repressed to 2-3% nominal (~0-1% real), the r-g differential was strongly negative at -3 to -5pp. Today, with g at ~2.0-2.5% real and r at ~2.0% real (10Y TIPS), r-g is approximately zero — meaning financial repression must operate through negative real rates rather than outgrowing the debt. The absence of capital controls means financial repression must be covert (regulatory directed buying, SLR exemptions, steepened yield curve penalties) rather than overt, adding fragility.


## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Leeper Regime Classification

The formal NK classification draws on Leeper (1991) and Bianchi-Melosi (2017, "Escaping the Great Recession"). Key evidence:

- **Revealed preference on the Taylor coefficient**: The Fed paused at 5.25-5.50% with core PCE at 4.3% (June 2023), implying a real rate of ~1.0-1.3%. A standard Taylor Rule with φ_π = 1.5 and r* = 0.75% would prescribe FFR ≈ 0.75 + 1.5(4.3-2.0) + 0.5(0.5) = 4.45% — roughly consistent with the actual pause point, but only if r* is at the TOP of its plausible range. With the HLW median r* of ~0.75%, the prescribed rate would be closer to 4.2%, suggesting the actual pause was ~100bp above prescription, yielding φ_π ≈ 1.2-1.3. However, the Fed then CUT from 5.50% to 4.25-4.50% with core PCE still at ~2.7%, implying φ_π on the easing side of approximately 0.8-1.0. The asymmetry (more hawkish on the way up, less hawkish on the way down) is exactly what the Bianchi-Melosi model predicts under "occasionally binding" fiscal constraints.

- **Fiscal feedback channel**: At $36T+ in outstanding debt with ~3.3% blended rate (rising toward ~4.5% as low-coupon debt matures), every 100bp increase in the Fed's rate generates approximately $250-320B in incremental annual interest expense (with a 1-2 year lag due to roll-off timing). This directly widens the deficit, increasing Treasury supply and pushing up term premium — partially undoing the tightening. The Kalecki identity formalizes the offset: interest payments to bondholders are income that gets partially recycled into consumption and investment.

- **Academic foundation**: Bianchi-Melosi (2017) estimate that the US moved toward PM/AF during 2009-2015 and partially returned to AM/PF during the taper tantrum episode. Their framework predicts regime switches are driven by the debt/GDP trajectory and political feasibility of consolidation. At ~125% debt/GDP with bipartisan opposition to both tax increases and spending cuts, the AM/PF preconditions are not met.

### Claim 2: Procyclical Fiscal Anomaly

The IS curve analysis:

- **Output gap estimation**: CBO estimates potential GDP at approximately $29.5T (Q4 2025 annualized). Actual GDP at ~$29.7-29.9T implies a positive output gap of +0.3-0.8%. Alternative approaches: (a) unemployment at 3.7-3.9% vs. CBO NAIRU of ~4.0-4.4% → positive gap by Okun's Law; (b) capacity utilization at ~78-79% vs. long-run average of ~80% → slightly negative by industrial sector metric (but this sector is shrinking as a share of GDP); (c) GDPNow and nowcast models suggest Q1 2026 growth of 1.5-2.5%, near trend.

- **Structural vs. cyclical deficit decomposition**: Total deficit ~6.5% of GDP. CBO automatic stabilizer estimate: ~0.5-1.0% of GDP is cyclical (given near-full employment, the cyclical component is minimal). Structural deficit: ~5.5-6.0% of GDP. Post-WWII mean structural deficit at sub-4% unemployment: ~1.5-2.0% of GDP. Standard deviation: ~1.0%. Current deviation: ~3.5-4.0pp ≈ 3.5-4.0 standard deviations above the historical mean at comparable employment levels.

- **Historical comparison**: The only peacetime precedent for structural deficits of this magnitude at full employment is zero (0/74 annual observations, as the KB confirms). The wartime comparison (1943-45) involved explicit price controls, rationing, and capital controls — a fundamentally different institutional environment.

### Claim 3: NKPC Fiscal Wedge

The divine coincidence breakdown under fiscal dominance:

- **Standard NK divine coincidence** (Blanchard-Gali 2007): With only demand shocks, stabilizing the output gap automatically stabilizes inflation. This relies on the central bank being able to freely adjust rates to close the output gap. Under fiscal dominance, the output gap includes a "fiscal wedge" — demand injected by the fiscal authority that the central bank cannot fully offset.

- **Quantifying the fiscal wedge**: The ~6.5% deficit contributes to aggregate demand through the multiplier channel. With multipliers of 1.2-1.8x (Claim 6), the demand impulse is ~7.8-11.7% of GDP — obviously too high, because this ignores the portion that is passive transfers rather than incremental demand. The incremental demand from the structural deficit above neutral (~3.5-4.0pp above equilibrium × multiplier of ~1.2-1.5x) ≈ 4.2-6.0pp of additional demand-side pressure that the Fed must offset. To offset this through interest rates alone, applying the IS curve sensitivity (a 1pp increase in real rates reduces output by ~0.5-1.0% with an 18-month lag, per Romer-Romer 2004), the Fed would need real rates approximately 4-6pp above neutral — implying nominal rates of ~7-9%. At $36T+ debt, this would generate ~$2.5-3.2T in annual interest expense (approximately the entire discretionary budget), triggering an immediate fiscal crisis. The Fed cannot do it. QED: the divine coincidence is broken.

- **Observable confirmation**: Core PCE at 2.6-2.8% despite the most aggressive hiking cycle since Volcker. Services inflation at ~3.5-4.0% despite unemployment remaining below 4%. The NKPC predicts exactly this: persistent above-target inflation when the fiscal wedge prevents output gap closure at the inflation-consistent rate.

### Claim 4: r* Identification Collapse

- **HLW model limitations**: The Holston-Laubach-Williams (2017) model estimates r* as a latent variable using a Kalman filter on output, inflation, and the policy rate. The model assumes AM/PF — the policy rate is set according to a Taylor Rule with known parameters. Under PM/AF, the policy rate is no longer a clean signal of the equilibrium rate because it is contaminated by fiscal constraints. Lubik and Matthes (2015) show that r* estimates can shift by ±200bp depending on the assumed monetary-fiscal regime.

- **Arithmetic of fiscal sustainability**: The debt dynamics equation: Δ(D/Y) = (r-g)·(D/Y) - pb, where D/Y is debt/GDP, r is the real interest rate, g is real growth, and pb is the primary balance (as % of GDP). For stability: pb ≥ (r-g)·(D/Y). At D/Y = 125%, g = 2.0-2.5%, and pb = -4.0 to -5.5% (structural primary deficit), the required real rate for stability: -4.5 = (r-2.5)·1.25, solving: r = -1.1%. This means the "fiscal-sustainability-consistent" real rate is approximately -1 to -2%, versus the HLW r* of ~0.5-1.5%. The gap of 2-3.5pp represents the degree of financial repression required to stabilize the debt/GDP ratio without fiscal adjustment.

- **SEP dispersion as evidence**: The Fed's Summary of Economic Projections shows the longer-run median FFR estimate has drifted from ~2.5% (2019) to ~3.0% (2025), with the range widening from ~2.0-3.0% to ~2.5-4.0%. This 125bp+ dispersion is unprecedented and reflects genuine disagreement about r* — which I attribute to the identification collapse caused by fiscal dominance distorting the signal.

### Claim 5: Mundell-Fleming Dynamics

- **Standard channel (supportive of dollar)**: IS-LM-BP with flexible exchange rates and high capital mobility: fiscal expansion → higher r → capital inflows → dollar appreciation → crowding out of net exports. US-Europe real rate differential at ~150bp, US-Japan at ~200bp+ → supporting dollar. TIC data: net foreign purchases of long-term US securities ~$80-120B/month through 2024-2025.

- **Stock-flow inconsistency (undermining dollar on secular horizon)**: Current account deficit at ~3.5-4.0% of GDP accumulates into net IIP deterioration. US net IIP: approximately -$20T (largest in history). Gourinchas and Rey (2007, "From World Banker to World Venture Capitalist") show that persistent CA deficits require either: (a) continued willingness of foreigners to accumulate dollar assets (flows), or (b) dollar depreciation to improve the trade balance (adjustment). The declining share of foreign official holdings (33% → 25% of Treasuries) signals erosion of price-insensitive demand.

- **Tipping point identification**: The Mundell-Fleming model predicts that the flow channel (capital inflows) dominates until the stock adjustment (net IIP) reaches a threshold. Empirically, the threshold correlates with foreign portfolio saturation — when the US share of foreign portfolios exceeds home-bias-adjusted benchmarks. With US equities at ~65% of MSCI World, the overweight is becoming strained. Lane and Milesi-Ferretti (2012) find external adjustment episodes typically begin 2-4 years after the peak capital inflow cycle.

### Claim 6: Fiscal Multiplier Configuration

- **State-dependent multiplier estimates**: The NK literature is clear that multipliers vary with the state of the economy and monetary policy stance:
  - At ZLB with accommodative fiscal: 1.5-2.5x (Christiano-Eichenbaum-Rebelo 2011)
  - During monetary accommodation: 1.0-1.5x (Ramey 2019 survey)
  - During monetary tightening: 0.5-0.8x (Mountford-Uhlig 2009)
  - Current configuration (partial monetary offset due to fiscal constraint): estimated 1.2-1.8x

- **Why partial offset rather than full**: The standard model predicts multipliers below 1.0 during tightening because the central bank raises rates to offset fiscal demand. But the fiscal dominance constraint (Claim 1) means the Fed is providing only ~60-80% of the textbook monetary offset. This is observable in the Financial Conditions Index: Goldman FCI eased ~100bp equivalent despite 525bp of hikes, implying that financial conditions are 75-150bp looser than the Fed intends.

- **Ricardian equivalence failure**: The Ricardian benchmark (consumers save 100% of deficit-financed transfers, anticipating future taxes) requires: (a) rational expectations, (b) infinite horizon planning, (c) perfect capital markets, (d) lump-sum taxation. All four assumptions fail empirically. Ramey (2019) surveys and estimates Ricardian offset at 20-30%. With 70-80% of deficit spending translating to demand, the fiscal impulse is substantial.

### Claim 7: Fiscal Dominance Plateau Forecast

The synthesis of Claims 1-6 produces the following internally consistent macro forecast:

- **Nominal GDP growth 5.0-6.5%**: Real potential growth of 2.0-2.5% + inflation of 2.5-3.5% + small positive output gap contribution of 0.3-0.5%. This is above the ~4.5-5.5% consensus because the fiscal wedge sustains demand above potential.

- **Core PCE 2.5-3.5%**: The NKPC with fiscal wedge (Claim 3) predicts persistent above-target inflation. The anchoring effect of expectations (still ~2.3-2.5% on 5Y5Y breakevens) prevents outright de-anchoring, but realized inflation runs 0.5-1.5pp above the 2% target. This is the Bianchi-Melosi "drift" — inflation rises gradually rather than discretely.

- **FFR range 3.75-4.75%**: The Fed maintains a mildly restrictive stance (real rate ~1.0-2.0%) but cannot tighten further due to the fiscal constraint. Easing cycles are limited to ~75-125bp due to inflation persistence. The effective policy bandwidth is compressed to ~155-240bp (confirming the KB concept toolkit_bandwidth_compression).

- **Unemployment 3.5-4.2%**: The NAIRU shift (Claim 9) means this range straddles the revised natural rate. Periods at the low end generate wage pressure; periods at the high end trigger political pressure for more fiscal stimulus, reinforcing the cycle.

- **Duration: 3-7 years**: The Bianchi-Melosi model shows PM/AF regimes can persist for extended periods when the fiscal trajectory worsens gradually (Japan analogue: 25+ years). The US lacks Japan's structural advantages (high domestic savings, current account surplus, homogeneous creditor base), suggesting a shorter horizon. The constraint is the r-g convergence: with the blended rate rising ~30-40bp/year as pre-2022 coupons roll off, the doom loop activation threshold is approximately 3-7 years out.

### Claim 8: Maturity-Dependent Correlation Structural Explanation

- **Bianchi-Melosi regime-switching and maturity**: The model's key insight is that AM/PF and PM/AF can coexist at different horizons. For cyclical frequency (1-3 year horizon), the Fed retains credibility — it responds to recessions and expansions. For secular frequency (5-30 year horizon), the fiscal trajectory dominates the price level path. This maps directly to maturity: 2Y bonds embed cyclical policy expectations (AM), while 30Y bonds embed the fiscal trajectory (AF).

- **Observable evidence**: 2Y-SPX rolling correlation: -0.25 to -0.35 (negative = Fed hedge working). 30Y-SPX rolling correlation: +0.05 to +0.15 (positive = fiscal supply channel dominating). The KB confirms this bifurcation across 6+ independent agents. The NK model provides the structural explanation: it is not merely an empirical pattern but a theoretical prediction of regime coexistence.

- **Transition signal**: When 2Y-SPX turns positive, the cyclical AM regime has collapsed — the Fed has lost credibility even at the business-cycle frequency. This could happen through: (a) the Fed being forced to cut rates during an inflation spike (financial stability dominates), (b) the Fed being unable to hike during an expansion (fiscal constraint fully binding), or (c) a political capture of the Fed (appointment of dovish governors, explicit rate-targeting mandates).

### Claim 9: NAIRU Shift Under Fiscal Dominance

- **Sectoral demand composition**: Fiscal transfer spending (Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, VA benefits, interest payments) disproportionately flows to healthcare, government, and transfer-dependent consumption sectors. Healthcare employment has grown by ~600K since 2022, government by ~400K, and construction (partially fiscal via infrastructure spending) by ~200K. These sectors face inelastic labor supply curves because they require specific skills, licensing, and cannot be offshored.

- **Unit labor cost confirmation**: The KB identifies a ~3.5% ULC threshold as the regime-determining variable for the correlation regime. Current services-sector ULC at ~4.5-5.0% is above this threshold, confirming persistent wage-price pressure in the sectors receiving fiscal demand. Manufacturing ULC at ~2.0-2.5% (benefiting from automation and import competition) is below threshold — the economy is exhibiting a dual Phillips Curve consistent with the sectoral demand shift.

- **NAIRU estimation**: Standard time-varying NAIRU models (e.g., Staiger-Stock-Watson 1997) estimate NAIRU through inflation-acceleration episodes. The 2022-2024 inflation episode, combined with unemployment remaining below 4%, suggests the inflation-consistent unemployment rate has risen. Applying the Ball-Mankiw (2002) hysteresis framework: prolonged fiscal stimulus shifts labor demand toward inelastic-supply sectors → wage pressure at lower aggregate unemployment → NAIRU increases by approximately 0.3-0.5pp. This is consistent with the persistence of services inflation despite overall disinflation.

### Claim 10: Limited Applicability of 1942-1951 Analogue

- **Solow-Swan growth decomposition**: Post-WWII potential growth components: labor force +1.5% (demobilization, baby boom, female labor force participation surge), TFP +2.5-3.0% (wartime technology diffusion, education expansion via GI Bill), capital deepening +0.5-1.0%. Total: ~5.0-5.5% real. Current: labor force +0.3% (demographics, immigration policy uncertainty), TFP +1.0-1.5% (pre-AI), capital deepening +0.5-0.8%. Total: ~2.0-2.5% real. The 2.5-3.0pp gap directly determines the r-g differential.

- **Post-WWII r-g differential**: g ≈ 5.5% real, r repressed to 0-1% real → r-g ≈ -4.5pp. Debt/GDP decline: ~120% to ~30% over 35 years. Current: g ≈ 2.0-2.5% real, r ≈ 2.0% real (10Y TIPS) → r-g ≈ 0pp. For the same deleveraging pace, the primary surplus requirement is approximately 4-5pp of GDP higher today than post-WWII.

- **Capital controls and Bretton Woods**: The post-WWII financial repression operated under: (a) Regulation Q interest rate ceilings on deposits, (b) Bretton Woods fixed exchange rates removing currency risk, (c) capital controls preventing arbitrage of repressed rates, (d) a creditor class (domestic savers) with no alternative to government bonds. In 2026: (a) no deposit rate ceilings, (b) floating exchange rates, (c) free capital mobility including crypto, (d) global alternatives to UST (bunds, JGBs, emerging market bonds). The "beautiful deleveraging" decomposition (40% inflation, 35% growth, 15% primary surplus, 10% repression) is not mechanically replicable under modern conditions because the growth channel is 2.5-3.0pp weaker and the repression channel requires tools that no longer exist.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the effective Taylor coefficient (φ_π ≈ 1.0-1.2) stable, or is it continuing to decline?** If declining, the PM/AF regime is deepening and the fiscal dominance plateau shortens. The next Fed tightening cycle (if it occurs) would provide crucial identification. A coefficient below 1.0 would mean the Taylor Principle is violated outright — qualitatively different from "barely satisfying" it.

2. **Can AI productivity growth (0.5-1.0pp sustained) meaningfully alter the r-g dynamics within the relevant 3-7 year horizon?** The Solow-Swan model requires broad-based TFP diffusion, not just tech-sector gains. Historical GPT (general purpose technology) diffusion lags have been 10-20 years (electricity: 1880s introduction → 1920s productivity surge; IT: 1970s introduction → 1990s productivity surge). Can AI compress this to 3-7 years? This is the single largest uncertainty in the entire fiscal dominance assessment. My NK framework has NO analytical edge on this question — it requires empirical monitoring of TFP data, which currently shows NO structural break (KB concept: no_aggregate_tfp_breakthrough, confidence 9).

3. **What is the correct model for the basis trade amplification mechanism, and does it shorten the fiscal dominance plateau by creating a path to acute crisis?** The NK model is a linearized approximation that does not capture non-linear financial crisis dynamics. The basis trade at $800B-$1.5T and 20-100x leverage introduces a fragility that is orthogonal to the NK framework. I can characterize the macro equilibrium around the crisis, but not the trigger mechanism or probability. This requires agent-based or stress-testing models, not NK optimization.

4. **Does the Ricardian offset change as debt/GDP rises?** The 20-30% Ricardian offset (Ramey 2019) was estimated on samples where debt/GDP averaged 40-70%. At 125% debt/GDP, rational agents may increase their savings rate in anticipation of future fiscal adjustment, potentially raising the Ricardian offset to 30-50%. If so, multipliers would be lower (0.8-1.2x rather than 1.2-1.8x) and the fiscal dominance plateau would be more benign — lower inflation, lower growth, slower debt accumulation. No reliable empirical estimate exists at current debt levels for the US.

5. **How should the NK model incorporate the SEC Treasury clearing mandate (CMESC Q2 2026) as an exogenous policy shock?** This is a calendar-identifiable regulatory catalyst that could trigger basis trade deleveraging independent of macro conditions. The NK model treats financial intermediation as a friction (Gertler-Karadi 2011), but the basis trade leverage and clearing mandate represent a specific institutional vulnerability that is not well-captured by standard financial accelerator models. The interaction between regulatory tightening of leverage and fiscal dominance dynamics is under-theorized.

6. **Is the maturity bifurcation (2Y negative, 30Y positive correlation with SPX) robust to different volatility regimes?** My Claim 8 provides the structural explanation but has not been stress-tested across: (a) VIX > 30 episodes, (b) rapid rate hiking, (c) basis trade unwind, (d) Fed QE restart. In the 2020 COVID episode, the bifurcation temporarily collapsed as ALL maturities went risk-off. If the bifurcation is regime-dependent rather than structural, its value as a fiscal dominance diagnostic is diminished.

7. **What is the political economy equilibrium path of fiscal consolidation under deep polarization?** The NK model is apolitical — it prescribes optimal policy without modeling political constraints. The KB identifies that no democracy has sustained financial repression for >15 years without an external anchor. But the model provides no guidance on whether the US can achieve the ~3.5-4.0pp primary balance improvement required for debt stabilization through any politically feasible combination of tax increases and spending cuts. The DOGE approach is structurally different from all 13 historical consolidation episodes. This is fundamentally a political science question, not an economics question, but it determines which branch of the fiscal dominance scenario tree is realized.

8. **How does the NKPC fiscal wedge interact with potential de-anchoring of inflation expectations?** My Claim 3 assumes expectations remain anchored (~2.3-2.5% on 5Y5Y breakevens) while realized inflation runs persistently above target. The NK model with backward-looking expectations (hybrid NKPC) predicts that persistent above-target inflation eventually shifts expectations upward, creating a non-linear acceleration. The Michigan survey 5-10Y expectations at ~3.0-3.2% (above breakevens) may be an early signal. If expectations de-anchor to 3.0-3.5%, the fiscal dominance plateau becomes an inflationary spiral — transitioning from the "boring middle" to a more acute regime. The threshold for de-anchoring is estimated at ~3-5 years of persistent above-target inflation (Reis 2021), which is within the plateau horizon.

9. **Is the global NK equilibrium consistent with multiple sovereigns simultaneously approaching fiscal dominance?** The Mundell-Fleming framework assumes US fiscal expansion is uniquely large (true: G7 divergence is at post-WWII extremes). But Japan at 250%+ debt/GDP, Italy at 140%+, France at 110%+ with rising deficits, and European defense spending ramp-up could push multiple DM sovereigns toward fiscal dominance simultaneously. In a multi-country NK model (Corsetti-Pesenti 2001), simultaneous fiscal expansion across countries produces: (a) higher global real rates, (b) ambiguous exchange rate effects, (c) potential for coordinated financial repression (an implicit "global Accord"). This scenario is entirely outside the historical sample and represents a genuinely novel configuration.

10. **What observable data would FALSIFY the fiscal dominance plateau thesis?** In the spirit of NK scientific rigor, I specify disconfirmation criteria: (a) core PCE falling below 2.3% for 4+ consecutive months while the fiscal deficit remains above 5% of GDP → the fiscal wedge is weaker than estimated; (b) the effective Taylor coefficient demonstrably rising above 1.5 (e.g., Fed hiking above 6.0% with debt/GDP above 120%) → the fiscal constraint is not binding; (c) 30Y-SPX correlation turning negative for 6+ months while the deficit remains elevated → the maturity bifurcation is not fiscal-driven; (d) primary balance improving by >2pp of GDP without recession → political economy is less constraining than assumed.
