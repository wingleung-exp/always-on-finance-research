The concentration of exportable surplus in 4-5 countries (US, Brazil, Argentina, Ukraine, Australia) makes the system fragile to correlated weather events or geopolitical disruption.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Asymmetric transmission):**
- Biological constraint: wheat requires 7-9 months from planting to harvest; corn 4-5 months; rice 3-6 months. No capital expenditure can accelerate this.
- 2021-22 evidence: despite 40-year-high food inflation, food volumes declined only 2-3% vs. 8-12% in 2008 (KB: demand_destruction_thermostat_break, confidence 7). The difference is attributable to fiscal transfers sustaining household incomes via the Kalecki channel.
- The asymmetry is structural: supply cannot respond faster than one growing season regardless of price signal; demand destruction is suppressed by fiscal policy operating through income transfer channels.

**Claim 2 (Fed "look-through" impossibility via EM intermediation):**
- Food represents 30-50% of CPI baskets in EM vs. 10-15% in DM (KB: agricultural_supply_shock_trilemma). A 30% grain price spike adds 9-15pp to EM food CPI but only 3-4.5pp to US food CPI.
- EM central banks cannot look through food price shocks — they are forced to tighten, creating capital account stress.
- 2007-08 and 2010-11 episodes: agricultural price spikes preceded EM current account deterioration by 2-3 quarters and EM sovereign CDS widening by 3-4 quarters.
- EM reserve drawdowns reduce a marginal buyer of US Treasuries, widening the term premium precisely when the Fed faces the trilemma. EM central banks hold ~$8T in FX reserves, with ~$4T in UST. A 5% drawdown = $200B of UST selling, equivalent to ~10% of annual US Treasury issuance.

**Claim 3 (Double lock mechanism):**
- The RFS mandates ~15 billion gallons of corn ethanol annually, consuming ~5.2-5.5 billion bushels (~36% of US corn crop).
- At current yields (~175-180 bu/acre), this represents ~30-32 million harvested acres dedicated to fuel, not food.
- SNAP FY2024 spending ~$110B; farm bill commodity programs (CCC outlays, crop insurance subsidies) ~$20-30B variable; total ~$130-140B.
- Senate representation: 20+ farm states with 40+ Senate seats. The 2023 debt ceiling negotiations demonstrated farm-state senator leverage on fiscal packages.
- KB (biofuel_mandate_inflation_ratchet, confidence 6) established the price floor mechanism but did not quantify the acreage diversion or the automatic stabilizer trap.

**Claim 4 (Proximity to non-linear thresholds):**
- Global wheat stocks-to-use at ~32.7% appears comfortable, but major exporter stocks-to-use is only ~16% (KB: exporter_stocks_to_use, confidence 7).
- The distinction matters because Chinese and Indian stocks are non-exportable (strategic reserves, domestic consumption mandates).
- Non-linear price response: empirically, when exporter stocks-to-use falls below ~15% for wheat, price elasticity shifts from approximately -0.3 to -0.05, meaning prices must rise 6x as much to clear the same supply shortfall.
- Corn exporter stocks-to-use at ~12-14% is already below the wheat threshold equivalent, though corn demand is more price-elastic due to feed substitution.

**Claim 5 (Regime-dependent pass-through):**
- Normal regime pass-through: USDA ERS estimates 15-25% pass-through from farm-gate to retail food prices, with 6-12 month lags.
- 2021-22 provided a natural experiment: fiscal transfers (stimulus checks, enhanced SNAP, child tax credits) totaling ~$2.5T reached households while commodity prices spiked. Food volumes declined only 2-3%.
- For comparison, 2008 food price spike with no fiscal offset produced 8-12% volume decline.
- The implied pass-through elasticity under fiscal conditions is 25-40% (higher price increases sustainable at maintained volumes).

**Claim 6 (Weather-fiscal correlation):**
- La Niña historically reduces US Midwest corn yields by 3-7% and Argentine soybean yields by 5-15% relative to trend.
- The 2010-11 episode (La Niña + Russian drought + QE2) produced a 75% wheat price spike and contributed to the Arab Spring.
- Current ENSO status and forecast would need to be verified, but the framework for analyzing the interaction is robust.

**Claim 7 (SNAP-farm bill consolidation trap):**
- SNAP is an automatic stabilizer: enrollment rises with unemployment. Congressional Budget Office estimates SNAP outlays increase $5-8B per percentage point of unemployment.
- Farm bill commodity programs (ARC, PLC) trigger payments when crop prices fall below reference prices. A fiscal consolidation that weakened the economy would simultaneously lower crop prices and increase commodity program outlays.
- This creates a fiscal convexity problem: consolidation attempts that reduce growth automatically increase agricultural spending, partially offsetting the consolidation.

**Claim 8 (Export ban contagion):**
- 2007-08: 33 countries imposed food export restrictions affecting ~11% of global food trade (FAO).
- 2022: India banned wheat exports (May), Indonesia banned palm oil exports (April-May), Argentina raised soy export taxes.
- Each ban reduces available exportable surplus, pushing other exporter stocks-to-use lower, triggering further bans. The World Bank estimates export restrictions added 8-15% to 2008 food prices above what fundamentals warranted.
