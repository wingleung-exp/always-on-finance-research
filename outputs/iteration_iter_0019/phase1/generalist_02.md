This connects to the Taylor Rule coefficient analysis from macro_strategist_01: a high φ_π (aggressive inflation fighting) not only anchors expectations but, through its willingness to trigger recessions, reinforces the safe-haven function of bonds.

- **Volatility Regimes:** The 1987 portfolio insurance analogue (Claim 5) connects to the VIX-MOVE taxonomy: the "portfolio insurance moment" would manifest as simultaneous VIX and MOVE spikes — the MOVE-VIX co-explosion regime that the KB identifies as the highest-risk state. ## Open Questions

1. **Is there a fiscal deficit threshold that reliably separates correlation regimes, and if so, what is it?** The conditional falsification (Claim 2) suggests ~5% GDP as a tentative threshold, but this is based on n=2 for the high-deficit condition. A formal cross-country panel study (extending beyond G10 to EM) with fiscal deficit as a conditioning variable would be the most valuable empirical contribution to this research stream.

2. **Does the "validating crisis" requirement (Claim 4) apply when the institutional framework has already conditioned investors to expect negative correlation?** The 2000–2020 negative correlation regime lasted long enough that most active portfolio managers have never operated in a positive-correlation environment. This could mean that the behavioral conditioning is so deep that even without a validating crisis, any moderate equity stress event may trigger enough Treasury buying to restore negative correlation temporarily. Alternatively, the conditioning could mean that the eventual repricing of the hedging relationship will be more violent because investors are positioned for a regime that no longer exists.

3. **How should the analogue framework weight the AI productivity channel against the fiscal channel?** The 1990s analogue shows that a genuine productivity boom can support negative correlation — but only alongside fiscal consolidation. Is the AI channel powerful enough to overwhelm fiscal deterioration on its own? No historical analogue offers a clean test because every previous productivity boom (1920s, 1950s, 1990s) coincided with either favorable fiscal positions or wartime/post-war dynamics.

4. **What is the "portfolio insurance moment" threshold for vol-targeting/risk-parity in the current regime?** The 1987 analogue provides structural guidance but not quantitative calibration. The specific realized vol level, correlation level, and market liquidity condition that would trigger cascade selling is unknown and likely depends on the distribution of vol targets, leverage ratios, and risk budgets across the $500B–$1T AUM — information that is not publicly available.

5. **Does the concentration of the S&P 500 in mega-cap tech create a "dual correlation regime" within equities?** Historical analogues (1960s Nifty Fifty, 2000s tech bubble) suggest that narrow market leadership can persist for years while the broad market exhibits different factor sensitivities. If the top 10 names (35% of index) behave as long-duration, rate-sensitive assets while the remaining 490 names behave as nominal-economy exposures, the aggregate "stock-bond correlation" is a misleading average of two different dynamics. No historical analogue features this degree of index concentration, making this genuinely novel.

6. **Is the 4-year duration of the current transitional regime (since Q1 2022) without a crystallizing event historically unprecedented, and if so, does it imply the regime will stabilize without a discrete event?** The base rate from Claim 4 suggests resolution within 12–36 months. At 4 years, we are well past the upper bound. This could mean: (a) the regime is actually more stable/oscillating than historical transitions, (b) a crystallizing event is overdue and the risk is building, or (c) the slow and oscillating nature of the transition has prevented threshold breaches that would have occurred under more acute stress. The analogue framework cannot adjudicate between these interpretations because there is no precedent for a 4-year transitional regime.

7. **How should the analogue analysis account for the rise of passive investing?** Passive equity AUM has grown from ~10% (1990s) to ~50%+ (2025) of total equity AUM. In a positive correlation regime, passive investors mechanically buy equities on inflows regardless of rate levels — potentially delaying the equity risk premium repricing identified in Claim 8. This interacts with the vol-targeting question: passive inflows are a structural damper on equity repricing, while vol-targeting is a structural amplifier of cross-asset contagion. The net effect is ambiguous and has no historical analogue.

8. **Is the cross-country evidence (Claim 1) truly separable, or are G10 correlation regimes increasingly driven by a single global factor?** The degree of co-movement in correlation regimes across countries has increased since the 2000s (globalization of capital flows, coordinated central bank policy). If G10 correlations are now 80%+ driven by a common global factor (US policy/rates), the "cross-country natural experiment" loses much of its identifying variation. The pre-2000 cross-country evidence (when correlations were more independent) may be more valid than the post-2000 evidence.
