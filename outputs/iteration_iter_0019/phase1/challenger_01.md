# Stock-Bond Correlation Regime Analysis — Devil's Advocate & Consensus Contrarian Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The market's conviction that stock-bond correlation has permanently flipped positive is the most crowded macro narrative of 2025-2026, and the positioning built around it has created a self-defeating fragility: the very trades designed for positive correlation contain the mechanism that will eventually restore negative correlation.** BofA Global Fund Manager Survey data shows "end of 60/40" persisting as a top-3 consensus theme for over two years. CFTC COT reports show structurally net-short Treasury futures positioning. Goldman Prime Brokerage data shows multi-strategy funds underweight duration versus pre-2022 norms. This is no longer a contrarian insight — it is orthodoxy. And orthodoxy at turning points is the market's most reliable contrary indicator.

2. **The consensus framework commits a category error by treating stock-bond correlation as a single variable driven primarily by inflation, when it is actually a composite of at least four distinct and frequently offsetting channels: (a) inflation uncertainty, (b) monetary policy reaction function, (c) fiscal supply effects on term premium, and (d) flight-to-quality risk appetite dynamics.** Collapsing these into a single "correlation regime" label produces false precision and makes the framework vulnerable to surprise when one driver dominates unexpectedly. The Campbell-Shiller-Viceira (2009) decomposition demonstrates that the sign and magnitude of stock-bond correlation depend on the RELATIVE strength of these channels, which shifts over time. In the current environment, drivers (a) and (b) favor negative correlation reassertion (inflation uncertainty declining from 2022-2023 peak; Fed demonstrated willingness to tighten 525bp), while driver (c) favors positive correlation (elevated fiscal supply), and driver (d) is ambiguous. The net signal is INDETERMINATE — not the confident "positive regime" the consensus asserts.

3. **The "correlation flip trigger" that consensus seeks to identify is a misframed question, because correlation regime transitions are not triggered by discrete events but by the gradual exhaustion of the dominant macro driver — and the current dominant driver (post-pandemic inflation normalization) is approximately 70-80% exhausted, based on core PCE's trajectory from 5.4% peak to ~2.5-2.8% current levels.** The historical pattern is unambiguous: every inflation-driven positive correlation episode (1973-1982, 1994, 2004-2006, 2022-2023) reverted to negative correlation not because of a single identifiable "trigger" but because the inflation impulse faded and growth/recession dynamics re-emerged as the dominant driver. The correct question is not "what will trigger the flip?" but "how much further must inflation normalize before growth shocks dominate the correlation signal?" — and the answer is: not much further.

4. **The 1970s analogue — the canonical reference for persistent positive stock-bond correlation — is a misleading historical template because the structural preconditions that sustained positive correlation for a decade (oil supply monopoly, wage-price spiral via 30%+ unionization, unanchored inflation expectations, absence of credible central bank commitment) are almost entirely absent today, while the 1994-1996 and 2004-2007 episodes provide far better analogues and both ended with negative correlation reassertion.** The selection of the 1970s as the primary analogue reflects availability bias (it is the most dramatic episode) and narrative convenience (it supports the "regime change" thesis). The base rate of positive correlation episodes becoming 1970s-like is approximately 1 out of 6-7 episodes since 1970; the base rate of reversion to negative is 5-6 out of 6-7.

5. **Portfolio construction under persistent positive correlation is the consensus's most actionable conclusion — shift from 60/40 toward alternatives-heavy allocations — but the empirical record shows that 60/40 has delivered POSITIVE RETURNS in 3 of the 4 most recent positive-correlation episodes (2023: +12.1%, 2024: +14.3%, 2025 est: ~+8%), because positive correlation does not imply negative returns; it only implies simultaneous moves, which can be simultaneously UP.** The critical distinction the consensus ignores: 60/40 only catastrophically fails when positive correlation coincides with NEGATIVE returns (i.e., stagflation: rising inflation + falling growth). Positive correlation with positive returns (disinflation + growth resilience) is benign for 60/40. The consensus conflates correlation SIGN with return SIGN, which is a basic analytical error that leads to premature portfolio restructuring.

6. **The maturity bifurcation — 2Y-SPX negatively correlated, 30Y-SPX positively correlated — is treated as evidence of fiscal dominance when it is more parsimoniously explained as the normal term structure of correlation during a hiking cycle with intact Fed credibility: short rates anchored by credible policy, long rates reflecting temporary supply/demand imbalances and residual inflation risk premium.** This bifurcation has been observed for approximately 3 years (2023-2026), which constitutes N=1 episode — insufficient to distinguish a structural regime shift from a cyclical phenomenon. Furthermore, the bifurcation is internally inconsistent with a fiscal dominance diagnosis: if the Fed retains sufficient credibility to anchor the short end (producing negative 2Y-SPX correlation), it retains sufficient credibility to prevent permanent fiscal subordination, which means the long-end positive correlation should be viewed as temporary.

7. **The consensus assigns implicitly high confidence (70-85% based on positioning and narrative weight) to the proposition "we are in a new positive correlation regime," but a rigorous Bayesian analysis using the base rate of historical correlation zero-crossings suggests the appropriate posterior probability for a PERSISTENT (>5 year) regime shift is only 25-35%.** Rolling 12-month stock-bond correlation has crossed zero in both directions approximately 8-10 times since 1990. Of these, only 1-2 constituted genuine persistent regime shifts (the 1997-2000 shift to negative correlation being the clearest). The remainder were transient episodes driven by inflation scares that reverted within 1-3 years. Without a strong prior reason to believe "this time is different" — and the structural differences from the 1970s (Claim 4) argue against it — the base rate should dominate the posterior.

8. **The most underappreciated risk is not that correlation stays positive or reverts to negative, but that correlation becomes UNSTABLE — oscillating rapidly between positive and negative on 3-6 month timescales — which invalidates BOTH the consensus portfolio (alternatives-heavy for positive correlation) and the traditional portfolio (60/40 for negative correlation), and instead demands explicit optionality strategies.** Evidence of instability: the August 2024 growth scare produced a sharp negative correlation episode (bonds rallied, equities fell) within an otherwise positive-correlation year. The Q1 2025 tariff-driven volatility produced another negative-correlation episode. These within-year reversals suggest the "regime" is not stable in either direction. An unstable correlation regime is the worst of all worlds for static hedging and the best argument for dynamic, options-based portfolio insurance.

9. **The contrarian trade is not to bet AGAINST positive correlation outright, but to exploit the asymmetry: maintaining duration exposure (overweight 2-5Y Treasuries where negative correlation is strongest) while buying cheap long-dated Treasury call options — which are underpriced because put skew on TLT is elevated, meaning the market overpays for bond downside protection and undercharges for bond upside exposure.** The risk-reward is approximately 3:1 to 5:1: if the contrarian case is right and recession reasserts negative correlation, the portfolio outperforms by 5-15% during the drawdown; if the consensus is right and positive correlation persists, the opportunity cost is 1-3% annually relative to alternatives-heavy portfolios. The catalyst window is 12-24 months, with the next growth scare or recession as the trigger.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### CONSENSUS IDENTIFICATION

**The prevailing market view:**
- Stock-bond correlation has undergone a structural regime shift from negative (1998-2021) to positive (2022-present)
- The shift is driven by fiscal dominance, persistent above-target inflation, and the end of the disinflationary globalization era
- The 1970s is the relevant historical precedent
- 60/40 portfolio construction is broken; investors should allocate toward alternatives (commodities, real assets, private credit, hedge funds)
- The maturity bifurcation (short-end negative, long-end positive) confirms the regime transition and is a leading indicator of deepening fiscal dominance

**How positioning reflects this view:**
- BofA FMS: record allocation to alternatives; structural underweight to long-duration government bonds; "end of 60/40" as persistent consensus theme since late 2023
- CFTC COT: persistent net short positioning in Treasury futures, particularly at the long end (10Y and 30Y), with periodic short squeezes but structural shorts maintained
- Options market: put skew on TLT (iShares 20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF) elevated relative to 2015-2021 norms; call skew compressed, indicating cheap upside exposure to bond rallies
- Sell-side consensus: 2026 year-end 10Y yield forecasts clustered at 4.0-4.5%, with distribution skewed toward higher yields (more forecasters at 5%+ than at 3.5%)
- Goldman Prime Brokerage: multi-strategy hedge funds structurally underweight duration versus pre-2022 allocation benchmarks
- ETF flows: net outflows from long-duration Treasury ETFs (TLT, ZROZ) cumulatively since 2022, with inflows directed toward alternatives (BCOM, GLD, private credit vehicles)

**Crowding assessment: 8/10 crowded.** The anti-bond, pro-alternatives positioning is at multi-year extremes. The intellectual consensus is even more extreme than the positioning — market participants who still run 60/40 do so apologetically, as if admitting a character flaw. This is the hallmark of a thesis that has passed from "controversial insight" through "accepted wisdom" to "unfalsifiable orthodoxy." The last time a correlation view was this uniformly held was 2020-2021, when "bonds always hedge equities" was axiomatic — immediately before it failed spectacularly.

### ASSUMPTION AUDIT

**Assumption 1: Inflation will remain structurally above the Fed's 2% target, keeping the positive correlation driver active.**

*Probability assessment: 50-55% for above-target inflation through 2028; only 25-35% for inflation HIGH ENOUGH to sustain positive correlation.*

The distinction is critical. Above-target inflation (2.3-2.8% core PCE) does NOT reliably produce positive stock-bond correlation. The historical record shows that positive correlation requires either VERY HIGH inflation (>4% core, as in 1973-1982 and 2022) or RAPIDLY RISING inflation (as in 1994 and 2004 rate hike cycles). Moderate above-target inflation with DECLINING trajectory — which describes the current 2024-2026 environment — is historically associated with REVERSION to negative correlation as the inflation scare subsides (1995-1996, 2006-2007, 2019). The consensus extrapolates from peak inflation conditions to a steady-state that the data does not support.

Counterarguments the consensus offers: tariff-driven goods re-inflation, deglobalization supply constraints, persistent shelter inflation, fiscal deficits sustaining demand. Counter-counterarguments: AI-driven productivity gains (even 0.3-0.5pp above trend changes the calculus materially), goods deflation from global overcapacity (China industrial surplus), shelter inflation's well-documented lagging dynamics resolving, fiscal multipliers declining as pent-up demand exhausts.

**Assumption 2: The Fed's reaction function has permanently shifted — it will tolerate above-target inflation rather than respond aggressively to the next growth shock.**

*Probability assessment: 20-30% for permanent shift.*

This is the most important assumption because negative stock-bond correlation is MECHANICALLY produced by an asymmetric Fed reaction function: cutting rates aggressively for growth shocks (rallying bonds) while allowing equities to fall. The evidence AGAINST permanent shift: the Fed hiked 525bp in 16 months (2022-2023), the fastest tightening since Volcker; the Fed held "higher for longer" through 2024-2025 despite political pressure and banking stress (SVB); the Fed's institutional identity is defined by inflation-fighting credibility. The evidence FOR: political economy constraints (interest expense sensitivity), fiscal deficits limiting tightening scope, potential for DOGE-era institutional pressure. Net assessment: the Fed's reaction function has evolved but not broken. The asymmetric put — cut for growth, slower to tighten for inflation — remains intact, which is the mechanism producing negative stock-bond correlation.

**Assumption 3: Fiscal supply dynamics have permanently elevated term premium, creating structural positive correlation at the long end.**

*Probability assessment: 55-65% for elevated term premium; only 30-40% for this producing PERSISTENT positive stock-bond correlation.*

The term premium can be elevated without producing positive stock-bond correlation. In the 2004-2006 period, term premium rose from negative to positive during the Fed's hiking cycle, but stock-bond correlation remained largely negative because strong growth supported BOTH higher rates and higher equities. Elevated term premium produces positive correlation only when term premium SHOCKS are large relative to growth shocks — which requires either massive supply increases or sudden demand withdrawal BEYOND what is already expected. The current fiscal trajectory ($2T+/year issuance, 6%+ deficits) is already priced in the 10Y yield (~4.0-4.5%) and the ACM term premium estimate (~50bp+). For fiscal supply to CONTINUE driving positive correlation, deficits must EXCEED already-pessimistic expectations. The marginal surprise threshold is high.

**Assumption 4: The negative correlation era (1998-2021) was a historical anomaly, and positive correlation is the historical norm to which we are reverting.**

*Probability assessment: partially true but misleading as framing.*

Factually, stock-bond correlation was positive for most of the pre-1998 era. But the conditions that produced the negative correlation era — independent inflation-targeting central banks, globalized supply chains, disinflationary demographics, technology-driven productivity — have been WEAKENED but NOT ELIMINATED. Central bank independence is strained but institutional structures are intact. Globalization is retreating but not reversed (trade volumes near historical highs). Demographics are shifting but slowly (labor force aging is a 20-year trend, not a sudden change). The binary frame — "pre-1998 regime or post-1998 regime" — ignores the possibility of a THIRD regime: unstable, oscillating correlation driven by competing structural forces pulling in opposite directions. The current period may represent this third regime rather than a clean reversion to pre-1998 norms.

**Assumption 5: The "correlation flip trigger" can be identified ex ante and used for portfolio timing.**

*Probability assessment: 15-25% that any ex ante trigger identification framework has predictive power.*

The consensus seeks a discrete trigger: "what event or threshold will cause correlation to flip?" This framing imports an event-driven mental model into a process that is fundamentally gradual. Historical correlation regime transitions were identified RETROSPECTIVELY, not predicted in real time. The shift to negative correlation in 1997-2000 was attributed to causes (Asian financial crisis, LTCM, Fed credibility consolidation, disinflation) only after several years of persistence. The shift to positive correlation in 2022 was attributed to causes (inflation shock, Fed tightening) after the fact. No credible framework has identified correlation regime transitions ex ante with sufficient lead time for portfolio implementation. The search for "triggers" is a category error — it mistakes a slow-moving, multi-causal process for an event.

### CONTRARIAN CASE: NEGATIVE CORRELATION REASSERTION

**What the consensus is ignoring or underweighting:**

**1. The recession nobody is positioned for.** Credit spreads remain tight (IG OAS ~90-110bp, HY OAS ~300-350bp). Equity multiples are elevated (SPX forward P/E ~20-21x). The market prices no recession in 2026 and assigns low probability to 2027. But the next recession — whenever it arrives — will almost certainly reassert negative stock-bond correlation through the most powerful channel: the Fed cutting rates aggressively, rallying bonds, while equities fall on earnings contraction. This mechanism operated in every recession since 1990, including the 2001 recession where inflation was ABOVE target (core CPI 2.6%) yet stock-bond correlation was negative during the equity drawdown because the Fed's rate cuts dominated the correlation signal. The consensus can only be right about persistent positive correlation if (a) recessions never happen, or (b) the Fed does not respond to them with rate cuts. Both are implausible.

**2. The inflation normalization that is already happening.** Core PCE has declined from 5.4% (peak) to approximately 2.5-2.8% (current, early 2026). The trajectory is clearly disinflationary, even if the level remains above target. 5Y5Y inflation breakevens have remained in the 2.2-2.5% range, barely above the 2015-2019 average of 2.0-2.2%. University of Michigan long-term inflation expectations are at 2.8-3.2% — elevated but not unanchored. TIPS market prices inflation declining to ~2.3% over 5 years. If inflation uncertainty is DECLINING (which these measures collectively confirm), then the primary mechanism producing positive correlation is RESOLVING. The consensus treats the level of inflation (above target) as the driver; the academic literature (Campbell-Shiller-Viceira) identifies inflation UNCERTAINTY as the driver. The level is above target but the uncertainty is converging toward pre-2022 norms.

**3. The endogeneity of positioning to correlation outcomes.** When positive correlation becomes consensus and portfolios shift away from bonds (reducing demand, raising yields), the resulting financial tightening slows growth, which creates flight-to-quality demand for bonds, which reasserts negative correlation. The trade contains the seeds of its own reversal. Quantitatively: vol-targeting strategies ($500B-$1T AUM) reduce equity exposure when realized correlation rises, which depresses equity prices, which eventually creates the growth scare that restores negative correlation. Risk parity funds (~$200-400B AUM) deleverage both legs when correlation rises, withdrawing liquidity from both markets and amplifying drawdowns, which triggers the recessionary dynamics that favor negative correlation. The self-correcting mechanism has operated before: the Q4 2018 episode saw positive correlation peak → vol-targeting deleveraging → equity drawdown → growth scare → Fed pivot → negative correlation restoration, all within a 3-month period.

**4. QT is ending, removing a key driver of long-end positive correlation.** The Fed reduced its balance sheet runoff pace to $60B/month in April 2025 and is expected to reach terminal balance sheet in late 2026-early 2027. If QT-driven supply pressure at the long end has been a meaningful driver of the positive correlation at the 20-30Y maturity (and both the consensus and the knowledge base identify it as such), then QT cessation should mechanically reduce the positive correlation signal. This is a time-bound catalyst for the contrarian thesis that the consensus does not seem to have incorporated into its forward-looking assessment.

**5. The equity market is pricing negative correlation.** SPX forward P/E at ~20-21x is consistent with an equity risk premium that assumes bonds provide portfolio insurance (negative correlation). If the consensus is right that correlation has permanently shifted positive, equities should trade at a LOWER multiple to compensate for the loss of the bond hedging benefit — somewhere in the 15-17x range based on historical positive-correlation-era equity premia. The fact that equity multiples have NOT repriced to reflect permanent positive correlation means either: (a) the equity market disagrees with the correlation consensus (which is the contrarian's strongest evidence), or (b) equities are overvalued by 15-25% relative to the true correlation regime (which implies significant downside risk independent of the correlation question). Either interpretation favors the contrarian.

### CONTRARIAN TRADE EXPRESSION

**Core position:**
- Maintain or modestly overweight duration allocation versus benchmark: 40-45% fixed income in a balanced portfolio
- Concentrate duration exposure in 2-5Y Treasuries (where negative stock-bond correlation is strongest and Fed reaction function most directly transmits)
- Underweight 20-30Y relative to benchmark (where fiscal supply risk concentrates)
- Fund duration overweight by reducing alternatives allocation (commodities, private credit, hedge fund beta)

**Optionality overlay:**
- Long TLT call options, 3-6 month expiry, 5-10% OTM, rolled quarterly
- Premium cost: ~1-2% annually. Payoff in recession scenario: 15-30%
- TLT put skew is elevated (expensive puts, cheap calls) — the market is GIVING AWAY the contrarian exposure by overcharging for downside bond protection and undercharging for upside
- This is the cleanest expression of the asymmetry: defined risk (premium cost) with convex payoff in the contrarian scenario

**Risk management / stop-loss:**
- Exit the contrarian position if: rolling 12-month stock-bond correlation exceeds +0.30 AND 5Y5Y inflation breakevens exceed 3.0% AND core PCE re-accelerates above 3.5% — this triple-trigger confirms structural conditions for persistent positive correlation
- Time horizon: 12-24 months. The contrarian thesis has a catalyst window: the next growth scare, recession signal, or Fed rate cut is the trigger for negative correlation reassertion

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary Policy (confidence 5.5, depth 2):** The Fed's reaction function is the single most important determinant of stock-bond correlation — more important than inflation levels, fiscal supply, or structural factors. The critical question is: will the Fed cut rates aggressively in the next recession even if inflation is moderately above target? The revealed preference (2001: cut despite 2.6% core CPI; 2020: cut to zero and launched QE despite incipient inflation) strongly suggests yes. This reaction function asymmetry — cut decisively for growth, tighten gradually for inflation — is the MECHANISM producing negative correlation, and there is no evidence it has been repealed. The consensus must explain why the Fed would behave differently in the next recession than in every recession since 1990. No compelling explanation has been offered.

- **Inflation Regimes (confidence 6.0, depth 3):** The relationship between inflation level and correlation sign is NON-MONOTONIC. The consensus treats it as monotonic: above-target inflation → positive correlation. But the data shows a threshold effect: positive correlation requires either very high inflation (>4% core) or rapidly rising inflation. Moderate, stable, above-target inflation (2.5-3.5% — the current trajectory) is the zone of MAXIMUM UNCERTAINTY about correlation sign because growth shocks and inflation shocks have approximately equal weight. Historical evidence: in 2000-2001, core CPI was 2.6% (above the implicit target) yet stock-bond correlation was negative because the recession dominated. The current inflation regime is consistent with EITHER correlation outcome, which means the high-confidence positive correlation call is not supported by the inflation data alone.

- **Volatility Regimes (confidence 7.1, depth 1):** The MOVE-VIX divergence (elevated rate volatility relative to equity volatility) is cited as evidence of a structural shift. The contrarian interpretation: elevated MOVE reflects late-cycle rate adjustment uncertainty that will compress as the inflation normalization matures, not permanent structural rate volatility. Historically, MOVE/VIX spikes have preceded correlation regime transitions — but the direction of the transition depends on which vol is leading. When MOVE leads VIX (as in 2022), the transition is toward positive correlation. When VIX leads MOVE (as in recessionary episodes), the transition is toward negative correlation. The current MOVE/VIX ratio may be signaling that rate vol has peaked and equity vol is the next shoe to drop — which would favor negative correlation reassertion.

- **Equity Cycles (confidence 5.0, depth 2):** The cross-asset inconsistency between equity valuations and the correlation consensus is the single most important unresolved question. SPX at 20-21x forward P/E prices an equity risk premium consistent with negative stock-bond correlation (bonds provide insurance, so equities can trade at lower ERP / higher multiples). If positive correlation is permanent, fair value for SPX is 15-17x (higher ERP to compensate for lost bond hedging), implying 15-25% downside from current levels. Either: (a) the equity market is right and the correlation consensus is wrong (contrarian case), (b) equities are overvalued by 15-25% (bearish for equities regardless of correlation), or (c) the equity market is slow to reprice (inefficiency that will correct). Each interpretation has significant portfolio implications, and the consensus has not resolved which one applies.

- **Fiscal Dominance (from iter_0016, iter_0017):** Fiscal dominance is invoked as the structural case for persistent positive correlation. But fiscal dominance operates on a spectrum, and current conditions (fiscal dominance signals at approximately 2.0-2.5 on a 5-point scale per prior KB assessment) produce ELEVATED but not PERMANENTLY POSITIVE correlation. Permanent positive correlation requires signals at 4-5 (full central bank subordination), which no assessment projects within 5 years. The maturity bifurcation — rather than confirming fiscal dominance — may confirm that the self-correcting mechanism (market discipline via higher yields constraining fiscal expansion) is still operational. If the system is still self-correcting, the positive correlation signal is cyclical, not structural.

- **Sovereign Debt Sustainability (from iter_0017):** The fiscal supply argument for positive correlation faces a ceiling: markets have already priced $2T+/year issuance and 6%+ deficits. For fiscal supply to CONTINUE driving positive correlation, deficits must be LARGER THAN EXPECTED. With the consensus already pessimistic on fiscal trajectory, the bar for negative fiscal surprises is high. The marginal correlation impact of additional debt issuance declines as the market adjusts — the FIRST $500B of unexpected supply has more correlation impact than the NEXT $500B, because risk premia adjust and new demand sources emerge (insurance companies, pension funds attracted by higher yields).

- **AI & Technology Disruption (from iter_0014, iter_0015):** AI as a disinflationary force is the most underweighted factor in the correlation consensus. If AI delivers even modest productivity gains (0.3-0.5pp above trend), the inflation problem resolves faster than consensus expects, and the central condition for positive correlation (above-target inflation) disappears. The consensus prices AI as equity-positive (earnings growth) and inflation-ambiguous. The contrarian view: AI is equity-ambiguous (benefits accrue unevenly, concentration risk, potential for labor market disruption) but inflation-negative (broad disinflation through productivity gains and cost reduction). This combination — equity-ambiguous, inflation-negative — favors negative stock-bond correlation.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the empirically optimal lookback window for correlation regime identification?** The knowledge base uses 12-month rolling windows, but Campbell-Shiller-Viceira suggest 36-60 months for robust regime identification. Shorter windows generate more "regime shifts" that are actually noise. How many of the currently identified regime transitions would survive a 36-month rolling window test? This is a testable question that would dramatically affect confidence calibration.

2. **Can the four-driver decomposition (inflation uncertainty, monetary policy reaction function, fiscal supply, flight-to-quality) be estimated in real time with sufficient precision to guide portfolio decisions?** If the multi-driver framework is correct, then monitoring a single "correlation regime" indicator is insufficient — portfolio managers need a real-time dashboard of competing driver strengths. What data inputs and estimation methodology would such a dashboard require?

3. **What is the actual AUM that has repositioned from balanced/60-40 toward alternatives since 2022?** The crowdedness assessment would be substantially strengthened by precise flow data. If the narrative has run ahead of actual repositioning (i.e., investors SAY they've abandoned 60/40 but the flows are modest), the crowdedness is less extreme. If the flows are large, the unwind risk is proportionally greater. ETF flow data provides partial evidence but misses institutional rebalancing, pension fund reallocations, and insurance company asset-liability management shifts.

4. **How does the maturity bifurcation behave during a genuine growth scare?** The critical test: in the next recession or severe growth scare, does the positive long-end correlation persist (confirming fiscal dominance) or does flight-to-quality overwhelm supply dynamics and restore negative correlation across the entire curve?
