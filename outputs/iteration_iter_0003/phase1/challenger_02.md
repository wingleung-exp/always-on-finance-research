# Labor Market Structure & Wage Dynamics — Behavioral Finance Critic & Historical Falsifier Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The Phillips Curve is an unreliable forecasting tool whose apparent slope has varied so dramatically across decades that treating it as a stable structural relationship is anchoring bias.** The relationship between unemployment and inflation has flattened, steepened, vanished, and re-appeared across different monetary regimes, yet analysts routinely invoke it as if it were a physical law.

2. **NAIRU/natural rate estimates have such large confidence intervals (often ±1–2pp) that point estimates are functionally useless for real-time policy or portfolio positioning.** The Fed's own retrospective revisions to NAIRU have been larger than the deviations from NAIRU that supposedly trigger policy responses.

3. **The "wage-price spiral" is invoked with far greater frequency and alarm than its historical occurrence warrants — it is a conjunction fallacy masquerading as a base-rate claim.** A true self-reinforcing spiral (wages → prices → wages in a positive feedback loop that accelerates) has only clearly occurred in the US during the 1970s under very specific institutional conditions (strong union coverage, cost-of-living adjustment clauses, accommodative monetary policy, and supply shocks).

4. **The immigration-labor-supply narrative (already in our KB at confidence 8) contains a double-counting risk that has not been resolved, and the "decaying tailwind" framing reflects recency bias about policy tightening.** Immigration policy is itself cyclical and subject to reversal; treating current tightening as permanent while treating prior expansion as temporary is asymmetric reasoning.

5. **"Labor hoarding" as a narrative carries strong survivorship and hindsight biases — firms that hoarded labor and survived are visible; firms that hoarded labor and went bankrupt are not.** The concept is real but its invocation as bullish for labor markets ignores the selection effects in the evidence base.

6. **Remote work's structural impact on productivity is genuinely unknowable at current data horizons, and confident claims in either direction (positive or negative) are overconfidence bias given the measurement challenges.** Aggregate productivity data cannot cleanly isolate the remote-work variable from simultaneous confounds (pandemic recovery, immigration, fiscal stimulus, AI adoption).

7. **Labor market data itself is subject to enormous revision risk that analysts systematically underweight.** The BLS preliminary nonfarm payrolls report has a 90% confidence interval of approximately ±130,000 jobs. CES/QCEW benchmark revisions in 2023-2024 revealed cumulative revisions exceeding 800,000 jobs. Markets and analysts treat preliminary prints as precise signals.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 — Phillips Curve Instability

The Phillips Curve's forecasting record is poor enough that it should carry a health warning. Key falsification points:

- **1960s:** Appeared stable; Samuelson-Solow (1960) presented it as a reliable policy menu.
- **1970s:** Collapsed. Unemployment and inflation rose simultaneously (stagflation), prompting Friedman and Phelps's expectations-augmented reformulation.
- **1990s–2019:** The curve flattened to near-zero slope. Unemployment fell from 6% to 3.5% with inflation barely budging. Stock and Watson (2019) found the Phillips Curve slope coefficient was statistically indistinguishable from zero for the post-1990 period.
- **2021–2023:** Suddenly appeared steep again. Inflation surged while unemployment was low. But disinflation then occurred *without* the unemployment increase the steep-curve interpretation predicted — the so-called "immaculate disinflation."
- **2024–2026:** The relationship is again ambiguous with labor markets cooling gradually.

The bias pattern: analysts selectively invoke whichever version of the Phillips Curve supports their existing view. Hawks cite the 2021–22 steep curve; doves cite the 1990s–2019 flat curve. This is textbook confirmation bias. The curve is not a single thing — it is regime-dependent, and the regime itself is not observable in real time.

### Claim 2 — NAIRU Estimation Failure

The CBO's retrospective NAIRU revisions tell the story:

- In 2000, CBO estimated NAIRU at ~5.2%. Unemployment fell to 3.9% without inflation. NAIRU was subsequently revised down.
- In 2007, estimated NAIRU was ~4.8%. Post-crisis, unemployment rose to 10%, then fell back to 4.4% by 2017 without inflation materializing at levels NAIRU-based models predicted.
- In 2019, unemployment hit 3.5% — well below *any* published NAIRU estimate — with core PCE still below 2%.
- Staiger, Stock, and Watson (1997) demonstrated that the 95% confidence interval for NAIRU was approximately 4.3% to 7.3% — a range so wide it encompasses nearly every observed US unemployment rate during non-recession periods.

The analytical implication: any claim structured as "unemployment is X basis points above/below NAIRU, therefore Y" should be flagged as false precision. The error bars on the input dominate the signal.

### Claim 3 — Wage-Price Spiral Base Rate

Historical falsification of the spiral claim:

- **True spiral (1970s):** Union membership was ~24% of the workforce. Major collective bargaining agreements covered millions of workers with explicit COLA clauses. The Fed under Burns accommodated. Oil shocks provided the supply-side catalyst. All four conditions held simultaneously.
- **Current conditions:** Union membership is ~10% (and ~6% private sector). COLA clauses are rare outside government. The Fed has explicitly committed to restrictive policy at the first sign of de-anchoring. No comparable supply shock is sustained.
- **2021–2023 test case:** Wages accelerated (ECI hit 5.1% Y/Y in Q1 2022), but the spiral did not self-reinforce. Wage growth decelerated from 2023 without requiring a recession, exactly contrary to spiral predictions.
- **International evidence:** Japan had tight labor markets for years (unemployment <3%) with no wage-price spiral. The Eurozone experienced real wage *declines* during 2021–2023 inflation despite tight labor markets.

The conjunction fallacy operates here: "wages are rising AND inflation is elevated" (both true at times) gets mentally upgraded to "wages are rising BECAUSE inflation is rising, which causes more inflation" — a specific causal chain that is less probable than either component.

### Claim 4 — Immigration Narrative Asymmetry

The KB entry (immigration_labor_supply_expansion) correctly notes CBO's ~3.3M net immigration figure for 2023. The "decaying tailwind under policy tightening" framing is where bias enters:

- **Recency bias:** Current policy tightening is treated as the new structural reality. But US immigration policy has reversed direction multiple times (restrictive under Trump 1.0, expansive under Biden, restrictive under Trump 2.0). Treating any single policy moment as permanent is extrapolation from the most recent data point.
- **Anchoring:** The 3.3M figure is itself a revision and an outlier year. Using it as the baseline for measuring "decline" biases the narrative toward a large negative impulse.
- **Unresolved double-counting (flagged in KB):** Immigration cannot simultaneously explain why potential GDP is higher (more workers) AND why the labor market isn't tight (more supply). These are the same mechanism, but different analysts invoke the one that supports their view.

### Claim 5 — Labor Hoarding Survivorship Bias

The term "labor hoarding" is typically used to explain why unemployment hasn't risen despite slowing growth. The evidence base:

- Firms that hoarded and subsequently grew are visible and vocal about their foresight.
- Firms that hoarded and subsequently laid off in large numbers (or failed) are recoded as having "over-hired" rather than having "hoarded and failed" — a framing effect.
- The 2022–2023 tech sector mass layoffs (Meta, Amazon, Google, Microsoft collectively laid off >50,000) were initially attributed to "over-hiring during COVID" rather than "failed labor hoarding." The same behavior gets different narrative labels depending on the outcome. This is pure hindsight bias.
- Labor hoarding is rational only when the cost of re-hiring exceeds the cost of carrying excess labor. This depends on the tightness of the labor market — making the argument partially circular.

### Claim 6 — Remote Work Productivity Unknowability

The knowledge gap around remote work's structural productivity effects is genuine, but what makes it a bias trap is overconfidence in either direction:

- **Positive claims** (Bloom et al. studies showing 13% productivity gains): These are typically from specific firm experiments with self-selected participants, not economy-wide measurements. Composition effects dominate.
- **Negative claims** (return-to-office advocates citing collaboration loss): These rely on input metrics (hours in office, meetings) rather than output metrics and suffer from status-quo bias (assuming pre-2020 work patterns were optimal because they were familiar).
- **Aggregate data:** Total factor productivity growth in 2020–2025 has been noisy, confounded by pandemic disruption, fiscal stimulus, supply chain normalization, and immigration shifts. Isolating the remote-work variable from this noise is an econometric problem that current data cannot solve.

### Claim 7 — Data Revision Risk

- BLS nonfarm payrolls: The standard 90% confidence interval is ±130,000 for the monthly change. A +150,000 print is not statistically distinguishable from 0 or +300,000.
- The March 2024 benchmark revision revised cumulative 2023 job gains down by ~818,000 — roughly 68,000/month of over-reporting for an entire year.
- The birth-death model (estimating net business creation) contributed a large share of these errors, systematically overestimating job creation during periods when business formation was disrupted.
- JOLTS data (job openings) has a response rate that fell from ~60% to ~30% in recent years, substantially increasing the noise in the series. Yet "JOLTS job openings" is cited as if it were a precise, stable measure.

Analysts who trade on preliminary data releases are implicitly treating noise as signal — availability bias (the number is published, therefore it is informative) combined with precision bias (the number has decimal places, therefore it is accurate).

## Open Questions

1. **Is there a detectable structural break in the Phillips Curve around 2020, or is the 2021–2023 episode another instance of supply-shock-driven temporary steepening?** This is testable with sufficient data but we likely need 3–5 more years of observations.

2. **What is the actual elasticity of labor supply to immigration policy changes?** The CBO figures give us a level estimate, but the *responsiveness* of immigration flows to enforcement changes (vs. economic conditions in origin countries) is poorly estimated. This matters enormously for the "decaying tailwind" narrative.

3. **Can wage growth decelerate to 3–3.5% (consistent with 2% inflation at trend productivity) without unemployment rising significantly above current levels?** The 2023–2024 period suggests yes, but the sample is too small for confident generalization. If possible, this would be the second "immaculate" outcome in recent years, which should make us either more confident in soft landings or more suspicious that we're in a non-representative sample.

4. **How should we calibrate forecasting confidence for labor market variables given the documented revision magnitudes?** If the preliminary data can be 68,000/month off for an entire year, what is the appropriate confidence interval for *any* real-time labor market claim? This is a meta-question that affects every other agent's analysis that relies on labor data.

5. **What is the correct base rate for a recession being visible in real-time labor data *before* it is obvious?** The Sahm Rule (0.5pp rise in 3-month average unemployment) has flagged accurately in the past, but it triggered briefly in mid-2024 without a recession following — either a false positive or a demonstration that even the best labor-market recession indicators have meaningful error rates.

6. **Does the gig economy / independent contractor growth systematically bias household survey vs. establishment survey divergence?** The growing wedge between the two surveys may reflect structural measurement failure rather than economic dynamics, which would mean the labor market is more (or less) tight than either survey alone suggests.
