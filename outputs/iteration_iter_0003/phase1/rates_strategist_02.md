## Key Claims

This supply shock is now reversing under immigration policy tightening, removing the key disinflationary force that allowed the Fed to avoid a hard landing.

3. **The transmission from the policy rate to labor market outcomes is structurally impaired in the current cycle because the fiscal deficit (6–7% GDP) is sustaining labor demand through the Kalecki profit channel even as monetary policy attempts to restrain it.** The Fed is tightening into a fiscal impulse that mechanically sustains corporate profits and hiring capacity. 6. **Financial conditions have systematically diverged from policy intent on the labor market channel — equity rallies and credit spread compression have loosened financial conditions even as the policy rate remains restrictive, sustaining the wealth effect and corporate hiring confidence, and effectively nullifying the asset price transmission channel to employment.**

7. **Global monetary policy divergence is creating labor market distortions through the exchange rate channel: the strong dollar (driven by rate differentials) is exporting tightness to EM labor markets while shielding U.S. labor demand via cheap imports, creating a paradox where U.S. monetary tightening may actually loosen domestic labor markets on net through the terms-of-trade effect.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — NAIRU uncertainty renders the reaction function fragile:**
The Staiger, Stock, Watson (1997) result showing NAIRU confidence intervals of 4.3–7.3% is among the most replicated in empirical macro (confirmed by 5 independent agents in iter_0002). Current unemployment at ~4.0–4.2% sits at the extreme lower bound of this interval. The Fed's dot plot and SEP projections imply a "longer-run" unemployment rate of ~4.0–4.2%, essentially asserting we are at NAIRU — but this is a point estimate within a 300bp confidence interval. The practical consequence: the Fed cannot distinguish between an economy at full employment and one still 1–2pp above the natural rate. This uncertainty is asymmetric — overestimating NAIRU leads to unnecessarily restrictive policy and avoidable unemployment; underestimating it leads to inflation overshoot. The Fed's revealed preference since 2022 suggests it is weighting the inflation risk more heavily, consistent with the asymmetric loss function documented after the 2021–2022 credibility shock.

**Claim 2 — Immigration did the Fed's job on labor supply:**
CBO data shows net immigration of ~3.3M in 2023 alone, with cumulative labor force addition of 3–4M workers above pre-pandemic trend. This mechanically raised potential GDP by 0.2–0.5pp annually (Solow accounting, KB confidence=8). More critically for labor market dynamics: it expanded the Beveridge curve outward, allowing job vacancies to fall from the extreme ~12M peak toward ~8M without a corresponding rise in unemployment. The vacancy-to-unemployment ratio normalized from ~2.0 to ~1.2 — a massive loosening that occurred predominantly through supply expansion rather than demand contraction. The Fed's rate hikes contributed to demand cooling, but the labor market rebalancing was disproportionately a supply-side story. This distinction matters profoundly because the immigration channel is now being reversed through executive action, and there is no monetary policy tool that can substitute for it.

**Claim 3 — Fiscal-monetary offset impairs transmission:**
The Kalecki identity (Profits = Investment + Govt Deficit + Net Exports − Household Saving + Dividends) at a 6–7% GDP deficit mechanically sustains ~$1.8–2.0T of corporate profit support (KB confidence=9, all 8 agents in iter_0002 converged). This translates to labor demand through two channels: (a) firms with profits above trend have less pressure to cut headcount, and (b) fiscal spending directly creates employment (government employment, contractors, transfer-recipient spending). Private GDP growth stripped of fiscal is only 1.0–1.5% — at that growth rate, the economy would likely be shedding jobs. The fiscal channel is effectively operating as an automatic stabilizer that offsets monetary tightening's labor market impact, creating the appearance that the transmission mechanism is working normally when in fact the labor market is being held up by fiscal rather than being cooled by monetary policy.

**Claim 4 — Phillips Curve convexity and the sacrifice ratio:**
The New Keynesian Phillips Curve flattened significantly in the 1990s–2010s era of well-anchored expectations, but the 2021–2023 inflation episode revealed that convexity reasserts near potential. The rapid disinflation from 5.6% to ~3% (supply chain normalization + base effects) followed by stalling at 2.5–3% is textbook convexity. Sacrifice ratio estimates of 1.5–3.0 for conventional disinflation balloon to 4–5x for the last mile. At a sacrifice ratio of 4, pushing core PCE from 2.8% to 2.0% (0.8pp) requires ~3.2pp-years of output gap — roughly equivalent to unemployment rising 1.5–2.0pp above NAIRU for 2 years. 5Y5Y inflation expectations at ~2.3% and Michigan 5–10Y at ~2.5% suggest expectations are not fully re-anchored at 2%, meaning the expectations channel cannot do the work alone.

**Claim 5 — Nonlinearity in unemployment dynamics:**
The Sahm Rule (0.5pp rise in 3-month average unemployment from 12-month trough triggers recession) has a perfect in-sample track record since 1970. The mechanism is feedback: rising unemployment → reduced income → reduced spending → further layoffs. This nonlinearity means the Fed's implicit projection of a gentle glide to 4.5% unemployment requires threading a needle that has never been threaded. Every historical episode of unemployment rising >0.5pp has accelerated into recession. The current ~4.0–4.2% rate provides only ~30–50bp of buffer before the Sahm threshold triggers, yet the Fed communicates as if it can park unemployment precisely in that band.

**Claim 6 — Financial conditions offset:**
The Goldman Sachs Financial Conditions Index and Bloomberg FCI both show that financial conditions tightened through late 2022/early 2023 but have subsequently loosened materially, driven by: equity market rally (~25% from October 2022 lows through 2024), credit spread compression (IG to ~90bp, HY to ~350–420bp), and a moderating dollar. The net effect: despite a 5.25–5.50% funds rate (later reduced to the current level), financial conditions are at best modestly restrictive — far less restrictive than the policy rate alone would suggest. For labor market transmission, this means the wealth effect channel (consumer spending supported by equity portfolios) and the corporate financing channel (cheap credit supporting capex and hiring) are both working against the Fed's attempt to cool labor demand.

**Claim 7 — Exchange rate channel paradox:**
The strong dollar (driven by rate differentials with ECB, BOJ, BOE all at lower policy rates or cutting faster) suppresses import prices, reducing goods inflation and improving consumer real purchasing power — which sustains labor demand. Simultaneously, the strong dollar reduces export competitiveness, which should cool manufacturing employment, but manufacturing is only ~8% of U.S. employment. On net, the terms-of-trade improvement from a strong dollar acts as a disinflationary supply-side boost to the U.S. economy, partially offsetting the demand-cooling intent of tight policy. The labor market consequence is counterintuitive: U.S. rate hikes, via the dollar, may actually support domestic labor demand on net while exporting labor market weakness to trade partners.

**Claim 8 — Remote work as structural shift:**
Pre-pandemic, labor supply in high-cost metros (NYC, SF, LA) was constrained by housing costs — workers couldn't afford to live where jobs were. Remote work partially decoupled this constraint, allowing firms to hire from broader geographies and workers to arbitrage cost-of-living differences. This increased effective labor supply elasticity — the Beveridge curve in knowledge-economy sectors shifted favorably as the matching function improved. For monetary policy transmission, this means: (a) housing cost sensitivity to rates is partially decoupled from labor supply — rate hikes that cool housing don't necessarily reduce labor supply in the same way; (b) regional labor market indicators (Atlanta Fed wage tracker by metro, BLS JOLTS by region) are noisier signals of national conditions; (c) wage convergence across geographies reduces the variance the Fed needs to manage.
