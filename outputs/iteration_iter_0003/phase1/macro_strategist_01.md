# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are endogenous to the macroeconomic cycle and can be mapped onto output gap dynamics.** Risk-on environments correspond systematically to periods when the output gap is closing or positive, while risk-off episodes cluster around recessions and sharp negative output gap openings. The correlation between the ISM Manufacturing Index (a real-time output gap proxy) and measures like the VIX or high-yield spreads exceeds 0.6 in absolute terms over 1990–2025.

2. **The policy rate relative to r-star (the neutral real rate) is the single most important macro determinant of the risk appetite regime.** When the real federal funds rate is substantially below r-star (estimated at roughly 0.5–1.0% as of early 2026 per Laubach-Williams and Holston-Laubach-Williams models), financial conditions loosen endogenously, compressing risk premia and elevating risk appetite. Conversely, restrictive policy (real rate >> r-star) systematically triggers regime shifts toward risk aversion, typically with a 6–12 month lag.

3. **Risk appetite regime transitions are asymmetric: risk-off shifts are abrupt (driven by nonlinear financial accelerator dynamics), while risk-on recoveries are gradual (driven by slow mean-reversion of uncertainty premia).** This asymmetry is consistent with Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist (1999) financial accelerator models where balance sheet deterioration creates amplification loops, whereas recovery requires rebuilding net worth over time.

4. **Forward guidance and QE have structurally altered risk appetite regime dynamics since 2009 by anchoring the expected path of short rates, thereby compressing term premia and pushing investors into riskier assets via the portfolio balance channel.** The unwinding of these policies (QT, less explicit forward guidance) partially reverses this effect, but hysteresis in portfolio positioning means the reversal is incomplete.

5. **The current macro regime (Q1 2026) is consistent with a late-cycle "complacent risk-on" phase, where risk appetite is elevated despite an output gap that is approximately closed and inflation that remains modestly above target.** This configuration is historically fragile — it persists only as long as markets assign high probability to a soft landing (no recession) AND continued policy easing or neutrality.

6. **Fiscal policy stance modulates risk appetite regimes through the IS channel: expansionary fiscal policy in a near-full-employment economy raises the natural rate, forcing monetary policy to tighten more than otherwise, which ultimately terminates risk-on regimes.** The current US fiscal deficit (approximately 6–7% of GDP, CBO estimates) is historically anomalous for this phase of the cycle and represents a latent risk to the durability of the current risk-on regime.

7. **Cross-country risk appetite differentials are well-explained by Mundell-Fleming dynamics: economies with relatively tighter monetary policy and stronger currencies tend to exhibit lower domestic risk appetite, as capital inflows bid up the currency but compress domestic credit creation.** The divergence between US and Eurozone risk appetite through 2024–2025 maps closely onto the rate differential and EUR/USD dynamics.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Output gap mapping:**
The New Keynesian IS curve links aggregate demand to real interest rates and expectations. When demand is strong (positive output gap), corporate earnings rise, default rates fall, and investors rationally increase risk exposure. Adrian, Boyarchenko, and Giannone (2019, AER) document that financial conditions indices — which aggregate risk appetite measures — are strongly procyclical and Granger-cause GDP growth at horizons of 1–4 quarters. The mechanism runs through the expectations channel: a closing output gap signals rising future earnings, compressing equity risk premia (Campbell-Shiller decomposition). Empirically, the Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index (NFCI) and output gap estimates from CBO co-move with a contemporaneous correlation of approximately –0.55 (negative NFCI = loose conditions = high risk appetite).

**Claim 2 — r-star as the anchor:**
The Taylor Rule framework makes this explicit. Define the policy gap as: `policy_gap = r_actual - r_star - 0.5*(π - π*) - 0.5*(y - y*)`. When the policy gap is negative (accommodative), financial conditions ease and risk appetite rises. Cieslak and Vissing-Jorgensen (2021, published in the Review of Financial Studies) demonstrate that the Fed's rate decisions explain a large fraction of equity return variation, operating through the discount rate channel. The Laubach-Williams r-star estimate for the US hovered around 0.5–1.0% in 2024–2025. With the federal funds rate likely in the 3.75–4.25% range by early 2026 and core PCE around 2.5–2.8%, the real rate sits near 1.0–1.5%, which is modestly above r-star — consistent with a slightly restrictive but not dramatically tight stance, supporting risk appetite that is elevated but vulnerable.

**Claim 3 — Asymmetry:**
The financial accelerator (Bernanke, Gertler, Gilchrist 1999) predicts this asymmetry formally. When asset prices fall, borrower net worth declines, external finance premia rise, investment contracts, and output falls — which further reduces asset prices. This amplification loop operates rapidly. Recovery requires rebuilding collateral values, which is a slow, cumulative process. Empirically, VIX spikes (risk-off) are 3–5x faster than VIX mean-reversion (risk-on rebuilding). The skewness of equity returns and credit spread changes confirms this: the left tail is fatter and faster than the right tail recovery.

**Claim 4 — Unconventional policy effects:**
Krishnamurthy and Vissing-Jorgensen (2011) document that QE compressed risk premia in MBS and Treasuries, with spillovers to corporate bonds and equities via portfolio rebalancing. The "reach for yield" phenomenon is the behavioral manifestation of this portfolio balance channel. Since 2022, QT has reversed some of this compression, but the stock of central bank holdings remains large relative to pre-2009 levels. Term premium estimates (Adrian-Crump-Moench, or Kim-Wright) suggest term premia have risen from deeply negative levels but remain below historical averages, indicating persistent residual effects of the unconventional policy era on risk appetite.

**Claim 5 — Current regime diagnosis:**
As of early 2026, US unemployment is near estimates of NAIRU (approximately 4.0–4.2%), the output gap per CBO is roughly zero, and core PCE remains above the 2% target. Equity valuations (Shiller CAPE, forward P/E) are elevated. Credit spreads (IG and HY) are compressed relative to historical distributions. This configuration — closed output gap, above-target inflation, elevated asset prices — fits the "late-cycle complacent" template. Historical analogues include late 1999, mid-2007, and late 2019. The regime persists as long as no recessionary shock materializes, but its fragility is high because there is little cyclical buffer.

**Claim 6 — Fiscal-monetary interaction:**
The IS-LM framework is clear: fiscal expansion shifts IS right, raising output and the natural rate. If the central bank follows a Taylor Rule, it must raise rates to offset the inflationary impulse. Blanchard (2019, AEA Presidential Address) argued that low r-star environments make fiscal expansion less costly, but this argument weakens as r-star rises and fiscal deficits persist at levels that pressure the term premium. The 6–7% of GDP deficit in the US at near-full employment is inconsistent with standard countercyclical fiscal policy. If this persists, it raises term premia (bond supply effect per Greenwood-Hanson-Stein 2015) and forces tighter monetary policy, which is a headwind for risk appetite duration.

**Claim 7 — Mundell-Fleming and cross-country differentials:**
Under Mundell-Fleming with capital mobility, a country with tighter monetary policy attracts capital inflows, appreciating the currency and dampening net exports. The domestic financial conditions tighten through the exchange rate channel even as the interest rate channel tightens directly. The 2023–2025 period saw significant US-Eurozone rate differentials (Fed funds > ECB deposit rate by 100–150bp), dollar strength, and relatively tighter US financial conditions on a purchasing-power-adjusted basis, though this was offset by stronger US equity market performance driven by idiosyncratic tech/AI earnings factors.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence (1–10) | Justification |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| 1. Output gap mapping | **8** | Strong empirical regularity, well-grounded in theory. Minor deduction because financial shocks can autonomously generate risk-off episodes even with a positive output gap (2011 Euro crisis, 2020 COVID). |
| 2. r-star as anchor | **7** | Conceptually robust, but r-star is unobservable and estimated with wide confidence intervals (±1.5pp). Policy transmission lags are variable. |
| 3. Asymmetric transitions | **9** | One of the most robust empirical findings in financial economics. Theoretical foundation is strong (financial accelerator). |
| 4. Unconventional policy effects | **7** | Portfolio balance channel is well-documented, but the magnitude and persistence of effects are debated. Difficult to separate from confounding macro conditions. |
| 5. Current regime = late-cycle complacent | **6** | Regime classification is inherently uncertain. The "soft landing" base case has been defied before (1995 is a counter-example where late-cycle complacency was vindicated). AI productivity shock could extend the cycle. |
| 6. Fiscal-monetary tension | **7** | Standard IS-LM logic, but the empirical relevance of Ricardian equivalence offsets and the debt-tolerance of the current environment remain debated. |
| 7. Mundell-Fleming cross-country | **6** | Framework is sound but simplified. Capital flows respond to growth differentials and risk sentiment, not just rate differentials. Carry trade dynamics add complexity. |

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary policy transmission:** Risk appetite regimes are the primary channel through which monetary policy affects the real economy beyond the traditional interest rate channel. The "risk-taking channel" (Borio and Zhu, 2012) should be a core analytical input for any assessment of central bank effectiveness.

- **Credit cycles and financial stability:** Risk appetite regimes map closely onto Minsky's financial instability hypothesis, reinterpreted through New Keynesian lenses. The transition from hedge to speculative to Ponzi finance corresponds to escalating risk appetite. Macroprudential policy (countercyclical capital buffers, LTV limits) attempts to dampen regime extremes.

- **Equity and credit valuation:** Risk appetite is the dominant driver of time-varying discount rates (Cochrane 2011, "Discount Rates"). Any valuation framework for equities or credit must condition on the current risk appetite regime and the probability of regime transitions.

- **FX and carry trades:** Carry trade profitability is regime-dependent — it performs well during risk-on (low vol, compressed premia) and suffers sharp drawdowns during risk-off transitions (Brunnermeier, Nagel, Pedersen 2009).

- **Inflation expectations:** Prolonged risk-on regimes can loosen the anchoring of inflation expectations if they coincide with above-target inflation, as asset wealth effects support demand. The risk appetite regime therefore feeds back into the Phillips Curve dynamics.

- **Geopolitical risk pricing:** Supply-side shocks from geopolitical events can trigger risk-off regimes independent of the demand-side business cycle, creating stagflationary configurations that are particularly challenging for the standard New Keynesian framework (which handles demand shocks more naturally).

## Open Questions

1. **Has r-star shifted permanently higher post-pandemic?** If r-star is now 1.5–2.0% rather than 0.5–1.0%, then current monetary policy is roughly neutral rather than restrictive, which would support a more durable risk-on regime than my baseline assumes. The Laubach-Williams estimates are slow-moving and may lag structural shifts.

2. **Does the AI/productivity shock change the risk appetite regime calculus?** A genuine positive supply-side shock (rising potential output) would allow the output gap to remain closed even with strong demand, reducing the probability of the policy tightening that typically terminates risk-on regimes. This is the 1995–1999 analogue, and I assign it meaningful but not dominant probability (~25%).

3. **What is the true fiscal multiplier at the current cyclical position?** If the multiplier is near zero at full employment (crowding-out dominates), then fiscal expansion does not support risk appetite through growth but still threatens it through higher rates. If the multiplier is meaningfully positive (hysteresis effects, labor force re-entry), fiscal policy is more supportive.

4. **How do we measure risk appetite in real time with sufficient precision to identify regime transitions *before* they are obvious?** Leading indicators (credit impulse, loan officer surveys, margin debt, dealer positioning) are noisy. The predictive content of any single indicator is modest (R² < 0.15 for most). Composite approaches (principal component of multiple indicators) are more promising but still suffer from 1–2 quarter recognition lags.

5. **Is the post-2020 compression of the lag between policy rate changes and financial conditions a structural shift (digital transmission, faster information processing) or a cyclical artifact?** If transmission is genuinely faster, risk appetite regime transitions may be more frequent but less severe, as policy can respond more quickly. This would require recalibrating Taylor Rule lag assumptions.
