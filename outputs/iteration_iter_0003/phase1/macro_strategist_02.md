# Risk Appetite Regimes — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite is endogenously generated by the financial system itself, not an exogenous psychological variable.** Periods of stability breed increasingly leveraged positions (Minsky's "stability is destabilizing"), meaning risk appetite regimes are a direct output of credit dynamics rather than an input to them.

2. **There are three distinct risk appetite regimes that map onto Minsky's borrower taxonomy: hedge-dominant (risk-off), speculative-dominant (risk-neutral/rising), and Ponzi-dominant (risk-on euphoria).** Transitions between these regimes are nonlinear and asymmetric — the shift from Ponzi-dominant to hedge-dominant is sudden and violent (the Minsky moment), while the reverse is gradual.

3. **The credit impulse (change in the change of credit) is the most reliable leading indicator of regime shifts**, superior to VIX, credit spreads, or survey-based sentiment measures, because it captures the actual flow of new purchasing power entering the system rather than pricing a derivative of that flow.

4. **Risk appetite regimes are fundamentally constrained by the sectoral balance identity.** A sustained risk-on environment in the private sector requires either a government deficit or a current account surplus (or both) to supply the net financial assets that validate increasingly leveraged private balance sheets. When neither is forthcoming, risk appetite is running on borrowed time — literally.

5. **The current global risk appetite regime (as of early 2026) has characteristics of late-stage speculative trending toward Ponzi finance in specific sectors**, particularly US corporate credit markets and select emerging market sovereign-to-private intermediation chains, sustained by still-elevated fiscal deficits that are under political pressure to contract.

6. **Mainstream "risk-on/risk-off" binary frameworks are analytically impoverished** because they treat risk appetite as a monolithic toggle rather than recognizing that different sectors of the economy can simultaneously occupy different Minsky stages — household balance sheets can be in hedge mode while corporate balance sheets are deep in speculative territory.

7. **Central bank forward guidance and rate policy do not control risk appetite regimes; they merely modulate the tempo of endogenous credit creation.** The key variable is bank willingness to lend and borrower willingness to lever, which are functions of recent profit history and asset price validation (Kalecki's profit equation operates as the fundamental feedback loop).

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1:** Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis (1986, *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*) provides the theoretical architecture. Empirically, every major financial crisis of the past four decades was preceded by a period of unusually low volatility and rising leverage — the 2004–2007 period is the canonical example, where realized volatility compression enabled the CDO machine to accelerate. Risk appetite didn't "shift" exogenously; the system manufactured it through successful credit extension and asset price validation. The BIS working papers on procyclicality of leverage (Adrian & Shin, 2010) provide the micro-foundations: mark-to-market accounting + VaR-based risk management = mechanical leverage expansion in calm markets.

**Claim 2:** The hedge/speculative/Ponzi taxonomy provides a structural classification:
- **Hedge regime**: Income flows cover both principal and interest. Private sector running financial surplus. Credit growth below nominal GDP growth. This characterized the 2009–2013 US household sector during deleveraging.
- **Speculative regime**: Income covers interest but not principal; refinancing is required. Credit growth exceeds income growth but debt-service ratios remain manageable due to low rates. This characterized 2014–2019 US corporate sector (BBB expansion, covenant-lite lending).
- **Ponzi regime**: Income covers neither principal nor interest; asset price appreciation is required to service debts. Credit growth disconnected from cash flow generation. Pre-2008 subprime housing, pre-2022 crypto-collateralized lending.

The asymmetry is empirically robust: the S&P 500 took 5 years to double from 2009 lows but lost 34% in 23 trading days in March 2020. The Minsky moment is a phase transition, not a gradual reversal.

**Claim 3:** The credit impulse — defined as the second derivative of credit (ΔΔCredit/GDP) — leads real economic activity by 2–4 quarters in most economies (Biggs, Mayer & Pick, 2009). It also leads risk asset performance. The mechanism is direct: new credit creates new deposits (endogenous money), which become new spending, which becomes new income, which validates the original credit decision (or doesn't). The VIX, by contrast, is a derivative of option pricing on equities — it's downstream of the flows that the credit impulse captures upstream. Flow-of-funds data from the Federal Reserve's Z.1 release, the ECB's quarterly sector accounts, and the BIS credit statistics are the primary data sources.

**Claim 4:** The sectoral balances identity (Wynne Godley's framework):

*Private Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance ≡ 0*

This is an accounting identity, not a theory — it is always true. Its implication for risk appetite regimes is powerful: if the private sector is running a deficit (spending more than its income, i.e., net borrowing), the government must be running a corresponding surplus-adjusted-for-foreign-balance, or the foreign sector must be supplying the offset. The Eurozone periphery before 2010 is the textbook case: private sectors running deficits, governments unable to run sufficient deficits (Stability and Growth Pact), and current accounts in deficit — a triple squeeze that made the risk-on regime inherently fragile. The US private sector currently benefits from large government deficits (~6% of GDP), which supply the net financial assets that cushion leveraged positions.

**Claim 5:** Current data points:
- US nonfinancial corporate debt/GDP remains elevated (~50%+), with significant refinancing walls in 2025–2027 from the 2020–2021 issuance wave.
- Credit spreads remain historically tight relative to leverage metrics, suggesting speculative-stage pricing.
- US fiscal deficit (~6% GDP) has been sustaining private sector financial surpluses, but bipartisan pressure for consolidation threatens this offset.
- Household balance sheets, by contrast, are in relatively stronger hedge-mode position post-2008 deleveraging, with mortgage debt-service ratios still historically moderate.
- The divergence between household and corporate fragility classifications is the critical structural feature of this regime.

**Claim 6:** The standard "risk-on/risk-off" (RORO) framework (originated by McGuire & Schrijvers at the BIS) collapses all assets into a single correlation regime toggle. This is useful for short-term trading but analytically bankrupt for identifying structural vulnerability. In 2006, "risk was on" by every RORO measure, but household mortgage balance sheets were in Ponzi territory while corporate balance sheets were relatively healthy. The crisis came from the sector-specific Minsky moment, not from a generic risk toggle.

**Claim 7:** Kalecki's profit equation: *Profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports + Capitalist Consumption – Worker Saving*. This means corporate profits — and therefore the cash flows that validate or invalidate speculative positions — are largely a function of spending decisions elsewhere in the economy. When the government deficit is sustaining profits, risk appetite appears well-founded. When that deficit contracts (fiscal austerity), profits fall, speculative positions fail to cash-flow, and the Minsky cycle accelerates downward. Central bank rate cuts cannot substitute for this — you can lower the cost of credit, but you cannot force profitable deployment of it (the "pushing on a string" problem Keynes identified).

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1 — Endogenous risk appetite | 9/10 | Core Minsky theory with extensive empirical backing from every post-1980 credit cycle. The only uncertainty is the relative weight of endogenous vs. exogenous shocks as triggers. |
| 2 — Three-regime Minsky taxonomy | 8/10 | Theoretically rigorous and empirically supported, but the boundaries between regimes are fuzzy in real-time and sector classification requires judgment calls on cash-flow adequacy. |
| 3 — Credit impulse as leading indicator | 7/10 | Strong empirical track record but data lags (Z.1 is quarterly with ~10-week delay), and the 2020 COVID shock showed that exogenous non-financial shocks can overwhelm credit-driven dynamics temporarily. |
| 4 — Sectoral balance constraint | 10/10 | This is an accounting identity. The analytical claim about its implications for sustainability is 9/10 — the identity must hold, the question is only about timing of adjustment. |
| 5 — Current regime assessment | 6/10 | This involves real-time classification, which is inherently uncertain. The corporate-speculative characterization is well-supported by leverage data but the timing of any transition is unknowable. Political dynamics around fiscal consolidation are particularly difficult to forecast. |
| 6 — RORO framework inadequacy | 8/10 | Well-demonstrated by the sector-specific nature of every major crisis. Some pushback warranted: RORO does capture genuine cross-asset correlation spikes during liquidation events. |
| 7 — Kalecki profit equation primacy | 8/10 | Empirically validated as an accounting identity with causal power, but the transmission lags are variable and fiscal multiplier debates introduce some uncertainty about magnitude. |

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary Policy Transmission**: Risk appetite regimes determine whether rate cuts/hikes actually transmit to the real economy. In a hedge-dominant regime, lower rates have minimal effect (balance sheet recession dynamics per Koo). In a speculative-dominant regime, rate changes modulate the speed of credit creation. This is why the same rate level can be "tight" or "loose" depending on the regime.

- **Asset Pricing / Valuations**: Risk appetite regimes are the macro envelope within which valuation metrics operate. High P/E ratios sustained during speculative regimes are not "irrational" — they reflect genuine cash-flow validation from credit-financed spending. They become irrational only when the credit dynamics reverse.

- **Currency & Balance of Payments**: The foreign sector balance is the release valve or constraint on domestic risk appetite regimes. A country running current account deficits (US) is exporting financial claims on its assets — this sustains foreign risk appetite (accumulating US assets) while the domestic private sector can run deficits. A sudden stop in capital inflows is a Minsky moment imposed from the external balance.

- **Fiscal Policy Effectiveness**: The sectoral balance framework makes fiscal policy assessment inseparable from risk appetite analysis. Fiscal consolidation during a speculative-to-hedge transition is procyclical by construction — it removes the net financial asset flow that was propping up private balance sheets (Eurozone 2010–2013 is the devastating case study).

- **Credit Markets & Spreads**: Credit spreads are a lagging, not leading, indicator of risk appetite regime shifts. They compress throughout the speculative phase and only blow out after the Minsky moment has already begun. The credit impulse leads spreads by 2–4 quarters.

- **Inflation Dynamics**: Risk appetite regimes interact with inflation through the credit channel. In a Ponzi-dominant regime, credit-financed asset purchases create asset price inflation without necessarily creating consumer price inflation (2010s), which blinds NAIRU-focused central banks to the building financial fragility. The disconnect between goods-price stability and balance-sheet fragility is the central policy error the Minsky framework identifies.

## Open Questions

1. **Quantifying regime boundaries in real-time**: What threshold of private debt-service-to-income ratio reliably marks the speculative→Ponzi transition? The BIS DSR data is useful but the critical thresholds vary by institutional context and interest rate environment. Is there a universal metric, or must it be calibrated per cycle?

2. **The role of non-bank financial intermediation (NBFI)**: Minsky wrote primarily about banks. The growth of shadow banking, private credit, CLOs, and algorithmic market-making has shifted much of the speculative/Ponzi activity outside the regulated banking perimeter. How does this change the dynamics of the Minsky cycle? Does it accelerate or dampen the transition? The flow-of-funds data captures some of this but with significant lags and classification challenges.

3. **China's sui generis risk appetite regime**: China's financial system — with state-directed credit, capital controls, and enormous local government financing vehicle (LGFV) debt — doesn't fit neatly into the Minsky taxonomy developed for market-based financial systems. Is the Chinese state's ability to direct credit and absorb losses a genuine stabilizer, or does it merely extend the speculative phase until the eventual Minsky moment is catastrophically larger?

4. **Fiscal trajectory uncertainty**: If the US government deficit contracts significantly (from ~6% to ~3% GDP via spending cuts), what is the speed of transmission to private sector financial balances, and can the private sector sustain current leverage metrics during the transition? Historical episodes (1937, 2010 EU austerity) suggest not, but the US dollar's reserve currency status provides unusual buffers.

5. **AI/productivity shock interaction**: If a genuine productivity shock is underway (plausible but unconfirmed), how does this interact with the Minsky cycle? Productivity-driven profit growth could validate currently speculative positions and extend the cycle. Alternatively, it could redirect credit into even more speculative AI-adjacent ventures (echoing the 1990s telecom bubble). The Kalecki profit equation doesn't distinguish between productivity-validated and credit-validated profits in real-time.

6. **Measurement of Ponzi finance in private credit markets**: Private credit (~$1.7T+ AUM) is by definition opaque. How much of this is effectively Ponzi finance (payment-in-kind toggles, NAV lending against illiquid marks)? The lack of mark-to-market and the absence of public disclosure means the Minsky classification of this sector is largely conjectural — a significant blind spot.
