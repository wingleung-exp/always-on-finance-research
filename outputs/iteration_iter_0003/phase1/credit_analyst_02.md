# Risk Appetite Regimes — Structured & Private Credit Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite in credit markets is best measured not by spread levels but by the structural terms borrowers can extract**: covenant-lite share, EBITDA addback magnitude, PIK toggle availability, and maturity extension ease are the true barometers of how aggressively capital is being deployed.

2. **CLO arbitrage economics function as the transmission mechanism between macro risk appetite and leveraged loan market conditions**: when CLO equity tranche returns compress below ~12-13% IRR thresholds, new CLO formation slows, removing the marginal bid for leveraged loans and forcing a regime shift regardless of what spread indices say.

3. **The current risk appetite regime (as of early 2026) is bifurcated**: public leveraged credit markets show moderate discipline after the 2024-2025 spread widening, while private credit continues to deploy at leverage multiples (6.5-7.5x+ on sponsor deals) and with documentation laxity that reflects peak-cycle risk appetite — creating a dangerous divergence.

4. **Risk appetite regime shifts in credit markets are asymmetric and non-linear**: the transition from risk-on to risk-off is governed by the maturity wall — appetite can remain elevated for years as long as near-term maturities are manageable, then collapse suddenly when a critical mass of borrowers face refinancing needs simultaneously in a tightening environment.

5. **Private credit's rise to ~$1.7-2.0 trillion AUM has created a structural risk appetite buffer that delays but amplifies regime transitions**: private lenders' ability to amend-and-extend, PIK interest, and mark-to-model rather than mark-to-market means stress is hidden longer, but the eventual repricing is more severe because problems compound beneath the surface.

6. **Rating agency activity — particularly the ratio of downgrades to upgrades in leveraged credit and the pace of CLO tranche downgrades — is a lagging but reliable confirmation signal of risk appetite regime change**, typically trailing market-implied shifts by 6-12 months.

7. **Semi-liquid private credit vehicles (interval funds, BDC non-traded vehicles) have introduced a new contagion channel**: redemption pressure in these structures can force selling into illiquid markets, creating a feedback loop that did not exist in prior credit cycles and that accelerates risk appetite regime collapse.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Terms over spreads:**
Spreads can compress mechanically through technical supply/demand (e.g., CLO demand absorbing loan supply) without reflecting genuine improvement in credit quality. The 2019-2021 cycle demonstrated this clearly: spreads were historically tight, but covenant-lite share exceeded 85% of institutional loans, and average EBITDA addbacks reached 25-40% of reported EBITDA on new sponsor deals. The true risk appetite was embedded in documentation, not in price. When analyzing regime, I track: (a) covenant-lite share of new issuance, (b) average pro forma leverage at origination, (c) magnitude and type of EBITDA addbacks permitted, (d) flexibility provisions in credit agreements (unlimited restricted payments baskets, J.Crew/Chewy-style trapdoor provisions). These structural indicators shift before spreads do and are far more predictive of eventual loss severity.

**Claim 2 — CLO arbitrage as transmission mechanism:**
CLOs purchase approximately 60-70% of newly issued institutional leveraged loans. When CLO economics work (AAA spreads tight enough relative to loan spreads to generate attractive equity returns), the CLO machine provides a continuous and price-insensitive bid. When economics break down — as they did briefly in late 2022 and again in mid-2025 when AAA spreads widened past SOFR+170 — new CLO formation drops sharply. The warehouse pipeline (loans accumulated by managers in anticipation of CLO pricing) becomes a source of forced selling rather than a source of demand. This is the single most important mechanical driver of risk appetite regime change in leveraged credit. Current CLO AAA spreads around SOFR+140-155 support moderate issuance, but the arbitrage is thinner than 2021 levels, constraining the pace of new formation and limiting how aggressively the market can absorb new risky supply.

**Claim 3 — Public/private bifurcation:**
Post-2023 spread volatility, public market underwriters have pulled back somewhat on the most aggressive structures. The leveraged loan market's average new-issue leverage has stabilized around 5.0-5.5x for broadly syndicated deals. But private credit — particularly the mega-funds ($10B+ direct lending vehicles) — are competing for the same sponsor deals at 6.5-7.5x leverage with documentation that includes uncapped addbacks, generous equity cures, and PIK flexibility that would not pass muster with CLO rating agency criteria. This bifurcation means the aggregate risk appetite in leveraged lending is higher than public market indicators suggest, because a growing share of the riskiest deals are originated in private markets that lack price transparency.

**Claim 4 — Maturity wall as regime trigger:**
Risk appetite does not erode gradually — it snaps. The mechanism is the maturity wall. As long as borrowers can kick maturities forward (via amend-and-extend or opportunistic refinancing), even weak credits survive and loss rates stay low, which validates continued risk-taking. The regime shift occurs when a sufficient concentration of maturities comes due in a period of tight financial conditions. The 2025-2027 maturity wall for leveraged loans and high-yield bonds is estimated at $800B-$1.1T (depending on the source and what gets refinanced early). If spreads remain at current levels, most of this can be refinanced, but at meaningfully higher coupons — squeezing interest coverage and creating a slow-moving deterioration in credit quality that could trigger a more abrupt repricing.

**Claim 5 — Private credit as shock absorber/amplifier:**
Private credit's mark-to-model framework means that a loan trading at 85 cents in the public market may still be marked at 97-98 in a private fund's NAV. This creates the illusion of stability and suppresses measured volatility, which keeps allocators comfortable and fundraising flowing. Amendment activity in private credit has surged — estimated 30-40% of deals originated in 2021-2022 have undergone some form of amendment — but these amendments are largely invisible to the market. The risk is that when actual defaults materialize, recovery values will be lower than NAVs implied, and investors will discover simultaneously that (a) they were overexposed and (b) the assets are worth less than reported.

**Claim 6 — Rating agency lag:**
Moody's and S&P leveraged loan downgrade/upgrade ratios typically turn negative 6-12 months after market spreads begin widening. CLO tranche downgrades lag even further because of structural protections (OC/IC tests) that mask collateral deterioration until it reaches critical thresholds. In the 2020 cycle, the downgrade wave for CLO mezzanine tranches peaked ~9 months after the initial shock. This lag means that by the time rating agencies confirm a risk appetite regime change, the market has already moved — but it also means that the agency actions can extend and deepen the regime shift by triggering forced selling from constrained investors.

**Claim 7 — Semi-liquid vehicle contagion:**
Non-traded BDCs and interval funds have raised $100B+ cumulatively. These vehicles offer periodic (typically quarterly) liquidity with gates. In a stress scenario, redemption queues build, gates are triggered, and the vehicles are forced to sell their most liquid positions (often the same broadly syndicated loans that CLOs hold) to meet redemptions. This creates a new correlation between private credit stress and public loan market pricing that did not exist before 2018. The feedback loop: private credit NAV declines → redemption requests → forced selling of liquid assets → public loan market price declines → further NAV markdowns.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1 — Terms over spreads | **9/10** | Well-established empirical pattern across multiple credit cycles; extensive documentation from S&P LCD, Pitchbook, and legal analyses of credit agreement erosion |
| 2 — CLO arbitrage transmission | **9/10** | Mechanically verifiable; CLO share of loan demand is a hard number, and the relationship between arbitrage economics and issuance pace is tightly correlated |
| 3 — Public/private bifurcation | **7/10** | Directionally confident but specific leverage multiples for private credit are estimated from limited disclosed data; true market-wide averages are uncertain due to opacity |
| 4 — Maturity wall as regime trigger | **8/10** | Historical pattern is clear (2008-2009, 2020), but the timing of when the wall becomes binding depends on rate/spread path that is inherently uncertain |
| 5 — Private credit shock absorber/amplifier | **7/10** | The logic is sound and directionally supported by amendment data, but the magnitude of NAV-to-reality gap is genuinely unknown because we have not stress-tested this generation of private credit at scale |
| 6 — Rating agency lag | **8/10** | Empirically demonstrated across multiple cycles with quantifiable lags; the specific timing in a future cycle is uncertain |
| 7 — Semi-liquid vehicle contagion | **6/10** | Theoretically well-grounded but untested at scale; the actual behavior of gates, redemption queues, and forced selling in stress is extrapolated from limited episodes (March 2020 was too brief to fully test) |

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Monetary policy & interest rate regimes**: The cost of CLO liabilities and the refinancing feasibility of the maturity wall are directly determined by the rate environment. A "higher for longer" regime compresses CLO arbitrage and makes the maturity wall more binding — both of which tighten credit risk appetite mechanically, regardless of investor sentiment.

- **Equity market valuations & volatility regimes**: Sponsor equity valuations for leveraged buyouts are anchored to public equity multiples. When equity risk appetite shifts, LBO purchase prices adjust with a lag, changing the leverage dynamics of new private credit origination. Equity volatility spikes also widen CLO liability spreads with a beta of roughly 1.5-2x.

- **Banking sector & financial conditions**: Bank willingness to hold leveraged loan warehouses and provide subscription lines to private credit funds creates a hidden linkage between bank risk appetite and non-bank credit creation. Regulatory tightening (Basel III endgame implementation, leveraged lending guidance) pushes activity to private credit but also removes the bank balance sheet backstop.

- **Fiscal policy & sovereign credit**: Government bond supply dynamics compete with corporate credit for fixed-income allocations. Periods of heavy Treasury issuance can crowd out demand for credit, raising the hurdle rate for CLO economics and dampening credit risk appetite.

- **Geopolitical risk**: Supply chain disruption and input cost volatility hit levered borrowers' EBITDA directly, making the adjusted addback figures even more suspect and raising the probability that the maturity wall becomes binding for borrowers whose earnings have deteriorated.

- **Inflation persistence**: Elevated inflation squeezes margins for leveraged borrowers (especially in consumer-facing sectors) while keeping base rates high, creating a dual headwind that is uniquely hostile to the highly levered capital structures that private credit has financed.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the true aggregate leverage multiple across all private credit?** No single data source captures this. Pitchbook and LCD cover subsets. The actual risk appetite in private credit may be higher or lower than available data suggests.

2. **How will semi-liquid private credit vehicles behave in a sustained drawdown?** We have no cycle-length test case. March 2020 was a V-shaped recovery that did not trigger sustained redemptions. What happens in a 12-18 month credit deterioration?

3. **Are CLO equity investors — increasingly insurance companies and permanent capital vehicles — structurally more patient than prior generations of CLO equity holders?** If so, the CLO arbitrage transmission mechanism may be dampened, with equity investors willing to accept lower returns for longer before pulling back.

4. **What is the actual overlap between CLO collateral pools and private credit portfolios at the borrower level?** If the same borrowers are levered in both markets, stress in one transmits directly to the other. The degree of overlap is not well-mapped.

5. **How much of the reported EBITDA in private credit portfolios would survive a forensic adjustment for addbacks?** Industry estimates range from 15-40% haircuts, but no systematic study exists. The answer determines whether current leverage multiples are 6.5x or effectively 8-10x on true cash earnings.

6. **At what spread and rate combination does the 2026-2028 maturity wall become a genuine crisis rather than a manageable drag?** Is there a threshold (e.g., HY index > 500bps, SOFR > 5%) beyond which refinancing becomes impossible for a critical mass of borrowers, or is the distribution of borrower quality dispersed enough to prevent a cliff-edge dynamic?
