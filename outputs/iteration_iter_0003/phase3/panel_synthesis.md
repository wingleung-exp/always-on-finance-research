# SYNTHESIS: Labor Market Structure & Wage Dynamics
**Topic:** labor_market_dynamics | **Category:** macro_frameworks | **Iteration:** iter_0003

## HIGH_CONFIDENCE

### 1. NAIRU point estimates are functionally useless for real-time policy or portfolio positioning
**Confidence: 9/10** | **Agents:** challenger_02 (9), rates_strategist_02 (9), generalist_01 (8), generalist_02, equity_analyst_01, equity_analyst_02 (6), fx_strategist_01 (6), fx_strategist_02 (8)

The Staiger-Stock-Watson 95% confidence interval of ~4.3–7.3% spans the entire policy-relevant range. With unemployment at ~4.0–4.2%, we cannot determine in real time whether the economy is above, at, or below the natural rate. CBO retrospective revisions to NAIRU have been larger than the deviations from NAIRU that supposedly triggered policy responses. This is the single strongest consensus claim — both sides of the pair_3 debate assigned 9/10, and no agent challenged the underlying econometrics. The direct implication: any analysis structured as "unemployment is X bp above/below NAIRU, therefore Y" is false precision, and this uncertainty propagates into every downstream claim about inflation transmission, Fed reaction functions, and output gaps.

### 2. Immigration-driven labor supply expansion (2022–2025) was the primary labor market rebalancing mechanism, more important than 525bp of rate hikes
**Confidence: 8/10** | **Agents:** All 8 agents cite CBO ~3.3M net immigration in 2023

The ~3–4M workers above pre-pandemic trend expanded the Beveridge curve, allowing vacancies to fall from ~12M to ~8M without unemployment rising. rates_strategist_02 framed this most sharply: immigration "achieved what 525bp of rate hikes could not — it loosened the labor market through the supply side, bypassing the demand-destruction channel entirely." The Solow accounting (3–4M workers × ~$80k/worker ≈ 0.3–0.4pp GDP growth) is mechanical and uncontested. The concentration in lower-skill service occupations (hospitality, food services, construction, healthcare) disproportionately relieved the sectors with the most acute post-COVID wage pressure. This is the single most convergent factual claim across all agents.

### 3. The immigration reversal is the most important near-term structural labor supply shock
**Confidence: 7/10** | **Agents:** All 8 agents agree on direction; generalist_01 (7), generalist_02 (8), equity_analyst_01 (7), equity_analyst_02 (7), rates_strategist_02 (8), fx_strategist_01 (7)

Policy tightening is reducing net immigration from the ~3.3M 2023 peak toward 0.5–1.0M or below. The mechanical labor market tightening is estimated at 0.5–1.0pp in effective labor supply gap (generalist_01) or ~0.6pp per 1M reduction (generalist_02). Both FX strategists trace second-order effects through the remittance channel (fx_strategist_02: Mexico ~$63B, ~4.5% GDP) and the dollar cycle (fx_strategist_01: removal of the "free lunch" disinflationary supply). **Confidence docked from 8 to 7** because challenger_02 correctly identifies recency bias in treating current tightening as permanent — immigration policy has reversed direction multiple times — and the pair_3 debate confirmed this: near-term direction is high-confidence, but medium-term projections are extrapolation. Speed and magnitude remain the key uncertainties (12–24 month lags are plausible based on historical analogues).

### 4. Wage-price spiral is not the base case under current institutional conditions
**Confidence: 8/10** | **Agents:** challenger_02 (8), generalist_02 (8), generalist_01 (6), rates_strategist_02 (implied), equity_analyst_01 (6)

generalist_02's conjunctive-conditions test is the strongest evidence: across 8 post-1960 episodes, all three conditions — unemployment <3.8%, wage growth >5%, and inflation expectations de-anchored — were necessary for a demand-driven spiral. Current conditions have zero of three met. challenger_02 adds the institutional falsification: union membership at ~10% (vs. ~24% in the 1970s), COLA clauses rare, Fed explicitly committed to restrictive policy. The 2021–2023 natural experiment is decisive: even with 2 of 3 conditions met (UE <3.8%, wages >5%), no spiral materialized. pair_3 debate confirmed challenger_02 was "decisively stronger" on this point. The remaining 15–20% tail probability (equity_analyst_01) was partially refuted in pair_1 debate as internally inconsistent with the same agent's deterioration thesis.

### 5. Labor market data is subject to revision risk that analysts systematically underweight
**Confidence: 9/10** | **Agents:** challenger_02 (9), rates_strategist_02 (implicit), generalist_01 (implicit)

BLS nonfarm payrolls 90% CI: ±130,000 jobs. The March 2024 benchmark revision revised cumulative 2023 job gains down by ~818,000 (~68,000/month for an entire year). JOLTS response rates have fallen from ~60% to ~30%. pair_3 debate called this "the single most practically actionable insight across both analyses" because every other claim in every other analysis relies on data whose precision is far lower than assumed. This is a meta-claim that should carry a mandatory disclaimer on all labor market analysis: a +150,000 payroll print is not statistically distinguishable from 0 or +300,000.

### 6. Credit markets lead labor markets in signaling downturns (sequencing: credit → equities → labor)
**Confidence: 9/10** | **Agents:** generalist_02 (9), confirmed decisively in pair_0 debate

generalist_02 documents the sequencing across 2000–01, 2007–09, and 1990: credit spreads widened 3–12 months before the Sahm Rule triggered; equities priced 60–80% of eventual drawdowns before unemployment confirmed recession. pair_0 debate called this "the single most important correction to conventional labor market monitoring" and found generalist_02 "wins this one decisively" over generalist_01's labor-as-primary-signal framework. Practical implication: monitor HY OAS for labor market turning points, not NFP. This directly corrects the common practice of treating payroll releases as leading indicators for asset allocation.

### 7. The fiscal deficit (Kalecki channel) is sustaining labor demand despite monetary tightening
**Confidence: 8/10** | **Agents:** rates_strategist_02 (8), generalist_01, equity_analyst_01, equity_analyst_02

The 6–7% GDP structural deficit mechanically sustains ~$1.8–2.0T of corporate profit support via the Kalecki identity (Profits = Investment + Govt Deficit + Net Exports − Household Saving + Dividends). rates_strategist_02 makes the sharpest case: private GDP growth stripped of fiscal is only 1.0–1.5%, at which rate the economy would likely be shedding jobs. The labor market isn't defying monetary policy — it's being held up by fiscal policy. pair_3 debate called this "Agent A's most original and underappreciated claim." This resolves the puzzle of stable employment despite the most aggressive tightening cycle in four decades.

### 8. Unit labor cost growth is the dominant variable for equity margins and valuations
**Confidence: 8/10** | **Agents:** equity_analyst_01 (7), equity_analyst_02 (8), confirmed in pair_1 debate

ULC = compensation/hour ÷ output/hour. With labor compensation at 55–60% of corporate costs, ULC mechanically governs margins. The dual-channel effect (margins + discount rate) is uncontested. Both equity agents and the pair_1 debate converge. **Critical unresolved factual issue:** equity_analyst_01 cites ULC at ~2.0–2.5% ("comfortable"), equity_analyst_02 cites 3.5–4.5% ("margin-incompatible"). pair_1 debate estimates the true current figure splits the difference at ~2.5–3.0%. The ~3.5% threshold above which margin expansion becomes impossible without productivity acceleration is well-grounded, even if the current reading is contested.

### 9. The labor market is the fulcrum variable for the stagflation/disinflation bifurcation
**Confidence: 8/10** | **Agents:** generalist_01 (8), equity_analyst_01 (8), rates_strategist_02 (8), fx_strategist_01 (8)

The labor market simultaneously determines the consumption floor (~70% of GDP is wage-income dependent), the wage-price transmission channel (ULC → services inflation), and the Fed reaction function (dual mandate). No other single observable has this trifecta. generalist_01's formulation is cleanest: "Whether the U.S. economy follows a disinflationary soft landing or a stagflationary path depends more on labor market dynamics than on any other single observable." This directly connects to the iter_0002 KB finding (stagflationary/disinflationary bifurcation, confidence 6).

## MODERATE_CONFIDENCE

### 10. Labor hoarding creates nonlinear cliff risk, not gradual cooling
**Confidence: 6/10** | **Agents:** generalist_01 (5), generalist_02 (6), equity_analyst_01 (8), equity_analyst_02 (7), challenger_02 (7), fx_strategist_02 (7)

Six agents converge on the mechanism: firms retaining excess workers creates fragility — stable payrolls followed by potential sudden decompression if demand crosses a threshold. generalist_02's margin threshold (~8–9% S&P 500 net margin vs. current ~11–12%) provides a measurable trigger. The JOLTS data pattern (low layoffs ~1.0% alongside declining hires rate) is the signature. **Confidence capped at 6** because: (a) challenger_02 correctly identifies survivorship bias — failed hoarding gets relabeled "over-hiring"; (b) pair_0 refuted generalist_01's "Minsky moment" analogy as misleading (this is standard inventory adjustment, not endogenous leverage); (c) hoarding is inferred from noisy productivity data, not directly measured; (d) if demand re-accelerates, hoarded labor becomes productive and the risk dissipates.

### 11. Phillips Curve is regime-dependent and unreliable for point forecasts, but directionally convex near potential
**Confidence: 6/10** | **Agents:** challenger_02 (9 on instability), rates_strategist_02 (7 on convexity), generalist_01 (implied), generalist_02 (implied)

Two distinct claims merged here: (a) the Phillips Curve's slope has varied so dramatically across regimes that point estimates of sacrifice ratios are false precision (challenger_02, high confidence, uncontested); (b) the curve is directionally convex near potential — last-mile disinflation is harder (rates_strategist_02, moderate confidence, partially survived debate). pair_3 confirmed challenger_02 was stronger: the "immaculate disinflation" of 2023–24 directly falsifies the steep-curve interpretation. The directional claim (convexity exists) survives; the specific sacrifice ratio of 4–5x does not (see REFUTED). Practical implication: use the Phillips Curve as a regime indicator, not a forecasting tool.

### 12. Cross-asset markets are pricing an internally inconsistent labor market narrative
**Confidence: 7/10** | **Agents:** generalist_01 (8), equity_analyst_02 (7, via reverse DCF)

Equities (S&P ~21x forward P/E) price robust consumption consistent with ~4.0% unemployment. Rates (pricing 75–100bp of easing) price enough softening for Fed cuts. Credit (HY OAS 350–420bp) leans equity-side. generalist_01 demonstrates these are arithmetically inconsistent without an exogenous positive supply shock that both generalist_01 and generalist_02 agree isn't materializing (AI productivity lag, confidence 8 in KB). equity_analyst_02's reverse DCF reinforces: current valuations require nominal revenue growth of ~6–7% for 10 years, which requires either above-target inflation (contradicts multiples) or heroic productivity (unproven). pair_0 called this "the most actionable insight" and pair_1 praised the reverse DCF as "the strongest valuation argument."

### 13. Stock-bond correlation regime is determined by labor market trajectory
**Confidence: 7/10** | **Agents:** generalist_01 (7), rates_strategist_02 (implied)

Tight labor + sticky inflation → positive stock-bond price correlation (diversification fails, stagflationary regime). Loosening labor → negative correlation (bonds hedge equities, disinflationary regime). generalist_01's formulation is clean and investable. pair_0 praised this as a novel contribution. The NAIRU uncertainty (HIGH_CONFIDENCE #1) complicates regime identification, but the framework itself is well-grounded in the monetary policy transmission literature.

### 14. Remote work has expanded effective labor supply for knowledge-economy roles (direction certain, magnitude unknown)
**Confidence: 5/10** | **Agents:** All 8 agents address this; confidence ranges 5–6/10

Every agent agrees remote work expands the geographic talent pool for knowledge work and exerts disinflationary pressure on white-collar wages. generalist_02's offshoring analogy (1990s expansion of tradeable labor supply) is instructive conceptually. **Confidence capped at 5** because: (a) generalist_02's "5–15%" quantification was refuted in pair_0 as false precision; (b) equity_analyst_01's "permanent structural tailwind" was refuted in pair_1 by equity_analyst_02's competitive-erosion argument; (c) return-to-office counter-trends are real; (d) challenger_02 correctly notes the measurement confounds make confident claims in either direction overconfidence. Direction: high confidence. Magnitude: genuinely unknowable at current data horizons.

### 15. US labor market tightness is the primary driver of the dollar cycle and EM capital flows
**Confidence: 8/10** | **Agents:** fx_strategist_01 (8), fx_strategist_02 (9)

Both FX specialists converge through different channels: fx_strategist_01 via rate differentials and the "US exceptionalism" narrative (DXY–employment-surprise correlation ~0.65), fx_strategist_02 via the cost-of-external-funding channel (US 2Y yield–EM flow correlation ~–0.7). pair_2 confirmed: together they trace the full transmission chain (US labor data → Fed pricing → dollar → EM flows). The mechanism is well-established across multiple cycles.

### 16. SBC is a systematic, underpriced earnings quality degradation tied to labor market tightness
**Confidence: 7/10** | **Agents:** equity_analyst_02 (9) — single agent, but data-grounded and unrefuted

The SBC gap widened from ~$8/share pre-COVID to ~$14–16/share, representing 1.5–2.0x P/E turn adjustment at the index level. Tech-sector SBC runs 8–15% of revenue. This is verifiable from public filings. pair_1 called it equity_analyst_02's "strongest unique contribution." **Confidence docked from 9 to 7** solely because only one agent makes this claim and no cross-validation occurred — but the data is public and the logic is mechanically sound.

### 17. EM formal/informal sector bifurcation renders DM Phillips Curve frameworks misleading for EM
**Confidence: 7/10** | **Agents:** fx_strategist_02 (8) — single agent, but well-documented

Informal sector comprises 40–80% of employment in India, Indonesia, Nigeria, and much of Latin America. Monetary policy transmits poorly to informal wages; official unemployment understates true slack. Turkey's 2021–23 episode illustrated masking of overheating. pair_2 praised this as "genuinely important and routinely ignored by sell-side macro research." ILO and World Bank documentation is extensive. Docked to 7 for single-agent sourcing.

### 18. Commodity-EM labor market procyclicality amplifies terms-of-trade shocks into macro crises
**Confidence: 8/10** | **Agents:** fx_strategist_02 (9), confirmed in pair_2 debate

Chile (copper → wage growth → REER appreciation → 38% USD/CLP reversal), Colombia (oil), Nigeria (oil) all document the pattern. pair_2 called this "one of the most robust empirical regularities in EM macro." The labor market is the amplification mechanism — it's not just the commodity price but the wage and fiscal rigidity that makes reversals painful. High confidence due to extensive historical evidence.

### 19. The Beveridge curve has not fully normalized, implying residual matching inefficiency
**Confidence: 5/10** | **Agents:** equity_analyst_02 (6), fx_strategist_01 (6), generalist_01 (6), generalist_02 (implied)

The vacancy-to-unemployment ratio at ~1.1–1.2 vs. pre-pandemic ~1.0 is a factual observation. But pair_0 debate partially refuted its use as a "leading indicator" — it's a level observation with contested interpretation (structural mismatch? measurement artifact? ghost postings?). Whether it implies a permanent NAIRU shift upward by 0.3–0.5pp (equity_analyst_02) or is still normalizing (generalist_02) is unresolved. Moderate confidence on the observation, low confidence on the inference.

### 20. Services dominance (~85% nonfarm payrolls) makes labor cost the deterministic driver of aggregate profitability
**Confidence: 7/10** | **Agents:** equity_analyst_02 (8), equity_analyst_01 (implied via sectoral dispersion)

Structural and well-documented in BLS data. Manufacturing is <10% of S&P 500 by weight. Traditional goods-sector margin frameworks (commodity inputs, factory utilization) apply to a shrinking share of the economy. pair_1 confirmed convergence. The only caveat: AI/automation could shift this faster than the gradual pace assumed.

## LOW_CONFIDENCE

### 21. Sectoral wage dispersion (services ~4.5–5% vs. goods ~3–3.5%) creates a within-quadrant factor rotation signal
**Confidence: 4/10** | **Agent:** equity_analyst_01 (7) — uncorroborated

The observation is grounded in Atlanta Fed and ECI data. The logical chain to factor rotation (quality/low-labor-intensity outperforms when dispersion is wide) is plausible. But no other agent addresses this as a systematic signal, and the alpha magnitude from this single variable is uncertain. Needs corroboration.

### 22. Exchange rate channel paradox: US rate hikes may support domestic labor demand on net via strong dollar terms-of-trade effect
**Confidence: 4/10** | **Agent:** rates_strategist_02 (5) — theoretically grounded but hard to measure

Mundell-Fleming logic is sound: strong dollar → cheap imports → consumer real purchasing power → sustained labor demand. But manufacturing is only ~8% of employment, limiting the export-competitiveness offset. The net employment effect is genuinely hard to measure. Novel and worth investigating, but the magnitude may be negligible.

### 23. Remote work creates "Dutch Disease" dynamics in small EM economies
**Confidence: 4/10** | **Agent:** fx_strategist_02 (5) — conceptually sound, empirically nascent

USD-denominated income to EM-based remote workers bids up skilled labor costs, crowding out local tradables. Observable in Colombia, Georgia, Costa Rica. pair_2 praised fx_strategist_02's nuance (seeing both the positive flow and negative wage-inflation effects). But data is poor and materiality varies enormously by country size.

### 24. AI displacement + immigration restriction creates a segmented labor market (white-collar excess, blue-collar shortage)
**Confidence: 4/10** | **Agents:** generalist_01 (Open Question 8), equity_analyst_01 (Open Question 4) — flagged but undeveloped

pair_0 identified this as "a genuinely novel observation that neither agent fully develops." The composition effect has direct inflation implications: services inflation (in-person labor scarcity) stays sticky even as headline unemployment is stable. Potentially important but entirely speculative at this stage.

### 25. Gig/contractor W-2 to 1099 reclassification flatters operating margins without changing economic costs
**Confidence: 3/10** | **Agent:** equity_analyst_02 (Open Question 6) — identified but unquantified

An earnings quality issue that could be significant but lacks any quantification. No other agent addresses it.

### 26. Japan's Shunto wage dynamics are "the most important structural FX story in G10"
**Confidence: 5/10** | **Agent:** fx_strategist_01 (7) — bold, specific, non-consensus

If Japanese wage growth sustains at 5%+, BoJ normalization narrows USD/JPY carry structurally. pair_2 noted this is "the kind of non-consensus conviction that either looks brilliant or foolish in 12 months." Sustainability depends on whether consumption validates the wage gains.

## REFUTED

### R1. Sacrifice ratio of 4–5x / unemployment must reach 5.5–6.5% for 2% inflation
**Originating agent:** rates_strategist_02 (7/10)
**Killed by:** challenger_02 in pair_3 debate
**Reasoning:** Depends on a stable Phillips Curve slope that challenger_02 demonstrates has been unstable for 50 years. The 2023–24 "immaculate disinflation" (inflation falling without unemployment rising) directly falsifies the implied model. This is precisely the false-precision trap challenger_02's framework warns against. The directional claim (last-mile disinflation is harder) survives; the specific numbers do not.

### R2. Sahm Rule has "perfect recession track record"
**Originating agent:** rates_strategist_02 (7/10)
**Killed by:** challenger_02 in pair_3 debate
**Reasoning:** The Sahm Rule triggered briefly in mid-2024 without a recession following. This refutes the specific claim of perfection. The rule remains highly informative, but rates_strategist_02's conclusion that "engineering a gentle rise to 4.5% has no historical precedent of success" is substantially weakened. The broader point (unemployment dynamics are nonlinear) survives in diminished form.

### R3. ERP is distorted by 30–50bp / S&P 500 is 5–8% overvalued due to misleading labor composition
**Originating agent:** equity_analyst_01 (5/10)
**Killed by:** pair_1 debate
**Reasoning:** The specific quantification is model-dependent — the ERP is not directly observable, and converting compositional labor deterioration into basis points requires a model equity_analyst_01 doesn't specify. The directional observation (compositional signals are weaker than headline) is valid; the number is not. Markets incorporate broader information than the headline unemployment rate.

### R4. Remote work supports "higher equilibrium P/E multiples" for knowledge-economy indices
**Originating agent:** equity_analyst_01 (6/10)
**Killed by:** equity_analyst_02 in pair_1 debate
**Reasoning:** Arbitrage erodes as participants exploit it. Competitive dynamics compress the geographic wage differential over time. equity_analyst_01 confuses a one-time margin step-up with a change in the growth rate of earnings. P/E multiples are driven by growth rates and discount rates, not transitory cost reductions.

### R5. Dollar cycle peaks 6–9 months after labor market deterioration begins (reliable trading signal)
**Originating agent:** fx_strategist_01 (7/10)
**Killed by:** pair_2 debate
**Reasoning:** Four data points (1985, 2002, 2017, 2022) in radically different macro regimes is not a pattern — it's anecdote collection. The 6–9 month range is wide enough to be nearly unfalsifiable as a trading signal. 3 months of timing error in FX can wipe out the entire move.

### R6. Remote work as "dollar recycling" reducing current account adjustment pressure
**Originating agent:** fx_strategist_01 (5/10)
**Killed by:** pair_2 debate
**Reasoning:** Cross-border remote work payments *do* appear in BOP data (under "other business services"). The share of US jobs that are both remote-capable and filled by foreign workers at significantly lower wages is a tiny fraction of total employment. Calling this "dollar recycling" conflates it with sovereign-scale mechanisms; the magnitude is noise in a $26T economy.

### R7. Immigration double-counting critique (immigration "cannot simultaneously" raise potential GDP and loosen the labor market)
**Originating agent:** challenger_02 (7/10)
**Killed by:** pair_3 debate
**Reasoning:** These aren't contradictory — they're the same mechanism from different angles. More workers raise potential GDP *by* increasing labor supply, which loosens the labor market relative to the same demand level. challenger_02 correctly identifies that analysts invoke whichever framing suits them, but the underlying economics is internally consistent.

## KEY_DISAGREEMENTS

### D1. Current ULC growth rate: ~2.0–2.5% vs. ~3.5–4.5%
**Agents:** equity_analyst_01 vs. equity_analyst_02
**Status:** Unresolved factual discrepancy about the same BLS series. pair_1 estimates the true figure splits the difference at ~2.5–3.0%. This is the single most important number to resolve — it determines whether margins are "comfortable" (equity_analyst_01) or under pressure (equity_analyst_02), and both agents call ULC the "most important" equity variable.
**Resolution path:** Pull current vintage BLS nonfarm business sector ULC data and specify the measurement period.

### D2. Is the labor market deteriorating beneath the surface (overvaluation) or tighter than it appears (margin risk)?
**Agents:** equity_analyst_01 (deteriorating → ERP distortion) vs. equity_analyst_02 (tighter → NAIRU shifted up)
**Status:** Nearly opposite diagnoses. equity_analyst_01 says markets are too optimistic about labor health; equity_analyst_02 says markets are too complacent about wage pressure. pair_1 found equity_analyst_02 more internally consistent. Both can be partially true (deteriorating *and* still tight enough for wage pressure in a Beveridge-curve-shifted world), but they lead to different portfolio implications (defensive vs. pricing-power tilt).
**Resolution path:** Requires clarifying whether the Beveridge curve shift is structural or transitory.

### D3. Immigration reversal: clear near-term shock vs. recency bias about policy persistence
**Agents:** rates_strategist_02 / generalist_01 (high-conviction near-term shock) vs. challenger_02 (recency bias — policy reverses cyclically)
**Status:** pair_3 confirmed both are partially right. Near-term (6–18 months), the reversal is real — executive orders are being enforced now. Medium-term (2+ years), treating current tightening as permanent extrapolates from a single policy moment.
**Resolution path:** Monitor enforcement data and border encounters; establish base rates for immigration policy duration across administrations.

### D4. Remote work: competed-away arbitrage vs. still-expanding structural shift
**Agents:** equity_analyst_02 ("largely played out") vs. equity_analyst_01 ("structurally positive"), with generalist_02 at 5/10
**Status:** pair_1 found equity_analyst_02's directional argument (competitive erosion) stronger, but partially refuted the "largely played out" timing call — adoption is still spreading to mid-market firms and beyond tech. The truth is likely in between: a one-time margin step-up that has partially but not fully played out.
**Resolution path:** Needs 2–3 more years of wage data in remote-feasible vs. in-person occupations.

### D5. Is the Beveridge curve shift permanent (structural NAIRU higher) or still normalizing?
**Agents:** equity_analyst_02 (NAIRU now 4.2–4.5%) vs. generalist_02 (still normalizing) vs. challenger_02 (unknowable)
**Status:** Directly determines whether current unemployment (~4.0–4.2%) represents meaningful slack or is already at/below the natural rate. The answer governs inflation forecasts, Fed reaction function expectations, and the probability of wage re-acceleration.
**Resolution path:** Track quarterly Beveridge curve position; the rate of normalization (or stabilization) over the next 2–4 quarters will be diagnostic.

### D6. Does the private sector financial surplus (2–3% GDP) insulate against Sahm Rule dynamics?
**Agents:** rates_strategist_02 (Open Question 6) — raised but unanswered
**Status:** If households and firms in surplus can absorb temporary unemployment shocks without the feedback loop activating, the Sahm Rule's predictive power is weakened this cycle. Untested hypothesis.
**Resolution path:** Requires modeling the interaction between sectoral financial balances and labor market nonlinearity — likely a topic for future iteration.
