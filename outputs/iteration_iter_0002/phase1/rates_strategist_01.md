# Economic Growth Models & Cycle Positioning — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The yield curve is the most reliable real-time encoding of the market's composite growth model, embedding expectations about both the level of growth (through the expected path of short rates) and the uncertainty around growth (through term premium).** Decomposing yields into these two components reveals whether the market is pricing a change in the growth trajectory or merely repricing the risk around an unchanged trajectory — a distinction that raw GDP prints cannot make.

2. **The 3-month/10-year spread remains the single best market-based recession predictor, but its signal has been structurally degraded in the post-COVID regime by term premium distortions from fiscal supply, QT, and basis trade positioning.** The spread inverted in late 2022 and remained inverted through much of 2023-2024, yet no recession materialized within the historical 12-18 month window — the first potential failure in its post-1970 track record. The explanation is that ~50-80bp of term premium elevation in the 10y artificially steepened the curve, masking what would have been a deeper and more persistent inversion on an expectations-only basis.

3. **The 5-year/5-year forward rate has repriced from ~2.5% (2019) to ~3.8-4.2% (2025-2026), encoding a structural upward revision to the market's estimate of long-run potential growth plus equilibrium inflation (r* + π*).** This is the yield curve's verdict on whether potential GDP has permanently shifted higher — and it says yes, by roughly 100-150bp in nominal terms versus the pre-COVID regime.

4. **The output gap — the difference between actual and potential GDP — is best tracked in real time through the yield curve's pricing of the Fed's reaction function, not through official CBO estimates that are revised with multi-year lags.** When the curve prices a terminal Fed funds rate above the estimated neutral rate, it is implicitly signaling a positive output gap (overheating); when it prices below neutral, a negative gap (slack). The current forward curve pricing of Fed funds settling around 3.5-4.0% versus a neutral rate debate centered on 2.5-3.5% suggests the market sees the output gap as approximately closed or slightly positive.

5. **Business cycle phase identification via the yield curve follows a predictable morphological sequence: (a) late-cycle flattening → (b) inversion → (c) early-recession bull steepening → (d) recovery bear steepening → (e) mid-cycle stability.** The current curve shape — dis-inverted but with an unusually high term premium component — places the economy in an ambiguous zone between late-cycle and mid-cycle that does not cleanly map to any historical analogue.

6. **Potential GDP revisions from AI-driven productivity gains and immigration-driven labor supply expansion should manifest in the yield curve as a rising neutral rate (higher 5y5y forwards) combined with compressed term premium (lower growth uncertainty), producing a curve that is higher in level but flatter in slope.** This is the "good steepening" vs. "bad steepening" distinction: productivity-driven steepening compresses term premium, while fiscal-supply-driven steepening expands it.

7. **The traditional leading indicators (Conference Board LEI, ISM new orders, housing starts, initial claims) have exhibited degraded predictive power in the post-COVID regime, but the yield curve components — specifically the term premium cycle and the eurodollar/SOFR strip — retain predictive information that these indicators miss.** The SOFR futures strip, by pricing the path of overnight rates 2-3 years forward, embeds information about expected growth that is forward-looking in a way that coincident survey data cannot match.

8. **The Sahm Rule (recession when the 3-month moving average of the unemployment rate rises 0.5pp above its 12-month low) triggered in mid-2024 without a subsequent recession, because immigration-driven labor supply expansion raised the unemployment rate without reflecting demand deterioration.** The yield curve correctly identified this as a supply-side event rather than a demand-driven recession: the curve did not exhibit the bull-steepening pattern that accompanies genuine demand collapses. This validates curve decomposition over single-indicator recession rules when the growth model itself is shifting.

9. **The maturity structure of Treasury issuance acts as an implicit government growth forecast: a Treasury that front-loads bill issuance signals confidence in near-term growth (willing to accept rollover risk), while a shift toward coupon issuance signals concern about future financing conditions.** The Treasury's decision to increase coupon auction sizes through 2023-2025 while maintaining elevated bill shares reflects a growth model that expects sustained nominal GDP growth sufficient to service higher coupon costs.

10. **The current cycle is best characterized as a "high-pressure equilibrium" — actual GDP running at or above potential, sustained by fiscal deficits of 6-7% of GDP, with the yield curve encoding this via elevated term premium and a forward curve that prices the Fed funds rate settling well above pre-COVID neutral estimates.** This is not a traditional late-cycle overheating (which would show as aggressive curve flattening from rate hikes) nor a mid-cycle expansion (which would show as moderate steepening with low term premium). It is a fiscally sustained growth regime that the term structure has never had to price before at this scale.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Yield Curve as Composite Growth Model

The 10-year Treasury yield can be expressed as: Y(10) = E[r̄] + TP(10), where E[r̄] is the average expected short rate over 10 years and TP(10) is the 10-year term premium. The expected short rate path is itself a function of the market's growth model: higher expected growth → higher expected neutral rate → higher expected short rates → higher E[r̄]. Term premium captures the uncertainty around that growth path — higher growth volatility, greater model uncertainty about potential GDP, or more dispersed recession probabilities all raise TP. The Adrian-Crump-Moench (ACM) model from the NY Fed and the Kim-Wright model from the Fed Board provide competing decompositions. In 2021, when the market was revising growth expectations sharply higher (post-vaccine, fiscal stimulus), the 10y yield rose ~80bp, with ACM attributing roughly 50bp to rising rate expectations and 30bp to rising term premium — the market was both raising its growth forecast and becoming more uncertain about it. This dual-signal is invisible in raw yields.

### Claim 2: 3m10y Spread Degradation

The 3m10y spread has predicted every US recession since 1970 with a 12-18 month lead, based on the logic that inversion means the market expects the Fed to cut rates in the future (because growth will weaken). The spread inverted in October 2022 and remained inverted for approximately 22 months. No recession followed within the standard window. The key insight is that the 3m10y spread mixes two signals: (a) expected rate path (the growth signal) and (b) term premium (the risk/supply signal). If we strip out term premium using ACM estimates, the "expectations-only" 3m10y spread was inverted even more deeply and for longer — implying the growth signal was genuinely negative. But the positive term premium in the 10y (~50-80bp from fiscal supply and QT) partially offset the inversion in the raw spread, making the raw signal look less alarming than the underlying expectations signal. The recession did not materialize because fiscal deficits of 6-7% of GDP provided a demand floor (the Kalecki profit channel identified in iteration 1). The leading indicator didn't fail — the growth model shifted underneath it.

### Claim 3: 5y5y Forward as Potential GDP Proxy

The 5y5y forward rate is the market's pricing of the 5-year rate, 5 years hence. It strips out cyclical policy expectations and approximates: 5y5y ≈ r* + π* + TP(forward), where r* is the real neutral rate, π* is long-run expected inflation, and TP(forward) is the forward term premium. Pre-COVID, 5y5y forwards were stable at 2.3-2.7%, consistent with r* ≈ 0.5%, π* ≈ 2.0%, and modest forward term premium. The repricing to 3.8-4.2% by 2025-2026 decomposes (roughly) into: r* repriced to ~1.0-1.5% (reflecting potential GDP revision upward), π* repriced to ~2.3-2.5% (reflecting stickier inflation regime), forward TP expanded by ~50bp (fiscal supply). TIPS-derived real 5y5y forwards have similarly repriced from ~0.5% to ~1.5-2.0%, confirming that a significant portion of the repricing is real, not inflation-driven. This is the yield curve's way of saying: potential GDP growth is higher than we thought pre-COVID.

### Claim 4: Output Gap via Fed Reaction Function Pricing

The Fed's Taylor Rule links the funds rate to the output gap and inflation gap: FFR = r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*). The forward curve prices the terminal Fed funds rate at approximately 3.5-4.0% (the "landing zone" implied by SOFR futures 2-3 years out). If we assume π ≈ 2.5% and π* = 2.0%, and r* = 1.0-1.5%, the Taylor Rule would prescribe: FFR = 1.25 + 2.5 + 0.5(0.5) + 0.5(output gap) = 4.0 + 0.5(output gap). For the forward curve pricing of ~3.75% to be consistent, the output gap must be approximately zero to slightly negative. This suggests the market's growth model prices actual GDP as roughly equal to potential — neither overheating nor recessionary. The CBO's output gap estimate, by contrast, is typically revised by 1-2pp even years after the fact, making real-time yield curve inference more timely.

### Claim 5: Curve Morphology and Cycle Phase

Historical yield curve shapes map to cycle phases with reasonable consistency:
- **Late cycle**: 2s10s flattens toward zero or inverts as the Fed tightens above neutral. The curve was deeply inverted (2s10s at -100bp) in mid-2023, the deepest inversion since 1981.
- **Recession onset**: Bull steepening — the front end rallies (rate cuts priced) faster than the long end (term premium may be rising on fiscal/uncertainty). The 2s10s typically re-steepens 50-100bp in the 6 months around recession start.
- **Early recovery**: Bear steepening — long end sells off on growth optimism while the front end remains anchored at low rates. 2s10s widens to 100-250bp.
- **Mid-cycle**: Moderate positive slope (50-150bp), stable, low term premium. The "Goldilocks" curve.

The current curve (2s10s at approximately +30 to +50bp as of early 2026) superficially resembles early-to-mid cycle. But the term premium decomposition reveals this is not a typical mid-cycle curve: term premium accounts for the majority of the positive slope, whereas in a genuine mid-cycle (e.g., 2015-2017), the slope was primarily driven by rate expectations (the market pricing a gradual hiking cycle). The current curve is a "term premium steepener" rather than an "expectations steepener" — a distinction with very different growth implications.

### Claim 6: AI/Immigration Potential GDP Revision in the Curve

A genuine upward revision to potential GDP from productivity gains (AI) or labor supply expansion (immigration) should produce specific yield curve signatures:
- **Higher 5y5y forwards**: Because r* rises with potential growth. Observed: 5y5y has risen ~150bp from pre-COVID levels. Consistent.
- **Lower term premium (eventually)**: Because higher productivity growth reduces output volatility and narrows the distribution of future growth outcomes. The historical analogue is the late 1990s, when the 10y term premium compressed even as yields rose, because the productivity boom reduced macro uncertainty. Not yet observed: term premium remains elevated due to fiscal supply dominance.
- **Flatter expectations curve**: Because higher potential growth allows the Fed to hold rates higher for longer without causing recession, compressing the expected rate path's slope. Partially observed: the SOFR strip is relatively flat 2+ years out.

The key tension: if AI/immigration are genuinely raising potential GDP, the yield curve should eventually transition from "high level + high term premium" (fiscal supply dominance) to "high level + low term premium" (productivity confidence). This transition has not yet occurred, suggesting the market has not fully endorsed the structural productivity story.

### Claim 7: Leading Indicator Degradation

The Conference Board Leading Economic Index (LEI) has been declining since early 2022 and has given a continuous "recession imminent" signal for over 3 years without a recession materializing. The ISM Manufacturing PMI spent 2022-2024 below 50 (contractionary territory) while overall GDP growth remained robust. These failures reflect a growth model that has shifted: manufacturing and goods-sector indicators no longer represent the growth engine when 75%+ of the economy is services, and when fiscal transfers sustain demand independently of credit conditions.

The SOFR futures strip, by contrast, aggregates all available information into a single forward-looking price. When the strip steepened (priced rate cuts) in late 2023, it was pricing a growth slowdown that partially materialized as a services-sector moderation in early 2024. When the strip flattened (priced "higher for longer") in early 2025, it correctly anticipated the sustained growth from fiscal support. The strip's advantage is that it is set by participants with capital at risk, rather than by survey respondents with no skin in the game.

### Claim 8: Sahm Rule Failure and Curve Validation

The Sahm Rule indicator crossed the 0.5pp threshold in July 2024 when the unemployment rate rose from a 3.4% low to approximately 4.1%. However, the rise was driven substantially by labor force expansion (immigration adding ~1-1.5 million workers to the labor force beyond trend) rather than by job losses. The yield curve read this correctly: in a demand-driven recession, the curve bull-steepens aggressively as the market prices emergency rate cuts. In July 2024, the curve exhibited only mild bull-flattening (the front end rallied modestly on expectations of a shallower rate path), and term premium barely moved. This is the signature of a supply-side labor market shift, not a demand collapse. Within 3 months, the unemployment rate stabilized and the Sahm signal proved to be a false positive. The curve's decomposition — showing stable term premium and modest expectations adjustment — was the more accurate growth signal.

### Claim 9: Treasury Maturity Structure as Growth Signal

The Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee (TBAC) and Treasury's Office of Debt Management choose the issuance mix based on their economic outlook and debt management objectives. In 2023-2024, the Treasury increased coupon auction sizes across all tenors (2y, 3y, 5y, 7y, 10y, 20y, 30y) by a cumulative ~23% while maintaining an elevated share of bill issuance (~20-22% of outstanding vs. the TBAC-recommended 15-20%). This "barbell" approach — heavy at both the short and long ends — signals confidence that nominal GDP growth will remain strong enough to service higher coupon costs, while using bills as a flexible buffer. The elevated bill share also suppresses term premium at the front end (bills have near-zero duration risk), distorting the curve slope. If the Treasury were to normalize the bill share toward 15%, the shift to coupon issuance would add approximately 20-30bp of term premium at the 5-10y point, all else equal.

### Claim 10: High-Pressure Equilibrium

The current macro regime — GDP growth of 2.5-3.0%, unemployment at 3.9-4.2%, core PCE inflation of 2.5-2.8%, and fiscal deficits of 6-7% of GDP — does not map to any standard business cycle phase. It is neither classic late-cycle (no credit excess visible, no aggressive Fed tightening), nor classic mid-cycle (deficits are far too large, inflation too sticky), nor early-cycle (no recent recession, no slack in the labor market). The yield curve reflects this ambiguity: the 2s10s slope is mildly positive (not the deep steepening of early-cycle or the inversion of late-cycle), but the level of yields is historically high and term premium is elevated.

This regime most closely resembles a "high-pressure economy" as described by Yellen (2016) and Okun (1973) — an economy deliberately run above potential to draw in marginal workers and stimulate investment. But historically, high-pressure economies were engineered by monetary policy (low rates), whereas this one is sustained by fiscal policy (large deficits). The yield curve has never had to price this combination at this scale. The term premium elevation is the market's way of demanding compensation for the uncertainty inherent in an unprecedented growth model.

## Confidence Assessment

1. **Yield curve as composite growth model**: 9/10. This is the foundational framework of rates analysis. The decomposition math is well-established; the only uncertainty is in the specific model estimates (ACM vs. Kim-Wright).

2. **3m10y spread degradation**: 8/10. The factual record (longest inversion without recession) is clear. The term premium explanation is well-motivated but the counterfactual — that a recession *would* have occurred absent fiscal support — is unprovable. An alternative explanation is simply that the spread's predictive power has always been overstated by a small sample (8 recessions).

3. **5y5y forward as potential GDP proxy**: 7/10. The repricing is factually clear, but the decomposition into r*, π*, and forward TP is model-dependent and imprecise. The TIPS-confirmed real rate repricing adds confidence, but TIPS liquidity premia can distort the signal by 20-40bp.

4. **Output gap via reaction function pricing**: 6/10. The logic is sound, but the Fed's own uncertainty about r* (the range of FOMC dot plot estimates spans 100bp+) means the curve-implied output gap inherits that uncertainty. This is a useful framework but not a precise measurement.

5. **Curve morphology and cycle phase**: 8/10. The historical patterns are well-documented. The current ambiguity (term premium steepener vs. expectations steepener) is a genuine analytical challenge, not a weakness of the framework.

6. **AI/immigration potential GDP revision in the curve**: 5/10. This is the most speculative claim. The predicted yield curve signatures are theoretically sound, but we lack the data to confirm whether the 5y5y repricing is genuinely driven by r* revision versus inflation/fiscal premia. The AI productivity hypothesis is far from proven and the yield curve cannot distinguish between competing explanations for its own repricing.

7. **Leading indicator degradation**: 7/10. The LEI and ISM failures are factual. The claim that SOFR strip retains predictive power is supported by recent experience but has a short out-of-sample record in the post-COVID regime. Historical back-tests of eurodollar strips show reasonable predictive power (Piazzesi & Swanson 2008), but the regime change caveat applies.

8. **Sahm Rule failure and curve validation**: 8/10. The July 2024 episode is a clean natural experiment. The curve's non-reaction correctly diagnosed a supply-side event. The only caveat is n=1 — a single episode does not establish that the curve will always outperform the Sahm Rule.

9. **Treasury maturity structure as growth signal**: 6/10. Debt management decisions are influenced by many factors beyond the growth outlook (market functioning, cost minimization, political considerations). Interpreting issuance choices as a growth forecast is an inference, not a direct signal.

10. **High-pressure equilibrium characterization**: 7/10. The descriptive claim about the current regime is high-confidence. The analytical claim that it is "unprecedented" and "unstable" is a judgment call. The fiscal-monetary combination is genuinely unusual by post-WWII standards, but the term "unprecedented" can be overapplied.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Labor Market Dynamics (labor_market_dynamics)
The yield curve's growth model is deeply entangled with labor market signals. The Phillips Curve — the relationship between labor market slack and inflation — determines how the Fed responds to labor data, which in turn shapes the expected rate path embedded in the curve. If the labor market is structurally tighter than pre-COVID (due to demographic shifts, reduced immigration, or changed preferences), the yield curve should price a higher terminal rate for any given level of GDP growth. The Sahm Rule episode (Claim 8) demonstrates that labor market indicators and yield curve signals can diverge when the labor supply growth model is shifting. The curve's decomposition provides a cross-check on whether labor market changes are demand-driven (curve bull-steepens) or supply-driven (curve stable).

### Equity Cycles (equity_cycles)
The equity discount rate is anchored by the risk-free rate, which is itself the yield curve's output. A permanently higher 5y5y forward (Claim 3) implies a permanently higher equity discount rate, which should lower equilibrium P/E ratios unless offset by higher earnings growth. The current equity market appears to price the higher earnings growth (from AI productivity) without fully incorporating the higher discount rate (from elevated term premium) — the same equity-rates divergence identified in iteration 1 (Claim 10 of the risk appetite analysis). The growth model question is whether AI productivity gains are large enough to simultaneously justify both higher r* and higher equity valuations. The yield curve's term premium signal — still elevated, not compressed — suggests the market has not reached conviction on this.

### Monetary Policy (monetary_policy)
The yield curve is the primary transmission mechanism for monetary policy, but it also constrains monetary policy by revealing the market's growth expectations. When the curve prices terminal Fed funds at 3.5-4.0%, it effectively tells the Fed that the market expects growth to sustain rates at that level. If the Fed disagrees (believes growth will weaken and rates should be lower), it faces a communication challenge. The "dot plot vs. market" gap — the divergence between FOMC participants' rate projections and market-implied paths — is a real-time measure of disagreement about the growth model between the central bank and the market.

### Fiscal Policy & Deficit Dynamics
The fiscal impulse trajectory (from the knowledge base, confidence 9/10) is the dominant driver of the current growth model. The yield curve's term premium directly prices fiscal supply expectations. If fiscal consolidation were to reduce deficits by 2pp+ of GDP (as identified in the knowledge base as the threshold for eroding the Kalecki profit income floor), the yield curve would likely: (a) see term premium decline (less supply), (b) see rate expectations decline (less growth), (c) bull-flatten as growth expectations fall more than term premium. This is the curve signature of a growth model downshift driven by fiscal withdrawal.

### Risk Appetite Regimes (from iter_0001)
The five-regime risk appetite model from iteration 1 is directly connected to the growth model through the output gap. In Regime 1 (complacent carry), the output gap is typically near zero or positive, and the growth model is stable. The transition to Regime 2 (early stress recognition) is often triggered by a growth model revision — when the market begins to price a negative output gap emerging. The yield curve's term premium decomposition (this analysis) and the risk appetite regime model (iteration 1) should be read as complementary frameworks: the curve tells you *what* growth expectations are changing, while the regime model tells you *how* risk appetite will respond to those changes.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the yield curve distinguish between an AI-driven r* increase and a fiscal-supply-driven term premium increase in real time?** Both raise 5y5y forwards, but they have opposite implications for growth sustainability. If the 5y5y repricing is 70% fiscal/30% AI productivity, the growth model is more fragile than if the ratio is reversed. TIPS breakevens partially help but are themselves contaminated by liquidity premia. Is there a market-based decomposition that separates these channels?

2. **What is the "right" level of term premium for the current fiscal trajectory?** If the US is structurally running 6-7% deficits with $2tn+ annual issuance, historical benchmarks for term premium (0-50bp in the QE era, 100-200bp in the 1990s-2000s) may be inadequate guides. Is the current 50-80bp ACM estimate too low (not yet reflecting the full fiscal supply), or is it pricing in an expectation of future fiscal consolidation that may not materialize?

3. **Has the post-COVID regime permanently broken the 3m10y spread's recession prediction record, or did fiscal deficits simply delay the recession signal?** If the fiscal support is withdrawn (through consolidation or forced austerity), does the "delayed recession" materialize — and if so, does the 3m10y's original inversion date retroactively validate the indicator with a much longer lead time?

4. **How should the yield curve framework incorporate the possibility that potential GDP has become more volatile, not just higher?** If AI adoption creates a wider distribution of potential growth outcomes (ranging from transformative productivity gains to mass technological unemployment), the curve should price both a higher expected path and higher term premium. Is the current elevated term premium partially an expression of this potential GDP uncertainty?

5. **What is the feedback loop between yield curve pricing and actual growth outcomes?** If the curve prices higher-for-longer rates, this tightens financial conditions, which may slow growth, which may eventually cause the curve to price rate cuts. This reflexivity means the curve is not a passive observer of the growth model but an active participant in it. How much of the current growth resilience reflects the market's own pricing of sustained growth (easing financial conditions via a positive wealth effect), and how much reflects genuine underlying momentum?

6. **Does the term premium cycle lead or lag the business cycle?** If term premium peaks before recessions (because fiscal supply peaks late-cycle and flight-to-quality compresses it at recession onset), then the term premium cycle may itself be a leading indicator of growth turning points. The empirical evidence is mixed and the sample since QE began is small.

7. **How do we incorporate the reserve currency status of the dollar into our yield curve growth model?** Foreign demand for Treasuries (as safe/reserve assets) creates an exogenous demand component that compresses US term premium below what domestic fundamentals would warrant. If de-dollarization gradually reduces this demand, the yield curve's term premium rises structurally — but this reflects a change in global portfolio allocation, not a change in US growth. Separating the two is essential for using the curve as a growth signal.
