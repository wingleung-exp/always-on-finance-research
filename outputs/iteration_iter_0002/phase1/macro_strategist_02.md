

# Economic Growth Models & Cycle Positioning — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Economic growth is demand-determined, not supply-determined: potential GDP is an endogenous variable shaped by the history of actual demand, not an exogenous ceiling set by technology and demographics.** Mainstream growth models (Solow, endogenous growth) treat potential output as supply-side-determined and actual output as gravitating toward it. The Post-Keynesian position, drawing on Kaldor's cumulative causation and Setterfield's path-dependence work, holds that potential GDP *follows* actual GDP — prolonged demand deficiency permanently destroys capacity through hysteresis in labor skills, capital depreciation, and business formation. The output gap is therefore not a mean-reverting deviation but a path-dependent artifact of policy choices.

2. **The conventional output gap framework is both theoretically incoherent and empirically unreliable, and its use in policy calibration systematically biases toward premature austerity.** Real-time output gap estimates from the CBO, IMF, and central banks are revised by an average of 1-2 percentage points ex post. The 2008-2014 period is damning: real-time estimates showed a closing output gap that ex post turned out to be enormous. The Beveridge-Nelson decomposition, HP filter, and production function approaches all embed the assumption that supply is independent of demand — an assumption the Post-Keynesian tradition fundamentally rejects.

3. **Credit creation by the banking system, not prior saving or technological progress, is the proximate driver of economic growth cycles.** In an endogenous money economy, the sequence is: banks extend credit → deposits are created → expenditure occurs → income is generated → some portion is saved. This reverses the classical savings-investment causation. Business cycle fluctuations are therefore primarily driven by the willingness and ability of banks to create credit and of borrowers to take on debt — not by productivity shocks or intertemporal optimization as in Real Business Cycle or New Keynesian DSGE models.

4. **The current U.S. cycle (as of early 2026) is best characterized as a fiscally-sustained expansion with moderate but rising private sector financial fragility, not a supply-side productivity boom.** Real GDP growth of approximately 2-2.5% is being sustained primarily by (a) a federal fiscal deficit of 6-7% of GDP providing the income floor via the Kalecki profit equation, and (b) residual momentum from post-COVID pent-up demand and immigration-driven labor supply expansion. Claims that AI is already boosting measured productivity growth are premature — aggregate TFP growth remains modest, and the productivity gains visible in tech-sector output are not yet propagating to the broader economy.

5. **The Kalecki-Levy profit equation, not the Solow residual, is the correct framework for understanding what drives aggregate corporate earnings and therefore the viability of the current expansion.** Aggregate profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports − Household Saving + Dividends. This accounting identity reveals that the current profit cycle is overwhelmingly driven by the government deficit component. Any framework for cycle positioning that ignores this fiscal profit channel is building on sand.

6. **Godley-style sectoral balance analysis reveals that the current expansion is conditionally sustainable: it persists as long as the government sector continues to run deficits large enough to accommodate the private sector's desired net saving and the current account deficit.** The private sector financial balance is currently in modest surplus (approximately 2-3% of GDP), consistent with a hedge/early-speculative phase rather than the late-speculative/Ponzi positioning that preceded 2001 and 2008. This is a more resilient configuration than prior late-cycle periods, but it is entirely dependent on continued fiscal deficits.

7. **Leading economic indicators have become structurally less reliable in the post-COVID regime due to (a) the unprecedented fiscal transfer mechanism that disrupted traditional recessionary dynamics, (b) the sectoral rotation from goods to services creating conflicting signals, and (c) the immigration-driven labor supply shock that has altered the relationship between employment growth and demand pressure.** The yield curve inversion that began in 2022 has produced the longest "false positive" recession signal in the indicator's history — not because the model is broken, but because the massive fiscal deficit prevented the private sector income contraction that inversions normally predict.

8. **The AI capital expenditure cycle represents either a genuine Schumpeterian creative destruction episode that raises potential GDP, or a classic Minsky speculative investment boom that will end in overcapacity and debt distress — and the sectoral financial balances, not technology forecasting, are the correct diagnostic tool for distinguishing between these outcomes.** If AI capex is financed primarily from retained earnings and equity (hedge finance), it is more likely to be self-sustaining. If it is increasingly debt-financed with returns dependent on continued revenue growth acceleration (speculative/Ponzi finance), the Minsky framework predicts an eventual unwind regardless of the technology's ultimate productive potential.

9. **Immigration-driven labor supply expansion has temporarily raised the economy's growth capacity, but mainstream models that treat this as a permanent increase in potential GDP are making the same error as the late-1990s "new economy" optimists — confusing a transitory labor supply shift with a structural productivity transformation.** From a Post-Keynesian perspective, what matters is whether this labor supply expansion is matched by corresponding demand expansion (investment, government spending, credit creation) or whether it creates deflationary wage pressure that ultimately undermines aggregate demand.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Demand-Determined Growth and Hysteresis:**
The foundational argument draws on Kaldor's growth laws (1966), particularly the Verdoorn relationship between output growth and productivity growth, which implies that productivity is *endogenous to demand*. Setterfield (2002, 2006) formalized this in path-dependent growth models where the "natural rate" of growth adjusts to the actual rate via induced technical change and labor force participation effects. The empirical evidence is striking: DeLong and Summers (2012) demonstrated that countries pursuing aggressive fiscal austerity during 2010-2013 experienced permanent output losses, with post-crisis GDP trajectories falling below pre-crisis trends by 10-15% with no sign of reversion. Ball (2014) showed that potential GDP estimates were systematically revised *downward* following the Great Recession — not because of a supply shock, but because demand weakness destroyed capacity. This is precisely what the hysteresis/supermultiplier models of Girardi and Pariboni (2016) and Freitas and Serrano (2015) predict: autonomous demand growth — driven by government spending, exports, and credit — determines the long-run growth path, and capacity adjusts to demand, not vice versa.

**Claim 2 — Output Gap Measurement Failure:**
Orphanides and van Norden (2002) provided the definitive empirical demolition of real-time output gap estimation, showing that revisions were so large as to make the estimates useless for real-time policy. The problem is not merely statistical — it is conceptual. The HP filter extracts a trend from actual data, meaning that if actual output falls, the estimated "potential" output falls with it — a circular reasoning problem. The production function approach requires estimating the NAIRU, which itself has been shown to be wildly unstable (Staiger, Stock, and Watson, 1997, found a 95% confidence interval of approximately 4.3% to 7.3% for the U.S. NAIRU — spanning the entire range of policy-relevant variation). The Congressional Budget Office's real-time estimates of potential GDP growth in 2006-2007 showed no indication of the massive output collapse to come, and by 2010 had retroactively revised potential *down* to close the apparent gap — essentially defining away the demand shortfall. From a Post-Keynesian perspective, this is not a measurement problem to be solved with better econometrics but a conceptual error embedded in the framework.

**Claim 3 — Credit Creation as Growth Driver:**
Endogenous money theory, formalized by Moore (1988) and developed by Lavoie (2014), demonstrates that in modern banking systems, loans create deposits — not the reverse. The Bank of England (2014, McLeay et al.) officially confirmed this mechanism, stating that "the majority of money in the modern economy is created by commercial banks making loans." Werner (2005, 2014) provided empirical verification that bank credit creation is the primary mechanism for money supply expansion and GDP growth. Bezemer and Hudson (2016) further distinguished between credit flowing to productive investment (GDP-generating) and credit flowing to existing asset purchases (non-GDP-generating but fragility-increasing). The implication for growth models is profound: the economy's growth trajectory is constrained not by savings or technology but by the credit system's willingness to lend and the private sector's willingness to borrow. This is why Keen (2011) showed that changes in private debt are strongly correlated with changes in unemployment and GDP — a relationship invisible in models without a banking sector.

**Claim 4 — Current Cycle Characterization:**
Applying the sectoral financial balances identity: Government deficit (~6-7% GDP) = Private surplus (~2-3% GDP) + Current account deficit (~3-3.5% GDP). The arithmetic shows that the private sector is in net surplus — it is accumulating financial assets faster than liabilities. This is a fundamentally different configuration from 1999-2000 (private sector deficit of ~5% GDP) or 2005-2007 (private sector deficit of ~3-4% GDP). The current expansion is being driven by the fiscal component, not by private sector leverage. This is confirmed by the Kalecki profit equation: with investment growth moderate and the household saving rate relatively low, it is the government deficit that is sustaining corporate profits. Measured productivity growth (nonfarm business sector output per hour) has been modest — approximately 1.5-2% annualized over the past two years — consistent with normal cyclical recovery rather than a technology-driven structural break. The AI productivity revolution, if it materializes in measured output, has not yet arrived in the aggregate data.

**Claim 5 — Kalecki-Levy Profit Equation:**
The Kalecki-Levy framework (Kalecki 1954, Levy 1983) provides an accounting identity for aggregate profits: Profits = Investment + Government Deficit − Household Saving + Dividends + Net Exports. This is not a theory but an identity derivable from the national accounts. Its power lies in revealing the *sources* of profit rather than treating profits as a residual return to capital. Currently, with investment contributing modestly, household saving low but positive, dividends and buybacks elevated, and net exports negative, the government deficit is the dominant source of aggregate corporate profits. Levy and Koo (among others) have demonstrated that this framework outperforms earnings models based on revenue growth or margin analysis for predicting aggregate profit cycles. The critical implication: if fiscal consolidation reduces the deficit by 2+ percentage points of GDP (as discussed in our prior iteration's fiscal_impulse_trajectory finding), the *mechanical* effect on aggregate profits would be of the same magnitude — a fact not reflected in any bottom-up earnings model used by sell-side analysts.

**Claim 6 — Sectoral Balance Sustainability:**
Godley and Lavoie (2007) developed the stock-flow consistent (SFC) modeling framework that integrates sectoral financial balances with portfolio behavior and stock-flow dynamics. The key insight is that a sector running a persistent deficit is *accumulating debt*, and the sustainability of that deficit depends on whether the resulting debt stock can be serviced from income flows. The private sector, unlike a currency-issuing government, faces a genuine budget constraint — it cannot create the currency in which its debts are denominated. Therefore, the current configuration where the private sector is in surplus is inherently more sustainable than a configuration where the private sector is in deficit. However, this sustainability is *conditional* on the government continuing to provide the offsetting deficit. The moment fiscal policy shifts toward surplus (or even a significantly smaller deficit without a corresponding improvement in net exports), the private sector is forced into deficit — and the Minsky cycle accelerates as private agents borrow to maintain spending. This is precisely the dynamic Godley (1999) identified before the dot-com crash and Godley and Wray (2000) warned about in the late Clinton surplus era.

**Claim 7 — Leading Indicator Degradation:**
The yield curve's predictive power rests on its ability to signal tightening credit conditions that reduce private sector income and spending. But when the government is running deficits large enough to offset the income contraction that tight money would normally produce, the transmission mechanism is short-circuited. The Conference Board's LEI has declined for 22+ months without a recession materializing — an unprecedented divergence. Similarly, the ISM manufacturing PMI spent extended periods below 50 while services PMI remained expansionary, reflecting sectoral rotation rather than broad weakness. The post-COVID labor market dynamics, including the immigration-driven expansion of labor supply (estimated at 2-3 million net immigration annually in 2023-2024, versus the pre-COVID norm of ~1 million), have altered the unemployment-vacancy (Beveridge curve) relationship, making standard Phillips curve and Okun's law estimates unreliable. From a Post-Keynesian perspective, this is not surprising: leading indicators are reduced-form relationships that embed structural assumptions about the economy's plumbing, and when the plumbing changes (as it has with pandemic-era fiscal interventions and labor supply shocks), the indicators break down.

**Claim 8 — AI Capex as Minsky Diagnostic:**
The Minsky classification of the AI investment cycle requires examining the financing structure, not the technology itself. Current data suggests a mixed picture: the hyperscaler capex boom (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) is primarily funded from enormous cash flows and retained earnings — this is hedge finance. However, the downstream ecosystem — AI startups, data center REITs, GPU leasing companies, enterprise AI implementation firms — increasingly relies on venture debt, private credit, and leveraged structures with returns dependent on revenue growth that has not yet materialized. If the downstream ecosystem's financing shifts from hedge to speculative to Ponzi, the pattern would mirror prior technology investment booms (railroads in the 1870s, telecom in the late 1990s) where genuine technological progress was accompanied by — and ultimately overwhelmed by — financial excess. The key monitoring variable is the debt service ratio of AI-adjacent companies: if interest coverage ratios decline while capex accelerates, the Minsky diagnosis points toward fragility.

**Claim 9 — Immigration and Growth Capacity:**
The immigration surge of 2023-2024 expanded labor supply by an estimated 1-2% cumulatively, with disproportionate impact in construction, hospitality, and healthcare. Mainstream models (CBO, Fed staff) have revised potential GDP estimates upward accordingly, treating this as an expansion of the production function. The Post-Keynesian critique is that labor supply alone does not determine output — effective demand does. If the additional workers are absorbed into employment (as they largely have been, given the elevated job openings), this raises actual GDP. But whether this translates to a permanent increase in *potential* GDP depends on whether the associated investment (housing, infrastructure, equipment) is forthcoming to match the labor supply. If instead the labor supply expansion suppresses wage growth (as Kalecki's "reserve army" dynamics would suggest), the effect is redistributive (from labor to capital) rather than expansionary in the aggregate — potentially weakening the consumption demand that drives the multiplier process. The early evidence suggests immigration has helped fill labor shortages without major wage suppression, but this conclusion is tentative and depends on continued demand strength, which itself depends on fiscal policy.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Risk Appetite Regimes (iter_0001):** The growth model framework directly connects to our prior iteration's findings. The fiscally-sustained expansion we identify here is the *cause* of the "fiscal-transfer-sustained risk-on" regime identified in the risk appetite analysis. The Kalecki profit channel (KB: kalecki_profit_channel, confidence 8) is the transmission mechanism linking fiscal deficits to the corporate earnings that validate asset prices and sustain risk appetite. The Minsky framework (KB: minsky_framework, confidence 7) applies to the growth model through the AI capex cycle and broader private sector leverage dynamics. The fiscal impulse trajectory (KB: fiscal_impulse_trajectory, confidence 9) is the single most important variable for both growth sustainability and risk appetite regime determination.

- **Labor Market Dynamics:** The immigration-driven labor supply expansion has direct implications for wage dynamics, Phillips curve relationships, and the NAIRU debate. From the Post-Keynesian perspective, the labor market is not a conventional supply-demand market — wages are set by institutional factors, bargaining power, and the Kaleckian "degree of monopoly." Immigration shifts the balance of power toward capital, potentially suppressing wage growth and weakening consumption demand even while boosting headline employment and GDP.

- **Monetary Policy Transmission:** The endogenous money framework fundamentally reframes monetary policy's role in the growth cycle. Central banks do not "create growth" by expanding the money supply — they set the price of reserves, influencing but not determining bank lending decisions. The current cycle illustrates this: despite restrictive monetary policy (Fed funds rate elevated), growth has been sustained because fiscal deficits have overwhelmed the monetary tightening channel. This is consistent with the Post-Keynesian view that fiscal policy is the primary demand management tool, with monetary policy playing a secondary, facilitating role.

- **Equity Cycles & Valuation:** The Kalecki profit equation provides the bridge between macro growth models and equity market valuation. If aggregate profits are primarily driven by the fiscal deficit, then equity valuations are implicitly a bet on the continuation of fiscal policy. This connects to the P/E expansion debate: elevated multiples may be "justified" by the fiscal profit floor, but they are simultaneously fragile because that floor is politically contingent.

- **Credit Cycles:** The credit creation mechanism is the engine of cyclical growth in the Post-Keynesian framework. The distinction between credit flowing to productive investment (GDP-generating) and credit flowing to asset markets (non-GDP-generating) is critical for understanding why credit booms can produce growth that is simultaneously real in the short term and unsustainable in the medium term. The private credit expansion and CLO dynamics identified in the knowledge base (KB: private_credit_opacity, clo_dynamics) are manifestations of this dynamic.

- **Commodity & Inflation Transmission:** Supply-side growth models treat commodity prices as exogenous cost shocks. The Post-Keynesian framework treats them as endogenous to the credit-demand cycle: strong demand growth driven by credit creation and fiscal deficits bids up commodity prices, which then feed into cost structures. This means that "supply-side inflation" is often misdiagnosed — the underlying cause is demand dynamics operating through credit and fiscal channels.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the supermultiplier estimate for the current U.S. economy, and how has it changed post-COVID?** The Sraffian supermultiplier model (Freitas and Serrano 2015) makes autonomous demand growth the driver of long-run GDP growth, with the multiplier-accelerator interaction determining the steady-state growth rate. Estimating this empirically for the current cycle — particularly decomposing autonomous demand into its government spending, export, and credit-driven components — would provide a rigorous Post-Keynesian growth forecast distinct from mainstream potential GDP estimates.

2. **How should we measure the credit impulse when non-bank lending (private credit, BNPL, crypto-adjacent lending) is growing faster than bank lending?** The traditional credit impulse metric based on bank lending data may be significantly underestimating total credit creation in the economy. This matters for growth cycle positioning because if unmeasured credit is boosting demand, the apparent fiscal-dependence of the current expansion may be overstated — and the fragility from private leverage may be understated.

3. **Is there a threshold level of fiscal deficit below which the current private sector financial surplus flips to deficit, triggering Minsky dynamics?** The sectoral balance identity tells us the relationship is arithmetic, but the behavioral response — how quickly private agents increase borrowing when fiscal transfers decline — is an empirical question with enormous implications for both growth sustainability and financial stability.

4. **How do we reconcile path-dependent potential GDP with the apparent productivity acceleration in narrow sectors (AI, biotech)?** If potential GDP is demand-determined in the aggregate but supply-side innovation is occurring in specific sectors, the interaction between sectoral productivity dynamics and aggregate demand could produce unusual outcomes — productivity gains that do not translate to aggregate growth if demand is insufficient, or aggregate growth that masks sectoral overcapacity.

5. **What would a properly specified Post-Keynesian business cycle indicator look like?** Given the failure of conventional leading indicators, is it possible to construct a composite indicator based on (a) the credit impulse, (b) the sectoral financial balance trajectory, (c) the Minsky fragility classification of key borrower categories, and (d) the fiscal impulse? This would operationalize the theoretical framework into a practical cycle-positioning tool.

6. **How persistent is the immigration-driven labor supply expansion?** Policy changes, demographic shifts in sending countries, and political dynamics could reverse or accelerate immigration flows. The growth implications are asymmetric: a sudden reduction in immigration would create labor shortages and wage pressure in specific sectors, potentially triggering inflationary dynamics that force monetary tightening — a stagflationary scenario that neither mainstream nor Post-Keynesian models handle gracefully.

7. **Does the Kalecki profit equation hold at the sectoral level, or only in the aggregate?** If the fiscal deficit disproportionately flows to specific sectors (defense, healthcare, infrastructure), the profit distribution across sectors may diverge significantly from what aggregate models predict. This matters for equity market positioning and for understanding which parts of the corporate sector are most vulnerable to fiscal consolidation.
