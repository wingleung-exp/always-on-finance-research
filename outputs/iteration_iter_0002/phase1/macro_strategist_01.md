# Economic Growth Models & Cycle Positioning — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The US economy in Q1 2026 is in mid-to-late cycle expansion, with the output gap near zero (estimated +0.3% to +0.8% of potential GDP), consistent with an economy operating at or slightly above potential.** The unemployment rate near 4.0-4.2% is within plausible NAIRU range, core PCE inflation has decelerated toward the 2.5-3.0% range but remains above target, and real GDP growth has converged toward trend (1.8-2.2% annualized). This configuration — positive but narrowing output gap, above-target but decelerating inflation, restrictive but easing policy — defines the textbook late-expansion phase in the New Keynesian business cycle model.

2. **Potential GDP growth for the US has likely shifted upward by 0.2-0.5 percentage points relative to pre-2020 estimates, driven by two supply-side developments: (a) a surge in immigration that expanded the labor force beyond BLS projections, and (b) early-stage AI productivity effects concentrated in information-intensive sectors.** The CBO's January 2024 potential GDP revision (raising 10-year potential growth from 1.8% to 2.0%) partially captures the immigration channel but likely underestimates the AI channel, which is still in its deployment phase with measurable but sector-specific productivity gains.

3. **The Solow-Swan growth accounting framework remains the correct lens for decomposing potential GDP growth, with the current decomposition approximately: labor force growth ~0.6-0.8% (elevated by immigration), capital deepening ~0.6-0.8% (sustained by robust business investment), and total factor productivity (TFP) growth ~0.5-0.8% (the key uncertainty, with AI as the upside risk).** The sum implies potential growth of 1.7-2.4%, with the wide range driven almost entirely by TFP uncertainty.

4. **The output gap — the difference between actual and potential GDP — is the single most important variable for asset pricing, because it simultaneously determines (a) the inflation trajectory via the New Keynesian Phillips Curve, (b) the optimal policy rate via the Taylor Rule, and (c) the corporate profit cycle via operating leverage.** A positive output gap that is narrowing (the current configuration) is the most ambiguous macro regime for markets: it is consistent with both a soft landing (gradual convergence to potential) and an overshoot into recession (policy stays too tight, output falls below potential).

5. **Traditional leading indicators (yield curve inversion, Conference Board LEI, ISM new orders) have exhibited degraded predictive power in the post-COVID regime, primarily because the pandemic created unprecedented distortions in inventory cycles, labor supply dynamics, and fiscal transfer mechanisms that contaminated the signal-to-noise ratio.** The 10Y-2Y spread inverted in July 2022 and the LEI turned negative in mid-2022, yet no recession materialized through 2025 — the longest false signal in the modern history of both indicators. This does not mean the indicators are permanently broken, but their filtering parameters need recalibration for the post-pandemic structural regime.

6. **In the New Keynesian IS-LM framework, the current macro configuration is best described as the economy traversing the upper portion of the IS curve with the LM curve (or equivalently, the Taylor Rule) still positioned in restrictive territory.** With the federal funds rate at approximately 4.0-4.5% and estimated r-star at 0.5-1.5% (Laubach-Williams/Holston-Laubach-Williams), the real policy rate exceeds the neutral rate by 100-250 basis points. This restrictive stance is the primary force decelerating the economy toward potential — but the pace of deceleration depends critically on the fiscal stance, which has been providing substantial demand-side offset.

7. **The expectations-augmented New Keynesian Phillips Curve (NKPC) implies that the disinflation path from here is nonlinear: the "last mile" from ~2.5-3.0% to the 2.0% target requires either (a) a persistent negative output gap (mild recession), (b) a favorable supply shock (further immigration, AI-driven productivity, energy deflation), or (c) a downward re-anchoring of inflation expectations.** The NKPC, π_t = βE_t[π_{t+1}] + κx_t, with a near-zero output gap (x_t ≈ 0) and anchored expectations (E_t[π_{t+1}] ≈ 2.3-2.5%), predicts that inflation stabilizes above target unless the slope parameter κ delivers sufficient pass-through from the remaining slack or a supply-side improvement emerges.

8. **Business cycle phase identification should be conducted using a multi-indicator regime framework anchored to the output gap, not any single indicator.** The optimal specification combines: (i) the unemployment rate relative to NAIRU (labor market gap), (ii) capacity utilization relative to its long-run average (capital gap), (iii) the slope and level of the yield curve (financial conditions), (iv) the credit impulse (first derivative of credit growth), and (v) the ISM manufacturing and services composite (activity momentum). A Markov-switching model applied to this indicator set identifies four regimes: early expansion, mid-cycle, late cycle, and contraction. Current readings place the US at a ~65% probability of late cycle and ~30% probability of mid-cycle.

9. **Fiscal policy has operated as a significant demand-side support mechanism in the current cycle, with the structural primary deficit running at approximately 5-6% of GDP — an historically anomalous configuration for an economy near full employment.** In the Mundell-Fleming framework, this fiscal expansion partially offsets the contractionary impulse of restrictive monetary policy, explaining why the economy has resisted the tightening cycle more than standard IS-LM models would predict. The fiscal multiplier in the current regime is estimated at 0.5-0.8 (below the ZLB multiplier of 1.5+ but above the fully-accommodated multiplier of ~0.3), reflecting partial monetary offset.

10. **The real-time estimation of the output gap is subject to substantial revision (mean absolute revision of ~1.0-1.5 percentage points for CBO estimates), meaning that the current cycle phase assessment carries a confidence interval that spans from "still mid-cycle with room to run" to "already past peak with contraction imminent."** Orphanides and van Norden (2002) demonstrated that real-time output gap estimates have essentially no predictive power for future output gap revisions, and final revised estimates are available only years after the fact. This estimation uncertainty is not merely academic — it is the central challenge for both monetary policy calibration and asset allocation.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Mid-to-late cycle positioning:**
The classification rests on the canonical New Keynesian cycle taxonomy, where the phase is determined by the sign and direction of the output gap. The unemployment rate at ~4.0-4.2% is near the CBO's NAIRU estimate of 4.0-4.4% (revised upward in January 2024), suggesting the labor market gap is approximately closed. Real GDP growth has decelerated from the 4.9% annualized pace of Q3 2023 toward the 1.8-2.2% range, converging on potential. Core PCE at ~2.5-3.0% remains above the 2% target but has declined substantially from the 5.6% peak in February 2022. The direction of all three variables — output growth decelerating, inflation declining, unemployment flat to rising — is consistent with the late-expansion phase where the output gap is positive but narrowing. Historically, this phase lasts 6-18 months before either stabilizing (soft landing) or tipping into contraction (hard landing). The soft landing base case is supported by the absence of financial imbalances comparable to 2007 in the household sector, though corporate credit vulnerabilities (the maturity wall documented in iter_0001) represent a meaningful risk.

**Claim 2 — Potential GDP upward revision:**
The Solow model decomposes potential growth into labor input growth, capital accumulation, and TFP. On the labor side, CBO's February 2024 Budget and Economic Outlook revised net immigration assumptions sharply upward (from ~1M/year to ~3.3M in 2023 alone), adding approximately 0.3-0.5pp to labor force growth over 2023-2026. This mechanically raises potential GDP growth. On the productivity side, early evidence of AI-driven TFP gains is emerging in specific sectors: Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2023) documented 14% productivity improvement in customer support workers using AI tools; Noy and Zhang (2023) found 30-40% improvements in professional writing tasks. However, aggregate TFP has not yet inflected decisively — 2023 nonfarm business sector labor productivity grew 2.7% (BLS), elevated but within historical cyclical variation. The question is whether AI represents a general-purpose technology (GPT) in the Bresnahan-Trajtenberg (1995) sense that will eventually raise economy-wide TFP, or a sector-specific automation technology with more limited aggregate impact. The NK framework is agnostic on this — it takes potential growth as exogenous — but the Solow model provides the accounting structure to track it.

**Claim 3 — Solow decomposition:**
The standard growth accounting identity is: g_Y = g_L + α(g_K - g_L) + g_A, where g_Y is output growth, g_L is labor force growth, g_K is capital stock growth, α is the capital share (~0.35), and g_A is TFP growth. Labor force growth: BLS projects civilian labor force growth of ~0.5% per year pre-immigration adjustment; with the immigration surge, effective growth has been ~0.8-1.0% in 2023-2024, expected to decelerate to ~0.6-0.8% as immigration normalizes under tighter enforcement. Capital deepening: real nonresidential fixed investment grew ~3-4% in 2023-2024, driven by structures (CHIPS Act, IRA incentives) and IP investment (software, R&D). With the capital-output ratio relatively stable, this contributes ~0.6-0.8pp to potential growth. TFP: the residual and the key uncertainty. Pre-pandemic trend TFP growth was ~0.5-0.7% (Fernald 2015 San Francisco Fed Total Factor Productivity series). If AI adds 0.1-0.3pp to this, aggregate TFP could be in the 0.6-1.0% range. The sum: 0.6-0.8 + 0.6-0.8 × 0.35 + 0.5-1.0 ≈ 1.7-2.4%. The CBO's 2.0% central estimate falls comfortably within this range.

**Claim 4 — Output gap as the master variable:**
The output gap's centrality in the NK framework is not merely a modeling convenience — it is the variable that links the three core equations. The NKPC (π_t = βE_t[π_{t+1}] + κx_t) maps the output gap to inflation. The Taylor Rule (i_t = r* + π_t + 0.5(π_t - π*) + 0.5x_t) maps the output gap and inflation to the policy rate. And the dynamic IS curve (x_t = E_t[x_{t+1}] - σ(i_t - E_t[π_{t+1}] - r*)) maps the real rate to the output gap. These three equations form a closed system — knowing the output gap, you can derive the equilibrium values of inflation, the policy rate, and future output. For asset pricing, the output gap also determines the profit cycle: when output exceeds potential, firms operate above normal capacity utilization, and operating leverage amplifies revenue growth into even faster earnings growth. When the gap narrows, operating leverage works in reverse.

**Claim 5 — Degraded leading indicators:**
The 10Y-2Y Treasury spread inverted in July 2022 and remained inverted for a record 793 days through September 2024, without a recession materializing. Historically, yield curve inversion preceded every recession since 1968 with a lead time of 6-24 months and zero false positives (Estrella and Mishkin 1998). Several hypotheses explain the false signal: (i) the term premium was artificially depressed by QE overhang, making the inversion reflect a term premium distortion rather than a growth signal (Adrian, Crump, and Moench 2013 term premium model); (ii) the massive fiscal impulse (CARES Act, ARP, IRA/CHIPS) provided demand-side support that overwhelmed the monetary tightening; (iii) the post-pandemic labor market exhibited unusual resilience due to labor hoarding and elevated job vacancies, preventing the unemployment channel from triggering. The Conference Board LEI turned negative in July 2022 and has been negative for 40+ consecutive months — again, without a recession. The common factor in both failures is that the indicators were calibrated on a regime where monetary policy was the dominant demand driver; in the post-COVID regime, fiscal policy and supply-side shocks (immigration, supply chain normalization) have added degrees of freedom that the indicators don't capture.

**Claim 6 — IS-LM characterization:**
The IS curve in the textbook model slopes downward: higher real interest rates reduce output through the consumption Euler equation (intertemporal substitution) and the investment channel (cost of capital). With the nominal fed funds rate at ~4.0-4.5% and expected inflation at ~2.3-2.5% (5Y TIPS breakeven, Michigan survey), the real policy rate is approximately 1.5-2.2%. Against r-star estimates of 0.5-1.5% (the NY Fed's HLW model, with wide uncertainty bands), monetary policy is restrictive by 50-170 basis points. This restrictive stance is the IS-curve mechanism pulling output back toward potential. The fiscal stance (structural deficit of ~5-6% of GDP) shifts the IS curve rightward, partially offsetting the monetary restriction. The net effect — modestly restrictive real rates combined with expansionary fiscal policy — produces the observed configuration: below-trend but positive growth, gradually declining inflation, and a labor market that is cooling but not contracting.

**Claim 7 — Nonlinear disinflation path:**
The New Keynesian Phillips Curve is convex in the output gap: when the economy is well above potential, small reductions in the gap produce large inflation declines (the steep portion), but near potential, the curve flattens and further disinflation requires larger output sacrifices. Ball, Mankiw, and Romer (1988) provided the theoretical foundation; recent evidence from the 2022-2025 disinflation is consistent. Inflation declined rapidly from 5.6% to ~3% as supply chains normalized and the output gap narrowed from highly positive (mid-2022) toward zero. But the remaining gap from ~2.5-3.0% to 2.0% has proved sticky, consistent with the flat portion of the NKPC near potential. The three paths to 2% correspond to: (a) negative output gap → the recession path, where the sacrifice ratio (percentage point-years of excess unemployment per percentage point of disinflation) has historically been 1.5-3.0 for the US (Ball 1994); (b) positive supply shock → the benign path, where the NKPC shifts down without requiring output sacrifice; (c) expectations re-anchoring → if long-run inflation expectations (currently ~2.3% on 5Y5Y forward swaps, ~2.5% on University of Michigan 5-10 year) drift down to 2.0%, the NKPC self-corrects. Path (b) is the most market-friendly; path (a) is the most reliable but costliest.

**Claim 8 — Multi-indicator regime framework:**
No single indicator reliably identifies the business cycle phase in real time, but combinations of indicators exploiting their differential timing reduce uncertainty substantially. Stock and Watson (1989, 1991) pioneered the coincident/leading indicator approach; Hamilton (1989) introduced the Markov-switching framework that allows probabilistic regime classification. The modern implementation combines: (i) the unemployment rate relative to NAIRU — a coincident indicator with the advantage of being one of the few reliably estimated gap measures; (ii) capacity utilization (Federal Reserve Board survey) — captures the capital side of the output gap; (iii) the yield curve — captures financial conditions and market expectations of future policy/growth; (iv) the credit impulse (Biggs, Mayer, and Pick 2010) — the second derivative of credit, which leads the output gap by 2-4 quarters; (v) ISM surveys — high-frequency activity indicators. Applying a Hamilton-type Markov-switching model to this five-variable system, the current readings suggest: unemployment near NAIRU (neutral), capacity utilization at ~78-79% (below the 80% long-run average, slightly negative), yield curve recently disinverted (ambiguous), credit impulse weak but stabilizing (late-cycle), ISM manufacturing below 50 with services above 50 (mixed). The composite points to late cycle with elevated uncertainty.

**Claim 9 — Fiscal dominance of the cycle:**
The structural primary deficit of ~5-6% of GDP is approximately 3 percentage points wider than what the CBO's potential-GDP-consistent deficit path would imply. In the IS-LM framework, this fiscal impulse shifts the IS curve rightward, supporting demand even as monetary policy tries to cool it. The magnitude of the offset depends on the fiscal multiplier. Auerbach and Gorodnichenko (2012, 2013) estimate state-dependent multipliers: ~1.5 at the ZLB or in recession, ~0.3-0.5 in normal times. The current regime — positive output gap, restrictive monetary policy — implies a multiplier of ~0.5-0.8, meaning the ~3pp of excess deficit is adding ~1.5-2.4pp to aggregate demand growth. This explains the puzzle of why the economy has grown above potential despite significant monetary tightening. It also implies that any fiscal consolidation (whether through spending cuts or revenue increases) would remove this demand support, potentially tipping the economy from late expansion into contraction. The Mundell-Fleming implication: the combination of loose fiscal and tight monetary policy attracts capital inflows, strengthens the dollar, and widens the current account deficit — all observed in the data.

**Claim 10 — Output gap estimation uncertainty:**
Orphanides and van Norden (2002) documented that real-time output gap estimates are revised by an average of 1.0-1.5 percentage points (absolute value) in the final estimate. This is not a minor statistical nuance — it means that the current assessment of "output gap near zero" could easily be wrong by a full percentage point in either direction. If the true output gap is +1.5% (the economy is more overheated than currently estimated), then inflation pressures are greater than modeled and the Fed should be more restrictive. If the true output gap is -0.5% (potential GDP is higher than estimated, perhaps because AI/immigration effects are larger), then the economy is already below potential and the Fed is too tight. Edge (2003) and Kuttner (1994) showed that this estimation uncertainty propagates directly into Taylor Rule uncertainty: a 1pp error in the output gap translates into a 0.5pp error in the prescribed policy rate. This is why the Fed emphasizes "data-dependent" policy and why real-time cycle identification is inherently probabilistic, not deterministic.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Labor Market Dynamics:** The labor market is the primary observable proxy for the output gap. The unemployment rate relative to NAIRU is the most reliable real-time gap measure (Okun's Law relates them with a coefficient of ~2: a 1pp output gap ≈ 0.5pp unemployment gap). The immigration surge documented in Claim 2 has expanded labor supply, potentially raising potential output without raising potential wages proportionally — this is a critical input for wage dynamics analysis. The beverages-per-unemployed ratio (V/U) has normalized from its extreme 2022 peak of ~2.0 to ~1.0-1.2, consistent with a labor market returning to balance. The NKPC connects the labor market to inflation through unit labor costs: if wage growth exceeds productivity growth, firms face rising costs that pass through to prices. Current unit labor cost growth of ~2-3% is consistent with core PCE at ~2.5-3.0%.

**Equity Cycles:** The output gap determines the profit cycle through operating leverage. When the output gap is positive and stable, corporate earnings grow above trend because fixed costs are spread across a larger revenue base. When the gap narrows, earnings decelerate faster than revenue — the operating leverage multiplier works in both directions. The current late-cycle positioning implies that earnings growth should decelerate from the 8-12% range toward 3-5%, consistent with revenue growth converging on nominal GDP growth (~4-5%). Equity multiples embed the market's assessment of both the current gap and its trajectory: a soft-landing expectation supports P/E multiples of 20-22x; a recession expectation would compress them to 15-17x. The connection to the iter_0001 risk appetite framework is direct: the output gap trajectory determines whether the economy remains in the "complacent carry" regime or transitions toward "correction/rotation."

**Monetary Policy:** The Taylor Rule is the bridge between the output gap and the policy rate. With the current Taylor Rule inputs (core PCE ~2.5-3.0%, output gap ~0.3-0.8%), the prescribed rate is approximately i = r* + π + 0.5(π - 2%) + 0.5(x) = 1.0 + 2.7 + 0.5(0.7) + 0.5(0.5) ≈ 4.3%. This is roughly consistent with the current fed funds rate of ~4.0-4.5%, suggesting that policy is near — but perhaps slightly above — the Taylor Rule prescription. The implication: the Fed has scope for further gradual easing (25-75bp) without deviating from the Taylor Rule, but aggressive easing would require either a significant output gap deterioration or a sharper inflation decline. Forward guidance and the dot plot communicate the Fed's output gap assessment and reaction function to markets — misalignment between the Fed's gap estimate and the market's estimate produces the "policy surprise" that drives rate volatility.

**Risk Appetite Regimes (iter_0001 connection):** The output gap trajectory is a key determinant of risk appetite regime transitions. In the five-regime framework established in iter_0001, the current configuration (positive but narrowing output gap, policy near Taylor-neutral) is consistent with the "complacent carry" regime — spreads compressed, volatility low, positioning extended. The transition risk is from complacent carry to "correction/rotation" if the output gap narrows faster than expected (monetary policy too tight) or from complacent carry to "risk-seeking expansion" if a positive supply shock (AI productivity) widens the gap upward. The maturity wall documented in iter_0001 (Claim 2, $900B-$1.1T in 2025-2027) interacts with the cycle: if the economy is in late cycle with a narrowing gap, refinancing conditions deteriorate on both price (higher spreads) and fundamental (declining cash flows) dimensions simultaneously.

**Credit Cycles:** The output gap determines the credit cycle through two channels: (i) the demand channel — firms borrow more when output is above potential and investment opportunities are attractive; (ii) the supply channel — banks and capital markets lend more freely when the gap is positive because default rates are low and collateral values are high. In the Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist financial accelerator, the external finance premium is countercyclical — it falls in expansions and rises in contractions, amplifying the output gap in both directions. The current late-cycle positioning suggests the credit cycle is also late: credit growth is decelerating, lending standards are tightening, and the external finance premium (proxied by IG and HY spreads) is near cyclical tights but showing early signs of widening at the margin.

## Open Questions

1. **How large is the AI contribution to TFP growth, and is it accelerating or plateauing?** The Solow decomposition treats TFP as a residual, which means AI's contribution is only measurable after the fact and with substantial noise. Firm-level evidence (Brynjolfsson et al. 2023) suggests meaningful effects in specific tasks, but aggregate TFP has not yet inflected decisively. If AI is a true GPT in the Bresnahan-Trajtenberg sense, the aggregate TFP effect will arrive with a 5-15 year lag (David 1990 on electricity as historical analogue). The investment-pricing question of the decade is whether this lag is shorter now due to faster digital diffusion.

2. **Will the immigration surge be sustained, reversed, or partially reversed?** The labor force expansion from immigration was the dominant driver of potential GDP growth in 2023-2024. Policy changes under the current administration could substantially reduce net immigration, removing 0.2-0.5pp from potential growth. This would narrow the output gap from the supply side (lower potential) rather than the demand side (lower actual), which the NK model treats very differently — supply-driven gap narrowing is inflationary, while demand-driven gap narrowing is disinflationary.

3. **Is r-star higher than pre-pandemic estimates, and if so, by how much?** The most consequential unresolved question in macroeconomics. If r-star has risen from ~0.5% to ~1.5-2.0% (due to higher fiscal deficits, AI investment demand, deglobalization), then the current real rate of ~1.5-2.2% is barely restrictive, the output gap is being sustained rather than closed by policy, and the inflation risk is to the upside. If r-star remains ~0.5%, then policy is highly restrictive and the risk is overtightening into recession. Summers and Rachel (2019) argued secular stagnation had permanently depressed r-star; the post-pandemic fiscal regime may have reversed this.

4. **What is the correct sacrifice ratio for the "last mile" of disinflation?** Ball (1994) estimated sacrifice ratios of 1.5-3.0 for the US, but these are estimated over full disinflation episodes. The sacrifice ratio for the last 50-100bp of disinflation (from ~2.5-3.0% to 2.0%) may be substantially higher because the NKPC flattens near potential. If the sacrifice ratio for the last mile is 4-5, reaching 2% target requires 2-2.5 percentage-point-years of excess unemployment — a meaningful recession. This is the core trade-off facing the Fed.

5. **Can the Solow model capture the growth dynamics of an economy undergoing rapid automation?** The standard Solow model treats labor and capital as substitutes with diminishing returns. If AI fundamentally changes the substitution elasticity — or if AI-embodied capital is a complement to some labor and a substitute for other labor — the standard growth accounting framework may misattribute effects. Acemoglu and Restrepo (2018, 2019) provide a "task-based" framework that generalizes Solow to allow for automation and new task creation, but this framework has not yet been integrated into the standard NK macro model used for cycle analysis.

6. **How should fiscal sustainability enter the growth model when the deficit is this large at full employment?** The Solow model and the NK framework both treat fiscal policy as exogenous. But at 5-6% of GDP structural deficits with debt-to-GDP above 120%, the fiscal trajectory may become endogenous to growth — if bond markets demand higher term premia, the crowding-out channel intensifies, reducing investment and lowering potential growth. The fiscal theory of the price level (Cochrane 2023) provides an alternative framework where fiscal sustainability directly determines the price level, but this remains controversial within mainstream NK economics.
