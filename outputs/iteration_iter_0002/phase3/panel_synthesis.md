**2. "The output gap is the single most important variable for asset pricing" (confidence 9/10).** (macro_strategist_01, Claim 4)

## KEY_DISAGREEMENTS

**1. Are leading indicators temporarily overridden or permanently degraded?**
- **Position A** (challenger_01, credit_analyst_01): Indicators "never stopped working" — they correctly read the private sector trajectory (contractionary) but were overwhelmed by unprecedented fiscal stimulus. When fiscal impulse contracts, they will reassert.
- **Position B** (generalist_01, macro_strategist_01, rates_strategist_01): The indicators have suffered "structural reliability degradation" due to services-dominant economy, manufacturing-centric indicator design, and permanently changed household balance sheet dynamics.
- **Why it matters:** If A is correct, the "delayed" recession signal from 2022 still threatens when fiscal withdraws. If B is correct, the signals are noise and new indicator frameworks are needed. The key test is prospective: if fiscal contracts and recession follows, A is validated.

**2. Is the output gap a valid but poorly measured concept, or a fundamentally flawed framework?**
- **Position A** (macro_strategist_01): The output gap is the "master variable" in the NK system — the concept is sound but measurement is noisy. Multi-indicator regime frameworks can approximate it.
- **Position B** (macro_strategist_02, challenger_01): The concept is either incoherent (demand determines supply) or operationally vacuous (unmeasurable ≈ nonexistent for practical purposes).
- **Why it matters:** This determines whether the entire NK policy framework (Taylor Rule, Phillips Curve, NAIRU) is the right lens or a systematically misleading one. The practical synthesis: the concept has *some* validity (capacity constraints exist) but precision claims are indefensible, and policy should be calibrated with far wider uncertainty bands than standard practice.

**3. How far advanced is the credit cycle — mid-cycle or disguised late-cycle?**
- **Position A** (credit_analyst_01): Mid-to-late cycle. Public indicators (HY OAS 350-420bp, distressed ratio <5%) point to mid-cycle.
- **Position B** (credit_analyst_02): Further advanced than visible. CLO arbitrage compression, cov-lite masking, EBITDA addbacks, and private credit opacity all bias public indicators toward false optimism in late cycle.
- **Why it matters:** If B is correct, the "stall speed" for growth (below which the credit cycle turns) is ~1.5-2.0% rather than the ~0-0.5% implied by public data. The economy is much closer to a credit stress trigger than visible indicators suggest.

**4. Is the potential GDP upward revision (AI + immigration) genuine and persistent, or procyclical and about to reverse?**
- **Position A** (rates_strategist_01, generalist_01, macro_strategist_01): Genuine revision, supported by TIPS real rate repricing and measurable labor supply expansion. 5y5y forwards encode market conviction.
- **Position B** (challenger_01): Procyclical revision repeating the 2006-2007 pattern. Immigration is reversing under policy tightening. AI won't materialize at aggregate level for 15-25 years. Potential GDP estimates will be revised back down.
- **Why it matters:** If A is correct, the output gap is wider, the Fed has more room, and equity multiples are justified. If B is correct, the output gap is about to close or go negative from the supply side, which is inflationary — creating a stagflationary trap.

**5. What happens to the credit cycle when fiscal support withdraws?**
- All agents agree on the conditional: fiscal consolidation >2pp GDP triggers stress. No agent has a credible estimate of fiscal trajectory timing or magnitude. DOGE spending cuts, TCJA expiration debates, and debt ceiling fights create multiple potential triggers, but political forecasting is inherently low-confidence. This is the single most important monitoring variable and the one with the least analytical clarity.
