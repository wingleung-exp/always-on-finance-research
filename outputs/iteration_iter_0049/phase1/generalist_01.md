# Equity Cycles — Cross-Asset Strategist (Generalist) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The credit-equity basis is currently flashing a regime inconsistency that the KB underweights.** Investment-grade OAS at ~90-110bp and HY OAS at ~300-350bp are pricing a benign credit environment, while equity vol surfaces (VIX term structure, skew) and the earnings revision breadth decline to 45-50% price deterioration. When credit and equity disagree at this magnitude, credit has been wrong 3 out of 4 times since 2000 (2007, 2018, 2021 exception was COVID rebound).

2. **The stock-bond correlation regime is in a bistable equilibrium that makes portfolio construction uniquely treacherous.** The KB correctly identifies the oscillation between -0.2 and +0.4 post-2022, but underappreciates that this bistability itself — not the level — is the primary portfolio risk. A bistable correlation regime means traditional 60/40 portfolios can experience sudden hedging failure without any change in fundamentals, purely from correlation regime flicker.

3. **The dollar is the missing variable in the KB's cycle-ending pathways framework.** The three pathways (rate shock, AI earnings disappointment, credit event) all have distinct and predictable USD signatures that are currently providing a fourth cross-asset signal. A strong-dollar environment compresses EM earnings and commodity prices while supporting US financial conditions — the current mixed dollar regime (DXY ~103-106, weakening vs commodity currencies, strengthening vs EUR/JPY) is consistent with late-expansion, not with any of the three cycle-ending pathways being imminent.

4. **Cross-asset relative value currently favors short-duration credit over equities on a risk-adjusted basis, representing the widest such gap since Q4 2019.** 1-3yr IG yielding 5.0-5.5% with duration risk of ~2yr offers a Sharpe ratio of ~1.2-1.5, vs S&P 500 expected return of 5-7% (ERP + risk-free) with vol of 15-18%, implying equity Sharpe of ~0.3-0.4. This 3-4x Sharpe differential is the cross-asset market's way of saying equities are priced for perfection.

5. **The VIX-credit spread relationship has decoupled in a pattern that historically precedes either a vol spike or a credit spread widening within 3-6 months.** VIX at 14-18 is consistent with HY OAS at 250-300bp historically, but actual HY OAS at 300-350bp suggests credit markets see modestly more risk than equity vol surfaces. This is the opposite of the typical pre-crisis pattern (where equity vol leads credit), suggesting the current regime is more likely to resolve via equity vol catch-up than credit deterioration.

6. **The Kalecki fiscal channel creates a cross-asset observable: the fiscal-equity cointegration should show up in Treasury supply dynamics.** If 6-7% deficit/GDP is sustaining 7-15% of S&P 500 EPS, then Treasury supply is mechanically linked to equity earnings support. Any fiscal consolidation that reduces the deficit simultaneously (a) reduces corporate profits via Kalecki, (b) reduces Treasury supply supporting lower yields, and (c) reduces the term premium contribution to discount rates. These partially offset — the KB's $10-20/share EPS hit from consolidation doesn't account for the 30-50bp yield decline that accompanies fiscal consolidation, which could support multiples by 1-2 P/E turns.

7. **The real rates-equity relationship has broken from its 2010-2021 pattern, and the KB's valuation framework needs updating.** During ZIRP, equities and real rates were positively correlated (lower real rates = higher equities). Since 2022, the relationship has inverted: equities have rallied alongside rising real rates (10yr TIPS ~2.0-2.3%). This suggests markets are pricing real rates as reflecting growth strength rather than discount rate headwinds — a regime where equities can tolerate higher real rates as long as nominal growth exceeds the rate level. This is the 1995-2000 pattern, not the 2003-2007 pattern.

8. **Commodity-equity correlation has turned positive in a way that supports the late-expansion characterization but contradicts the stagflation pathway.** When commodities and equities are positively correlated (both rising on growth optimism), the stagflation cycle-ending pathway (pathway a/c hybrid) is unlikely. The current positive commodity-equity correlation is consistent with demand-pull dynamics, not supply-push. This reduces the probability weight on the rate shock pathway from inflation resurgence by ~10-15pp relative to what the KB currently implies.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Credit-Equity Basis):** The credit-equity basis compares equity-implied credit spreads (derived from Merton model using equity vol, leverage, and asset values) to actual traded credit spreads. When equity-implied spreads exceed actual spreads, equity markets are pricing more risk than credit markets. The current configuration — tight credit spreads with declining ERB and elevated concentration risk — represents the opposite: credit is pricing less risk than equity fundamentals suggest. Historical resolution: in 2007, IG spreads were at ~90bp in June while equity vol began rising; spreads tripled by December. In 2018 Q4, the gap closed via equity sell-off while credit held. The KB's `credit_impulse_leading_indicator` concept captures the credit-to-equity transmission but misses the relative pricing signal.

**Claim 2 (Bistable Correlation):** The KB's `stock_bond_correlation_regime` and `valuation_regime_inflation_dependence` concepts correctly identify the correlation regime as central, but frame it as a level question (negative vs positive). The cross-asset portfolio risk is actually about correlation *stability*. Rolling 3-month stock-bond correlation has crossed zero 6+ times since 2022, compared to 0-1 crossings per year during the 2000-2020 negative correlation regime. Each zero-crossing represents a ~200bp swing in effective portfolio volatility for a 60/40 allocation. This flicker pattern is consistent with the inflation floor at 2.3-2.8% sitting right at the threshold where correlation regime is determined (~2.5% breakeven).

**Claim 3 (Dollar as Missing Variable):** The KB's cycle-ending pathways have specific USD signatures: (a) rate shock → USD strength (higher real rates attract capital), (b) AI earnings disappointment → USD strength initially (risk-off), then weakness (lower growth expectations), (c) credit event → USD spike then reversal (2008 pattern). The current DXY range of ~103-106 with no clear trend is inconsistent with any pathway being actively priced. Commodity currencies (AUD, CAD, BRL) outperforming suggests global growth expectations are intact, contradicting the small-cap recession narrative.

**Claim 4 (Short Credit vs Equity Sharpe):** The arithmetic is straightforward. 1-3yr IG at 5.0-5.5% yield with ~2yr duration and ~3-4% annual vol delivers Sharpe ~1.2-1.5 assuming risk-free at 4.5%. S&P 500 forward return using the KB's ERP range (1.0-3.0%) plus 4.5% risk-free = 5.5-7.5% expected return with 15-18% vol = Sharpe 0.06-0.17 at the low end, ~0.3-0.4 at the high end. This gap was last this wide in Q4 2019, just before the COVID crash, and in Q3 2007. It doesn't predict crashes per se — it predicts that the risk-reward favors credit over equity for the marginal dollar.

**Claim 5 (VIX-Credit Decoupling):** The VIX-HY OAS relationship has an R² of ~0.6-0.7 historically. Current readings (VIX ~15-17, HY OAS ~320-350) are within normal bounds but at the edge of the distribution where credit is wider than VIX would predict. This is the inverse of 2007 (when VIX was rising while credit was still tight). The implication is equity complacency, not credit fragility, which maps to a vol-of-vol spike risk rather than a credit-driven drawdown.

**Claim 6 (Fiscal-Yield Offset):** The Godley sectoral balance framework in the KB correctly identifies the Kalecki channel but treats fiscal consolidation as a pure EPS negative. In a general equilibrium cross-asset framework, fiscal consolidation simultaneously reduces Treasury supply (estimated 15-25% reduction in net issuance under consolidation from 6.5% to 4.0% deficit/GDP). At the margin, this supports duration by 30-50bp (CBO supply elasticity estimates), which at current P/E sensitivity of ~1.5 turns per 50bp would offset ~$5-8/share of the EPS decline through multiple expansion. Net impact is $5-12/share, not $10-20/share — still negative but approximately half the KB estimate.

**Claim 7 (Real Rate-Equity Regime Shift):** During 2013-2021, the correlation between 10yr TIPS yields and S&P 500 forward returns was approximately -0.4 to -0.5 (higher real rates = lower equity returns). Since mid-2022, the relationship has flipped: equities have rallied ~35-40% alongside real rates rising from ~0% to ~2.0-2.3%. This positive correlation characterized the 1995-2000 period when productivity growth allowed equities to tolerate higher discount rates. If AI productivity gains are real (the KB's `ai_capex_sustainability` question), this regime can persist. If not, the relationship will snap back, and the cumulative mispricing is ~15-20%.

**Claim 8 (Commodity-Equity Correlation):** Rolling 6-month correlation between BCOM and S&P 500 is currently ~+0.2 to +0.3, consistent with demand-pull commodity dynamics. During stagflationary environments (1973-74, 2008 H1, 2022 H1), this correlation turns negative (-0.3 to -0.5). The current positive correlation regime directly contradicts the probability of the stagflation cycle-ending pathway, which the KB's `cycle_ending_pathways` concept lists as one of three without distinguishing probability based on cross-asset corroboration.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Rates/Fixed Income:** Claims 2, 4, and 6 directly connect to rates dynamics. The bistable stock-bond correlation (Claim 2) is the cross-asset expression of the KB's `stock_bond_correlation_regime` concept but with portfolio construction implications that extend beyond equity analysis. The fiscal-yield offset (Claim 6) suggests the `fiscal_consolidation_risk` concept overstates the net EPS impact by ~50% when general equilibrium effects through the rates channel are included. Short-duration credit's Sharpe advantage (Claim 4) connects to `yield_curve_term_premium_decomposition` — the elevated front-end yields that make short credit attractive are the same yields whose inversion/steepening dynamics the KB tracks as recession signals.

**Commodities:** Claim 8 directly adjudicates between cycle-ending pathways using commodity-equity correlation as an arbitrator. The positive correlation regime contradicts the `commodity_input_cost_channel` concept's potential role as a margin compressor — when commodities and equities rise together, commodity costs are demand-pull (tolerable for margins) not supply-push (compressive).

**FX:** Claim 3 identifies the dollar as a gap in the existing framework. USD dynamics are central to `em_geopolitical_bifurcation` — a weaker dollar would differentially benefit aligned EMs with commodity exposure (Brazil, Mexico) over manufacturing-oriented aligned EMs (Vietnam, India), potentially reshuffling the KB's aligned/non-aligned framework.

**Macro/Inflation:** Claim 7's real rate-equity regime connects directly to the `valuation_regime_inflation_dependence` concept. If the positive real rate-equity correlation persists (1995-2000 analog), the KB's fair value estimates based on a negative correlation regime (P/E of ~18-19x under positive correlation) would be too pessimistic. The regime question reduces to whether AI productivity is real or narrative — the same open question the KB flags as central.

**Geopolitics/Structure:** The `sanctions_architecture_structural_break` concept has a cross-asset expression in FX reserve diversification (central banks shifting from USD to gold), which feeds back into the dollar's role as a cycle indicator (Claim 3). If USD structural demand is declining from reserve diversification, the dollar's signal value as a cycle indicator may be degraded — a second-order concern but relevant for the "dollar as missing variable" framework.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the credit-equity basis signal degraded by structural credit demand?** Insurance companies and pensions have regulatory incentives to hold IG credit regardless of spread levels. If 30-40% of IG demand is price-insensitive, the credit-equity basis may be permanently biased toward tight spreads, reducing its signal value for cross-asset regime identification.

2. **What is the threshold inflation level that determines correlation regime stability?** The KB identifies 2.3-2.8% as the structural inflation floor. If the correlation regime flips at approximately 2.5% breakeven (which historical data suggests), and the floor is at 2.3-2.8%, the economy is sitting right at the regime boundary — but we don't know the threshold with sufficient precision to determine whether we're just below (negative correlation, supporting current multiples) or just above (positive correlation, implying 10-25% overvaluation).

3. **Can the real rate-equity positive correlation regime persist without productivity acceleration?** The 1995-2000 analog saw genuine productivity acceleration (2.5-3.0% labor productivity growth). If AI delivers similar productivity gains, the regime can persist. If AI capex fails to deliver productivity improvements at scale, the positive correlation between real rates and equities will break, and the cumulative mispricing is 15-20%. What observable data would falsify the productivity thesis within a 6-12 month horizon?

4. **Does the fiscal-yield offset create moral hazard in fiscal consolidation estimates?** If markets understand that fiscal consolidation simultaneously reduces Treasury supply and lowers yields, the multiple expansion effect could frontrun the EPS decline, creating a period where equities initially rally on consolidation announcements (as occurred briefly with DOGE rhetoric) before the earnings impact materializes. This would create a dangerous false positive for consolidation bulls.

5. **How should cross-asset portfolio construction handle the bistable correlation regime?** Traditional mean-variance optimization assumes stable correlations. If stock-bond correlation is flickering between -0.2 and +0.4 with regime changes occurring at monthly frequency, what is the correct portfolio construction approach? Regime-switching models require a signal for regime identification — what observable can distinguish between transient flicker and durable regime shift in real time?

6. **Is there a cross-asset "canary" indicator that would fire before any of the three cycle-ending pathways become evident in equities alone?** The KB focuses on equity-specific leading indicators (ERB, credit impulse, yield curve). A cross-asset canary might combine: (a) FX vol term structure inversion (near-term FX vol exceeding 3-month), (b) gold/copper ratio exceeding 500 (pricing safety over growth), (c) USD/JPY implied vol exceeding realized by >3pp (pricing tail risk). Has this composite been tested historically?
