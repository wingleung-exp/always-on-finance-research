# Equity Cycles — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The Taylor Rule currently prescribes a fed funds rate of ~4.5-5.0%, implying policy is approximately neutral-to-slightly-restrictive, not accommodative — the equity market is pricing a loosening that the macro framework does not yet justify.** The prescribed rate depends critically on the output gap (+0.5-1.5%), core PCE (~2.7-2.8%), and the assumed r-star. Using standard Taylor (1993) coefficients (1.5 on inflation gap, 0.5 on output gap) with r-star at 1.0-1.5%, the prescription clusters around 4.5-5.0%. The market's forward curve pricing ~100bp of cuts over 12 months implies either a growth downturn not yet visible in headline data or a belief that the Fed will cut preemptively — neither of which is consistent with the late-expansion assessment at confidence 7 in the KB.

2. **The fiscal multiplier at current cycle positioning is 0.3-0.6, not the 1.5-2.0 often implicitly assumed — meaning the Kalecki fiscal floor is real but thinner than headline deficit numbers suggest.** The state-dependent multiplier literature (Auerbach-Gorodnichenko 2012, Ramey-Zubairy 2018) is unambiguous: at positive output gaps, fiscal multipliers are well below unity. The current 6-7% deficit/GDP generates mechanical Kalecki profit support, but each marginal dollar of government spending produces only $0.30-0.60 of GDP, meaning the relationship between deficit size and earnings support is sub-linear. The KB's estimate of $20-40/share fiscal contribution to S&P 500 EPS should be haircut to $15-25/share after accounting for multiplier attenuation.

3. **R-star uncertainty of ±100-150bp (Laubach-Williams standard errors) propagates into a ±15-20% fair value range for equities, making the entire valuation framework fundamentally underdetermined.** The Gordon Growth Model sensitivity is approximately: 100bp increase in r-star implies ~3-4 P/E turn compression, or ~15-20% fair value decline from ~21-22x. The KB correctly identifies this as an open question but understates its severity. If r-star is 2.0% rather than 0.5-1.0%, the entire late-expansion thesis shifts to "significantly overvalued" rather than "fairly valued with late-cycle risks." The 5y5y forward at 3.8-4.2% is pricing something closer to r-star of 1.5-2.0%, which markets have not yet fully transmitted to equity valuations.

4. **The New Keynesian Phillips Curve slope has flattened from ~0.30 (pre-2000) to ~0.10-0.15, creating a regime where the output gap can close without generating the inflation signal that historically triggered the Fed tightening that ended equity cycles.** This flattening (documented in Del Negro et al. 2020, Hazell et al. 2022) means the economy can run a positive output gap longer before inflation forces a policy response. This is a structural reason why the current cycle has extended beyond historical averages — it is not a structural break per se, but a parameter shift within the existing NK framework. However, if inflation expectations de-anchor (survey-based measures have drifted up 30-50bp since 2021), the Phillips Curve re-steepens rapidly, collapsing the extension.

5. **Monetary transmission lags of 12-24 months (Romer-Romer 2004 updated estimates) imply the cumulative impact of 2022-23 tightening is still propagating — the small-cap earnings recession and credit impulse deceleration are lagged effects, not leading indicators of a new shock.** The KB treats small-cap underperformance and credit impulse deceleration as current signals, but from a NK transmission perspective, these are the expected lagged consequences of the 525bp tightening cycle. The question is whether the cumulative restrictive effect is sufficient to close the output gap or whether the fiscal offset (Kalecki channel) has neutralized the transmission. If the fiscal multiplier is indeed 0.3-0.6 at this cycle stage, the monetary transmission is partially attenuated but not fully offset.

6. **The Mundell-Fleming trilemma creates a dollar strength channel that supports US equity valuations through capital inflows but simultaneously exports tightening to EM and small open economies, creating a divergent global cycle.** High US real rates attract capital inflows (~$800B-1T cumulative portfolio inflows since 2022), mechanically supporting US equity prices through Gabaix-Koijen multiplier effects. This is the open-economy complement to the inelastic markets hypothesis: foreign demand for US assets creates price-insensitive buying that reinforces US equity valuations while draining liquidity from EM. The EM geopolitical bifurcation documented in the KB is partially a Mundell-Fleming effect, not purely geopolitical.

7. **The NAIRU is likely 50-100bp lower than pre-pandemic estimates (~3.5-4.0% vs 4.0-4.5%), supported by labor force composition shifts and potentially early AI productivity effects — this mechanically extends the equity cycle by widening the output gap at which inflation pressures bind.** Evidence: unemployment has remained at 3.7-4.2% for 18+ months without accelerating inflation beyond the structural floor, inconsistent with NAIRU at 4.5%. Revised CBO estimates and Fed staff analysis have converged on lower NAIRU estimates. Each 50bp NAIRU reduction extends the output gap at which the Phillips Curve constraint binds by approximately 0.3-0.5% of GDP, translating to 6-12 additional months of cycle extension.

8. **The interaction between earnings quality deterioration and the output gap creates a measurement problem: if true earnings are 10-20% overstated, the implied output gap from corporate profit data is wider than headline suggests, meaning the economy may be closer to potential than earnings-based frameworks indicate.** The KB documents earnings quality deterioration at confidence 6. From a macro perspective, if non-GAAP earnings overstate true economic profit by 10-20%, then the corporate profit share of GDP — a key input to output gap estimation — is also overstated. This means the Kalecki channel is simultaneously overestimated (profits include non-economic accruals) and the output gap is closer to zero than headline earnings imply.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Taylor Rule Prescription:**
Standard Taylor Rule: r = r* + π + 0.5(π - π*) + 0.5(y - y*)
With r* = 1.0-1.5%, π = 2.7-2.8% (core PCE), π* = 2.0%, y-y* = +0.5-1.5%:
r = 1.25 + 2.75 + 0.5(0.75) + 0.5(1.0) = 4.875%
Range: 4.3-5.3% depending on parameter choices. Current effective FFR at ~4.5-4.75% places policy approximately at the Taylor prescription — neutral, not restrictive. The market forward curve pricing cuts implies either output gap closure ahead or a dovish Fed deviation. Neither is assured.

**Claim 2 — Fiscal Multiplier State-Dependence:**
Auerbach-Gorodnichenko (2012): multiplier of 0.0-0.5 in expansions vs 1.0-2.5 in recessions. Ramey-Zubairy (2018): multiplier below 0.8 when unemployment is below 6.5%. Current unemployment ~4.0% places us firmly in the low-multiplier regime. The arithmetic: 6.5% deficit × $28T GDP = $1.82T spending. At multiplier 0.4, GDP contribution is ~$730B. Corporate profit share of GDP (~11-12%) implies ~$80-90B profit contribution, or ~$17-19/share on S&P 500 diluted share count of ~4.8B. This is meaningful but roughly 20-40% below the un-adjusted Kalecki calculation.

**Claim 3 — R-star Propagation:**
Holston-Laubach-Williams (2023 update) estimates r-star at 0.7-1.2% with 95% CI of approximately -0.5% to 2.5%. Del Negro et al. (2017 DSGE approach) estimates 0.4-1.0%. Market-implied (5y5y TIPS) suggests 1.5-2.0%. The range matters enormously: at r-star = 0.75%, fair P/E is ~22-24x (equities fairly valued). At r-star = 2.0%, fair P/E is ~17-19x (equities 10-25% overvalued). This is not a nuance — it is the single largest source of uncertainty in the equity cycle framework.

**Claim 4 — Phillips Curve Flattening:**
The slope coefficient in the NKPC (the relationship between output gap and inflation) has declined from ~0.30 pre-2000 to ~0.10-0.15 post-2000 (Hazell et al. 2022, McLeay-Tenreyro 2020). At a slope of 0.12, a +1.5% output gap produces only ~0.18pp additional inflation — well within noise and insufficient to trigger a policy response. This explains why the cycle extends: the feedback loop (positive output gap → inflation → tightening → recession → equity drawdown) operates with much lower gain than historically.

**Claim 5 — Transmission Lags:**
Romer-Romer (2004) estimate peak output effects at 24 months, with the distribution skewed 12-36 months. The tightening cycle began March 2022 and paused mid-2023. Peak cumulative effect would be expected Q1 2025 through Q3 2025. The small-cap earnings recession since mid-2023 and credit impulse deceleration are temporally consistent with lagged transmission, not fresh deterioration. This distinction matters for forecasting: if these are lagged effects, they may be approaching maximum impact, not accelerating.

**Claim 6 — Mundell-Fleming Capital Flow Channel:**
Under floating exchange rates with high capital mobility (Mundell-Fleming conditions satisfied for the US), fiscal expansion + tight monetary policy produces currency appreciation and capital inflows. The USD DXY appreciation of ~15-20% from 2021 lows and cumulative portfolio inflows are textbook Mundell-Fleming. The Gabaix-Koijen multiplier transforms these inflows into disproportionate price impact: $800B inflows × 3-5 multiplier = $2.4-4.0T market cap support. This is a non-trivial fraction of total S&P 500 market cap gains since 2022.

**Claim 7 — NAIRU Revision:**
Beveridge curve shifts post-pandemic (Figura-Waller 2022) suggest improved matching efficiency after the pandemic reset. Labor force participation among 25-54 year olds has recovered to pre-pandemic levels. Sustained low unemployment without wage-price acceleration provides revealed-preference evidence for lower NAIRU. The Fed's own Summary of Economic Projections has revised longer-run unemployment estimates down from 4.5% to ~4.0-4.2% since 2019.

**Claim 8 — Earnings Quality / Output Gap Interaction:**
If GAAP P/E is 25-27x and non-GAAP is 21-22x, then ~15-22% of reported non-GAAP earnings are adjustments. Corporate profits as measured in NIPA (which uses GAAP-like methodology) already run ~$300-400B below the S&P 500 aggregate non-GAAP figure. NIPA-based output gap estimates would show a narrower positive gap than earnings-based estimates, suggesting less slack than the corporate profit data implies.

## Connections to Other Topics

**To monetary policy / rates:** Claims 1, 3, and 4 directly link the equity cycle to the monetary policy reaction function. The Taylor Rule assessment (Claim 1) determines whether the Fed is behind or ahead of the curve, which feeds into the yield curve term premium decomposition already in the KB. The flattened Phillips Curve (Claim 4) is the structural reason the Fed can be patient, connecting to the steepening trap concept — if the Phillips Curve re-steepens, the Fed tightens aggressively, triggering pathway (a) of cycle-ending scenarios.

**To fiscal policy:** Claim 2 refines the fiscal_consolidation_risk and kalecki_fiscal_channel concepts by introducing multiplier state-dependence. The KB's estimate of fiscal consolidation impact ($10-20/share EPS reduction) should be adjusted upward in a recession scenario (multiplier rises to 1.0-2.0, amplifying consolidation pain) but the current-state impact is smaller than headline arithmetic implies.

**To international / EM:** Claim 6 connects to em_geopolitical_bifurcation by adding the Mundell-Fleming transmission channel. Aligned EMs benefit from US capital outflow spillovers via trade linkages, while non-aligned EMs face both geopolitical discount and Mundell-Fleming dollar tightening. This is a dual headwind not fully captured in the current KB.

**To AI capex / concentration:** The r-star uncertainty (Claim 3) is existential for the AI capex thesis. If r-star is 2.0%, the hurdle rate for AI capex ROIC justification rises by 100-150bp, potentially converting hedge-financed capex from value-creating to marginal. The ROIC-WACC spread convexity concept in the KB is directly conditioned on r-star.

**To inflation dynamics:** The structural inflation floor of 2.3-2.8% from prior iterations interacts with Claims 4 and 7. If the Phillips Curve is flat and NAIRU is lower, inflation can persist at 2.5-3.0% without triggering aggressive tightening — the "last mile" problem. This supports the valuation_regime_inflation_dependence concept: the economy may settle into a regime where inflation is too high for the pre-2020 valuation paradigm (P/E 22-24x) but too low to force a definitive regime shift (P/E 15-18x), leaving equities in an uncomfortable intermediate range (~18-21x).

## Open Questions

1. **Is r-star 1.0% or 2.0%?** This is not an incremental question — it determines whether equities are fairly valued or 15-20% overvalued. The 5y5y forward suggests the market is pricing closer to 1.5-2.0%, but equity multiples still reflect 0.5-1.0%. This inconsistency must resolve, and the direction of resolution is the single most consequential macro variable for the equity cycle.

2. **Can the flattened Phillips Curve re-steepen without warning?** The 2021-22 episode showed inflation can accelerate rapidly when supply shocks interact with demand stimulus. If tariff escalation or commodity supply disruption produces a supply shock while the output gap is positive, the flat Phillips Curve provides false comfort — the nonlinearity at capacity constraints means the curve may appear flat until it suddenly isn't.

3. **Does the Mundell-Fleming capital flow channel break down under fiscal sustainability concerns?** The framework assumes confidence in US sovereign credit. If the deficit trajectory undermines confidence (bond vigilante scenario), capital flows reverse, the dollar weakens, and the equity support mechanism in Claim 6 reverses. This is a nonlinear tail risk not well-captured by the linear Mundell-Fleming framework.

4. **How should we treat the fiscal multiplier when the composition of spending shifts from transfer payments to investment (AI infrastructure, CHIPS Act)?** Investment spending may have higher long-run multipliers through the supply side (Solow residual effects) even if short-run demand multipliers are low. This matters for whether the current fiscal stance is building future productive capacity or simply borrowing demand from the future.

5. **Is the monetary transmission mechanism structurally altered by the shift from bank-intermediated credit to capital markets funding?** If large corporations fund via bond markets (fixed rate, long duration) rather than bank loans (floating rate, short duration), the interest rate channel of monetary policy is weaker for large caps but stronger for small caps — which is exactly the bifurcation pattern we observe. This would explain the cap-weighted vs equal-weighted divergence as a monetary transmission asymmetry rather than a concentration or quality story.

6. **What is the correct baseline for "normal" equity cycle behavior — the post-1983 Great Moderation sample, or the full post-war sample?** The Great Moderation featured structurally lower inflation volatility, more credible central banks, and deeper financial markets. If the regime has shifted (inflation floor at 2.3-2.8%, potential central bank independence erosion), the relevant baseline may be the pre-1983 sample where equity cycles were shorter, more volatile, and produced lower real returns. This connects directly to the US survivorship bias concept in the KB.
