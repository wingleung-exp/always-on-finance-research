# Yield Curve Dynamics — Geopolitical Risk & Political Economy Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Geopolitical fiscal lock-in is now the dominant structural driver of term premium rebuilding, exceeding QT and inflation uncertainty effects.** Non-discretionary spending commitments — NATO defense floors (2-3%+ of GDP across allied nations), industrial policy packages (CHIPS Act $280B, IRA $369B, EU Chips Act EUR 43B), and supply chain reshoring — create a fiscal supply floor for sovereign debt that cannot be reduced through normal budgetary politics, permanently elevating term premium relative to the 2012-2021 QE/low-supply era.

2. **Foreign official demand for USTs is in structural decline driven by geopolitical realignment, not merely rate differentials, removing the marginal buyer that historically compressed the term premium by 50-100bp.** The weaponization of dollar reserves (Russia 2022 freeze of ~$300B), active reserve diversification by China (reducing UST holdings from ~$1.1T peak to ~$770B), petrodollar erosion as Saudi Arabia and Gulf states diversify settlement currencies, and non-aligned EM central bank gold accumulation (1,000+ tonnes annually since 2022) collectively reduce the structural bid for long-duration USTs. This is not a cyclical phenomenon — it is a strategic geopolitical response with no mean-reversion mechanism.

3. **The yield curve's recession-signaling power — historically 7/7 — is being degraded by geopolitical contamination of the term premium.** When term premium contains a large geopolitical risk component (defense spending uncertainty, sanctions risk, reserve diversification pressure), inversions and steepening can be driven by geopolitical developments rather than pure monetary policy expectations. The 2022-2023 inversion was partly a term-premium compression story (as established in KB), but the current post-inversion steepening also contains geopolitical fiscal noise that obscures the recession signal. The curve's signal-to-noise ratio has structurally deteriorated.

4. **US election cycles create systematic, predictable yield curve distortions through three channels: pre-election fiscal stimulus, post-election policy uncertainty, and partisan fiscal regime shifts.** Pre-election years see deficit expansion (average +0.5-1.0pp of GDP) as incumbents spend for reelection, steepening the curve at the belly. Post-election quarters see elevated term premium as markets price new administration's fiscal trajectory. The 2024 election cycle was illustrative: the 10Y term premium widened ~30bp between September-November 2024 as markets priced potential tariff-driven inflation and fiscal expansion under either candidate.

5. **The geopolitical correlation ratchet creates a secular one-way pressure on term premium: escalation rapidly increases it, while de-escalation produces only slow, partial normalization.** This is because geopolitical escalation creates permanent sunk fiscal costs (defense industrial base expansion, supply chain duplication, sanctions compliance infrastructure) that persist even after the precipitating crisis subsides. The Russia-Ukraine conflict added 0.5-1.0% of GDP in European defense commitments that will persist for decades regardless of ceasefire outcomes. Each geopolitical escalation episode ratchets the term premium floor higher.

6. **A Taiwan contingency is the single largest underpriced geopolitical tail risk for yield curve dynamics, with potential to produce 150-300bp of curve steepening within weeks.** A military conflict or comprehensive sanctions regime against China would simultaneously: (a) eliminate ~$770B of Chinese UST holdings as demand source (potential forced selling or freeze), (b) trigger extreme safe-haven flows into front-end USTs while long-end sells off on fiscal expansion expectations, (c) require $500B-$1T+ in emergency defense/economic stabilization spending, and (d) disrupt $700B+ in bilateral trade requiring fiscal backstops. Markets price this at roughly 5-10% probability over 5 years, but the yield curve impact conditional on occurrence is catastrophically asymmetric.

7. **BOJ normalization and European fiscal expansion are creating international yield curve spillover effects that structurally reduce UST relative value and require higher term premium to attract flows.** BOJ's exit from YCC and gradual normalization (policy rate moving toward 0.75-1.0%) has raised 10Y JGB yields from ~0% to ~1.0-1.5%, reducing the yield advantage that attracted Japanese life insurers and pension funds to USTs via FX-hedged basis. Simultaneously, European defense fiscal expansion (Draghi report's $800B annual investment call, Germany's EUR 500B infrastructure fund) is expanding sovereign supply. The $3-4T annual Japanese private sector foreign bond demand and $1-2T European institutional demand that historically compressed UST term premium is being partially redirected.

8. **Central bank independence is being eroded by the political economy of high debt loads, and this erosion is priced into the yield curve as higher long-end inflation risk premium.** When sovereign debt/GDP exceeds ~100% (US at ~125%, Japan at ~260%, Italy at ~140%), each 100bp rate increase adds 1-2.5% of GDP in interest costs, creating intense political pressure to constrain central bank tightening. Markets observe this through: (a) explicit political rhetoric targeting central banks (Trump's Fed criticism, Erdogan's rate-cut demands), (b) fiscal dominance signals where Treasury issuance patterns implicitly constrain monetary policy, and (c) appointments of dovish governors. The resulting long-end inflation risk premium is 20-40bp above what pure inflation expectations models would suggest.

9. **Sanctions architecture has created a two-tier global yield curve environment with divergent dynamics, altering traditional cross-market arbitrage relationships.** Allied-bloc curves (US, EU, UK, Japan, Australia) are increasingly correlated through shared geopolitical fiscal burdens, synchronized defense spending cycles, and capital flow concentration within the sanctioned perimeter. Non-aligned curves (China, India, Brazil, Saudi Arabia) are progressively decoupling as these nations develop alternative clearing, settlement, and reserve systems (mBridge, CIPS, bilateral currency swaps). The traditional assumption that global rates converge through arbitrage is breaking down at the seams of the sanctions perimeter.

10. **The "steepening trap" is amplified and made more dangerous by geopolitical fiscal commitments — bear steepening from geopolitical supply pressure masquerades as bull steepening from growth optimism.** When the Fed cuts rates and the curve steepens, markets historically celebrate normalization. But if steepening is concurrent with rising geopolitical spending commitments (defense budgets, industrial policy, sanctions-driven reshoring), the long end is pricing fiscal supply risk rather than growth expectations. The current 2s10s at +20-40bp is ambiguous: it could reflect healthy normalization or the early stage of a geopolitically-driven bear steepening that precedes fiscal stress.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Geopolitical Fiscal Lock-In

The evidence for non-discretionary geopolitical fiscal commitments is extensive and quantifiable:

- **NATO defense commitments**: The 2024 Vilnius summit raised the floor from 2% to "at least 2%" of GDP, with political pressure toward 3%. For the US (already at ~3.5%), this represents maintenance of historically elevated levels. For Europe, this implies EUR 100-200B annually in additional spending above pre-2022 baselines. Germany's Zeitenwende fund (EUR 100B) and subsequent constitutional debt brake reform to exempt defense spending from fiscal rules demonstrate the political irreversibility.
- **Industrial policy**: CHIPS Act ($280B authorized), IRA ($369B minimum, likely $800B+ with uptake), EU Chips Act (EUR 43B), and equivalent Japanese/Korean semiconductor programs represent multi-year fiscal commitments with political constituencies (new factories, employment) that make rollback nearly impossible.
- **Supply chain reshoring**: McKinsey estimated $4.6T in global reshoring investment needed by 2030, much of it subsidized by sovereign fiscal incentives.

The transmission to term premium is direct: higher primary issuance → larger term premium required to clear supply. Treasury issuance has exceeded $2T annually and is trending toward $2.5T+ as these commitments layer onto existing entitlement spending. The KB's existing concept of term premium dynamics (50-80bp ACM, 80-130bp adjusted) is consistent with this driver exceeding QT's 30-60bp contribution.

### Claim 2: Foreign Official Demand Decline

- **China**: UST holdings declined from $1.1T (2013) to ~$770B (late 2025), a strategic reduction accelerated post-2022 sanctions precedent. PBOC has simultaneously increased gold reserves by ~300 tonnes.
- **Russia**: Complete elimination as UST holder post-sanctions. Precedent effect on other nations' willingness to concentrate reserves in USTs.
- **Saudi Arabia/Gulf**: PetroYuan settlement agreements, Saudi BRICS membership (2024), and reduced petrodollar recycling as oil revenues diversify.
- **Central bank gold purchases**: 1,037 tonnes (2023), 1,045 tonnes (2024), continuing at pace in 2025-26 — this is the mirror image of reduced UST demand.
- **KB anchor**: dedollarization_pace concept confirms 0.5-0.8pp annual decline in USD reserve share, but notes this is "tactically irrelevant" against daily FX turnover. I partially disagree for yield curve purposes: marginal buyer composition at Treasury auctions matters more than aggregate FX turnover. The decline in foreign official holdings as a share of outstanding Treasuries (from ~34% to ~24% over the past decade) directly impacts term premium through reduced price-insensitive demand.

### Claim 3: Recession Signal Degradation

The historical record (inversions preceding recessions in 1969, 1973, 1980, 1981, 1989, 2000, 2006) was established in an era where:
- Term premium was relatively stable or declining
- Foreign central bank demand was a stable, slowly-growing bid
- Geopolitical fiscal commitments were relatively small (post-Cold War "peace dividend")
- Central banks did not hold large portions of the sovereign curve

All four conditions have reversed. The KB's concept of vol-regime dependence is correct (MOVE index matters for signal interpretation), but does not fully capture the geopolitical noise channel. Specifically:
- Geopolitical escalation → higher defense spending expectations → bear steepening (long end up)
- Geopolitical de-escalation → partial reversal → incomplete flattening
- These movements overlay the expectations channel and reduce the signal-to-noise ratio

The practical implication: yield curve models that decompose into expectations + term premium using traditional ACM/Kim-Wright frameworks may attribute geopolitical supply pressure to "term premium" but fail to distinguish it from recession-relevant term premium changes.

### Claim 4: Election Cycle Effects

Evidence from recent cycles:
- **2016**: 10Y rose ~85bp post-Trump election (Nov-Dec 2016) on fiscal expansion expectations, steepening the curve ~40bp
- **2020**: Post-election "blue wave" pricing steepened 2s10s by ~30bp as markets priced larger stimulus
- **2024**: Pre-election uncertainty widened term premium; post-election curve repriced on tariff/fiscal expectations
- **Academic evidence**: Julio & Yook (2012) document reduced corporate investment in election years; Pastor & Veronesi (2013) show political uncertainty commands a risk premium

The belly (5Y) is most sensitive because it spans the full presidential term: a new president's fiscal trajectory is most impactful at the 3-5Y horizon.

### Claim 5: Geopolitical Correlation Ratchet

The KB concept geopolitical_correlation_ratchet directly supports this: "escalation triggers rapid positive correlation spikes while de-escalation produces slow, partial normalization." Applied to term premium:

- **Russia-Ukraine (2022)**: European gas prices spiked 10x, adding ~200-300bp to European break-even inflation. Term premium on Bunds rose ~80bp. After gas prices normalized (~75% decline from peak), Bund term premium declined only ~30bp — permanent residual from defense spending commitments.
- **US-China tech decoupling (2019-present)**: Each escalation (entity list additions, CHIPS Act, export controls) added ~5-10bp to UST term premium through fiscal spending expectations. No subsequent de-escalation removed it.
- **Mechanism**: Geopolitical escalation creates fiscal commitments with political constituencies (defense contractors, chip fab workers). These constituencies lobby against rollback, making the fiscal expansion permanent.

### Claim 6: Taiwan Contingency

Scenario analysis (conditional on occurrence):

**Phase 1 (Days 1-7)**: Safe-haven flow into front-end USTs (2Y yield drops 50-100bp). Simultaneously, long-end sells off (30Y yield rises 50-100bp) on expectations of massive fiscal response. Net: 2s30s steepens 100-200bp.

**Phase 2 (Weeks 2-8)**: Chinese UST liquidation or freeze (depending on sanctions structure) removes $770B demand base. MOVE index spikes to 200+ (from current ~100). Basis trade unwind (KB: $800B-$1T at 50-100x leverage) amplifies long-end selling. 30Y term premium rises 100-200bp.

**Phase 3 (Months 2-12)**: Emergency fiscal legislation ($500B-$1T defense supplemental). Fed likely forced to resume QE to absorb Treasury market dysfunction (as in March 2020, when $1.6T was required for a smaller shock). Curve shape becomes a policy variable, not a market signal.

The current pricing gap: CDS on Chinese sovereign is ~50-70bp, implying <5% 5Y cumulative probability of severe distress. Taiwan-related conflict risk is embedded in this low price. Yet the conditional yield curve impact would be the largest since WWII.

### Claim 7: International Spillovers

- **BOJ normalization**: Japanese life insurers hold ~$400B in foreign bonds (primarily USTs). As JGB yields rise, the hedging cost for UST positions increases (FX basis at ~150bp) while domestic returns improve. Net unhedged yield advantage of USTs over JGBs has compressed from ~300bp (2022) to ~150bp (early 2026). Marginal reallocation to JGBs reduces UST demand.
- **European fiscal expansion**: Germany's debt brake reform, EU defense bonds discussion, and national defense spending increases will add EUR 200-400B annually in European sovereign supply. This competes with USTs for the same institutional demand pool.
- **Transmission**: International yield curve spillovers operate through (a) portfolio substitution (institutional asset allocation), (b) FX-hedged relative value (cross-currency basis), and (c) global savings pool competition (total sovereign supply vs. total investable savings).

### Claim 8: Central Bank Independence Erosion

The political economy logic is straightforward: when debt/GDP is high, rate increases are fiscally painful. At 125% debt/GDP and 5% effective interest rate, US federal interest expense is ~6% of GDP (~$1.2T annually). Each 100bp increase adds ~$300B annually with a lag.

Evidence of erosion:
- **US**: Trump's repeated criticism of Fed Chair Powell, floating of dismissal (legally constrained but politically unprecedented at this intensity). Congressional proposals to audit the Fed (FORM Act variants).
- **Turkey**: Erdogan fired three central bank governors (2019-2021) who raised rates, causing lira depreciation of ~80%. Eventually reversed, but demonstrated the fiscal dominance equilibrium.
- **UK (Truss, 2022)**: Mini-budget fiscal expansion without BoE coordination produced 100bp+ Gilt yield spike in days — a validation event for how markets price fiscal-monetary conflict.
- **Japan**: BOJ maintained YCC for years partly due to political pressure related to fiscal sustainability of JGB yields.

The yield curve prices this as a steepening bias: even if the front end is anchored by current policy rates, the long end embeds a premium for the possibility that future central banks will be constrained from fighting inflation. This is distinct from (and additive to) traditional inflation expectations.

### Claim 9: Two-Tier Global Yield Curve

The sanctions architecture (KB: sanctions_architecture_structural_break) removed ~$3-5T from investable universe and imposed $40-60B annual compliance costs. For yield curves specifically:

- **Allied bloc convergence**: US, EU, UK, Japan curves move together because they share: (a) similar defense fiscal pressures, (b) correlated energy price exposure, (c) capital flow concentration as sanctioned-jurisdiction capital is redirected within the alliance.
- **Non-aligned bloc divergence**: China's yield curve (10Y at ~2.1% vs US 4.3%) reflects entirely different policy constraints — property sector deflation, capital controls, and PBOC's operational independence from global rate cycles. India's curve reflects domestic inflation dynamics and RBI's autonomy within capital account restrictions.
- **Broken arbitrage**: Traditional carry trades assumed free capital flow between sovereign curves. Sanctions, capital controls, and compliance costs have created friction that prevents convergence. The US-China 10Y spread (~220bp) is not arbitrageable for most global institutions.

### Claim 10: Geopolitical Steepening Trap

The KB's steepening_trap concept (recessions arrive during steepening, not inversion) must be extended to include a geopolitical dimension. Two types of steepening:

- **Benign steepening**: Fed cuts → front end falls → curve steepens → reflects growth normalization expectations. This is the market's preferred narrative.
- **Malignant steepening**: Geopolitical fiscal commitments → long end rises → curve steepens → reflects fiscal supply pressure and term premium rebuilding. This is the dangerous version.

The current 2s10s at +20-40bp is ambiguous precisely because both forces are operating simultaneously. The distinguishing diagnostic is the source of the steepening: if the 2Y is falling (benign) versus the 10Y rising (potentially malignant). Current data shows mixed signals with both ends moving, but the long-end has been more volatile — consistent with geopolitical fiscal supply pressure contributing to steepening.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Monetary Policy (monetary_policy)
Central bank independence erosion (Claim 8) is the bridge between geopolitical/political economy dynamics and monetary policy transmission. If fiscal dominance constrains central bank tightening, the yield curve loses its monetary policy signaling function and becomes primarily a fiscal risk barometer. This connects to the KB's fiscal_dominance_five_signal_sequence — geopolitical fiscal lock-in accelerates progression through the fiscal dominance sequence by making discretionary spending cuts politically impossible.

### Credit Cycles (credit_cycles)
Term premium rebuilding driven by geopolitical fiscal supply (Claim 1) transmits to credit markets through the risk-free rate benchmark: higher long-end Treasury yields raise the floor for corporate borrowing costs, potentially triggering credit stress in leveraged issuers who financed at QE-era spreads. The steepening trap (Claim 10) is particularly relevant to credit: if steepening reflects fiscal stress rather than growth, credit spreads should widen — but if markets interpret steepening as benign, spread compression continues until a disorderly repricing.

### Rates-Equity Correlation (rates_equity_correlation)
The KB's correlation_regime_shifts concept is directly linked to geopolitical term premium dynamics. Geopolitical fiscal lock-in pushes correlation toward the positive regime (stocks and bonds falling together) because higher sovereign supply raises discount rates for equities while simultaneously increasing bond yields. The maturity bifurcation (2Y negative correlation, 30Y positive) is exactly what the geopolitical supply story would predict: front-end is monetary policy (inversely correlated with risk assets), long-end is fiscal supply (positively correlated).

### Sovereign Debt (sovereign_debt)
The reflexive term premium loop (KB concept) is accelerated by geopolitical fiscal commitments: geopolitical spending → higher issuance → higher term premium → higher interest costs → yet more issuance → yet higher term premium. If this loop combines with the geopolitical ratchet (Claim 5), the term premium floor keeps rising with each geopolitical episode, bringing the reflexive loop's critical threshold closer.

### Fiscal Policy (fiscal_policy)
Geopolitical fiscal constraints (KB concept) explain why traditional fiscal consolidation analysis fails in the current environment. Markets expecting deficit reduction from spending cuts must account for the 2-3% of GDP floor created by defense and industrial policy commitments. Yield curve models that assume mean-reverting fiscal deficits will systematically underestimate term premium.

### Energy Transition (energy_transition)
Energy transition spending (IRA $369-800B+, EU Green Deal) overlaps with geopolitical spending through the concept of "energy security." Fiscal commitments justified on both energy transition and geopolitical grounds (domestic semiconductor manufacturing, critical minerals processing, grid hardening) are doubly locked-in, as they serve two political constituencies simultaneously.

### Commodities (commodities)
The geopolitical supply fragmentation premium (KB concept) transmits to the yield curve through inflation expectations. Structural fragmentation of energy and commodity supply chains embeds a 50-100bp inflation floor above the pre-2020 regime. This supports a structurally higher neutral rate, steeper curve at the long end, and persistent term premium.

### Corporate Credit (corporate_credit)
Strategic geopolitical zombies (KB concept) represent a new category of credit risk where national security rationale sustains firms that would otherwise fail. These firms issue debt priced off the sovereign curve with an implicit government backstop, further compressing the information content of corporate credit spreads and muddying the yield curve's signaling for credit conditions.

## Open Questions

1. **Can we empirically decompose the geopolitical contribution to term premium?** Current ACM/Kim-Wright models attribute term premium to a residual without distinguishing fiscal supply, inflation uncertainty, geopolitical risk, and foreign demand components. A geopolitical sub-model (using defense spending legislation, sanctions events, and foreign official holdings data) might allow cleaner decomposition. What share of the current 50-130bp term premium is geopolitical?

2. **What is the threshold at which foreign official demand decline becomes self-reinforcing?** The KB notes dedollarization proceeds at 0.5-0.8pp/year and is tactically irrelevant. But is there a tipping point where declining foreign demand raises term premium, which further discourages foreign holdings (higher duration risk), creating a vicious cycle? Where is that threshold — 50% reserve share? 45%?

3. **How should we calibrate Taiwan contingency probability?** The 5-10% over 5 years estimate is widely cited but poorly grounded. Political science models, military readiness assessments, and economic interdependence metrics give conflicting signals. A Bayesian framework incorporating US-China diplomatic tone, PLA exercises, semiconductor stockpiling, and sanctions pre-positioning would be more robust but is not yet formalized.

4. **Is the geopolitical correlation ratchet permanent or does it decay over longer horizons?** Post-Cold War, defense spending declined from ~6% to ~3% of GDP over two decades, suggesting the ratchet can reverse — but only over generational timescales. Does the current geopolitical environment more closely resemble the Cold War onset (ratchet up for decades) or the post-Gulf War period (temporary escalation followed by peace dividend)?

5. **What is the cross-elasticity between allied and non-aligned yield curves?** If the sanctions-driven two-tier system persists, traditional models of global rate determination break down. How much does Chinese monetary easing affect UST yields in the new regime? Is the transmission coefficient declining toward zero, or merely attenuated?

6. **How would markets distinguish benign from malignant steepening in real-time?** The diagnostic I propose (source: front-end falling vs. long-end rising) is a starting point, but could be refined with: (a) decomposing steepening into real rate vs. breakeven components, (b) tracking defense appropriations bill progress as a steepening driver, (c) monitoring Treasury auction tails and foreign participation as demand indicators.

7. **What is the interaction between election cycles and geopolitical risk appetite?** Do lame-duck periods increase geopolitical risk-taking (and therefore term premium) as outgoing administrations have reduced electoral accountability? The Biden-Trump transition period and its implications for Ukraine/Taiwan policy suggests this channel exists but is poorly studied.

8. **How do geopolitical risks interact with the basis trade amplification mechanism?** The KB documents $800B-$1T in basis trades at 50-100x leverage. A geopolitical shock that produces sudden long-end selling would trigger basis trade unwinds. But would the safe-haven front-end bid be sufficient to prevent repo market dysfunction, or would the unwind overwhelm both ends of the curve simultaneously?
