# Yield Curve Dynamics — Structured & Private Credit Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The yield curve's most important function for credit markets is not recession signaling — it is determining CLO arbitrage viability, which is the on/off switch for ~65-70% of institutional leveraged loan demand.** The 2s10s slope and the absolute level of SOFR jointly determine whether CLO equity tranches can clear their 12-15% IRR hurdle. This mechanical linkage matters far more to credit creation than the probabilistic recession signal that dominates macro commentary.

2. **Term premium decomposition is not merely an academic exercise — it determines whether the maturity wall produces orderly refinancing or a liquidity crisis.** Supply-driven term premium (from $2T+ annual net Treasury issuance and QT runoff) raises the all-in refinancing cost for sub-IG borrowers without any offsetting growth improvement, making it qualitatively worse for credit than expectations-driven curve moves. A 50bp increase in term premium translates approximately 1:1 into higher CLO AAA tranche yields, collapsing arbitrage economics, whereas a 50bp increase from growth expectations raises base rates but also improves borrower cash flows and investor risk appetite.

3. **The "steepening trap" operates with a distinct and more severe transmission mechanism in structured credit than in equity markets.** When the curve steepens from the front end falling (Fed cutting), CLO liability costs decline but the signal is recessionary — defaults rise, OC tests trip, and the CLO machine enters a vicious feedback loop. The historical pattern (1990, 2001, 2007) maps directly onto CLO vintage analysis: the best CLO equity returns are earned during steepening when the manager can reinvest at wider spreads, but only for CLOs with active reinvestment periods. Post-reinvestment CLOs facing the current $250-350B expiration cliff cannot benefit and instead suffer from rising defaults on legacy portfolios.

4. **Curve inversion created the conditions for the current private credit explosion, and curve normalization now threatens the sustainability of that $1.7T asset class.** During 2022-2024 inversion, private credit's floating-rate, SOFR-plus lending generated 12-14% unlevered yields that attracted massive LP inflows. As the curve normalizes and SOFR declines, private credit yields compress toward 9-10%, eroding the "illiquidity premium" argument precisely when vintage 2021-2022 deals begin hitting performance issues. The yield curve shape is thus the single best predictor of private credit fundraising and deployment pace.

5. **The interaction between curve shape and CLO reinvestment expiration creates a regime-dependent credit supply function that is fundamentally different from the bank-intermediated model the yield curve's recession-signaling power was calibrated on.** The yield curve's 7-for-7 recession prediction record was established when bank lending dominated credit creation. In a world where CLOs, private credit, and direct lending comprise the majority of sub-IG credit supply, the curve's signal must be read through the lens of structured credit economics, not bank NIM dynamics.

6. **International yield curve spillovers operate primarily through the CLO AAA tranche market, where Japanese institutional investors are the marginal buyer, creating a yen-carry-trade feedback loop.** A steepening of the JGB curve (from BOJ normalization) draws Japanese capital home, widening CLO AAA spreads in USD, which cascades into CLO formation economics and ultimately leveraged loan supply. This channel is under-monitored because it crosses the usual analytical silos between rates, credit, and FX.

7. **The current curve configuration (+20-40bp 2s10s) combined with SOFR at ~4.3-4.5% places CLO arbitrage in a marginal-viability zone where small curve moves produce binary outcomes for credit supply.** At SOFR ~3.5%, CLO equity IRRs clear hurdle rates, formation is robust, and the leveraged loan market is well-bid. At SOFR ~5.5%, arbitrage is dead. The current position at ~4.3-4.5% means CLO formation is occurring but with compressed equity returns, making the system acutely sensitive to curve moves of as little as 50bp in either direction.

8. **Post-QE yield curve signals are systematically degraded as leading indicators for credit stress because private credit borrowers (~$1.7T) exist entirely outside the transmission channels the curve traditionally forecasts through.** Private credit deals are floating-rate (SOFR+500-700bp), privately negotiated, and repriced on manager-determined marks, not market clearing. Yield curve movements that would trigger spread widening and capital rationing in public leveraged loan markets produce delayed and smoothed effects in private credit, creating a false sense of stability in aggregate credit conditions.

**Claim 3 — Structured Credit Steepening Trap Mechanics:**
The KB's steepening_trap concept (confidence 7.0) correctly identifies that recession arrives during steepening. For CLOs specifically, the mechanism is as follows: (a) The Fed cuts rates, reducing SOFR and CLO liability costs — this is initially positive for CLO equity returns. (b) But the Fed is cutting because the economy is weakening, which raises default rates on leveraged loan portfolios. (c) The CCC bucket in CLO portfolios begins to swell toward the 7.5% OC test threshold (per KB). (d) Once OC tests trip, the CLO must redirect interest payments from equity to retire senior tranches — equity is wiped out. (e) The CLO stops buying new loans, removing the marginal bid. The steepening trap for structured credit is thus a sequence: lower funding costs appear beneficial → defaults rise → OC tests trip → credit supply contracts → defaults accelerate. The 18-month lag between steepening onset and CLO OC test failure matches the historical 12-18 month lag between curve un-inversion and recession declaration.

**Claim 4 — Curve Shape and Private Credit Sustainability:**

The curve's normalization path thus determines whether private credit experiences a gentle fundraising slowdown or a Minsky moment as lower yields coincide with vintage deterioration.

**Claim 5 — Structural Shift in Credit Intermediation:**
The yield curve's recession-forecasting record was built during 1960-2006, when banks intermediated the bulk of credit. A flat/inverted curve squeezed bank NIMs, reduced lending, and tightened credit — a direct, mechanical transmission. Today, banks hold a minority of sub-IG credit. CLOs, private credit funds, and BDCs collectively intermediate more. These vehicles have entirely different yield curve sensitivities: CLOs are sensitive to the curve shape through arbitrage economics (short-end liability costs vs. floating-rate asset income); private credit funds are primarily sensitive to the absolute level of SOFR (which determines floating-rate income); BDCs are sensitive to both SOFR level and equity market conditions (affecting their ability to raise capital). The yield curve's signal must now be filtered through these multiple, sometimes contradictory channels.

If Japanese demand withdraws, CLO AAA spreads widen from SOFR+140-170 toward SOFR+200+, collapsing new-issue CLO arbitrage. This is a yield-curve-to-yield-curve international transmission channel that operates through the structured credit plumbing.

**Claim 7 — Binary Regime at Current SOFR:**
Per KB, CLO arbitrage is "functioning at SOFR ~3.5%, dead at SOFR ~5.5%." Current SOFR at ~4.3-4.5% places the system in the marginal zone. This knife-edge sensitivity means yield curve movements of a magnitude that would be unremarkable in a rates context produce regime changes in credit supply.

**Claim 8 — Private Credit as Signal Degrader:**
The KB establishes at confidence 7.5 that private credit exists outside all public indices, default statistics, and financial conditions indices. Private credit borrowers' exposure to yield curve movements is filtered through: (a) manager discretion on marking (NAV smoothing with autocorrelation 0.5-0.7 per KB), (b) PIK toggles that defer cash interest and mask the impact of higher floating rates, and (c) the absence of mark-to-market requirements for unfunded LP commitments. This 12-18 month smoothing delay means the yield curve's real-time signaling power for aggregate credit conditions has degraded in proportion to private credit's market share growth.
