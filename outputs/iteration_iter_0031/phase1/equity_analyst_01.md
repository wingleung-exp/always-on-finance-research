## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Yield Curve as Decomposed Equity Signal

The raw 2s10s spread conflates two orthogonal signals. An inverted curve can mean either (a) the market expects rate cuts due to recession (bearish equities via earnings), or (b) term premium compression from QE/flight-to-quality (ambiguous for equities). The equity strategy implication diverges completely:

- **Expectations-driven inversion** → overweight defensives, underweight cyclicals. The earnings recession is coming. Historical template: 2006-2007.
- **Term-premium-driven inversion** → the signal is contaminated. The 2022-2023 inversion, with ACM term premium compressed by 80-120bp and MOVE elevated above 120, did not produce a recession on its historical schedule. Equity markets rallied 25%+ through the inversion.

The KB confirms this with confidence 7/10: "pure inversion is insufficient in the QT era — inversion from expected-rate compression signals recession while inversion from term premium compression may not." For equity strategists, this means every curve-based allocation signal must pass through the ACM or Kim-Wright filter first. The 30-50bp divergence between models introduces estimation uncertainty, but directionally both confirm the current steepening is term-premium-led.

### Claim 2: Supply-Driven Term Premium as Equity Headwind

The critical insight is the tripartite decomposition of what drives rates higher:

| Driver | Equity Impact | Historical Template |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| Growth-driven rate rise | Positive (earnings growth > multiple compression) | 2003-2006 |
| Policy-driven rate rise | Ambiguous (Fed is reacting to data) | 2016-2018 |
| Supply-driven term premium | Negative (pure discount rate shock, no growth offset) | 2013 Taper Tantrum, 2023 Q3 |

Current $2T+ annual Treasury issuance is creating persistent supply pressure. The KB documents this clearly: "supply-driven term premium from post-2020 fiscal expansion is a qualitatively different and worse type of tightening than policy-driven, with no growth offset." The 2023 Q3 episode is the clean template — the 10Y rose from 4.1% to 5.0% driven overwhelmingly by term premium, and the S&P 500 fell ~10% in sympathy. Critically, earnings estimates did not decline materially during that drawdown — it was purely a discount rate event.

For sector allocation, this means the rate-sensitive sectors (Utilities, REITs, homebuilders) are not the primary victims. Rather, the long-duration growth stocks (30-40x forward PE names where 70%+ of present value derives from cash flows beyond year 5) are most exposed. A 75bp term premium increase translates to approximately 8-12% fair value decline for a company with a 15-year modified duration versus only 3-5% for a company with a 5-year modified duration.

### Claim 3: ERP Inconsistency with Correlation Regime

This is the most important structural claim for equity allocation. The math:

- The 2010-2020 era justification for sub-3% ERP rested on: (1) low inflation, (2) negative stock-bond correlation (bonds as hedge), (3) central bank put. All three reduced the true risk of equity ownership.
- In 2026: (1) structural inflation floor at 2.3-2.8%, (2) long-end stock-bond correlation has turned positive (+0.05 to +0.15 for 30Y-SPX per KB), (3) the central bank put is constrained by fiscal dominance.

The Gordon Growth Model sensitivity is stark. At 4.5% nominal 10Y yield and 2.5-3.0% ERP, the implied cost of equity is 7.0-7.5%. But if the correlation regime demands a higher ERP — say 3.5-4.0% to compensate for loss of hedge — the implied fair P/E drops from ~21x to ~17-18x. That's a 15-20% downside from current levels, just from re-rating the risk premium, with zero change in earnings.

The KB entry on `valuation_regime_inflation_dependence` (confidence 8/10) confirms: "fair value at ~18-19x if correlation regime shifted persistently positive, implying 10-25% overvaluation."

### Claim 4: Factor Rotation to Quality/Low Vol

The factor rotation logic chains directly from the term premium environment:

**Why Value underperforms in term-premium-driven tightening:**
- Russell 1000 Value carries 22-25% Financials weight plus 5-7% REITs (per KB: `factor_rotation_cre_repricing`)
- Financials' net interest margins are positively correlated with curve steepness *from expectations*, but term-premium-driven steepening raises funding costs without corresponding loan demand growth
- Value companies carry higher leverage on average (1.5-2.0x net debt/EBITDA vs 0.5-1.0x for Quality), making them more sensitive to refinancing cost increases
- The CRE exposure in Value (regional banks, REITs) adds further vulnerability during the extend-and-pretend cycle

**Why Quality outperforms:**
- High return on equity, low leverage, stable margins
- In a rising-term-premium environment, the "bird in hand" of near-term cash flows is more valuable than distant growth
- Quality factor has historically outperformed by 200-400bp annualized during the 12 months following curve un-inversion (1990, 2001, 2007 templates)

**Why Size (small-cap) underperforms:**
- Russell 2000 companies refinance 15-20% of their debt annually on floating or short-maturity terms
- Each 100bp increase in effective funding costs reduces aggregate small-cap EPS by an estimated 4-6%
- Small-caps' higher domestic revenue share (78% vs 60% for large-caps) means they benefit less from international diversification of the term premium shock

### Claim 5: Steepening Trap

The steepening trap is one of the most robust patterns in equity macro. The sequence:

1. Curve inverts (t=0) → equity markets often rally because the inversion is gradual and the economy is still expanding
2. Curve un-inverts/steepens (t+12 to t+24 months) → markets treat this as "the danger has passed"
3. Recession arrives during steepening (t+14 to t+30 months) → equity drawdown of 20-55%

Historical record (KB-confirmed, confidence 7/10):
- **1989**: 2s10s un-inverted in March 1990 → recession began July 1990 → S&P fell 20%
- **2001**: 2s10s un-inverted in December 2000 → recession began March 2001 → S&P fell 49% peak-to-trough
- **2007**: 2s10s un-inverted in June 2007 → recession began December 2007 → S&P fell 57% peak-to-trough

Current positioning: 2s10s at +20-40bp, having un-inverted in late 2024. We are in the historical window where the steepening trap activates. The equity market consensus appears to be pricing this as a "soft landing confirmed" signal, which is precisely the mistake the market made in early 1990, early 2001, and mid-2007.

The critical nuance: in the 1993-1995 cycle, the curve un-inverted and no recession followed. The distinguishing variable was the fiscal trajectory — the Clinton consolidation reduced term premium by 80-100bp, providing a genuine growth tailwind. The current fiscal trajectory ($2T+ deficits) is the opposite.

### Claim 6: BOJ Normalization Spillover

This is the most structurally underpriced risk in the global yield curve complex. The mechanism:

1. BOJ holds ~$1.2T in UST equivalents through the Japanese institutional channel (lifers, GPIF, Norinchukin, postal savings)
2. Each 25bp BOJ rate hike narrows the Fed-BOJ spread, reducing the carry incentive for Japanese UST holdings
3. At a BOJ policy rate of 0.75-1.0% (plausible by late 2026/early 2027), JGB 10Y yields of 1.5-2.0% would compete with currency-hedged UST yields for the first time since 2007
4. Marginal repatriation of even 10-15% of the Japanese UST channel ($120-180bn) would add ~20-30bp to US term premium

For US equities, this is a pure discount rate shock with no offsetting benefit. Unlike Fed tightening (which at least signals growth strength), BOJ normalization transmits through the supply channel — more duration for the market to absorb. The MOVE index spikes in October 2023 and August 2024 both had a significant BOJ-related component.

**Equity sector implication:** the sectors most exposed are those with the longest-duration cash flows and the tightest credit spreads — essentially the mega-cap tech names that dominate the S&P 500. These stocks' valuations were built on a world of abundant, cheap, price-insensitive duration buyers. That world is eroding.

### Claim 7: Post-QE Signal Contamination

The yield curve predicted 7 of 7 pre-2008 recessions with a lead time of 12-24 months. This record is the foundation of every "yield curve as equity timing signal" strategy. But the information structure has changed:

**Pre-QE**: The curve was primarily driven by rate expectations and a relatively stable term premium. Inversion meant the market expected rate cuts. Signal-to-noise was high.

**Post-QE**: QE/QT directly manipulates term premium by 100-200bp. The Fed's balance sheet went from $900B (2008) to $9T (2022) to ~$7T (2026). This makes the curve an unreliable expectations signal without volatility adjustment.

The diagnostic I propose: **MOVE-adjusted curve signal**.
- When MOVE < 90: curve signal reliable (the 2019 inversion correctly predicted slowdown)
- When MOVE 90-120: curve signal partially contaminated (adjust for term premium)
- When MOVE > 120: curve signal unreliable without full ACM decomposition

Currently (March 2026), MOVE is in the 90-110 range, placing us in the partially contaminated zone. This means the current +20-40bp steepness cannot be taken at face value — it may be masking a more bearish expectations component underneath a large term premium add-on.

### Claim 9: Concentration Risk and Duration Sensitivity

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary Policy (depth 3, confidence 5.6):** The yield curve is the primary transmission mechanism for monetary policy to equity markets. The disconnect between the Fed's policy rate and long-end yields (driven by term premium) is a measure of how much control the Fed has lost over financial conditions. If the Fed cuts 100bp but term premium rises 100bp simultaneously, the equity market experiences *zero* easing. This is the fiscal dominance channel — monetary policy becomes subordinate to Treasury supply dynamics.

**Credit Cycles (depth 4, confidence 6.0):** The term premium tripartite decomposition maps directly to credit spread behavior. Supply-driven term premium increases widen credit spreads because they tighten financial conditions without growth offset. The credit-equity basis — where credit leads equity by 2-3 months at cycle turns — should be monitored alongside the term premium signal. If HY spreads widen 50bp+ while the curve is steepening, the steepening trap probability increases materially.

**Rates-Equity Correlation (depth 6, confidence 6.2):** This is the most deeply researched adjacent topic. The maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation (2Y-SPX negative, 30Y-SPX positive) is the diagnostic fingerprint of a term-premium-driven market. This bifurcation directly informs optimal hedging strategy: use 2Y Treasuries (still negatively correlated with equities) rather than 30Y (now positively correlated) for portfolio hedging. The traditional 60/40 portfolio using aggregate bonds (duration ~6-7 years) is caught in the middle.

**Sovereign Debt (depth 3, confidence 5.6):** The $2T+ annual deficit is the root cause of the term premium rebuild. The deficit-term-premium doom loop (wider deficits → higher term premium → higher debt service costs → wider deficits) is a slow-moving but potentially powerful feedback mechanism.

**Real Estate Cycles (depth from iter_0030):** The CRE extend-and-pretend cycle is directly connected to term premium dynamics. Higher term premium raises cap rates, depresses CRE valuations, and increases the probability that extend-and-pretend breaks down. **Inflation Regimes:** The structural inflation floor of 2.3-2.8% is what prevents the pre-2020 regime from returning.
