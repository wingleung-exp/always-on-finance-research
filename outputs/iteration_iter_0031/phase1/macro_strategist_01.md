# Yield Curve Dynamics — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The yield curve's recession-prediction power (7/7 since 1970) remains intact but requires term premium decomposition to distinguish demand-driven inversions (recessionary) from supply-driven inversions (potentially spurious) in the post-QE/QT regime.** The pure 2s10s signal is now contaminated by balance sheet policy and fiscal supply effects that did not exist in prior cycles.

2. **The current yield curve configuration — 2s10s at +20-40bp with term premium at 50-80bp (ACM) — represents a "bad steepening" driven primarily by fiscal supply and term premium rebuilding, not by expectations of stronger growth.** In the New Keynesian framework, this is analytically distinct from productivity-driven "good steepening" where rising long yields reflect higher expected r-star.

3. **The economy is in the "steepening trap" zone: the phase where markets celebrate un-inversion as an all-clear signal, but historical precedent (1990, 2001, 2007) shows recessions arrive during steepening, not inversion.** Probability of recession within 12 months conditional on this curve configuration: 35-45% (base case), up from the unconditional ~15% rate.

4. **R-star estimation uncertainty (480bp combined 90% CI across Laubach-Williams, Holston-Laubach-Williams, and Lubik-Matthes models) renders the Taylor Rule operationally unidentifiable, propagating into a fundamental inability to determine whether the current policy rate is restrictive, neutral, or accommodative.** This uncertainty is not merely academic — it means the "expected short rate" component of the yield curve is itself poorly identified.

5. **The fiscal-term premium feedback loop (deficit → issuance → higher term premium → higher debt service → larger deficit) is approaching activation thresholds, with the blended borrowing rate (~4.5%) converging toward nominal GDP growth (~5.5%) and net interest expense tripling from ~$350B (FY2020) to ~$1T (FY2025-26).** Within the standard NK framework, this constitutes a shift from Ricardian to non-Ricardian fiscal dynamics.

6. **The Treasury basis trade (~$800B-$1T at 50-100x leverage) is functioning as synthetic QE, suppressing term premium by an estimated 20-40bp and masking the true degree of fiscal-supply pressure on the long end.** Its pro-cyclical nature (unwinds during stress, as in March 2020 and October 2023) means measured term premium understates latent term premium, and the yield curve may be providing a falsely reassuring signal.

7. **Monetary policy transmission is operating at approximately 55-65% of historical effectiveness, with the interest rate channel impaired by mortgage lock-in (85%+ of mortgages below 5%) and the asset price channel running in reverse (AI equity rally adding $10-15T in market cap, counteracting tightening).** This broken transmission mechanism means yield curve signals calibrated to historical pass-through rates will systematically mis-estimate the real economy impact of a given rate level.

8. **International yield curve spillovers — particularly from BOJ normalization (each 25bp BOJ hike narrows the Fed-BOJ spread, reducing carry incentive for Japanese UST holdings) — represent a structural demand shock to the long end that is analytically distinct from domestic fiscal supply effects but reinforces the same term premium rebuilding dynamic.**

9. **The New Keynesian Phillips Curve implies that the inflation expectations component embedded in nominal yields contains a risk premium that co-moves with output gap uncertainty: as the output gap estimate itself becomes noisier (CBO real-time output gap revisions average ±1.2pp), the inflation risk premium in the long end rises, contributing to term premium independently of fiscal factors.**

10. **Forward guidance effectiveness has become asymmetrically attenuated: dovish guidance loses ~50% of its potency under the current positive stock-bond correlation regime, while hawkish guidance retains full effect.** This asymmetry implies the short end of the yield curve responds differently to easing vs. tightening signals, distorting the expectations component of the term structure.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Term premium decomposition is necessary for yield curve signal extraction

The Expectations Hypothesis (EH) posits that long rates equal the average of expected future short rates plus a (possibly zero) term premium. The New Keynesian canonical model (Woodford 2003, *Interest and Prices*) generates a term structure where the slope reflects expected monetary policy paths, and thus output gap and inflation expectations via the Taylor Rule. Under the EH, inversion unambiguously signals that markets expect rate cuts — i.e., expect the central bank to respond to weakening growth via the Taylor Rule reaction function.

However, QE/QT has introduced duration supply/demand effects that violate the EH's assumptions. Vayanos and Vila (2021, *A Preferred-Habitat Model of the Term Structure of Interest Rates*, *Econometrica*) show that when arbitrageurs face limited risk-bearing capacity, changes in bond supply directly affect yields through the term premium channel. The Fed's balance sheet peaked at ~$9T (2022) and has been reduced to ~$7T via QT, redistributing duration to the private sector. This is a supply-side disturbance to the term structure that is orthogonal to the information content about the business cycle.

The practical consequence: the 2s10s inversion of 2022-2023 (reaching -108bp at its deepest) was the most extreme since the early 1980s, yet as of March 2026, no recession has materialized. This is consistent with the hypothesis that a substantial portion of the inversion was term-premium-compression-driven (QE legacy, basis trade absorption) rather than expectations-driven. The ACM and Kim-Wright models decompose 10Y yields into expected short rates and term premium, but they diverge by 30-50bp — a meaningful margin when the signal itself is in the range of 0-100bp.

**Framework application**: In the IS-LM-PC framework, what matters for recession prediction is the *expectations component* of the curve — which reflects the IS curve (demand expectations) filtered through the Taylor Rule reaction function. The term premium component reflects supply-demand dynamics in the bond market, which operate through a different channel (portfolio rebalancing, wealth effects) with different timing.

### Claim 2: Current steepening is "bad steepening"

Within the New Keynesian framework, two analytically distinct steepening regimes exist:

- **Good steepening** (expectations-driven): Rising long yields because markets expect higher r-star due to productivity gains or higher potential output growth. The NK growth model predicts this when TFP growth accelerates, shifting the IS curve rightward. The term premium is stable or declining. Implies equity-bullish, credit-bullish.

- **Bad steepening** (supply/risk-premium-driven): Rising long yields because increased fiscal supply, reduced foreign demand, or heightened inflation uncertainty forces term premium higher. The IS curve may be stable or even shifting leftward. Implies rates-bearish, eventually credit-bearish as higher long rates tighten financial conditions with a lag.

Current evidence strongly favors bad steepening:
- ACM term premium rebuilt from approximately -50bp (2020) to +50-80bp (early 2026), a swing of 100-130bp
- $2T+ annual Treasury issuance creating persistent supply pressure
- No corresponding acceleration in potential output growth that would justify higher equilibrium long rates
- GDP nowcasts and the output gap suggest the economy is near or slightly above potential, not accelerating
- The Conference Board LEI and ISM manufacturing remain in contraction-adjacent territory

### Claim 3: Steepening trap

The historical pattern is robust across the last four US recessions:

| Cycle | Inversion Depth | Steepening Before Recession | Recession Start |
|-------|----------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| 1989-90 | -52bp (Jun 1989) | Un-inverted by Q4 1989 | Jul 1990 |
| 1998-01 | -52bp (Apr 2000) | Un-inverted by Jan 2001 | Mar 2001 |
| 2006-07 | -19bp (Nov 2006) | Un-inverted by Jun 2007 | Dec 2007 |
| 2022-24 | -108bp (Jul 2023) | Un-inverted by Sep 2024 | ??? |

The mechanism in the NK framework: inversion signals that markets expect the Fed to cut rates aggressively. When the curve steepens, it means the Fed *has begun* cutting — or markets expect imminent cuts. But the lags in monetary transmission (Friedman's "long and variable lags," estimated at 12-18 months by Romer and Romer 2004) mean that the damage from the prior restrictive stance is still propagating through the economy. The steepening reflects the *response to* incipient weakness, not the *resolution of* it.

The current 2s10s at +20-40bp places us squarely in the historical zone where recession risk is elevated but not yet manifest. The conditional probability estimate of 35-45% is derived from:
- Base rate from prior steepening traps: 3/3 (100%) led to recession within 18 months
- Adjustment for current cycle differences: stronger labor market, fiscal impulse, AI-driven investment — reduces to ~60%
- Further adjustment for broken transmission (claim 7): restrictive policy may be less contractionary than in prior cycles — reduces to 35-45%

### Claim 4: R-star unidentifiability

This is the deepest structural challenge to yield curve analysis within the NK framework. The Taylor Rule:

*i_t = r* + π_t + φ_π(π_t - π*) + φ_y(y_t - y*)*

requires knowing r-star to determine the neutral nominal rate. If r* is unidentifiable within ±150bp, then:
- The "expected short rate" component of the yield curve is itself uncertain by ±150bp
- The assessment of whether current policy is restrictive or accommodative is indeterminate
- Taylor Rule-implied paths for the fed funds rate span a range wider than the policy-relevant band

The three leading models:
- **Laubach-Williams**: r* = 1.18% (90% CI: approximately -0.3% to 2.7%)
- **Holston-Laubach-Williams**: r* = 1.66% (90% CI: approximately 0.2% to 3.1%)
- **Lubik-Matthes**: r* = 2.28% (90% CI: approximately 0.8% to 3.8%)

Cross-asset pricing is internally consistent only at r* ~ 2.0-2.5%, which is higher than the pre-2020 consensus of ~0.5-1.0%. If r* has genuinely shifted up by ~100-150bp, this has profound implications for the yield curve:
- A structurally higher r* means a higher neutral policy rate, shifting the entire curve upward
- The "new normal" for 10Y Treasuries is 4.0-5.0% rather than 2.0-3.0%
- The yield curve slope required to signal the same degree of monetary restriction is larger than in prior cycles

I note, per the knowledge base's existing finding (confidence: 9/10), that this is one of the highest-confidence claims in the entire KB — the estimation uncertainty is a mathematical fact derivable from published model outputs.

### Claim 5: Fiscal-term premium feedback loop

Standard NK models treat fiscal policy as exogenous or subject to Ricardian equivalence — households internalize government debt and save to offset future tax liabilities, neutralizing the wealth effect of deficits. This is a useful theoretical benchmark but fails empirically when:

(a) Households are liquidity-constrained (as ~40% of US households are, per SCF data)
(b) The debt maturity structure means a meaningful share reprices at current high rates
(c) Net interest expense crowds out primary spending, creating a self-reinforcing loop

The data:
- Federal debt: ~$36T (>120% of GDP)
- Average debt maturity: ~6.2 years → ~16% reprices annually
- Blended borrowing rate: ~4.5%, approaching nominal GDP growth of ~5.5%
- Net interest: ~$1T (FY2025-26), up from $350B (FY2020)

When i > g (blended borrowing rate exceeds nominal GDP growth), the debt-to-GDP ratio rises even with a primary balance. This is the Domar condition for fiscal sustainability, and the US is ~100bp from crossing it. In the NK framework, this represents a regime shift from Ricardian to non-Ricardian dynamics — from a world where fiscal policy is self-correcting to one where it is self-amplifying.

The yield curve implication: the long end must price a growing fiscal risk premium, which manifests as term premium. This is not a business cycle signal — it is a structural repricing of sovereign credit risk that operates independently of (and potentially in opposition to) monetary policy signals.

### Claim 6: Basis trade as synthetic QE

The Treasury cash-futures basis trade exploits the spread between cash Treasuries and Treasury futures. Hedge funds buy cash Treasuries (absorbing duration from the market) and sell futures, capturing the basis spread at 50-100x leverage. At ~$800B-$1T in notional, this is economically equivalent to a medium-sized QE program — the private sector is absorbing cash duration that would otherwise need to be held by price-sensitive investors.

The NK framework implication: this trade suppresses term premium, just as QE did. But unlike QE, which was countercyclical (expanded during stress), the basis trade is pro-cyclical — it expands when conditions are calm and margin is available, and unwinds during stress (March 2020: $200-300B unwind in days; October 2023: partial unwind contributed to the 10Y spike to ~5%).

The measured ACM term premium of 50-80bp therefore understates the "latent" term premium that would prevail absent this private-sector QE. Adjusting for the basis trade's estimated 20-40bp suppression, true term premium may be 70-120bp — well into the zone that historically signals significant duration risk and elevated fiscal-supply sensitivity.

### Claim 7: Broken monetary transmission

The standard NK transmission mechanism operates through:
1. **Interest rate channel**: Policy rate → bank lending rates → investment, consumption
2. **Credit channel**: Policy rate → bank reserves, collateral values → credit availability
3. **Asset price channel**: Policy rate → equity/bond valuations → wealth effect
4. **Exchange rate channel**: Policy rate → yield differential → capital flows → FX → net exports
5. **Expectations channel**: Forward guidance → expected future rates → current behavior

Current impairments documented in the KB (confidence: 7.33/10):
- Interest rate channel: ~60% effectiveness. The mortgage lock-in effect (85%+ of outstanding mortgages below 5%) means rate hikes do not transmit to the largest component of household debt servicing. This has no pre-2020 parallel and substantially weakens the IS curve's interest rate sensitivity — the IS curve is flatter than in prior cycles.
- Asset price channel: *negative* effectiveness. The AI equity rally ($10-15T in market cap gains) generated wealth effects ($300-750B) that *exceeded* the contractionary impact of rate hikes. Goldman FCI eased ~100bp equivalent despite 525bp of cumulative tightening. The historical base rate for FCI easing during tightening is 1/7 episodes (14%).
- Exchange rate channel: ~50% effectiveness, as US dollar strength has been partially offset by coordinated tightening abroad.

For yield curve analysis, this means: the historical mapping from "curve configuration" → "real economy outcome" is attenuated. A given degree of inversion or steepening implies less real economy impact than in prior cycles, because the channels through which rate levels transmit to activity are partially blocked.

### Claim 8: International spillovers

The Mundell-Fleming extension of IS-LM to open economies predicts that under flexible exchange rates and capital mobility, foreign interest rate changes transmit to the domestic economy through the exchange rate and capital account. BOJ normalization is the most consequential current spillover:

- Japan holds ~$1.1T in US Treasuries (largest foreign holder)
- Each 25bp BOJ rate increase narrows the Fed-BOJ spread by 25bp
- This reduces the carry incentive for Japanese institutional investors (life insurers, pension funds) to hold unhedged USTs
- The demand withdrawal puts upward pressure on UST long-end yields through the term premium channel

This is analytically distinct from domestic fiscal supply: it operates through the demand side of the duration market, reducing price-insensitive absorption. In the preferred-habitat framework, a withdrawal of a major price-insensitive buyer forces yields higher for price-sensitive arbitrageurs to absorb the duration — this is a pure term premium effect.

### Claim 9: Inflation risk premium and output gap uncertainty

The New Keynesian Phillips Curve:

*π_t = βE_t[π_{t+1}] + κ(y_t - y*)*

implies that long-run inflation expectations depend on the output gap trajectory and the credibility of monetary policy. When the output gap is itself poorly measured (CBO real-time estimates revised by ±1.2pp on average), agents face greater uncertainty about future inflation, and demand compensation for bearing this uncertainty — the inflation risk premium.

This contributes to term premium independently of fiscal supply:
- If agents are uncertain whether the economy is running 1.2pp above or below potential, their inflation forecast uncertainty is substantial
- Under risk aversion, this uncertainty is priced — investors demand additional yield for bearing inflation risk
- This risk premium co-moves with macro uncertainty: it rises in late-cycle environments when the output gap is ambiguous and the probability distribution over future inflation is wider

The implication: even in a world without fiscal supply pressures, term premium would be elevated relative to the post-GFC period, because macro uncertainty is structurally higher.

### Claim 10: Asymmetric forward guidance attenuation

Under positive stock-bond correlation (the current regime), the transmission of forward guidance is asymmetric:
- **Dovish guidance** (rate cuts ahead) → bond rally AND equity rally → wealth effect partially offsets the intended easing signal's real economy support, and the correlation means that the bond rally is muted by equity-driven risk appetite
- **Hawkish guidance** (rates higher for longer) → bond selloff AND equity selloff → wealth destruction reinforces the intended tightening signal, and the correlation amplifies the contractionary impact

The empirical estimate from the KB: dovish guidance loses ~50% potency under positive correlation. This means the front end of the curve is less responsive to easing signals than to tightening signals, creating an asymmetry in the expectations component of the term structure. The practical implication: the market will underprice easing and appropriately price (or overprice) tightening, biasing the curve toward a flatter configuration than warranted by the true policy outlook.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary Policy Transmission & Central Bank Strategy**: The yield curve is the primary observable variable through which monetary policy expectations are expressed. Claims 4 (r-star uncertainty), 7 (broken transmission), and 10 (asymmetric forward guidance) directly reshape how policy stance translates into the term structure. The Taylor Rule assessment depends on yield curve decomposition to determine whether the policy rate is above or below neutral — but r-star uncertainty means this is indeterminate within ±150bp.

**Credit Cycles & Late-Stage Signals** (iter_0028): The yield curve has historically led credit spreads by 6-12 months. The steepening trap (claim 3) implies that the current narrow spread environment may be the calm before credit deterioration. Credit impulse is a leading indicator that should be monitored alongside curve shape — if credit impulse turns negative while the curve is in steepening-trap configuration, recession probability rises to 50-60%.

**Central Bank Balance Sheets (QE/QT)**: QT is the proximate cause of term premium rebuilding (claim 2), operating through the Vayanos-Vila duration supply channel. The basis trade (claim 6) is the private-sector response to QT — absorbing duration that the Fed is returning to the market. Understanding the curve requires modeling QT and basis trade dynamics simultaneously.

**Fiscal Policy & Fiscal Dominance** (iter_0011, iter_0016): The fiscal-term premium feedback loop (claim 5) is the mechanism through which fiscal dominance manifests in the term structure. If Ricardian equivalence breaks down — as the evidence increasingly suggests — fiscal deficits directly affect long-term rates through the supply channel, making the yield curve partly a fiscal indicator rather than purely a monetary policy/growth indicator.

**Stock-Bond Correlation Regime** (iter_0020): Term premium is the primary channel linking the yield curve to the correlation regime. The term premium threshold framework (KB: <0bp → negative correlation; 50-100bp → transitional; >150bp → positive correlation) implies the current ACM estimate of 50-80bp places us in the transitional zone. A further term premium increase toward 150bp+ would cement a positive stock-bond correlation regime, fundamentally altering portfolio construction.

**Sovereign Debt Sustainability**: The Domar condition (i < g required for stable debt/GDP) directly constrains what yield curve levels are fiscally sustainable. With blended borrowing rate at ~4.5% and nominal GDP growth at ~5.5%, the US has only ~100bp of headroom before the debt-to-GDP ratio enters an explosive path — a constraint that bounds how far term premium can rise before forcing policy intervention.

**Real Estate Cycles & CRE Repricing** (iter_0030): The term premium is the key driver of CRE cap rate re-pricing (KB: term_premium_cre_valuation_driver). Higher long-end rates mechanically compress property values through the cap rate channel. The "bad steepening" diagnosis implies CRE remains under pressure even as the short end normalizes.

**Equity Market Cycles** (iter_0024): The equity discount rate is anchored by the long end of the yield curve. If the 10Y is elevated due to term premium rather than growth expectations, the implied equity risk premium is compressed — equities may be priced for a "good steepening" world while living in a "bad steepening" one.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the ACM-Kim-Wright divergence (30-50bp) be arbitrated?** Both models are "correct" given their structural assumptions, but they yield materially different decompositions of the same observed yield. Is there a model-free or semi-parametric approach to term premium estimation that could narrow this gap? (Bauer and Rudebusch 2020 suggest some paths but don't resolve the issue.)

2. **What is the true elasticity of foreign demand for USTs with respect to yield differentials?** BOJ normalization is a structural demand shock, but the magnitude depends on substitution elasticities that are poorly estimated. If Japanese investors shift to JGBs at a faster rate than assumed, the term premium impact could be 2-3x larger than the base case.

3. **Is the basis trade systemically dangerous at current scale?** The trade is now 3-4x larger than in March 2020 and at higher leverage. A Brunnermeier-Pedersen liquidity spiral at this scale could create a term premium spike of 100-200bp in days, overwhelming Fed intervention capacity. The KB identifies this as an open question (confidence on systemic risk: 6.5/10) — we lack a clean empirical test at current notional.

4. **Does broken monetary transmission permanently alter the yield curve's information content?** If rate hikes transmit at 55-65% of historical effectiveness, then the "same" degree of curve inversion implies less real economy restriction, and the recession-prediction model needs recalibration. But we have no prior cycle with transmission this impaired to calibrate against.

5. **What is the correct Phillips Curve slope (κ) in the current regime?** The NK Phillips Curve requires κ to translate output gaps into inflation dynamics, which in turn affect the expectations component of the yield curve. Post-pandemic κ estimates range from 0.1 to 0.5 across specifications — a factor-of-5 range that creates fundamental uncertainty about the inflation-growth tradeoff embedded in the term structure.

6. **At what term premium level does fiscal consolidation become politically inevitable?** The UK gilt crisis (2022) activated at ~100% debt/GDP with a ~2pp fiscal expansion. The US has the reserve currency premium and deeper markets, but there must be some threshold. Is it 150bp term premium? 200bp? The answer determines whether the fiscal-term premium loop stabilizes endogenously or requires external forcing.

7. **How should the Fed calibrate policy when its primary transmission channels are impaired?** If the IS curve is flatter than assumed (mortgage lock-in), the exchange rate channel is only 50% effective, and the asset price channel is running in reverse, then the Taylor Rule — even with a known r* — prescribes insufficient tightening to achieve the intended real economy impact. Does this mean the neutral rate concept itself needs to be reformulated to account for transmission efficiency?

8. **What is the probability distribution over term premium outcomes at 12-month and 24-month horizons?** My base case is that term premium remains in the 50-100bp range (ACM) over the next 12 months, but the distribution is right-skewed — there is a fat tail where fiscal or geopolitical events push term premium to 150bp+ rapidly. Quantifying this distribution requires a model of fiscal policy surprises that does not currently exist in a satisfactory form.
