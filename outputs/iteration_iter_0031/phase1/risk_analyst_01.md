## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Steepening Trap as Crisis Mechanics**

The steepening trap is not a statistical curiosity but a mechanistic inevitability of crisis sequencing. The causal chain: (a) tightening cycle inverts the curve; (b) economic damage accumulates with a lag; (c) the Fed begins cutting (front end falls), steepening the curve; (d) recession arrives during or shortly after the steepening because the damage was already baked in during inversion. The celebration of un-inversion conflates the *direction* of curve change with the *level* of economic stress. In 1990, 2001, and 2007, the curve had already steepened by 50-150bp before recession was officially recognized. The current 2s10s at +20-40bp with the Tier 2b signpost active places us in the exact window where the steepening trap has historically sprung. The critical question is whether this steepening is expectations-driven (rate cuts anticipated → genuine growth concern signal) or term-premium-driven (fiscal supply → noise). The 30-50bp divergence between ACM and Kim-Wright models means this question cannot be answered with full precision, introducing irreducible uncertainty at the most operationally critical juncture.

**Claim 2 — Basis Trade as Amplification Mechanism**

The basis trade exploits the spread between Treasury cash bonds and futures, funded in repo, at leverage ratios that create acute fragility. The mechanism: if Treasury prices fall (from any cause — auction failure, geopolitical shock, term premium spike), the leveraged position generates margin calls. Forced selling of Treasuries to meet margin drives prices lower, generating more margin calls — a textbook margin spiral. The critical structural change since March 2020: the trade has grown from ~$500B to $800B-$1T+, while dealer balance sheet capacity has not expanded commensurately. The notional-to-dealer-balance-sheet ratio is at unprecedented levels. This means the marginal capacity to absorb forced selling in a stress event is lower than in March 2020, even as the potential forced selling volume is higher. The SEC clearing mandate adds a regulatory trigger: central clearing requires higher initial margin and daily variation margin, mechanically reducing the leverage multiple available. Even a gradual phase-in creates uncertainty about future leverage capacity, which could trigger preemptive position reduction.

**Claim 3 — Deficit-Term Premium Doom Loop Mechanics**

The arithmetic is straightforward but the dynamics are non-linear. Net interest expense has risen from ~$350B (FY2020) to ~$1T (FY2025/26). With average debt maturity of ~6.2 years, approximately 16% of outstanding debt reprices annually at current market rates. If term premium rises by 50bp, the annual incremental debt service cost is approximately $25-30B in the first year, compounding as more debt rolls over. The UK gilt crisis validated this mechanism for a developed-market sovereign: starting from ~100% debt/GDP, a ~2pp fiscal expansion triggered a self-reinforcing term premium spike of ~100bp in 4 days. The U.S. enjoys a reserve currency premium that raises the activation threshold, but this premium is not infinite and has been eroding (indirect bidder share declining from 72% to 65%; T-bill share rising from 15% to 22%, indicating Treasury itself is front-loading shorter-duration issuance to avoid long-end repricing).

**Claim 4 — SEC Clearing Mandate as Calendar Catalyst**

The SEC approved CME Securities Clearing Inc. on December 2, 2025, with implementation scheduled for Q2 2026. Historical precedent: post-2008 OTC derivatives clearing mandates compressed carry trades and forced deleveraging in those markets over 6-18 months. The basis trade operates on similar mechanics — thin spread, massive leverage, counterparty opacity. Central clearing directly addresses the opacity (improving counterparty transparency) while simultaneously increasing margin requirements. Cross-margining offsets could dampen the impact, but the direction of effect is unambiguous: less leverage capacity → smaller basis trade → reduced synthetic demand for Treasuries → higher term premium. The timing coincides with Q2 quarter-end repo stress, creating a compound risk window.

**Claim 5 — BOJ Normalization Contagion**

Japanese institutions hold ~$1.1T in U.S. Treasuries. The carry incentive depends on the Fed-BOJ rate differential. Each 25bp BOJ hike narrows this differential and reduces the hedging-adjusted yield advantage of USTs for Japanese investors. At current levels, the break-even for unhedged JGB-UST carry is approximately 100bp of further BOJ normalization. Beyond this, repatriation becomes incrementally attractive. The compound problem: this reduction in Japanese UST demand arrives simultaneously with QT runoff and fiscal supply pressure, meaning the duration absorption problem intensifies from both supply and demand sides. The convexity of the demand function — as price-sensitive buyers become dominant, each marginal unit of supply requires disproportionately higher term premium — transforms what might individually be manageable flows into a potentially non-linear term premium response.

**Claim 6 — Vol Regime Dependence of Curve Signals**

The empirical evidence from the 2019 vs. 2022-23 inversions is the strongest single piece of evidence for signal degradation. In 2019: MOVE was low (~50-60), the inversion was expectations-driven (markets pricing recession risk), and the curve correctly signaled the slowdown that would have become recession absent COVID. In 2022-23: MOVE was elevated (~100-130), the inversion included 80-120bp of term premium compression (i.e., the "natural" curve was much less inverted or not inverted at all), and no recession materialized on the historical 12-18 month timeline. The regression relationship shows term premium explains 50-65% of 10Y variation in ACM models when conditioned on MOVE. This means in high-MOVE environments, roughly half the information content of the curve is noise, not signal. With MOVE structurally elevated (structural floor arguably higher post-QT due to reduced Fed backstop expectations), the curve's signaling precision is permanently impaired relative to the pre-QE era.

**Claim 7 — Super-Additive Duration Absorption**

The pre-pandemic norm was $500-800B/year in net Treasury duration absorption, predominantly by price-insensitive buyers (Fed via QE, foreign central banks via reserve accumulation, Japanese insurance/pension via carry). The current requirement is $2.0-2.6T/year. The buyer base has shifted to hedge funds (basis trade), asset managers (yield-seeking), and households (through MMFs rolling into T-bills). Each of these buyer categories has leverage constraints, return hurdles, or behavioral triggers that create selling pressure at stress thresholds. The super-additivity hypothesis: removing buyer A (e.g., BOJ) doesn't just reduce demand by A's share — it forces remaining buyers to absorb A's share at a higher price (term premium), which moves some of them closer to their own selling thresholds. Weak auction tails in late 2023-2025 provide suggestive evidence; the October 2023 30-year auction tail (5.3bp) occurred at exactly the moment when multiple demand sources were simultaneously retreating.

**Claim 8 — Correlation Regime Transition Risk**

The term premium threshold framework identifies three zones: ~0bp or below (negative stock-bond correlation regime), 50-100bp (transitional/unstable), 150bp+ (positive correlation regime). Current ACM at 50-80bp sits squarely in the transitional zone. The crisis mechanics concern: global portfolio construction embeds $15-20T+ in strategies calibrated to negative stock-bond correlation (60/40, risk parity, volatility targeting). A rapid transition through the zone — say, term premium from 80bp to 150bp+ over 1-2 quarters — would simultaneously: (a) impair Treasury's hedging value, (b) trigger risk-parity deleveraging as realized correlation shifts, (c) force 60/40 rebalancing that amplifies the move. The 12-18 month lead time observed in historical transitions (1967-68, 1998-2000) is not reassuring — it means we may be mid-transition now with the most disruptive phase still ahead.

**Claim 9 — Reflexive Loop Buffer Assessment**

The r-g gap is the critical state variable. Current: blended borrowing rate ~4.5%, nominal GDP growth ~5.5%, gap ~1pp. For context: in the 2010s, the gap was 2-3pp (rates near zero, nominal growth 3-4%). A 100bp term premium shock would compress the gap to approximately zero; a 200bp shock (less than the UK gilt crisis magnitude) would invert it. The question is whether the U.S. reserve currency premium provides 100bp, 200bp, or more of additional buffer. The UK example suggests the buffer for even a G7 sovereign is finite. The U.S. case is different (global reserve currency, deeper markets, Fed as lender of last resort), but the erosion of the reserve premium (declining foreign official holdings as share of total, de-dollarization trends at the margin) means the buffer should be treated as shrinking, not constant.

**Claim 10 — CRE-Curve Interaction**

Floating-rate CRE loans originated in 2020-2021 (when SOFR was near zero) typically included 2-3 year interest rate caps. These caps have been expiring or rolling at vastly higher strike prices since 2023-2024. A property that was cash-flow positive with a cap at 0.5% SOFR may be cash-flow negative at 4.5%+ SOFR. This creates a performing-to-non-performing cliff that is calendar-driven (cap expiry dates) and correlated with the same term premium dynamics driving curve steepening. The "bad steepening" scenario (fiscal supply → higher long rates → wider term premium) does not lower SOFR (that requires Fed cuts), so CRE borrowers face the worst of both worlds: elevated short rates AND rising long rates widening their refinancing costs at maturity.
