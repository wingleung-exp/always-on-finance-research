# Commodity Price → Inflation Transmission Mechanisms — Cross-Asset Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The cross-asset inflation pricing across equities, rates, commodities, and FX is currently incoherent on the commodity-inflation transmission question — and the incoherence itself is the most investable signal.** Breakeven inflation (5Y at ~2.3-2.5%) prices benign commodity passthrough. Equity multiples (S&P at ~21x forward) embed a "cost-push absorbed by margins" assumption. The dollar remains strong despite commodity firmness, violating the standard dollar-commodity inverse. And commodity curves are in varying states of backwardation/contango that imply different supply-demand balances across energy, metals, and agriculture. These four signals cannot all be correct simultaneously. Either breakevens are too low (commodities transmit more inflation than priced), or equity margins are too generous (cost-push compresses margins more than analysts model), or the dollar strength is suppressing what would otherwise be higher imported inflation readings. The cross-asset consistency check fails, as it did in iter_0002 and iter_0004 for different reasons — and importantly, commodity-inflation transmission is the *mechanism* by which many of those prior inconsistencies would resolve.

2. **The commodity-to-CPI transmission mechanism operates through three distinct channels with radically different speeds, and the cross-asset implications of each channel are different enough to require separate portfolio responses: (a) direct price passthrough (1-3 months, affects headline CPI, transitory by nature), (b) input-cost propagation (3-12 months, affects PPI then core goods, partially persistent), and (c) second-round wage-price effects (12-36 months, affects services CPI, potentially self-reinforcing).** The critical analytical error — which current market pricing appears to make — is treating all commodity price increases as Channel (a) events (transitory, "look-through-able") when the structural conditions for Channel (c) activation are stronger than at any point since the 1970s, specifically because of labor market tightness (knowledge base: labor_market_stagflation_fulcrum) and fiscal demand support (knowledge base: fiscal_deficit_kalecki_channel).

3. **The stock-bond correlation sign — the single most consequential variable for multi-asset portfolios — is determined by which commodity-inflation channel dominates.** If commodity shocks transmit only through Channel (a) (headline, transitory), the stock-bond correlation remains negative (bonds hedge equities, as the Fed looks through it). If Channel (b) dominates (input-cost margin compression), the correlation moves toward zero (equities fall on margins, bonds rally on growth fears, partial hedge). If Channel (c) activates (second-round wage-price), the correlation flips positive (both stocks and bonds fall, as the Fed must tighten against rising inflation expectations — the 2022 template). The knowledge base already identifies this bifurcation (stock_bond_correlation_labor_driven, stagflationary_disinflationary_bifurcation) but has not yet mapped the specific commodity-transmission channel that triggers each correlation regime. This is the missing analytical link.

4. **Energy price passthrough is highly asymmetric across sectors, creating a relative-value opportunity within equities that the commodity-inflation lens reveals.** The passthrough elasticity varies from ~0.8 for airlines and trucking (near-complete, fast) to ~0.3 for food/beverage manufacturing (partial, lagged by 2-4 quarters via hedging programs) to ~0.05-0.10 for technology and professional services (negligible direct, but exposed to wage second-round effects). Current equity market pricing does not adequately differentiate passthrough vulnerability — sector P/E dispersion does not correlate with passthrough elasticity, suggesting a mispricing that commodity-to-inflation analysis can exploit.

5. **The dollar-commodity relationship is the critical mediating variable for the US inflation transmission, and the current breakdown of the historical inverse (strong dollar AND firm commodities simultaneously) implies a structural shift in the transmission mechanism.** Historically, dollar strength suppresses US commodity import prices, partially offsetting commodity price increases in domestic CPI. But the current co-movement (strong dollar, firm commodities) implies either: (a) commodity strength is demand-pull from non-US sources (China stimulus, EM industrialization), in which case the dollar offsets and US passthrough is muted; or (b) commodity strength reflects supply constraints (geopolitics, underinvestment in capex post-2015) that persist regardless of dollar moves, in which case the dollar offset is temporary and the eventual passthrough is larger and more abrupt when the dollar mean-reverts. The resolution of this ambiguity is a first-order portfolio construction question.

6. **Food price inflation transmission in developed markets operates through a fundamentally different mechanism than energy — with longer lags, higher political sensitivity, and stronger second-round activation potential — yet receives almost no cross-asset attention because food commodities lack the financialization and derivatives liquidity of energy.** Wheat, corn, and soybean prices feed into food CPI with a 6-12 month lag (vs. 1-3 months for gasoline), through a multi-step supply chain: farm gate → processing → packaging → retail. The lag means food inflation arrives *after* headline energy inflation has peaked and the "transitory" narrative has taken hold, creating a policy surprise when food CPI acceleration re-ignites just as energy CPI is decelerating. The 2022-2023 episode followed this exact pattern. For cross-asset portfolios, this means the second wave of inflation — the food wave — is systematically under-hedged.

7. **The current regime of fiscal dominance (knowledge base: fiscal_deficit_kalecki_channel, confidence 9) structurally amplifies commodity-to-inflation transmission by providing the demand-side fuel that converts first-round commodity price shocks into persistent second-round inflation.** In a standard cycle, commodity price spikes are self-limiting: higher prices → demand destruction → price reversal. But when fiscal deficits at 6-7% of GDP sustain household incomes and corporate profits through the Kalecki channel, the demand destruction mechanism is blunted. This means first-round commodity shocks persist longer, giving more time for Channel (b) input-cost propagation and Channel (c) second-round wage-price transmission to activate. The fiscal channel is the amplifier that the 1970s analogy (often invoked but rarely properly specified) actually requires — and unlike the 1970s, the current fiscal channel is ~3x larger relative to GDP.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Cross-Asset Inconsistency on Commodity-Inflation):**
The cross-asset consistency check evaluates whether different markets tell a coherent story about commodity-inflation transmission:
- **Rates/Breakevens:** 5Y breakevens at ~2.3-2.5% imply the market expects CPI to average near the Fed's 2% target over the medium term, with minimal persistent commodity passthrough. The 5Y5Y forward breakeven (a proxy for long-term inflation expectations) at ~2.2-2.4% confirms this — the market prices commodity shocks as transitory.
- **Equities:** S&P 500 forward P/E at ~21x prices operating margins near cycle highs (~12-13%), implying that cost-push from commodities is fully absorbed or passed through without margin compression. This is inconsistent with the breakeven signal if commodity passthrough is genuinely minimal — if passthrough is low, it means firms *absorb* the cost (margins compress) rather than pass it through (inflation rises). Both breakevens and equity margins being benign simultaneously requires the specific condition that commodity prices themselves are expected to fall, which the commodity curve does not confirm.
- **Dollar-Commodity:** The dollar (DXY ~104-107) remains firm while broad commodity indices have stabilized above 2019 levels. The historical inverse typically implies only one can persist — either dollar weakens (commodity-supportive) or commodities fall (dollar-supportive). Both being firm simultaneously suggests structural supply constraints or non-US demand sources that the standard model doesn't capture.
- **Cross-asset verdict:** The four signals are mutually inconsistent. Resolution requires at least one market to reprice.

**Claim 2 (Three-Channel Transmission Framework):**
The three channels have distinct characteristics:
- **Channel (a) — Direct passthrough (1-3 months):** Gasoline prices feed directly into headline CPI with 2-4 week lag. The passthrough coefficient for crude oil → retail gasoline is ~0.5-0.7 (a $10/bbl increase in WTI produces ~$0.15-0.25/gallon increase at the pump within 4-6 weeks). This is well-understood and the Fed explicitly "looks through" it. Market pricing treats most commodity-inflation as this channel.
- **Channel (b) — Input-cost propagation (3-12 months):** PPI components (chemicals, metals, transportation costs) feed into core goods CPI with variable lags. Passthrough coefficients are industry-specific: ~0.4-0.6 for manufacturing, ~0.2-0.3 for construction, ~0.1-0.2 for services. This channel compresses margins if firms cannot pass through (equity-negative, potentially bond-positive) or feeds core inflation if they can (bond-negative, ambiguous for equities).
- **Channel (c) — Second-round wage-price (12-36 months):** The critical channel. When commodity prices raise headline inflation, workers demand higher wages to maintain real incomes. If the labor market is tight enough (knowledge base: labor_market_stagflation_fulcrum — "the labor market is the fulcrum variable"), wage increases exceed productivity growth, raising unit labor costs, which feed back into services prices. This is the *only* channel that can make commodity inflation persistent and self-reinforcing. The 1970s inflation was dominated by Channel (c) activation; the 2014-2015 oil price decline did *not* activate Channel (c) in reverse (wages did not fall). The asymmetry matters: commodity price increases can become entrenched through wages, but decreases do not reliably deflate through wages.

**Claim 3 (Stock-Bond Correlation Regime):**
The knowledge base concept stock_bond_correlation_labor_driven (confidence 7) establishes that labor market tightness determines the correlation sign. The commodity transmission framework adds the *mechanism*:
- If commodity shock → Channel (a) only → Fed looks through → no wage response → stock-bond correlation stays negative. This is the "2014-2019 template."
- If commodity shock → Channel (b) → margin pressure → equities fall, growth worry → bonds rally → stock-bond correlation near zero or weakly negative. This is the "2015-2016 manufacturing recession template."
- If commodity shock → Channel (c) → wage-price spiral risk → Fed tightens → both bonds and equities fall → stock-bond correlation flips positive. This is the "2022 template."
The regime classification depends on labor market slack at the time of the commodity shock. With the unemployment rate at ~4.0-4.2% and the Beveridge curve still showing elevated mismatch (knowledge base: labor_market_dynamics), the activation threshold for Channel (c) is lower than in any cycle since the 1970s.

**Claim 4 (Sectoral Passthrough Asymmetry):**
Energy passthrough elasticities by sector (based on input-output analysis of the BEA's use tables and historical pricing behavior):
- **Airlines:** Fuel is 25-35% of operating costs. Passthrough coefficient ~0.8 within 1-2 quarters (fuel surcharges, fare adjustments). Equity sensitivity: high and fast.
- **Trucking/Logistics:** Fuel is 20-30% of costs. Passthrough ~0.6-0.7 within 2-3 quarters (contractual repricing lags). Equity sensitivity: high, lagged.
- **Food manufacturing:** Energy + agricultural inputs are 40-50% of COGS, but hedging programs (typically 6-12 months forward) delay passthrough. Passthrough ~0.3-0.4 with 2-4 quarter lag. Equity sensitivity: moderate, significantly lagged.
- **Technology/Professional services:** Direct energy/commodity exposure is <5% of costs. But indirect exposure through wages (Channel (c)) and office/data center energy is non-trivial for hyperscale compute. Passthrough ~0.05-0.10 direct, but potentially 0.3-0.5 through wages with 12-24 month lag. Equity sensitivity: low direct, high second-round.
The relative value opportunity exists because the market applies a relatively uniform "cost-push risk discount" across sectors rather than differentiating by passthrough speed and magnitude.

**Claim 5 (Dollar-Commodity Mediation):**
Historical data: the correlation between DXY and broad commodity indices (Bloomberg Commodity Index) has averaged approximately -0.4 to -0.6 since the floating rate era. In the current episode, the 12-month rolling correlation has moved toward -0.1 to -0.2, a significant weakening. Two hypotheses:
- **(a) Non-US demand pull:** If China's stimulus and EM industrial demand are the primary commodity price driver, then dollar strength reflects US rate differentials while commodity strength reflects non-US growth. In this case, the dollar *is* offsetting US inflation — commodity prices in USD terms are lower than they would be under a weaker dollar, and US CPI passthrough is genuinely muted. **Portfolio implication:** long commodities hedged with long USD is the right expression.
- **(b) Supply constraints:** If geopolitics (Russia-Ukraine, Middle East, trade restrictions) and underinvestment (O&G capex still ~40% below 2014 peak in real terms) are the primary driver, then commodity strength persists regardless of dollar moves. The dollar's current strength masks the underlying passthrough, which will arrive in full when the dollar mean-reverts (potentially triggered by narrowing rate differentials). **Portfolio implication:** commodity longs need to be paired with USD shorts (or left unhedged in foreign currencies) to capture both the commodity price and the FX-amplified passthrough.
The resolution hinges on whether the commodity term structure reflects demand strength (contango — expectation of future demand) or supply tightness (backwardation — spot premium reflects current scarcity). The energy complex in backwardation supports hypothesis (b).

**Claim 6 (Food Inflation Lag and Policy Surprise):**
The food CPI transmission chain:
- Farm gate prices respond to harvest cycles, weather, and fertilizer/energy costs (which themselves have a commodity-inflation dimension — circular dependence).
- Processing and manufacturing adds 2-3 months of lag and ~30-40% markup over raw input cost.
- Retail pricing adds another 1-3 months of lag and ~50-70% markup (reflecting labor, rent, transportation).
- Total farm-gate to retail CPI lag: 6-12 months.
The 2022-2023 sequence confirmed this pattern: wheat prices spiked in March 2022 (Russia-Ukraine), energy inflation peaked in June 2022, but food CPI didn't peak until August 2023 — a 17-month lag from the initial commodity shock. By the time food CPI was accelerating, the "peak inflation" narrative was already dominant in markets, creating a repricing when food inflation re-ignited the debate. Cross-asset portfolios were systematically under-hedged for this second wave because breakeven inflation positioning had been unwound on the "peak headline" signal.

**Claim 7 (Fiscal Amplifier for Commodity Transmission):**
The Kalecki profit identity (Profits = Investment + Govt Deficit + Net Exports - Household Saving + Dividends) explains why the demand destruction channel is blunted:
- In a non-fiscal-dominant regime: commodity price spike → higher costs → consumers cut spending → demand falls → prices self-correct. The auto-stabilizer works.
- In the current fiscal-dominant regime (deficit at 6-7% of GDP): commodity price spike → higher costs → *but* fiscal transfers and sustained employment income maintain consumer spending → demand does *not* fall → prices do not self-correct → first-round price level shift has time to propagate into Channel (b) and Channel (c).
- The fiscal channel also sustains corporate profits (knowledge base: fiscal_deficit_kalecki_channel, confidence 9), meaning firms face less margin pressure and therefore have less incentive to resist input cost increases — they pass them through instead. This *increases* the passthrough coefficient across all three channels.
- The 1970s comparison is often invoked for commodity-inflation transmission, but the fiscal channel is typically omitted. US federal deficits in 1973-1974 were ~1-2% of GDP. Today they are 6-7%. The fiscal amplifier is approximately 3-4x larger, meaning the demand destruction auto-stabilizer is proportionally weaker, and the persistence of commodity-inflation shocks is proportionally greater.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Inflation Regimes (high relevance, explicit connection to explore):** The three-channel transmission framework provides the *mechanism* for inflation regime identification. An inflation regime where only Channel (a) is active is fundamentally different from one where Channel (c) has activated. The regime identification question from the knowledge base (stagflationary_disinflationary_bifurcation, confidence 6) should be updated to incorporate the commodity transmission channel as a leading indicator for which inflation regime is developing.

**Commodity Supercycles (high relevance, explicit connection to explore):** If commodities are in a structural supercycle (underinvestment, green transition demand, deglobalization), then the passthrough analysis moves from "episodic shock response" to "persistent structural pressure." A supercycle framework would increase the probability weight on Channel (b) and (c) activation because the first-round price pressures never fully abate, providing continuous fuel for second-round effects. The investment implication is profound: in a supercycle, commodity inflation hedging becomes a permanent portfolio allocation, not a tactical overlay.

**Monetary Policy Transmission (high relevance, explicit connection to explore):** The Fed's ability to contain commodity-inflation transmission depends on whether it can affect the real economy enough to generate demand destruction before Channel (c) activates. The knowledge base concept private_credit_rate_insensitivity (confidence 6) suggests that rate hikes are less effective at slowing credit creation, which means the Fed needs to hike *more* to achieve the same demand destruction — making the "overshoot and crash" scenario more likely than a smooth deceleration. The fiscal channel (confidence 9) further blunts the Fed's transmission mechanism.

**Growth Models & Cycle Positioning (iter_0002):** The Kalecki profit identity implies that commodity price shocks *redistribute* rather than destroy national income — they transfer from consumers to commodity producers and from importing to exporting nations. The growth impact depends on the marginal propensity to spend of the recipients vs. the losers. In the current US cycle, fiscal transfers largely neutralize the consumer loss, making the net growth impact of commodity shocks smaller than historical models predict — but the inflation impact is correspondingly larger, because the demand adjustment that historically limited passthrough is suppressed.

**Credit-Equity Linkage (iter_0004):** Commodity-to-inflation transmission affects credit markets through the margin compression channel (Channel (b)). For leveraged borrowers already facing refinancing at higher rates (knowledge base: maturity_wall_2025_2027), commodity cost pressure on top of higher debt service costs could push interest coverage ratios below 1.0x faster than the pure rates-driven analysis suggests. The credit-equity linkage framework from iter_0004 should incorporate commodity input costs as a second margin stressor alongside rates.

**Labor Market Dynamics (iter_0003):** Channel (c) — the second-round wage-price effect — operates directly through the labor market. The knowledge base concept labor_market_stagflation_fulcrum (confidence 8) identifies the labor market as the key determinant of the stagflation/disinflation bifurcation. The commodity transmission framework adds precision: it is not just "labor market tightness" generically that matters, but specifically whether commodity-driven headline inflation is high enough and persistent enough to shift wage expectations. The empirical threshold for wage expectation anchoring breakdown appears to be headline CPI above ~4-5% for more than 6 consecutive months, based on the 2021-2023 episode.

**Risk Appetite Regimes (iter_0001):** Commodity-inflation transmission is a potential regime shift catalyst. A persistent commodity shock that activates Channel (c) would force a shift from the current "risk-on" regime (benign inflation, accommodative policy stance, negative stock-bond correlation) to a "stagflationary" regime (rising inflation, restrictive policy, positive stock-bond correlation). The regime shift framework from iter_0001 should incorporate commodity price level and persistence as an input variable for regime transition probabilities.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the current passthrough coefficient by channel, and has it changed post-COVID?** The 2020-2023 inflation episode may have permanently altered passthrough coefficients by demonstrating to firms that aggressive price increases are accepted by consumers. If the passthrough coefficient has ratcheted higher (a behavioral/expectational shift), all three channels transmit more commodity inflation than historical estimates suggest. Need: updated empirical passthrough estimates, potentially from ECB or BIS working papers published in 2024-2025.

2. **What is the energy-to-food circular dependence, and does it create an amplification loop?** Energy prices affect fertilizer costs (natural gas → ammonia/urea) which affect crop yields and prices, which feed back into food inflation. If energy and food inflation are co-reinforcing rather than independent, the total passthrough is larger than the sum of the individual channels. Need: input-output analysis quantifying the energy → agriculture → food → CPI amplification multiplier.

3. **At what commodity price level and duration does Channel (c) actually activate in the current labor market?** The knowledge base establishes labor market tightness as the fulcrum, but the *threshold* — the specific headline CPI level and duration needed to shift wage expectations — is poorly specified. The 2021-2023 episode provides one data point (CPI above 5% for ~18 months partially activated Channel (c)), but the sample size is one. Need: cross-country evidence on the activation threshold, potentially from EM economies with more frequent commodity-inflation episodes.

4. **How do commodity hedging programs at the corporate level affect the timing and magnitude of Channel (b) passthrough?** Airlines hedge 12-24 months forward on jet fuel; food companies hedge 6-12 months on agricultural inputs. These hedging programs create a "delayed passthrough" effect that smooths the CPI impact but also extends the duration. As hedges roll off, firms face spot prices — potentially much higher — creating a second wave of cost pressure well after the initial commodity shock. Need: data on corporate hedging ratios by sector and their rolloff schedule.

5. **Does the green transition represent a structural increase in the passthrough coefficient for industrial metals (copper, lithium, nickel)?** If electrification and grid buildout create permanently higher demand for metals, and if supply response is constrained by permitting and ESG-related capex reduction, then industrial metals passthrough to CPI could increase structurally — not just through commodity prices but through capex costs for infrastructure, housing, and manufacturing. This would be a novel passthrough channel not well-captured by historical models.

6. **How should a multi-asset portfolio hedge the "Channel (c) activation" tail risk specifically?** Traditional commodity-inflation hedges (TIPS, commodity futures, REIT/resource equities) have different performance profiles across the three channels. TIPS protect against Channel (a) and (b) but not against the equity leg of Channel (c) (stocks fall in stagflation). Commodity futures protect against all three channels but carry negative roll yield in contango. What is the optimal hedge portfolio for a 60/40 investor specifically targeting the Channel (c) activation scenario?

7. **Is the breakeven inflation market systematically mispricing commodity-inflation tail risk because of structural supply-demand imbalances in the TIPS market?** If TIPS demand is driven by institutional mandates (pension liability matching, insurance regulation) rather than inflation views, the breakeven rate may not accurately reflect the market's true inflation expectation. Need: decomposition of TIPS demand by investor type and motive, and comparison of breakeven-implied inflation to survey-based and market-based alternatives (e.g., inflation swaps, Michigan survey, SPF).
