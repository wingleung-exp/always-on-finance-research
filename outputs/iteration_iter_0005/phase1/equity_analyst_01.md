# Commodity Price → Inflation Transmission Mechanisms — Top-Down Equity Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Commodity-to-CPI passthrough operates on a two-stage lag structure that creates predictable sector rotation windows.** Energy price shocks transmit to headline CPI within 1–3 months (first round), but the second-round effects — wage demands, services price adjustments, and embedded inflation expectations — take 6–18 months to materialize. This lag structure creates a sequenced impact on equity sectors that can be mapped to the macro quadrant framework.

2. **The equity risk premium compresses mechanically during commodity-driven inflation episodes because discount rates rise faster than earnings revisions.** When commodity prices spike, bond yields reprice within weeks (via breakeven inflation), but sell-side earnings estimates adjust sluggishly over 2–3 quarters. This creates a window where the ERP narrows or inverts — equities become expensive relative to bonds on a forward-looking basis even if trailing P/E ratios appear unchanged.

3. **The passthrough coefficient from commodity prices to core inflation has structurally declined since the 1970s–80s but is regime-dependent, not fixed.** In anchored-expectations regimes (post-Volcker through 2019), the passthrough from a 10% oil price increase to core CPI was roughly 0.1–0.3pp over 12 months. In unanchored regimes (1970s, and arguably 2021–2022), the coefficient rises to 0.5–1.0pp+ as second-round effects dominate. The regime itself — not the commodity price level — determines equity market severity.

4. **Food and energy commodities have asymmetric transmission paths that favor different equity factor exposures.** Energy price shocks are margin-destructive for transport, chemicals, and consumer discretionary but margin-accretive for energy producers — this creates a value-vs-growth rotation signal. Food commodity shocks transmit more directly to consumer staples margins and EM consumption, affecting quality and low-volatility factors differently than energy shocks.

5. **The USD acts as a critical modulator of commodity-inflation transmission, creating a feedback loop that amplifies or dampens equity market impact.** A strong USD dampens commodity-inflation passthrough for US equities (cheaper imports) while amplifying it for non-US markets. Conversely, USD weakness during commodity rallies (as in 2002–2008 and 2021–2022) creates a double-hit: rising input costs AND rising domestic-currency commodity prices for US firms. This USD regime is the single most underappreciated variable in commodity-equity linkage models.

6. **Commodity-driven inflation regimes systematically favor the value factor over growth by 400–800bps annualized, driven primarily by the discount rate channel rather than earnings.** Growth stocks, with longer-duration cash flows, suffer disproportionately from rising real and nominal rates associated with commodity inflation. The 2022 episode confirmed the historical pattern: the Russell 1000 Value outperformed Growth by ~22% as the energy-driven inflation spike drove the Fed to tighten aggressively.

7. **The margin impact of commodity inflation is non-linear: firms can absorb cost increases up to a threshold (typically 1–2 standard deviations of input cost), beyond which pricing power becomes the dominant equity selection criterion.** Below the threshold, margin compression is broad and moderate. Above it, dispersion explodes — firms with genuine pricing power (branded consumer goods, mission-critical software, regulated utilities with pass-through clauses) dramatically outperform, creating a quality-factor tailwind.

8. **Second-round effects are the key variable for determining whether a commodity shock causes a temporary equity drawdown (5–10%) or a sustained bear market (20%+).** First-round commodity price shocks that remain contained — i.e., core inflation does not durably exceed central bank targets, wage-price spirals do not ignite — produce buyable equity dips. When second-round effects materialize (as measured by rising core services inflation and accelerating unit labor costs), the central bank response function shifts to restrictive, creating a regime change that turns the equity drawdown into a cyclical bear market.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Two-stage lag structure:**
The BLS CPI component weights show energy has ~7% direct weight and ~5% indirect weight (through transport and production costs). Historical vector autoregression (VAR) studies (Blanchard & Galí 2007, Hamilton 2009) document headline CPI response within 1–3 months of oil shocks. The second-round channel works through: (a) inflation expectations shifting upward (University of Michigan survey, breakevens), (b) wage negotiation incorporating recent inflation (ECI data lags by 6–12 months), and (c) services sector repricing (shelter CPI lags by 12–18 months due to lease renewal cycles). For equity strategists, this means the initial sector rotation (long energy, short transport/airlines) is well-known, but the second wave (short growth/long value as rate expectations reprice) is where the larger alpha lies.

**Claim 2 — ERP compression mechanics:**
Consider the 2022 episode: WTI crude rose from ~$75 to $120+ between December 2021 and June 2022. The 10-year Treasury yield moved from 1.5% to 3.5% over roughly the same period. The S&P 500 forward earnings yield (inverse of forward P/E) was approximately 5.5% at end-2021, implying an ERP of ~4.0% (5.5% – 1.5%). By mid-2022, forward earnings estimates had barely moved (consensus 2023 EPS was still ~$240), so the earnings yield remained ~5.5%, but with 10yr at 3.5%, the ERP had collapsed to ~2.0% — well below the 20-year median of ~3.5%. This mechanical compression preceded the S&P 500 drawdown to 3,600. The Fed model framework, for all its limitations, correctly flagged the danger. Historical parallels include 1973–74 (oil embargo → rates spike → ERP inversion → 48% bear market) and 1990 (Gulf War oil shock → milder ERP compression → 20% correction).

**Claim 3 — Regime-dependent passthrough coefficient:**
The IMF's World Economic Outlook (various editions) and academic literature (Choi et al. 2018, ECB Working Paper Series) document the secular decline in oil-price passthrough from ~0.8 in the 1970s to ~0.2 in the 2000–2019 period. Key structural reasons: (a) declining energy intensity of GDP (US energy consumption per unit of real GDP fell ~50% from 1975 to 2020), (b) more credible central banks anchoring expectations, (c) globalization providing supply-side offsets. However, the 2021–2022 episode showed the coefficient is not fixed: when commodity shocks coincide with loose monetary policy, fiscal stimulus, and supply-chain disruption, the passthrough reverts toward historical highs. The relevant equity market insight is that the passthrough coefficient is itself a regime indicator — tracking its real-time evolution (via breakeven inflation term structure and core CPI momentum) is critical for timing factor rotation.

**Claim 4 — Asymmetric food vs. energy transmission:**
Energy shocks operate primarily through the cost channel (production inputs, transport) and the discount rate channel (via CPI → rate expectations). They create clear winners (energy producers) and losers (airlines, trucking, chemicals). Food commodity shocks operate more through the consumption channel — they compress real wages and consumer purchasing power, particularly for lower-income cohorts. Historically, food price spikes have a stronger effect on EM equity markets (where food constitutes 25–40% of CPI baskets vs. 8–14% in DM) and on consumer staples margins in DM (where raw material costs can comprise 40–60% of COGS for packaged food companies). The factor implications differ: energy shocks favor value and high-beta energy stocks; food shocks favor quality (firms with pricing power to pass through costs) and penalize low-quality consumer-facing businesses.

**Claim 5 — USD modulator effect:**
The DXY and commodity prices exhibit a well-documented negative correlation (roughly –0.5 to –0.7 over rolling 12-month periods). For a US-based equity strategist, this creates two distinct regimes: (a) USD-strong/commodity-weak (e.g., 2014–2016): commodity deflation dampens CPI, ERP expands, growth outperforms, EM underperforms; (b) USD-weak/commodity-strong (e.g., 2003–2007, 2021–2022): commodity inflation amplified in USD terms, ERP compresses, value and international equities outperform. The feedback loop operates through: commodity prices rise → inflation rises → Fed tightens → USD strengthens → commodity prices moderate (self-correcting), OR commodity prices rise → Fed perceived behind the curve → real rates fall → USD weakens → commodity prices accelerate further (self-reinforcing). The path taken determines whether the equity impact is a correction or a bear market.

**Claim 6 — Value over growth in commodity-inflation regimes:**
The empirical record from Kenneth French's factor data and AQR's Cliff Asness research shows value outperformance during rising inflation periods. The mechanism is primarily duration: a growth stock priced at 40x earnings has a longer implied cash flow duration (~25 years) than a value stock at 10x earnings (~10 years). A 200bp rise in discount rates reduces the present value of the growth stock by roughly 40% but the value stock by only 18% (simplified DCF sensitivity). The 2022 experience was textbook: Russell 1000 Value returned roughly –7% while Growth returned approximately –29%. Energy (the ultimate "value" sector in early 2022) returned +59% for the year. Historical parallels: 1973–74, 1977–80, and 2000–2002 (though the latter was not commodity-driven, it shared the rising-rate characteristic).

**Claim 7 — Non-linear margin threshold:**
PPI data and corporate earnings call transcripts from 2021–2022 demonstrate the threshold effect. In Q1–Q2 2021, most companies absorbed modest input cost increases (PPI running +3–5% YoY) with stable gross margins. By Q3 2021–Q1 2022 (PPI +8–12% YoY), a sharp divergence emerged: companies with pricing power (Procter & Gamble, Coca-Cola, Microsoft) maintained or expanded margins, while companies without (Dollar Tree, many mid-cap industrials) saw 200–500bp margin compression. The S&P 500 equal-weight gross margin peaked at ~34% in Q2 2021 and compressed to ~31% by Q4 2022, but this average masked massive dispersion. The quality factor (high ROE, stable earnings) outperformed by ~600bps in 2022, confirming the pricing power thesis.

**Claim 8 — Second-round effects as bear market determinant:**
The key distinction: the 1990 Gulf War oil shock produced a ~20% equity correction but no sustained bear market because core CPI peaked at 5.3% and wage growth remained contained — the Fed eased within months. The 1973–74 oil embargo produced a 48% bear market because second-round effects materialized: core CPI exceeded 10%, wages accelerated, and the Fed was forced to maintain tight policy far longer. The 2022 episode fell in between: second-round effects partially materialized (core CPI reached 6.6%, wage growth hit 5.5%), producing a 25% drawdown, but the absence of a full wage-price spiral (real wages were negative throughout) allowed a recovery once commodity prices peaked. The monitoring framework: track core services ex-housing CPI (the Fed's preferred second-round indicator), unit labor costs (quarterly, lagged), and inflation expectations (5y5y breakevens). When all three accelerate simultaneously, shift to bear market positioning.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence (1–10) | Justification |
|-------|-------------------|---------------|
| 1. Two-stage lag structure | **8** | Well-established in academic literature and confirmed across multiple commodity shock episodes. The specific timing windows vary by cycle, but the sequential nature is robust. |
| 2. ERP mechanical compression | **9** | This is near-mechanical: bond yields lead, earnings revisions lag. The 2022 episode provided textbook confirmation. Slight uncertainty around whether the Fed model framework applies as cleanly in all rate environments. |
| 3. Regime-dependent passthrough | **8** | Strong empirical support for both the secular decline and the regime-dependency. The precise coefficient in any given episode is uncertain, but the directional claim and the regime-switching framework are well-supported. |
| 4. Asymmetric food vs. energy paths | **7** | Directionally well-supported, but the factor implications are less cleanly documented. Food price transmission is particularly noisy in DM because of processing and distribution margins that buffer raw material costs. |
| 5. USD modulator effect | **8** | The USD-commodity negative correlation is robust and the two-regime framework is well-established. The self-correcting vs. self-reinforcing feedback loop distinction is more interpretive and less precisely measurable in real-time. |
| 6. Value over growth (400–800bps) | **7** | Historically well-supported, but the magnitude is sensitive to the specific period and factor construction. The 2022 episode was unusually extreme; typical outperformance may be closer to the lower end of the range. Also, the growth-vs-value trade has structural composition shifts (tech dominance) that complicate historical analogies. |
| 7. Non-linear margin threshold | **6** | The qualitative pattern is evident in 2021–2022 data, but the specific threshold (1–2 SD) is an approximation. Threshold depends on sector, competitive dynamics, and demand elasticity — hard to generalize precisely. |
| 8. Second-round effects as bear market determinant | **8** | The historical pattern is clear across 1973–74, 1990, 2008, and 2022. The specific monitoring indicators (core services ex-housing, ULC, 5y5y) are well-chosen but not infallible leading indicators. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**→ Inflation Regimes (inflation_regimes):** Commodity transmission is the primary mechanism through which inflation regime shifts are triggered. The passthrough coefficient is itself an indicator of which inflation regime the economy occupies. An anchored regime has low passthrough; regime transitions are signaled by rising passthrough coefficients. The knowledge base's existing work on inflation regimes (depth 3, confidence 6.0) should be directly linked — commodity shocks are the most common exogenous trigger for regime transitions.

**→ Commodity Supercycles (commodity_supercycles):** The supercycle framework determines whether a commodity price shock is transitory (geopolitical supply disruption) or structural (multi-year demand-supply imbalance). Structural supercycles have far more severe equity market implications because they sustain the second-round effects that turn corrections into bear markets. The KB's existing copper-deficit and oil-supply concepts from iter_0002 are directly relevant.

**→ Monetary Policy (monetary_policy):** Central bank reaction functions are the critical intermediary between commodity inflation and equity market outcomes. A commodity shock that triggers aggressive tightening (Volcker 1979–82, Fed 2022) has dramatically different equity implications than one met with accommodation (2007–08 initial response). The Taylor Rule residual — whether the Fed is ahead of or behind the commodity-inflation curve — is the key signpost for equity positioning.

**→ Credit-Equity Linkage (credit_equity_linkage, iter_0004):** Commodity-driven inflation that tightens financial conditions creates a feedback loop into credit markets. Rising rates compress both equity valuations and credit spreads simultaneously (as in 2022). The credit channel can amplify the equity drawdown if corporate leverage is high — highly-levered value stocks may not benefit from the value rotation if credit risk reprices against them.

**→ Labor Market Dynamics (labor_market_dynamics, iter_0003):** Second-round effects operate primarily through the labor market. The KB's existing concept of commodity-EM labor procyclicality (confidence 8) is directly relevant — commodity exporters experience wage-price spirals that DM importers may avoid. For equity allocation, this means EM commodity exporters can face both margin expansion (revenue side) and margin compression (cost side via wages) simultaneously.

**→ Yield Curve Dynamics (yield_curve_dynamics):** Commodity-driven inflation shocks typically flatten or invert the yield curve (short rates rise faster as markets price Fed response). The yield curve shape is a critical input to equity sector allocation: curve flattening favors financials underweights, while the level of rates rising favors value. The interaction between curve shape and rate level creates nuanced sector signals.

**→ FX Regimes (fx_regimes, fx_rates_divergence):** The USD modulator effect (Claim 5) directly connects to FX regime analysis. Dollar cycle positioning determines whether commodity inflation is amplified or dampened for different regional equity markets. This is the primary channel for international equity allocation decisions during commodity shock episodes.

## Open Questions

1. **Has the passthrough coefficient structurally re-risen post-COVID, or was 2021–2022 an outlier?** The confluence of fiscal stimulus, supply-chain disruption, and commodity shocks made it impossible to isolate the commodity passthrough cleanly. If the coefficient has structurally risen (due to deglobalization, reshoring, and tighter labor markets), the equity market implications are significantly more bearish for duration/growth assets on a secular basis.

2. **How does the energy transition alter commodity-inflation transmission?** The shift from fossil fuels to metals-intensive renewables changes which commodities matter for inflation. Copper, lithium, and rare earths may replace oil as the primary commodity-inflation channel over the next decade. The equity sector implications would be radically different — favoring mining over traditional energy, and penalizing different industrial subsectors.

3. **What is the ERP steady-state in a world of structurally higher commodity-driven inflation?** If the post-GFC era of low inflation and compressed bond yields was anomalous, and we are entering a period of higher structural inflation (commodity-driven or otherwise), the "fair" ERP may be lower than the 2010–2019 average of ~4%, implying structurally lower P/E multiples. This question is critical for index-level targets.

4. **How do commodity inflation hedging strategies interact at the portfolio level?** Long commodity equities (energy, mining) provide a natural hedge against commodity-inflation-driven drawdowns, but the hedge ratio is unstable — during 2008, energy equities initially outperformed but then collapsed alongside everything else as the growth shock dominated the inflation shock. What signals distinguish a "hedgeable" commodity inflation episode from one that devolves into a broad risk-off event?

5. **What is the real-time monitoring framework for passthrough regime shifts?** Identifying the shift from an anchored to unanchored regime in real-time (not retrospectively) is the key unsolved problem for equity strategists. Candidate indicators include: the slope of the breakeven inflation term structure, the gap between headline and core CPI, unit labor cost acceleration, and small business pricing intentions surveys — but the optimal composite signal is undefined.

6. **How does the concentration risk in cap-weighted indices (particularly the S&P 500's mega-cap tech weighting) alter the index-level response to commodity inflation versus historical episodes?** The top 10 S&P 500 stocks have minimal direct commodity exposure but maximum rate sensitivity. This means the index may be more vulnerable to commodity-inflation-driven rate shocks than historical factor studies (which use equal-weighted portfolios) would suggest.
