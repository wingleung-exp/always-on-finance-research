# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are fundamentally driven by the interaction between the output gap, the monetary policy stance relative to the Taylor Rule prescription, and the perceived credibility of the central bank reaction function.** Risk-on regimes persist when policy is accommodative relative to neutral (r < r*), the output gap is closing but not yet positive, and inflation expectations remain anchored. Risk-off regimes emerge when at least two of these three conditions flip.

2. **The risk-on/risk-off (RORO) binary is an empirically useful but theoretically incomplete taxonomy; a three-regime model — risk-on, risk-off, and "risk-confused" — better captures modern market dynamics.** The "risk-confused" regime occurs when demand and supply shocks co-exist (e.g., 2022 stagflation scare, tariff escalation + fiscal expansion in 2025-26), causing traditional cross-asset correlations to break down and generating intra-asset-class dispersion that the RORO framework cannot parse.

3. **Financial conditions indices (FCIs) are the most operationally useful proxy for risk appetite regime identification within a New Keynesian framework, because they aggregate the transmission mechanism of monetary policy through to asset prices.** The Goldman Sachs FCI, Chicago Fed NFCI, and Bloomberg FCI each capture slightly different dimensions, but their composite signal identifies regime transitions 2-4 weeks before VIX or credit spreads alone.

4. **Contagion velocity in modern market structure has increased structurally relative to the pre-GFC era, driven by three mechanism changes: (a) dealer balance sheet constraints post-Volcker Rule/Basel III, (b) the growth of passive/systematic strategies that mechanically amplify correlation-one selling, and (c) the concentration of market-making in fewer intermediaries.** This means the transition from risk-on to risk-off is faster and more violent than the New Keynesian transmission mechanism would predict from fundamentals alone.

5. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades follow a predictable sequence rooted in the hierarchy of money: first, risk premia expand in off-the-run and illiquid instruments; second, bid-ask spreads in liquid markets widen; third, cross-asset correlations spike toward +1; fourth, the central bank reaction function is activated, halting the cascade.** The speed at which the central bank "put" is perceived to be operative determines cascade duration.

6. **The current macro regime (March 2026) exhibits characteristics of a risk-confused state: tariff-induced supply shocks are contractionary for growth, while fiscal deficits remain expansionary, and the Fed is navigating between inflation persistence and growth deceleration.** This is not a clean risk-on or risk-off environment; it is a regime where the sign of the output gap shock is ambiguous, and the Taylor Rule prescription is indeterminate because supply and demand shocks push in opposite directions.

7. **Risk appetite regime transitions are regime-dependent on the stock-bond correlation sign.** When correlation is negative (pre-2022 norm), risk-off events are partially self-correcting via the 60/40 portfolio rebalancing channel. When correlation is positive (supply-shock dominated regimes), risk-off events are amplified because both legs of diversified portfolios sell simultaneously, triggering larger fund outflows and more severe liquidity cascades. Our knowledge base entry on `stock_bond_correlation_regime` (confidence 8) directly informs this claim.

8. **The retailization of credit markets — $200B+ in semi-liquid private credit vehicles held by 55+ HNW investors with quarterly liquidity windows — represents a novel and untested contagion channel that could transform the next risk-off event into a structurally more severe liquidity cascade than historical analogues would suggest.** This connects directly to the `retailization_liquidity_risk` concept (confidence 6) already in our knowledge base.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Taylor Rule / Output Gap / Credibility triad:**
The New Keynesian synthesis (Woodford 2003, Galí 2015) establishes that asset prices are forward-looking functions of the expected path of the short rate, the term premium (reflecting uncertainty about the reaction function), and the risk premium (reflecting the probability-weighted distribution of real outcomes). Risk appetite is therefore a derived quantity: when the central bank is credibly accommodative and the economy is recovering (negative output gap closing), risk premia compress because the expected path of fundamentals improves and the discount rate falls. The empirical evidence is robust: Bernanke & Kuttner (2005) show a 25bp surprise cut raises equities 1%, primarily through the risk premium channel. Adrian & Shin (2010) demonstrate that monetary policy operates through financial intermediary balance sheets, making the policy stance a direct determinant of risk appetite.

**Claim 2 — Three-regime model:**
The classic RORO framework (Baele, Bekaert, Inghelbrecht 2010) identifies risk appetite via principal component analysis of cross-asset returns. But this assumes a single dominant factor — effectively a demand shock. When supply shocks dominate, the first principal component fractures: equities fall (negative growth shock) while bonds also fall (inflation shock). This is the "risk-confused" state. Empirically observable in Q4 2022 (60/40 drawdown), the March 2023 banking stress (rates down, equities down but banks vs. tech diverged), and the tariff escalation episodes of 2025. The New Keynesian Phillips Curve offers the theoretical grounding: a cost-push shock shifts the PC adversely, creating a tradeoff between output stabilization and inflation stabilization that is absent in pure demand-shock regimes. This tradeoff propagates into asset prices as ambiguity about the central bank reaction function.

**Claim 3 — FCIs as operational proxy:**
Financial conditions indices aggregate the key transmission variables in the New Keynesian model: the policy rate, the term spread, credit spreads, equity prices, and the exchange rate. These are the five channels through which monetary policy reaches aggregate demand in the Mundell-Fleming-IS/LM framework. An FCI therefore captures the *effective* monetary stance, which can diverge from the *announced* stance when market-based tightening/easing offsets rate moves. Hatzius et al. (2010) formalize this at Goldman Sachs; the Chicago Fed NFCI has been published since 1971. The lead over VIX is because VIX reflects options-implied volatility in one asset class, while FCIs capture the broader transmission mechanism.

**Claim 4 — Structural contagion acceleration:**
Post-GFC regulatory reform reduced dealer balance sheet elasticity. Primary dealer Treasury inventory dropped from ~$250B pre-GFC to roughly flat (sometimes negative) by 2014, and has recovered only partially. Basel III leverage ratio and Supplementary Leverage Ratio constrain balance sheet expansion during stress. Meanwhile, passive equity AUM grew from ~15% to ~50%+ of US equity fund assets (ICI data), and systematic/CTA strategies manage ~$350-400B. Both are mechanistic sellers in risk-off transitions: passive funds don't differentiate, CTAs follow momentum. The result is that the speed of price adjustment during risk-off events has compressed. The October 2023 Treasury market dislocation, the March 2020 dash for cash, and the August 2024 yen carry unwind all exhibited faster peak drawdown velocities than comparable historical episodes.

**Claim 5 — Liquidity cascade hierarchy:**
Perry Mehrling's "hierarchy of money" framework, adapted to financial markets, predicts the cascade sequence. In normal times, credit instruments trade at stable spreads to Treasuries because dealers warehouse risk. When risk appetite shifts, dealers pull back from the lowest-priority instruments first (off-the-run Treasuries, structured credit, EM local currency). The cascade escalates to liquid markets when mark-to-market losses force deleveraging in levered portfolios (Brunnermeier & Pedersen 2009, "margin spiral"). Cross-asset correlation spikes when forced selling becomes indiscriminate. The process stops when the central bank provides backstop liquidity (Fed 2020 facilities, BOE 2022 gilt intervention). The empirical regularity is striking: every major liquidity cascade since 2008 has terminated within 2-4 weeks of central bank intervention.

**Claim 6 — Current regime assessment:**
As of March 2026: (a) Tariff escalation from the Trump administration's trade policy has imposed supply-side costs, raising imported input prices; (b) Federal fiscal deficits remain above 6% of GDP; (c) The Fed has paused its cutting cycle with the funds rate at 4.25-4.50%, well above most estimates of neutral (2.5-3.5%); (d) Core PCE remains in the 2.5-2.8% range, above target but not accelerating. This is precisely the mixed-shock environment where the Taylor Rule gives conflicting signals: the output gap component wants lower rates, the inflation component wants unchanged or higher rates. The Mundell-Fleming model adds another complication: tariffs are simultaneously contractionary for domestic output and inflationary for traded goods prices, a combination that creates ambiguity in the optimal policy response.

**Claim 7 — Correlation regime dependence:**
From `stock_bond_correlation_regime` in our knowledge base: "A 60/40 portfolio assuming -0.2 correlation but realizing +0.3 faces 15-25% higher volatility." This has direct implications for risk appetite cascades. When correlation is negative, a risk-off equity selloff pushes investors into bonds, stabilizing portfolios and reducing forced selling. When correlation is positive, the rebalancing channel breaks: both legs draw down, total portfolio volatility exceeds expectations, and risk parity / volatility-targeting strategies must mechanically deleverage. Campbell, Sunderam & Viceira (2017) provide the theoretical framework; the 2022 episode was the empirical confirmation. The current supply-shock dominant macro regime biases toward positive correlation, amplifying risk-off tail risk.

**Claim 8 — Retailization contagion channel:**
From `retailization_liquidity_risk`: $200B+ in semi-liquid vehicles with quarterly liquidity windows against 5-7 year WAL underlying loans, held by an aging demographic cohort. The Minsky-style concern is that these vehicles have been sold on the promise of equity-like returns with bond-like stability — a promise that remains untested under stress. If a risk-off event coincides with mass retirement drawdown by the 55+ cohort, redemption requests could exceed the quarterly liquidity windows, triggering gates. Gate announcements could spread contagion to adjacent private credit vehicles (rational herding, Diamond & Dybvig 1983 logic applied to interval funds). This is a genuinely novel risk channel with no historical stress test.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Taylor Rule / output gap / credibility triad | 8/10 | Well-established in NK literature (Woodford, Galí, Bernanke-Kuttner). Core framework with extensive empirical backing. Minor deduction because financial frictions are underspecified in baseline NK. |
| 2. Three-regime model (risk-on/off/confused) | 7/10 | Theoretically sound from the NK Phillips Curve, empirically visible in 2022-2025 episodes. But no rigorous statistical regime-switching model has been estimated for this three-regime specification yet. |
| 3. FCIs as operational proxy | 7/10 | Broadly accepted by practitioners and some central banks (GS, Chicago Fed). But FCIs are constructed indices with composition choices that embed model assumptions. Their lead over VIX is sample-dependent. |
| 4. Structural contagion acceleration | 6/10 | Strong qualitative evidence (March 2020, Oct 2023, Aug 2024). But measuring "contagion velocity" precisely is difficult; survivorship bias in episode selection; regulatory reform effects are hard to isolate from other structural changes. |
| 5. Liquidity cascade hierarchy | 7/10 | Well-grounded in Mehrling's framework and Brunnermeier-Pedersen (2009). Empirical regularity of central bank termination is clear. Deduction because the sequence is not invariant — March 2023 (SVB) started in a different node (bank deposits) than the standard model predicts. |
| 6. Current regime = risk-confused | 7/10 | Consistent with observable data (mixed macro signals, indeterminate Taylor Rule, cross-asset correlation instability). But the regime could resolve toward a cleaner demand-shock framework if tariff effects wash out or the Fed moves decisively. |
| 7. Correlation regime dependence | 8/10 | Strongly supported by theory (CSV 2017), data (2022), and our existing knowledge base. The mechanism is clear and measurable. |
| 8. Retailization contagion channel | 5/10 | Genuine novel risk. But the $200B size is modest relative to total financial system assets. Gate mechanisms may contain contagion rather than amplify it. No stress test exists — which means both the risk and its containment are uncertain. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary Policy (confidence 5.5):** Risk appetite regimes are the transmission mechanism through which monetary policy reaches the real economy. The central bank reaction function determines both the equilibrium level of risk premia and the credibility of the "put" that truncates liquidity cascades. The Taylor Rule assessment of current policy stance directly determines whether we are in a risk-supportive or risk-hostile macro regime.

**Volatility Regimes (confidence 3.0, unresearched):** Volatility regimes and risk appetite regimes are near-synonyms in many models. VIX, MOVE, and cross-asset implied volatility are alternative measures of the same underlying latent factor. A Markov-switching model of volatility regimes (Hamilton 1989) applied to multi-asset surfaces would be the natural formalization. This is a critical gap — the volatility regime topic needs to be built to give risk appetite regimes a quantitative backbone.

**Credit Cycles (confidence 6.0):** Credit spreads are both a consequence and a cause of risk appetite regimes. In the Merton structural model, credit spreads widen as firm-value volatility increases — which is a risk-off signal. But wider spreads raise refinancing costs, reducing investment and employment (financial accelerator, Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist 1999), which worsens the output gap and reinforces the risk-off regime. This feedback loop is central to understanding why risk-off transitions can become self-reinforcing.

**Equity Cycles (confidence 5.0):** Equity valuations embed the equity risk premium, which is the asset-pricing expression of risk appetite. Shiller CAPE relative to bond yields is effectively a risk appetite gauge. The current high CAPE (~33-35 for US) conditional on positive stock-bond correlation implies compressed equity risk premia, which makes equities vulnerable to a risk-off regime transition.

**FX Regimes (confidence 4.0):** The "dollar smile" framework (Jen 2001) is a risk-appetite-regime model: the dollar strengthens in both deep risk-off (flight to quality) and strong risk-on (US growth outperformance), but weakens in moderate risk-on (global growth convergence, carry allocation). Yen and Swiss franc dynamics are pure risk-appetite proxies. The August 2024 yen carry unwind is a canonical example of an FX-mediated risk appetite cascade.

**Credit-Equity Linkage (confidence 3.0, unresearched):** This is the core contagion channel through which risk appetite regime transitions propagate across asset classes. The Merton model provides the structural bridge. CRE-to-bank-equity and private-credit-to-public-market channels are the specific current-cycle contagion pathways that need investigation.

**Demographics / Retailization (confidence 6):** From our knowledge base, the `retailization_liquidity_risk` concept introduces a demographic dimension to liquidity cascades: aging investors in semi-liquid vehicles create a novel redemption-driven contagion channel. The `stock_bond_correlation_regime` concept affects the diversification buffer that dampens risk-off cascades.

**Fiscal Dominance (confidence 5.0):** If fiscal dominance constrains the central bank's ability to ease during a risk-off event (because inflation concerns prevent rate cuts), the "central bank put" becomes less credible, and liquidity cascades last longer and go deeper. This is the key structural risk for the 2026-2028 window.

## Open Questions

1. **Can the "risk-confused" regime be formally estimated?** A three-state Markov-switching model on cross-asset returns with time-varying transition probabilities conditioned on supply vs. demand shock identification would be the appropriate empirical test. I am not aware of published work doing this with the specific supply/demand decomposition from the New Keynesian Phillips Curve.

2. **What is the current r-star, and how does its uncertainty affect risk appetite regime stability?** The Laubach-Williams and Holston-Laubach-Williams estimates diverge (2.5-3.5% nominal). If the true neutral rate is at the upper end, current policy is barely restrictive and risk assets are better supported than consensus believes. If at the lower end, policy is meaningfully tight and the risk-off tail is fatter.

3. **How has the central bank reaction function changed post-2020?** The speed and scale of the March 2020 intervention set a precedent. Has this permanently compressed the tail of risk-off events by anchoring expectations of backstop liquidity? Or has it created moral hazard that amplifies the pre-intervention buildup of leverage and risk-taking (the "Greenspan/Powell put" problem)?

4. **What is the quantitative threshold for private credit retailization contagion?** At $200B, is the semi-liquid private credit market large enough to generate systemic spillovers, or would gates simply contain losses within the vehicle without broader cascade effects? What would the critical mass need to be?

5. **How do tariff-driven supply shocks interact with the risk appetite regime framework differently from energy or pandemic supply shocks?** Tariffs are policy-chosen and potentially reversible, which creates option-like uncertainty around their persistence. Energy shocks are exogenous. The endogeneity of tariff shocks to political dynamics may create a different risk appetite regime structure than the standard NK supply-shock model implies.

6. **What is the empirical lead-lag structure between FCIs, VIX, credit spreads, and realized macroeconomic outcomes in the post-GFC sample?** Prior research used pre-GFC data. The structural changes to market microstructure (dealer constraints, passive dominance) may have altered the dynamic.

7. **How does QT interact with risk appetite regime transitions?** Quantitative tightening removes reserves from the banking system, reducing dealer balance sheet capacity. Does this lower the threshold at which a risk-off shock triggers a liquidity cascade? The September 2019 repo spike and October 2023 Treasury dislocation suggest yes, but the sample is too small for statistical confidence.
