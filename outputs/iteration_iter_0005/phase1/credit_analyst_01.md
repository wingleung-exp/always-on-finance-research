## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Bifurcated Credit Regime as Structural Novelty

The four-phase credit taxonomy (compression → complacency → repricing → distress) from iter_0004 remains the correct framework, but the current cycle introduces a structural wrinkle: the public and private credit markets are in *different phases*. Public HY sits in late complacency (HY OAS 15-20th percentile post-GFC, covenant-lite >80%, new issue concession near zero). Private credit, by contrast, is exhibiting early-repricing characteristics *masked by mark-to-model*: PIK toggle exercise rates have risen from ~5% to ~15-20% of performing loans in sponsor-backed portfolios; amendment activity has surged (amend-and-extend volumes exceeding $300-400B over the past 18 months); EBITDA addback inflation persists (reported leverage ~6.5x vs. effective leverage ~8-10x for B-rated private borrowers).

This is not merely a data gap. The bifurcation is itself a regime feature because it creates a false signal environment: public market participants observe tight spreads and conclude that risk appetite is robust, while the private market's early-repricing signals are invisible. When the private market's delayed recognition of losses eventually surfaces — through BDC earnings misses, semi-liquid vehicle redemption pressure, or sponsor equity write-downs — the *surprise* component amplifies the repricing in public markets. The precedent is real estate in 2007: private CMBS losses were visible to insiders 12-18 months before public markets repriced.

### Claim 2: CLO Arbitrage Threshold — Refined Assessment

CLOs purchase 60-70% of institutional leveraged loans. The formation economics are straightforward: when AAA tranche spreads widen past SOFR+170-180bp, CLO equity IRR drops below the ~12% threshold required to attract equity capital, and new formation stalls. Current AAA spreads at SOFR+140-155bp leave ~25-30bp of cushion.

The iter_0005 refinement: private credit direct lending vehicles have partially substituted for CLO demand in the leveraged loan market, originating an estimated $200-300B annually in loans that would previously have been syndicated into CLO warehouses. This substitution means the CLO shutdown threshold may not produce the same immediate "marginal bid disappears" effect as in 2022, because private lenders can absorb some of the overflow. However, this substitution operates at lower credit quality (private lenders are taking the B-/CCC-adjacent paper that CLOs would reject) and with less transparent pricing. The net effect: the CLO threshold remains a robust signal for *liquid* leveraged loan market stress, but the overall credit market's sensitivity to CLO shutdown has partially shifted to the private channel, where the stress will manifest later and less visibly.

The 2022 calibration point: when AAA spreads hit SOFR+200bp+, CLO formation effectively stopped. Leveraged loan prices fell 5-7 points. Private credit vehicles, however, continued lending — and much of the vintage of loans originated during that period is now in the amendment-and-extend pipeline.

The upgrade/downgrade ratio has now decelerated for four consecutive quarters (from ~2.2x to ~1.3x, with the most recent quarter showing further deterioration toward ~1.1x), while HY OAS has tightened or remained flat. Historical precedent:

The current episode is now at 4 quarters from a spread starting point of ~310-330bp. The 2012 false positive occurred when QE3 overwhelmed the fundamental deterioration. The relevant filter: is there a concurrent policy impulse large enough to override the signal? The fiscal impulse remains positive but decelerating; no QE is in place; the Fed is in a cautious cutting posture. This makes the current signal more analogous to 2006-2007 or 2014-2015 than to 2012.

The 30% false positive rate cited in iter_0004 drops to ~15-20% when conditioned on the absence of concurrent large-scale asset purchases or equivalent monetary impulse.

### Claim 4: Minsky-Phased Default Forecasting

Standard top-down default models (linking defaults to GDP, unemployment, financial conditions) capture ~60% of variance in normal periods but only ~25% at regime transitions. The Minsky phase overlay from iter_0004 (MC-2) provides the missing conditioning variable:

- **Household sector (hedge phase):** Debt service ratios historically moderate, home equity high. Default contribution: minimal and below-cycle. This sector will *not* be the source of default acceleration.
- **Public corporate sector (speculative-mature):** HY default rates at ~2.5-3.0% trailing. Top-down models forecast 3.0-3.5% baseline. The conditional distribution is asymmetric: a 100bp spread widening would push 12-month defaults to 4.5-5.5% as the vulnerable tail (ICR <1.5x, ~15-18% of B-rated issuers) loses market access.
- **Private credit (Ponzi-fragile):** The "reported" default rate of ~2-3% is fictitious as a measure of credit distress. Amend-and-extend has converted what would be defaults into performing-but-impaired loans. The *economic* default rate — incorporating loans where the borrower cannot service debt from cash flow without PIK, sponsor equity injections, or maturity extensions — is likely 8-12% for 2020-2021 vintage private credit loans.

The aggregate default rate forecast therefore depends entirely on when the private credit masking breaks down. On a 12-month horizon: public HY defaults of 3.0-4.0% (base), 5.5-7.0% (stress). On a 24-month horizon: if private credit losses surface, the combined public-plus-private default equivalent could reach 6-8% on a blended basis, with private credit contributing the bulk of the increment.

### Claim 5: Maturity Wall × Term Premium Interaction

The maturity wall analysis from iter_0004 (MC-3) needs a critical update: issuers refinancing from the 2020-2021 vintage face not only spread risk but also a structurally higher base rate. A BB issuer that locked in a 4.5% coupon in 2021 (T+150bp when the 5Y Treasury was 0.8%) now faces refinancing at 5.8% (Treasury) + 175-200bp (spread) = 7.5-8.0% even in a *benign* spread environment. Under a repricing scenario (spreads +200bp), the cost rises to 9.5-10.0%.

The interest coverage impact: a company with EBITDA of $100M and $500M of debt refinancing from 4.5% to 8.0% sees annual interest expense rise from $22.5M to $40M — a 78% increase. ICR drops from 4.4x to 2.5x. For B-rated issuers with already-thin coverage, this pushes ICR below the 1.5x distress threshold.

The term premium channel (HC-2 from iter_0004: structural shift from negative to positive) means this is not a cyclical phenomenon that mean-reverts if the Fed cuts. Even aggressive rate cuts would only reduce the Treasury component; the term premium component (~50bp+) and the spread component are independent.

### Claim 6: EBP Signal Degradation

The Gilchrist-Zakrajšek excess bond premium decomposition requires a universe of publicly traded corporate bonds with observable market prices. Private credit's growth from <$500B (2015) to $1.7-2.0T (2025) has removed approximately 30-40% of the relevant credit universe from price discovery. The EBP calculated from observable public bonds therefore captures risk appetite only in the *visible* portion of the market.

If private credit mark-to-model is systematically overstating credit quality (as the evidence on addbacks and PIK suggests), then the true risk appetite embedded in the full credit universe is even more compressed than the visible EBP indicates. The EBP signal is directionally reliable but magnitude-impaired — it is reading the *most optimistically priced* segment of the credit market as representative of the whole.

### Claim 10: Fiscal-Term Premium Tension — Credit Market Resolution

The observable consequence: the BB-CCC spread differential should widen *before* aggregate HY spreads move. This is the early signal — a rotation within credit quality tiers, not a uniform repricing. Current BB-CCC differential: monitoring for widening from current compressed levels (~250-300bp) toward the 400-500bp range that historically signals late-complacency breakdown.

2. **Has the CLO-to-private-credit substitution permanently raised the CLO shutdown threshold?** If private credit can absorb $100-200B of annual loan demand that previously required CLO formation, then the CLO shutdown triggers a *narrower* market stress (liquid leveraged loans only) rather than a *systemic* credit withdrawal. The substitution's durability under stress is untested.

3. **What is the false-positive-adjusted signal value of the fundamental-spread divergence?** The raw 4/5 hit rate (80%) with one QE-related false positive suggests conditioning on monetary policy regime improves accuracy to ~85-90%. But n=5 is too small for statistical confidence. Extending the sample to include IG divergence episodes (potentially doubling the observations) would help.

4. **Can the BB-CCC differential widening prediction (Claim 10) be backtested as a leading indicator?** If the fiscal-sustains-IG-but-erodes-HY hypothesis is correct, prior episodes of fiscal expansion with rising term premium should show BB-CCC widening preceding aggregate HY spread widening. 5. **What does the macro-regime × credit-phase matrix predict for the *next* 12 months under each macro scenario?** The matrix (Claim 7) is static. A dynamic version would assign transition probabilities between cells conditional on observable macro variables (fiscal impulse direction, term premium level, inflation composition). 6. **How large is the semi-liquid private credit vehicle redemption channel at scale?** $100-300B in non-traded BDCs and interval funds is the estimated exposure. The BREIT/SREIT episode (2022-2023) showed redemption gating under moderate stress. At what AUM threshold does redemption pressure create forced selling of underlying loans, and does the private loan secondary market have sufficient depth to absorb it without a 15-20 point price dislocation?

7. **Is the upgrade/downgrade ratio a reliable indicator when the rating agencies themselves have recalibrated post-2020?** Moody's and S&P were criticized for slow downgrades during COVID. If they have since become more proactive (faster to downgrade, slower to upgrade), the current ratio deceleration may partially reflect methodological tightening rather than pure fundamental deterioration. Separating rating agency behavior change from issuer credit quality change would sharpen the signal.
