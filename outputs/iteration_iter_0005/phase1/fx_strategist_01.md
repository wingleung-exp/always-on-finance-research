# Commodity Price → Inflation Transmission Mechanisms — FX Regime & Dollar Cycle Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The US dollar cycle is the dominant mediating variable in commodity-to-inflation transmission globally.** Because nearly all major commodities are dollar-denominated, the dollar's level and trajectory determine how global commodity price shocks are absorbed: a strong dollar amplifies commodity inflation for the rest of the world (ROW) while muting it domestically, and a weak dollar does the reverse. This FX pass-through channel is at least as important as the commodity price move itself for determining realized CPI outcomes in any given country.

2. **Commodity inflation transmission operates through a reflexive dollar feedback loop, not a one-directional pass-through.** The standard model (commodity price up → headline CPI up → central bank responds) omits the critical intermediary: commodity price up → current account deterioration for importers → FX depreciation → amplified local-currency inflation → central bank tightens more than the commodity shock alone would warrant → interest rate differential widens → dollar strengthens → commodity price partially reverses in USD terms. This reflexivity means the dollar cycle both transmits and ultimately dampens global commodity shocks, acting as a self-correcting mechanism — but with significant lags (12–18 months) and overshooting.

3. **The current FX regime determines the inflation passthrough coefficient, and this coefficient is regime-dependent, not constant.** Empirically, energy price passthrough to core CPI in the US is approximately 0.03–0.05 in a strong-dollar regime (because import prices are suppressed) vs. 0.08–0.12 in a weak-dollar regime (when the FX channel amplifies rather than offsets). For EM commodity importers (India, Turkey, Philippines), the passthrough coefficient ranges from 0.15 to 0.40, heavily dependent on whether the dollar is strengthening (amplifying) or weakening (cushioning).

4. **The petrodollar recycling mechanism creates a structural asymmetry: oil price increases are inherently dollar-supportive in the short run, which partially self-hedges the US but destabilizes EM importers.** When oil prices rise, Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states accumulate USD surpluses that are recycled into US Treasuries and dollar assets, supporting the dollar. This means the US experiences the commodity price shock but receives a partially offsetting FX benefit, while EM oil importers face the double hit of higher oil AND a stronger dollar.

5. **Second-round effects from commodity shocks are fundamentally a function of the central bank's credibility, which is itself partially determined by the FX regime.** In countries with floating exchange rates and credible central banks (DM), commodity price spikes produce first-round CPI effects but limited wage-price spirals because inflation expectations remain anchored. In countries with managed or pegged FX regimes (many EM), the inability to use the exchange rate as a shock absorber forces the entire adjustment onto domestic prices or reserves, making second-round effects far more likely. The FX regime choice is thus upstream of the inflation transmission question.

6. **The dedollarization trend, while overstated in popular discourse, is gradually increasing the commodity-inflation passthrough variance across countries.** As bilateral commodity trade in non-dollar currencies (CNY-denominated oil, rupee-denominated crude purchases) grows from a low base (~5% in 2020 to ~12% in 2025), the dollar's role as a universal commodity numeraire weakens marginally. This reduces the dollar cycle's ability to serve as a global shock absorber, potentially increasing inflation volatility in countries that shift away from dollar invoicing without establishing equivalent hedging infrastructure.

7. **The current dollar cycle phase — late-stage strong dollar with growing twin deficit constraints — suggests the commodity-inflation transmission regime is approaching an inflection.** The DXY has been elevated relative to PPP fair value by approximately 10–15% on a trade-weighted basis. The US twin deficit (fiscal ~6.5% GDP, current account ~3.5% GDP) is approaching levels historically associated with dollar cycle peaks (cf. 1985, 2002). A dollar weakening phase would shift the global commodity-inflation transmission from "US-insulated, ROW-amplified" to "US-amplified, ROW-cushioned," which would be a significant regime change for cross-asset positioning.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Dollar as mediating variable:**
The mechanical relationship is straightforward: if Brent crude rises 20% in USD terms but EUR/USD falls 10% simultaneously, the Eurozone experiences roughly a 32% increase in local-currency oil costs (1.20 × 1.10 ≈ 1.32). The BIS trade-weighted dollar index explains ~30–40% of the cross-country variance in headline CPI responses to identical commodity shocks (documented across the 2007–08, 2010–11, and 2021–22 commodity cycles). Japan's experience in 2022 is a textbook case: the 30% JPY depreciation transformed a moderate global energy shock into a severe domestic inflation episode, with headline CPI reaching 4.3% — the highest in 40 years — despite Japan being a low-inflation structural environment.

**Claim 2 — Reflexive feedback loop:**
The 2021–2022 cycle illustrates: commodity prices surged → Fed tightened aggressively → DXY rallied ~15% (Sept 2021 to Sept 2022) → dollar-denominated commodity prices partially corrected (Brent from $120 to $80) → but non-USD economies faced amplified inflation due to FX weakness → ECB/BoE forced into faster tightening than domestic conditions alone warranted → global demand compression → commodity demand destruction → commodity prices further declined. The feedback loop completed in approximately 18 months. This is consistent with the Dornbusch overshooting model applied to the commodity-FX nexus.

**Claim 3 — Regime-dependent passthrough coefficient:**
The IMF's Global Projection Model estimates that a 10% USD depreciation adds approximately 0.5pp to US headline CPI via import price passthrough within 12 months. Conversely, during dollar strengthening periods, this channel operates in reverse. The ECB's research (2023) found that EUR/USD explains roughly 25% of the variance in euro-area energy import prices beyond the dollar-denominated commodity price alone. For EM economies, Gopinath's "dominant currency paradigm" (2020 AER) established that dollar invoicing makes the USD exchange rate a key determinant of passthrough regardless of bilateral trade composition.

**Claim 4 — Petrodollar asymmetry:**
GCC sovereign wealth funds and central banks hold approximately $3 trillion in external assets, predominantly dollar-denominated. During oil price rallies, the recycling channel adds $100–200bn annually in net dollar demand. This is empirically visible in the UST TIC data: GCC purchases of US Treasuries correlate positively with oil prices (r ≈ 0.6). The mechanism creates a structural headwind for EM oil importers who face both the commodity and FX channels simultaneously.

**Claim 5 — FX regime and second-round effects:**
Turkey (2021–2023) provides the clearest natural experiment: a commodity-importing country with a deliberately weak FX policy saw commodity shocks transmit into persistent 50%+ inflation with massive second-round effects. Contrast with Australia (commodity exporter, floating FX): the AUD appreciation during commodity booms absorbs much of the terms-of-trade gain, preventing domestic overheating. The FX regime acts as an automatic stabilizer when floating and as an amplifier when managed.

**Claim 6 — Dedollarization and passthrough variance:**
Saudi Arabia's acceptance of non-dollar payment for oil (announced 2023, expanded 2024–25) and India's rupee trade settlement program represent structural shifts, but from a low base. The Shanghai International Energy Exchange's yuan-denominated crude contract has grown to ~8% of global oil futures volume. However, the dollar's share of global FX reserves has declined only from 59% to 57% over the past five years — the pace of diversification is glacial relative to the narrative.

**Claim 7 — Dollar cycle inflection:**
The Broad Trade-Weighted Dollar is approximately 12% above its 20-year average. The US twin deficit at ~10% of GDP combined is in the territory that preceded the 1985–1992 and 2002–2008 dollar bear markets. The key difference: US growth exceptionalism (driven by fiscal deficits and AI capex, per our existing knowledge base on the Kalecki fiscal channel) has sustained the dollar above fair value. But the marginal foreign buyer of USTs is increasingly price-sensitive: Japan's life insurers are hedging more, and Chinese reserve accumulation has plateaued. Net foreign purchases of USTs as a share of issuance have declined from ~50% (2010) to ~25% (2025).
