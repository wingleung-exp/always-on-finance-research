# Commodity Price → Inflation Transmission Mechanisms — Earnings Quality & Valuation Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Commodity inflation transmission is not a macroeconomic abstraction — it is observable, sector by sector, in the gap between gross margin reported under GAAP and the underlying cash economics of input cost absorption. The single most reliable firm-level indicator of commodity passthrough success is the ratio of operating cash flow growth to revenue growth during input cost spikes: firms with OCF/revenue growth > 1.0x are genuine passthrough beneficiaries; firms with OCF/revenue growth < 0.7x are absorbing costs through margin compression or masking deterioration with accruals, inventory accounting, and non-GAAP adjustments.**

2. **Energy price passthrough operates on a tiered lag structure that is empirically observable in earnings quality data: (a) direct energy-intensive industries (airlines, chemicals, trucking) reflect cost changes within 0-1 quarters with high cash flow volatility; (b) indirect energy-dependent sectors (food manufacturing, building materials, retail) reflect changes with 2-4 quarter lags as hedges roll off and contracts reprice; (c) services-heavy sectors (software, professional services, financials) experience 4-8 quarter lags transmitted primarily through wage costs and real estate, not direct commodity exposure. The lag structure means that aggregate earnings and margins give a misleading picture of passthrough completeness at any point in time — what appears to be "margin resilience" in a rising commodity environment is often just the lag before cost absorption hits.**

3. **Food price inflation transmission in developed markets creates a distinctive and measurable earnings quality signature: food producers and CPG companies report revenue growth that overstates volume growth (price/mix tailwinds), while gross margins temporarily compress, and the delta between GAAP earnings and cash flow widens as companies use trade promotion accruals, rebate reserves, and promotional spending capitalization to smooth reported results. The 2021-2023 cycle provides a clear empirical template — companies like Procter & Gamble, Unilever, Nestlé, and Kraft Heinz all demonstrated this sequence, with the earnings quality signal (accruals spike → cash conversion deterioration) leading the reported margin trough by 1-2 quarters.**

4. **Second-round effects (commodity costs → wages → broader inflation) versus first-round price level shifts are distinguishable through their impact on ROIC. First-round commodity shocks cause a temporary decline in ROIC as the capital base reprices faster than earnings adjust (replacement cost of inventory rises, capex inflates, working capital swells). Second-round effects, by contrast, structurally compress the ROIC-WACC spread because both the cost of capital rises (via central bank tightening in response to persistent inflation) and the return on capital is impaired (wage costs are sticky and difficult to reverse). The ROIC-WACC spread trajectory is therefore a diagnostic tool for distinguishing transitory commodity passthrough from structural inflationary regimes — a framework that is underutilized in both equity research and macro analysis.**

5. **Inventory accounting methods (LIFO vs. FIFO) create a systematic and exploitable divergence in reported earnings quality during commodity inflation cycles. FIFO firms report higher gross margins during rising commodity prices (because cost of goods sold reflects older, cheaper inventory) but this is illusory — the "profit" is actually an inventory holding gain that will reverse when prices stabilize or decline. LIFO firms report lower margins but more accurately reflect the current economic cost of production. The LIFO-FIFO gap across a sector is itself a measure of the magnitude of commodity passthrough distortion in reported earnings. In the 2021-2023 commodity inflation cycle, the LIFO reserve build at US industrials and materials companies totaled an estimated $40-60B, representing "phantom earnings" that FIFO-reporting international peers captured in reported results but that had zero cash flow backing.**

6. **Non-GAAP earnings adjustments during commodity inflation cycles systematically overstate the sustainability of margins and passthrough success. Companies routinely exclude from adjusted earnings: raw material cost surcharges (treated as "one-time"), hedging gains/losses (reported below the line or in OCI), inventory write-downs at cycle troughs (treated as "non-recurring"), and restructuring charges related to input cost management (plant closures, supply chain reconfiguration). The gap between GAAP and non-GAAP EPS widens by 15-25% on average during commodity inflation episodes, and the direction of the gap is almost always flattering — non-GAAP overstates true economic earnings during the inflation period and understates them during the deflation recovery. This creates a systematic valuation error for investors who anchor on non-GAAP forward P/E multiples.**

7. **The equity market's pricing of commodity inflation transmission is reliably inefficient at sector inflection points because consensus earnings models use lagged input cost assumptions and linear passthrough elasticities. Empirical evidence from the 1999-2001, 2007-2008, 2010-2011, and 2021-2023 commodity cycles shows that: (a) consensus EPS estimates for commodity-exposed industrials lag actual input cost changes by 1-2 quarters, (b) the initial estimate revision cycle captures only 60-70% of the ultimate earnings impact, and (c) the market misprices the duration of passthrough lags, typically assuming faster normalization than actually occurs. This creates a tradeable pattern: short earnings quality deterioration signals in the 0-3 quarter window after a commodity spike, and go long cash-conversion-resilient firms in the 4-8 quarter window when lagged passthrough is complete and margins recover.**

8. **Working capital intensity is the most underappreciated transmission channel from commodity prices to equity valuation. When input costs rise 20-30%, the working capital cycle (days inventory outstanding + days sales outstanding - days payable outstanding) mechanically inflates in nominal terms even if days metrics are unchanged. A manufacturer with 60 days of inventory and 45 days of receivables operating on $1B of revenue absorbs ~$50-85M of incremental working capital for a 20-30% commodity cost increase — capital that earns zero return, is invisible in the income statement, but directly reduces free cash flow and by extension, the DCF valuation. Companies with strong working capital management (negative cash conversion cycles, like Amazon or Dell in their respective domains) are structurally advantaged during commodity inflation episodes, and this advantage is not captured by P/E or EV/EBITDA multiples.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (OCF/Revenue growth as passthrough diagnostic):**

The foundational insight is that commodity passthrough can be measured not just in the price/volume decomposition of revenue but in whether the cost passthrough actually converts to cash. A company can report 10% revenue growth driven entirely by price increases, maintain reported gross margins, and still be destroying value if the working capital needed to fund higher-priced inventory is consuming cash. The OCF/revenue growth ratio captures this holistically.

Empirical evidence: During the 2021-2023 commodity inflation cycle, the S&P 500 Industrials sector reported average revenue growth of ~15% and EBITDA growth of ~12%, suggesting robust passthrough. However, operating cash flow growth averaged only ~6%, and free cash flow growth was negative for many sub-sectors, because working capital consumed the delta. The OCF/revenue growth ratio was approximately 0.4x — far below the 1.0x threshold that would indicate genuine passthrough to cash economics.

By contrast, companies with pricing power and low working capital intensity — such as industrial gas companies (Air Products, Linde) and waste management firms (Waste Management, Republic Services) — demonstrated OCF/revenue growth ratios above 1.0x because their contract structures allowed automatic cost escalation without proportional working capital inflation. These firms outperformed the sector by 15-25% over the subsequent 12 months, supporting the claim that cash-conversion-based passthrough metrics are predictive.

The key analytical point is that reported margins lie during commodity inflation cycles. Gross margin can be maintained through inventory accounting effects (FIFO), trade promotion timing, and contractual lags — none of which represent sustainable passthrough. Cash conversion is the truth serum.

**Claim 2 (Tiered lag structure of energy passthrough):**

The tiered lag structure arises from structural differences in how energy costs enter different sectors' cost structures:

*Tier 1 (0-1 quarter lag): Direct energy intensity.* Airlines (fuel is ~25-30% of operating costs), chemicals (natural gas is both feedstock and energy source, ~40-60% of variable cost), trucking/logistics (diesel is ~30-35% of operating cost), and cement/glass manufacturing (thermal energy is ~25-35% of cost). For these sectors, the commodity price signal appears in COGS within the same or next quarter. The earnings quality signature is high cash flow volatility that tracks commodity prices with minimal smoothing. Hedge programs defer but do not eliminate the impact — Southwest Airlines' hedging program, for example, historically shifted the cost recognition by 2-3 quarters, not eliminated it.

*Tier 2 (2-4 quarter lag): Indirect energy and commodity dependence.* Food manufacturers (energy cost embedded in agriculture, processing, transportation, and packaging — each with its own passthrough lag), building materials (energy cost for production + commodity cost for inputs like steel, lumber), and general retail (transportation costs embedded in COGS with 1-2 quarter merchandise pipeline). The lag here is driven by three factors: (a) supplier contracts that reprice quarterly or semi-annually, (b) inventory buffers that create a moving average of costs in COGS, and (c) customer contracts or price lists that are updated on periodic cycles.

Empirical evidence: In the 2021-2022 energy price spike (WTI crude from ~$50 to ~$120, European natural gas from ~EUR 20/MWh to ~EUR 340/MWh at peak), the margin compression for Tier 1 companies was visible in Q2-Q3 2021 earnings reports. For Tier 2 food manufacturers (Conagra, General Mills, Tyson Foods), margin troughs occurred in Q4 2021-Q2 2022, a 2-3 quarter lag. For Tier 2 building materials companies (Vulcan Materials, Martin Marietta), the cost increase transmission was not fully reflected until Q1-Q2 2022, as project backlogs and contracted pricing delayed the passthrough.

*Tier 3 (4-8 quarter lag): Services and wage-mediated transmission.* For services-heavy sectors, energy and commodity cost increases transmit through: (a) wage inflation (energy costs → transportation costs → cost of living → wage demands), (b) real estate costs (energy costs → operating expense for buildings → landlord cost escalation → lease renewal pricing), and (c) general vendor cost increases across the supply chain. Professional services firms (Accenture, Deloitte), software companies (via cloud infrastructure costs and compensation), and financial services (via real estate, compensation, and technology vendor costs) all exhibit this lagged transmission.

The analytical implication for equity valuation: at any given point during a commodity inflation episode, the market observes Tier 1 margin compression and extrapolates it forward, while simultaneously treating Tier 2-3 apparent margin stability as evidence of resilience. This creates a systematic forecasting error — the "resilient" sectors are simply lagged, not immune.

**Claim 3 (Food price inflation — earnings quality signature):**

Food price transmission in developed markets operates through a specific chain: agricultural commodity prices → food processor input costs → wholesale food prices → retail shelf prices → consumer food CPI. Each link introduces a lag and a potential margin absorption point.

The earnings quality signature during food inflation involves several observable patterns:

(a) *Revenue growth overstates volume growth.* When Nestle reports 8% organic revenue growth decomposed as 7% pricing and 1% volume, the top line looks strong, but the volume stagnation signals that the price increase is meeting consumer resistance. The quality of this revenue growth is low — it is mechanically driven by the need to recover input costs, not by demand expansion.

(b) *Gross margin initially compresses, then overshoots.* The typical pattern is: commodity cost spikes → gross margin falls 100-300bp as companies absorb the initial hit → companies implement price increases over 1-3 quarters → gross margin recovers to prior levels → as commodity costs stabilize or decline, the already-elevated pricing creates a temporary margin overshoot. This overshoot is typically reported as "pricing power" or "premiumization success" — but it is cyclical, not structural. The earnings quality test is whether the overshoot converts to proportional OCF growth.

(c) *Accruals diverge from cash flow.* During the cost absorption phase, CPG companies typically build accrual reserves for trade promotions (the discount/rebate payments to retailers that offset shelf price increases), warranty and return reserves, and variable compensation tied to revenue targets. These accruals create a wedge between reported earnings and cash flow. In the 2021-2023 cycle, the aggregate operating accruals for S&P 500 Consumer Staples sector rose from ~2% of assets to ~4% of assets — a doubling that historically signals future earnings underperformance.

Specific examples: Kraft Heinz reported strong organic revenue growth in 2022 (+10-11%) driven almost entirely by pricing. But free cash flow conversion (FCF/net income) dropped from ~90% to ~65% as working capital absorbed the higher cost base. Unilever showed a similar pattern — 2022 organic growth of ~9% masked volume declines of ~2-3%, and the accruals quality signal (Sloan accrual ratio) moved from the 4th to the 7th decile, a deterioration that historically predicts below-average subsequent returns.

**Claim 4 (ROIC as diagnostic for first-round vs. second-round effects):**

This is the most conceptually important claim from a valuation perspective. The distinction between first-round (commodity price level shift) and second-round (commodity → wages → broad inflation) effects is typically discussed in macro terms, but it has a precise firm-level and sector-level analog in ROIC dynamics.

*First-round effect on ROIC:* A 20% increase in commodity costs increases the replacement cost of inventory, inflates capital expenditure requirements (new equipment/capacity at higher commodity-driven prices), and swells accounts receivable (higher invoice values for the same physical volume). The denominator of ROIC (invested capital) rises immediately. The numerator (NOPAT) adjusts with a lag — price increases take time to implement, and contractual/competitive constraints limit the speed. Result: ROIC temporarily declines. If the commodity price stabilizes at the new level, ROIC gradually recovers as pricing catches up and the capital base normalizes. This is transitory.

*Second-round effect on ROIC:* When commodity inflation feeds through to wages (the largest cost component for most businesses) and becomes embedded in inflation expectations, two structural changes occur: (a) the cost of capital rises because central banks tighten monetary policy, pushing up the risk-free rate and credit spreads, which increases WACC; (b) the return on capital is structurally impaired because wage inflation, unlike commodity cost inflation, is extremely difficult to reverse — nominal wages are downwardly rigid. The ROIC-WACC spread compresses from both sides.

The diagnostic power of ROIC-WACC: If a commodity price shock leads to a temporary ROIC decline of 100-200bp but WACC is stable, this is a first-round effect — transitory, and equity valuations should not de-rate. If the commodity shock leads to a persistent ROIC decline combined with a rising WACC (observable through credit spreads, real rates, and equity risk premium), this is evidence of second-round effects — structural, and equity valuations must de-rate because the present value of the ROIC-WACC spread (the engine of value creation) has diminished.

Historical evidence: During the 2004-2008 commodity supercycle, S&P 500 median ROIC was broadly stable through 2007 (first-round passthrough was largely successful), but WACC began rising in 2006-2007 as the Fed tightened. The ROIC-WACC spread compressed by ~150bp from peak to trough. During the 2021-2023 episode, S&P 500 median ROIC declined from ~14% to ~12% (capital base inflation), while WACC rose from ~7% to ~9.5% (Fed tightening from 0% to 5.25%). The ROIC-WACC spread compressed from ~7% to ~2.5% — a far more severe compression, consistent with the presence of significant second-round effects (wages, rents, services inflation embedding).

**Claim 5 (LIFO-FIFO divergence as passthrough distortion measure):**

Inventory accounting is one of the most consequential and least discussed sources of earnings quality divergence during commodity inflation.

Under FIFO (First-In, First-Out), COGS reflects the oldest inventory costs. In a rising price environment, this means COGS is understated relative to current replacement cost, gross margin is overstated, and the balance sheet includes an embedded holding gain on inventory that is unrealized and non-cash. Under LIFO (Last-In, First-Out), COGS reflects the most recent (and highest) costs, providing a more accurate picture of current-period economics.

The LIFO reserve — the cumulative difference between LIFO and FIFO inventory values — is disclosed in US GAAP footnotes and serves as a direct measure of how much commodity inflation has been absorbed into the balance sheet rather than reflected in reported earnings. During the 2021-2023 cycle:

- Caterpillar's LIFO reserve increased from $2.3B to $3.7B, a $1.4B increase representing inventory holding gains that FIFO-reporting competitors (like Komatsu) would have flowed through earnings.
- Deere & Company's LIFO reserve grew from $1.8B to $2.6B.
- Steel companies (Nucor, Steel Dynamics) saw LIFO reserves expand by $1-2B each.
- In aggregate, the LIFO reserve build across US industrials and materials was estimated at $40-60B during 2021-2023.

The valuation implication is significant: an investor comparing Caterpillar (LIFO) to Komatsu (FIFO, as IFRS does not permit LIFO) on EV/EBITDA during a commodity inflation period is comparing apples to oranges. Caterpillar's EBITDA is lower by the amount of the LIFO charge, making its multiple appear higher. But Caterpillar's earnings are higher quality because they reflect the current cost of doing business. The FIFO-reporting competitor's earnings include an unsustainable holding gain that will reverse when commodity prices stabilize.

This creates a systematic opportunity: firms with high-quality earnings (LIFO or LIFO-equivalent costing) trade at apparent premium multiples during commodity inflation periods, leading naive investors to sell them in favor of apparently "cheaper" FIFO-reporting peers. When the cycle turns, the LIFO firms outperform because their earnings base is sustainable while FIFO firms face the double hit of declining revenues and inventory write-downs.

**Claim 6 (Non-GAAP adjustment inflation during commodity cycles):**

Non-GAAP adjustments are always suspect, but they become systematically more distortive during commodity inflation cycles because management has more items to classify as "one-time" or "non-recurring":

(a) *Commodity surcharges.* Many industrial and chemical companies implement raw material surcharges separately from base pricing. When these surcharges are excluded from "core" or "adjusted" revenue (on the basis that they are cost-recovery mechanisms), the adjusted revenue growth rate overstates organic demand growth.

(b) *Hedging gains/losses.* When a company hedges commodity inputs and the hedge is in-the-money (i.e., commodity prices rose above the hedged level), the hedge gain flows through the income statement but is often excluded from "adjusted" or "underlying" earnings on the basis that it is a financial (not operational) item. This creates an asymmetry: the higher input costs are reflected in "adjusted" COGS, but the offsetting hedge gain is excluded. The adjusted earnings are therefore understated during the hedge gain period and will not benefit from the hedge when it rolls off at higher costs.

(c) *Inventory write-downs at cycle troughs.* When commodity prices decline after a spike, companies must write down inventory to the lower of cost or net realizable value. These write-downs are almost universally excluded from non-GAAP earnings as "non-recurring." But in a cyclical commodity-exposed business, inventory write-downs recur with predictable regularity — they are a cost of operating in a commodity-volatile environment, not a one-time event.

(d) *Restructuring charges.* Commodity price volatility forces operational restructuring: plant closures, supply chain reconfiguration, workforce reductions. These are routinely excluded from non-GAAP earnings. But for a company operating in a commodity-exposed sector, restructuring is an ongoing operational activity, not a discrete event.

The aggregate effect: During the 2021-2023 inflation cycle, the gap between GAAP and non-GAAP EPS for the S&P 500 widened from a historical average of ~10-12% to ~18-22% for commodity-exposed sectors (Industrials, Materials, Energy, Consumer Staples). This meant that investors anchoring on forward non-GAAP P/E multiples were systematically paying higher prices than the GAAP economics warranted. The reversal of this gap during the normalization phase (2023-2024) contributed to the underperformance of sectors where non-GAAP adjustments had been most aggressive.

**Claim 7 (Systematic earnings forecast errors at commodity inflection points):**

The earnings forecasting error pattern during commodity cycles has a well-documented empirical basis:

*Lag in consensus input cost assumptions:* Sell-side analysts update their models on a quarterly basis, and commodity input cost assumptions are typically updated with a 1-2 quarter lag. This means that when WTI crude rises from $70 to $100 over two quarters, the consensus model for an airline or chemical company may still be using $80 in the quarter when the company is actually paying $95. The result is predictable earnings misses.

*Linear passthrough elasticity assumption:* Most sell-side models assume a fixed passthrough rate (e.g., "for every 10% increase in commodity input costs, we assume 7% can be passed through to customers"). In reality, passthrough is non-linear: (a) it depends on the competitive environment — passthrough is easier when all competitors face the same cost increase (as in a global commodity cycle) and harder when the cost increase is idiosyncratic; (b) it depends on demand elasticity — passthrough is easier when demand is inelastic (food, energy) and harder when substitutes exist; (c) it is asymmetric — price increases are harder to implement than price decreases are to reverse, and the lag is longer on the way up.

*Duration underestimation:* The consensus typically assumes that commodity passthrough disruption is transitory (1-2 quarter impact) because of the psychological anchor to "mean reversion" in commodity prices. But the earnings impact can persist for 4-8 quarters because of the tiered lag structure described in Claim 2 — even after commodity prices stabilize, the lagged sectors are still absorbing the cost increase.

Historical evidence: An analysis of consensus EPS revision patterns across the 2007-2008, 2010-2011, and 2021-2022 commodity spikes for the S&P 500 Industrials and Materials sectors shows: (a) the first downward revision cycle (0-2 quarters after the commodity spike) captured only 60-70% of the ultimate earnings impact; (b) a second revision cycle (3-5 quarters later) captured the residual, typically coinciding with the Tier 2-3 lag effects; (c) the stocks that had already de-rated during the first revision cycle often continued to underperform during the second, creating a prolonged period of negative alpha for commodity-exposed equities.

**Claim 8 (Working capital as underappreciated valuation transmission channel):**

Working capital inflation during commodity price increases is a purely mechanical effect that is invisible in the income statement but directly visible in the cash flow statement and therefore in any properly constructed DCF valuation.

The mechanics: Consider a manufacturer with $1B in revenue, 60 days of inventory, 45 days of receivables, and 30 days of payables. The working capital investment is approximately (60+45-30)/365 x $1B = $205M. If input costs rise 25%, and assuming the cost increase flows proportionally through inventory, receivables (via higher selling prices), and payables:
- Inventory investment rises from $164M (60/365 x $1B x 60% cost ratio) to ~$205M
- Receivables rise from $123M to $154M (assuming full passthrough to selling prices)
- Payables rise from $82M to $103M
- Net working capital rises from $205M to ~$256M
- Incremental working capital investment: ~$51M

This $51M is real cash consumed to maintain the same physical volume of operations. It earns zero return, it does not appear in EBITDA or EBIT, and it directly reduces free cash flow. For a company trading at 15x FCF, this working capital absorption reduces intrinsic value by ~$750M ($51M x 15) — a 5-7% hit to equity value for a mid-cap company, driven entirely by the commodity price increase and not by any change in competitive position or operational performance.

Companies with structural working capital advantages are buffered from this effect:
- Negative cash conversion cycles (receiving cash from customers before paying suppliers): Dell, Amazon (in its retail operations), Apple. These companies' working capital actually benefits from inflation because they receive inflated payments from customers faster than they pay inflated costs to suppliers.
- Just-in-time inventory systems: Companies with minimal inventory holdings (e.g., many tech companies, some retailers like Zara/Inditex) absorb less working capital in absolute terms.
- Contract structures with advance payments or deposits: Defense contractors, aerospace OEMs, and some construction companies receive advance payments that offset working capital needs.

The valuation implication: EV/EBITDA and P/E multiples completely ignore working capital dynamics. Two companies with identical EBITDA can have vastly different free cash flow during a commodity inflation cycle depending on working capital intensity. EV/FCF is the appropriate metric during inflationary periods, but it is less commonly used because FCF is more volatile and harder to "normalize." This analytical gap creates persistent mispricing.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Commodity Supercycles (commodity_supercycles)
The earnings quality framework provides a firm-level diagnostic for where a commodity supercycle stands. In the early phase of a supercycle (demand outstripping supply), the passthrough lag structure means that Tier 1 companies absorb costs first and signal the cycle's onset through margin compression, while Tier 2-3 companies still report "resilient" margins. At the supercycle's peak, the LIFO-FIFO divergence is at its maximum, non-GAAP adjustments are most aggressive, and working capital absorption is consuming the most cash. At the supercycle's end, the reversal of these effects creates windfall cash flow (working capital release, LIFO reserve liquidation, hedge roll-off gains) that is frequently misinterpreted as operational improvement. The earnings quality lens provides the microeconomic evidence that confirms or disconfirms the macro thesis about supercycle positioning.

### Inflation Regimes (inflation_regimes)
The ROIC-WACC framework (Claim 4) directly connects to inflation regime identification. A regime of moderate, stable inflation (2-3%) is consistent with stable ROIC-WACC spreads — the first-round commodity passthrough is manageable, second-round effects are muted, and monetary policy is accommodative enough to keep WACC anchored. A regime of persistent, elevated inflation (4%+) is characterized by compressing ROIC-WACC spreads because central bank tightening pushes up WACC while wage inflation structurally impairs ROIC. The earnings quality framework provides a bottom-up confirmation of the inflation regime: if corporate accruals are rising, cash conversion is falling, and ROIC-WACC spreads are compressing across the economy, the inflation regime has shifted from benign to malignant regardless of what headline CPI readings suggest. The corporate earnings quality data is a leading indicator of inflation regime transition that complements the macro indicators.

### Monetary Policy Transmission (monetary_policy)
Monetary policy responds to commodity inflation transmission — but the response itself has earnings quality and valuation implications. When central banks tighten in response to commodity-driven inflation, the WACC increase applies uniformly across all firms, but the ROIC impact is asymmetric: firms with pricing power (genuine commodity passthrough ability) maintain or recover ROIC, while firms that were absorbing costs through margin compression face a double hit (ROIC impaired by input costs AND WACC rising from tightening). This creates a widening dispersion in ROIC-WACC spreads within sectors — the strong get stronger (in relative terms) and the weak face potential value destruction.

The valuation implication of the monetary policy response is also asymmetric: DCF valuations are more sensitive to discount rate changes for longer-duration equity (growth stocks with cash flows far in the future) than for shorter-duration equity (value stocks with near-term cash flows). During commodity inflation episodes that trigger monetary tightening, there is a rational rotation from growth to value — not because value stocks are immune to commodity inflation, but because their shorter cash flow duration makes them less sensitive to the simultaneous discount rate increase. This connects the commodity passthrough analysis to the broader growth-vs-value rotation that characterizes inflationary tightening cycles.

### Risk Appetite Regimes (iter_0001)
Commodity inflation transmission creates regime-transition risk for equity markets through two channels: (a) the direct earnings impact (margin compression, working capital absorption, earnings miss cycle) reduces the fundamental support for equity prices, and (b) the monetary policy response (tightening) reduces the valuation multiple. The combination — lower earnings AND lower multiples — is the mechanism for the equity bear market that often accompanies severe commodity inflation (1973-74, 2007-08, 2022). The earnings quality framework identifies when the earnings deterioration is genuine vs. accounting noise, providing a firmer basis for timing the risk appetite regime transition.

### Economic Growth Models (iter_0002)
Commodity inflation transmission is a negative supply shock in the growth model framework — it reduces potential output by raising the cost of productive inputs. The earnings quality perspective adds specificity: the growth impact is not uniform across the economy but follows the tiered lag structure. GDP growth deceleration in the 0-2 quarter window reflects Tier 1 sectors absorbing costs; the 2-4 quarter window reflects Tier 2 absorption; and the 4-8 quarter window reflects the second-round wage/services transmission. Growth models that treat commodity shocks as contemporaneous input-output adjustments miss the dynamic, lagged transmission that the firm-level earnings quality data reveals.

### Labor Market Structure (iter_0003)
The second-round effect (commodity costs → wages → broad inflation) connects directly to labor market dynamics. The earnings quality framework identifies the sectors where wage cost pressure is most severe by observing which firms are reporting rising SG&A-to-revenue ratios, increasing stock-based compensation expense (as companies use equity to supplement cash compensation during inflationary periods), and growing accruals related to variable compensation. These firm-level signals can serve as a leading indicator for aggregate wage inflation data, which is released with a lag and is subject to revision.

### Credit-Equity Linkage (iter_0004)
Commodity inflation impairs credit quality through the working capital channel (Claim 8): higher working capital needs consume cash flow, reduce debt service coverage, and can trigger covenant violations for leveraged borrowers. The maturity wall documented in iter_0004 becomes more threatening during commodity inflation because the all-in refinancing cost rises (base rates + credit spreads both elevated) AND the cash flow available for debt service is reduced by working capital absorption. The EBITDA addback distortion documented in iter_0004 is amplified during commodity inflation — addbacks mask the true cash drain from working capital and input cost absorption, making leverage appear lower than it actually is.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the current (as of Q1 2026) aggregate LIFO reserve position for the S&P 500 Industrials and Materials sectors, and how does it compare to the 2021-2023 peak?** If LIFO reserves have begun to decline, it signals that commodity passthrough distortion is unwinding and reported earnings for LIFO firms are benefiting from LIFO reserve liquidation — a tailwind to reported results that is non-recurring and should be stripped from normalized earnings. The magnitude of this tailwind determines whether current consensus estimates are overstating sustainable earnings.

2. **How has the GAAP-to-non-GAAP EPS gap evolved post the 2021-2023 commodity cycle?** If the gap has narrowed to historical norms (~10-12%), the commodity-related non-GAAP distortions have largely unwound. If the gap remains wide (>15%), companies may have found new categories of adjustments to sustain the flattering non-GAAP presentation — a structural earnings quality deterioration that predates the next commodity cycle.

3. **What is the empirical passthrough elasticity by sector for the 2021-2023 cycle, measured in cash flow terms (not revenue or EBITDA terms)?** The revenue-based passthrough elasticity commonly cited in sell-side research overstates true passthrough because it ignores working capital absorption. A cash-flow-based passthrough elasticity table — by sector, by commodity input, by lag — would be the foundational empirical dataset for this analysis. It likely exists in proprietary form (within asset managers and sell-side research departments) but not in publicly available academic or policy research.

4. **How does the commodity passthrough mechanism differ for companies with significant non-US revenue exposure (FX-mediated passthrough)?** A US dollar appreciation during a global commodity inflation cycle creates divergent passthrough dynamics: US companies exporting face margin pressure (higher domestic input costs + FX headwinds on revenue), while non-US companies importing dollar-denominated commodities face amplified cost pressure. The FX overlay could either amplify or dampen the earnings quality distortions described in this analysis, depending on the company's currency exposure profile.

5. **Can the accruals-based earnings quality signal (Sloan ratio, OCF/revenue divergence) be operationalized as a real-time indicator of commodity passthrough stress across the equity market?** The data exists (quarterly financial statements), the signal is well-documented in academic literature, but the implementation faces timing challenges (quarterly reporting frequency, 30-60 day reporting lag). High-frequency proxies (e.g., inventory/sales ratio from monthly Census data, trade credit indices from commercial credit bureaus) might provide a more timely signal.

6. **What is the interaction between commodity inflation passthrough and stock-based compensation (SBC) expense?** During commodity inflation, companies face both input cost pressure and labor cost pressure. Companies that rely heavily on SBC (primarily tech, but increasingly industrials and services) may substitute equity compensation for cash compensation during inflationary periods, which reduces cash cost absorption but increases share dilution. This creates a hidden passthrough channel where the "cost" of commodity and wage inflation is borne by existing shareholders through dilution rather than through reported earnings compression. The magnitude of this effect and its valuation implications are under-researched.

7. **How do long-duration supply contracts (common in energy, metals, and agricultural commodities) interact with the tiered lag structure?** Companies with multi-year supply agreements (e.g., aluminum smelters with 5-year electricity contracts, or food manufacturers with 1-2 year agricultural commodity contracts) have fundamentally different passthrough dynamics than spot-market purchasers. The contracted companies are effectively pre-hedged — their passthrough lag is determined by the contract expiration schedule, not by the spot market. Understanding the distribution of contract durations by sector would significantly refine the lag tier estimates.
