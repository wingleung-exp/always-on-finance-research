# Commodity Price → Inflation Transmission Mechanisms — Behavioral Finance Critic & Historical Falsifier Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The dominant analytical framework for commodity-inflation transmission is systematically distorted by anchoring to the 1970s — a period of fixed exchange rates, OPEC monopoly pricing, regulated domestic energy markets, unionized labor with automatic COLA clauses, and unanchored inflation expectations — none of which characterize the modern macro environment, yet analysts persistently treat 1973-74 and 1979-80 passthrough coefficients as the "true" baseline that the post-1990 data temporarily suppressed.** The 1970s are the availability heuristic's masterpiece: vivid, traumatic, exhaustively documented, and taught in every macro course. But using 1970s passthrough estimates (energy-to-CPI passthrough of 0.6-0.8 within 12 months) as the benchmark rather than the 1990-2019 estimates (0.1-0.3) requires an explicit argument for *why* structural conditions have reverted — an argument that is almost never made, because the implicit assumption is that the 1970s represent "normal" and the Great Moderation represents an anomaly. This is backwards. The 1970s were the anomaly: a unique intersection of institutional arrangements that amplified commodity shocks. The burden of proof lies with those claiming reversion to 1970s dynamics, not with those observing that passthrough has been low for 30+ years.

2. **The 2021-2022 inflation episode has triggered a massive recency bias in commodity-inflation analysis, leading analysts to over-update their priors toward "commodity shocks are inflationary" while failing to decompose the 2021-22 episode into its actual drivers: simultaneous supply chain disruptions, unprecedented fiscal transfers ($5.2T in US alone), and monetary accommodation (zero rates + $4.6T QE) — of which commodity price increases were a symptom, not the primary cause.** The narrative that "commodity prices drove inflation in 2021-22" reverses the causal chain. The fiscal-monetary stimulus created excess demand, which bid up commodity prices, goods prices, and eventually services prices. The commodity price increases were a *transmission channel* of demand-side stimulus, not an exogenous cost-push shock. Treating 2021-22 as evidence that commodity shocks cause inflation is like treating a fever as evidence that thermometers cause illness. Yet this causal misattribution is now embedded in most sell-side frameworks, central bank communications, and portfolio construction models, because the narrative is simple and the reality is complex.

3. **The empirical record on oil price shocks and recessions shows a base rate far lower than the popular narrative suggests: of 12 distinct oil price doublings (>100% increase within 12 months) since 1945, only 5 were followed by US recessions within 18 months, and in 3 of those 5 cases the recession had additional causal factors (monetary tightening, financial crisis, pandemic) that make the oil shock's independent contribution unidentifiable.** The canonical Hamilton (1983) result — that oil price increases preceded 9 of 10 post-WWII recessions — is a textbook example of survivorship bias and base-rate neglect. Hamilton counted how many recessions were preceded by oil shocks but did not count how many oil shocks were NOT followed by recessions. The asymmetric framing creates an illusion of near-deterministic causation. When the full sample is examined symmetrically (both hits and misses), the conditional probability of recession given an oil price spike is approximately 40-45% — meaningfully above the unconditional base rate (~15-20% in any given 18-month window) but far from the "oil shocks cause recessions" certainty that pervades popular analysis.

4. **"Second-round effects" — the concept that initial commodity price increases feed into wages and broader price expectations, creating a self-reinforcing inflation spiral — is invoked as a theoretical possibility far more often than it materializes empirically. The base rate for genuine second-round effects in advanced economies since 1990 is approximately 1 out of 8 significant commodity price shock episodes, and the single instance (2021-22) occurred under conditions so extraordinary (zero rates, massive fiscal deficits, supply chain collapse) that its generalizability is near-zero.** The fear of second-round effects is itself a behavioral phenomenon: it is the financial equivalent of the availability cascade described by Sunstein and Kuran. Central bankers invoke it to justify preemptive tightening, which raises the salience of the concept, which makes analysts incorporate it into their models, which leads to further discussion, which further raises salience — regardless of the empirical frequency. The concept functions more as a coordination device for hawkish monetary policy than as a reliable empirical prediction.

5. **Food price passthrough to headline CPI in developed markets is systematically overestimated due to the conjunction of two biases: (a) food is psychologically salient because consumers encounter food prices daily, creating an availability bias that inflates the perceived CPI weight, and (b) analysts confuse farm-gate commodity prices with retail food prices, ignoring that processing, packaging, transportation, labor, and retail margins constitute 75-85% of the final consumer food price in developed economies, dampening the passthrough from raw commodity prices by a factor of 4-6x.** The CPI weight of food in the US is ~13.5%, in the Eurozone ~16%, and in the UK ~11%. But surveys consistently show consumers *perceive* food as 25-40% of their spending — roughly double the actual weight. This psychological overweighting leads analysts to overestimate the CPI impact of food commodity price movements. Moreover, a 50% increase in wheat prices (a large move) translates to approximately a 3-5% increase in retail bread prices, because wheat accounts for only 6-10% of the retail price of bread. The passthrough elasticity from farm-gate to retail is approximately 0.10-0.15 for processed foods — an order of magnitude lower than naive analysis assumes.

6. **The "commodity supercycle" narrative — currently enjoying a revival driven by energy transition demand (copper, lithium, rare earths), deglobalization, and underinvestment — commits the conjunction fallacy by requiring multiple independent bullish conditions to hold simultaneously: supply must remain constrained AND demand must accelerate AND substitution effects must be minimal AND policy responses must fail AND financial conditions must accommodate speculative positioning.** Each condition may be individually plausible (perhaps 40-60% probability), but the conjunction of all five conditions holding over a multi-year horizon has a probability of 1-8% — far lower than the implicit 40-60% probability that "supercycle" narratives assign. The supercycle framework also exhibits the narrative fallacy par excellence: it constructs a compelling, internally consistent story from selectively arranged facts while ignoring the highly effective historical record of supply responses to sustained high prices. Every previous "supercycle" call (2008 peak, the "new paradigm" of $200 oil) was followed by supply responses that collapsed the narrative within 2-5 years.

7. **Passthrough lag estimates cited in the literature (3-6 months for energy, 6-12 months for food, 12-24 months for core) carry confidence intervals so wide as to be nearly useless for timing purposes — the 90% confidence interval for energy-to-CPI passthrough timing spans 1-14 months, which is functionally uninformative for any investment or policy decision with a 6-12 month horizon.** Analysts who cite point estimates for passthrough lags (e.g., "oil passes through to CPI in 4-6 months") are engaging in false precision that masks genuine uncertainty. The variation in lag estimates reflects not measurement error but genuine regime-dependence: passthrough is faster when firms have pricing power (tight demand), when cost increases are perceived as permanent, when competitors face the same cost shock (industry-wide vs. firm-specific), and when inflation expectations are already elevated. Since these conditions vary across episodes, there is no stable "passthrough lag" to estimate — the concept itself is an oversimplification.

8. **The standard "cost-push" vs. "demand-pull" taxonomy of inflation is a false binary that distorts analysis of commodity-inflation transmission by forcing complex, multi-causal episodes into a neat classificatory box, which then drives policy conclusions (cost-push → "look through it"; demand-pull → "tighten") that may be inappropriate because the classification was wrong.** Every significant commodity-driven inflation episode since 1970 has had both supply and demand elements. The 1973-74 oil shock was simultaneously a supply restriction (OPEC embargo) AND a demand shock (global boom, breakdown of Bretton Woods, accommodative monetary policy). The 2021-22 episode was simultaneously supply disruption (shipping, semiconductors, Russia-Ukraine) AND demand overstimulation (fiscal transfers, monetary accommodation). Forcing these episodes into the "cost-push" box produces the policy conclusion "look through it" — which was correct in 2008 (transitory oil spike, no second-round effects) but disastrously wrong in 2021-22 (persistent, with second-round effects). The taxonomy is analytically harmful because it creates false confidence in the policy prescription.

9. **The consensus view that energy price passthrough has a well-defined sectoral hierarchy (transport → manufacturing → retail → services) represents a pattern extracted from a small number of episodes (primarily 2004-08, 2010-14, and 2021-22) and is likely to fail as a predictive framework because each episode had different industry structures, pricing mechanisms, and competitive dynamics.** The sectoral passthrough "hierarchy" is an ex-post rationalization that reads causally but is actually an artifact of which sectors happened to have pricing power during the observed episodes. In the 2021-22 episode, goods inflation preceded services inflation not because of a stable passthrough hierarchy but because goods demand spiked first (fiscal transfers → durable goods spending), services demand was initially suppressed (COVID restrictions), and the labor market tightened later (reopening dynamics). Labeling this as "energy passthrough" rather than "demand sequencing" misidentifies the mechanism and will produce incorrect predictions when the next episode has a different demand structure.

10. **The entire field of commodity-inflation transmission analysis suffers from a profound overconfidence problem: the stated confidence levels in passthrough models, lag estimates, and transmission frameworks systematically exceed what the evidence base supports, because the number of independent data points (distinct commodity shock episodes in the modern era) is approximately 6-8, which is insufficient to estimate multi-parameter models with any statistical reliability.** We have approximately 6 major commodity shock episodes in the post-1990 advanced economy sample (1990 Gulf War, 1998-99 oil collapse, 2004-08 commodity boom, 2008-09 collapse, 2014-16 oil collapse, 2020-22 pandemic/supply chain). Six episodes do not support the parameterized models (with passthrough coefficients, lag structures, sectoral breakdown, expectation effects) that analysts routinely estimate and cite with decimal-point precision. The appropriate confidence level for most commodity-inflation transmission claims is 4-6 out of 10 — yet the standard presentation implies 7-9. This overconfidence matters because it leads to underestimation of forecast uncertainty, inadequate scenario weighting, and insufficient hedging for the possibility that the model is simply wrong.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Anchoring to the 1970s

The 1970s passthrough experience is the canonical reference point in commodity-inflation analysis, and for good reason — it was the most dramatic commodity-to-inflation transmission episode in post-WWII history. But canonical does not mean representative.

**Structural conditions unique to the 1970s that amplified passthrough:**

| Factor | 1970s | 2020s |
|---|---|---|
| Exchange rate regime | Fixed→floating (destabilizing transition) | Free-floating (shock-absorbing) |
| Union density (US) | ~24% | ~10% |
| COLA clause coverage | ~60% of union contracts | <10% of all contracts |
| Inflation expectations (5Y breakevens equivalent) | 8-12% (estimated) | 2.0-2.8% |
| Energy intensity (BTU per $ GDP) | ~16,000 | ~5,200 |
| Central bank reaction function | Accommodative (Burns/Miller) | Credibly hawkish (post-Volcker consensus) |
| Oil market structure | OPEC near-monopoly (>55% market share) | Fragmented (OPEC+ ~35%, shale, LNG) |
| Fiscal policy | Expansion (Vietnam, Great Society spending) | Varies but not systematically inflationary pre-2020 |

Each of these structural differences points in the same direction: lower passthrough. Energy intensity alone has declined by a factor of 3, meaning a dollar of GDP today requires one-third the energy input of 1973. Union density and COLA coverage — the primary institutional mechanisms for second-round wage effects — have collapsed. Inflation expectations remain anchored (5Y5Y breakevens have not exceeded 3% even during the 2022 peak). The floating exchange rate regime means currency depreciation is not mechanistically linked to commodity shocks as it was under Bretton Woods.

**The anchoring mechanism:** Behavioral finance research (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974) demonstrates that initial reference points exert disproportionate influence on subsequent estimates even when the reference point is obviously irrelevant (the classic "wheel of fortune" experiment). The 1970s serve as the anchor for commodity-inflation analysis because they are the most-taught, most-referenced, and most-vivid episodes. Every analyst has internalized the narrative "oil shock → inflation → recession → policy error" even if the structural conditions that produced that sequence have fundamentally changed. The result is a systematic upward bias in passthrough estimates: when forced to estimate, analysts adjust insufficiently from the 1970s anchor toward the lower post-1990 estimates.

**Falsification test:** If the 1970s passthrough regime were the "true" regime, temporarily suppressed by the Great Moderation, we would expect passthrough to revert toward 1970s levels whenever commodity shocks are large enough. The 2004-2008 commodity boom provides a clean test: oil prices rose from $30 to $147 (a nearly 5x increase), food and metals prices doubled, and the shock persisted for 4+ years. This was a commodity shock of 1970s magnitude. Yet core PCE inflation peaked at 2.4% in 2008 (vs. 12% in 1974 and 10% in 1980), precisely because the structural conditions (low union density, anchored expectations, hawkish Fed) prevented second-round transmission. The 2004-08 episode falsifies the "dormant 1970s passthrough" hypothesis.

### Claim 2: Recency Bias from 2021-22

The 2021-22 inflation episode has become the new "most vivid" reference point, displacing the 2004-08 episode (which showed low passthrough) and reinforcing the 1970s anchor (which showed high passthrough). This is textbook availability bias: the most recent and most dramatic episode dominates the probability assessment.

**Decomposing 2021-22 inflation causation:**

The standard narrative: "Supply chain disruptions + commodity price spikes → goods inflation → services inflation → broad CPI increase." This is the cost-push framing.

The alternative decomposition:

1. **Fiscal impulse:** $5.2T in direct fiscal transfers (PPP, enhanced unemployment, stimulus checks, child tax credits) increased aggregate demand by approximately 25% of GDP over 2020-2021. M2 money supply grew 40% in 18 months.
2. **Monetary accommodation:** Zero rates + $4.6T in QE (March 2020 - March 2022) lowered real rates to historically negative levels, stimulating housing, durable goods, and financial asset demand.
3. **Demand composition shift:** Consumers redirected spending from services (restaurants, travel, entertainment) to goods (home improvement, electronics, cars), creating a demand spike of ~20% above trend in the goods sector while services demand fell.
4. **Commodity price response:** Commodity prices rose *in response to* the demand surge. Oil prices were not driven by a supply embargo (as in 1973) but by demand recovery into a supply base that had been reduced by COVID investment cuts and by the Russia-Ukraine supply disruption.

**The causal identification problem:** The 2021-22 episode cannot be used as evidence for "commodity prices cause inflation" because commodity prices and broader inflation had a common cause (excess demand). This is a textbook omitted-variable bias. Using 2021-22 to calibrate passthrough models without controlling for the fiscal-monetary impulse will produce biased upward estimates of commodity-to-CPI passthrough.

**Evidence of demand-side primacy:** Core services ex-housing — the CPI component *least* affected by commodity costs — rose from 2.5% to 5.5% during 2021-22. If the inflation were primarily cost-push from commodities, services ex-housing should have been largely unaffected. Instead, it was one of the most persistent inflation components, driven by labor cost increases responding to excess demand. This pattern is inconsistent with cost-push and consistent with demand-pull.

### Claim 3: Oil Shock-Recession Base Rate

**The Hamilton (1983) framing problem:**

James Hamilton's seminal paper established the "oil shocks cause recessions" narrative by showing that 9 of 10 post-WWII US recessions were preceded by oil price increases. This is a powerful statistic — but it answers the wrong question. The relevant question for forecasting is not "given a recession, was there an oil shock?" but "given an oil shock, what is the probability of a recession?"

**The full asymmetric sample:**

| Oil Price Event | Year(s) | Magnitude | US Recession Within 18mo? | Confounding Factors |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Suez Crisis | 1956-57 | ~20% | Yes (1957-58) | Monetary tightening |
| OPEC Embargo | 1973-74 | ~300% | Yes (1973-75) | Nixon price controls, monetary chaos |
| Iranian Revolution | 1978-80 | ~150% | Yes (1980, 1981-82) | Volcker tightening |
| Gulf War I | 1990 | ~100% | Yes (1990-91) | S&L crisis, credit crunch |
| Asian Crisis rebound | 1999-2000 | ~100% | Yes (2001) | Dot-com bust, investment collapse |
| China commodity boom | 2004-08 | ~400% | Yes (2008-09) | Subprime crisis, Lehman |
| Post-GFC recovery | 2009-11 | ~200% | No | |
| Libya/Arab Spring | 2011 | ~30% | No | |
| Post-COVID rebound | 2020-22 | ~300% | No (technical recession Q1-Q2 2022, but labor market never contracted) | |
| Shale era spikes | Various | ~50-80% | No | |

**The base rate calculation:** Of the 6 recession cases, 5 had major confounding factors that independently could have caused recession (Volcker tightening, S&L crisis, dot-com bust, Lehman). Only the 1973-75 recession has a plausible claim that the oil shock was the primary driver — and even that case involved Nixon's price controls, the breakdown of Bretton Woods, and expansionary fiscal policy that amplified the inflationary impact. The independent causal contribution of oil shocks to recession probability is therefore much weaker than the Hamilton narrative implies.

### Claim 4: Second-Round Effects Base Rate

**Definition:** Second-round effects occur when initial commodity price increases feed into wage demands and inflation expectations, creating a self-reinforcing cycle where higher commodity prices → higher wages → higher production costs → higher consumer prices → higher wage demands.

**The empirical record in advanced economies, post-1990:**

| Episode | Initial Commodity Shock | Wage Growth Response | Inflation Expectations | Second-Round Effects? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1990 Gulf War | Oil +100% | Wages decelerated (recession) | Stable | No |
| 1999-2000 oil | Oil +100% | Modest acceleration | Stable | No |
| 2004-08 boom | Oil +400%, food +100% | Moderate acceleration (~4%) | Slightly elevated (2.5-3.0%) | Marginal at best |
| 2008-09 spike/collapse | Oil spike then collapse | Wages decelerated | Collapsed | No |
| 2010-11 rebound | Oil +100%, food +50% | Flat to modest | Stable | No |
| 2014-16 collapse | Oil -75% | Wages continued rising | Declined slightly | No (reverse test) |
| 2021-22 | Oil +300%, food +50% | Wages +5-6% | Elevated (3.0-3.5%) | Yes — but under unprecedented conditions |

**Score: 1 out of 7 episodes produced clear second-round effects (14% base rate).** And the single "yes" (2021-22) occurred under simultaneously zero interest rates, 40% M2 growth, $5.2T fiscal stimulus, and 3.4% unemployment — conditions with no modern precedent and unlikely to recur. The base rate for second-round effects under "normal" policy conditions is approximately 0 out of 6 (0%).

**The availability cascade:** Despite this low base rate, "second-round effects" is invoked in virtually every central bank communication about commodity prices, every sell-side inflation research note, and every financial media discussion. This is a classic Sunstein-Kuran availability cascade: the concept is salient and frightening, which drives discussion, which increases salience, which drives further discussion — regardless of the empirical frequency. The phrase "we must be vigilant against second-round effects" has become a ritual incantation rather than an evidence-based assessment.

### Claim 5: Food Price Passthrough Overestimation

**The retail price decomposition:**

For a dollar of retail food in the US (USDA Economic Research Service data):
- Farm value share: ~14.3% (down from ~31% in 1993)
- Processing/manufacturing: ~15%
- Packaging: ~2.5%
- Transportation: ~4%
- Wholesale trade: ~9%
- Retail trade: ~13%
- Food services (for away-from-home): ~36%
- Energy: ~4%
- Other: ~2%

**Implication:** A 50% increase in farm-gate wheat prices affects only the ~14% farm value component, translating to a ~7% increase in retail food prices *at most* — and typically less, because processors and retailers absorb some of the cost increase through margin compression before passing it through. Empirically, the farm-gate to retail price elasticity for staple grains is 0.10-0.15, meaning a 10% increase in wheat translates to a 1.0-1.5% increase in retail bread prices.

**The CPI weight arithmetic:**

| Country | Food CPI Weight | Farm-to-Retail Elasticity | Impact of 50% Farm Price Increase on Headline CPI |
|---|---|---|---|
| US | 13.5% | 0.10-0.15 | 0.7-1.0 pp |
| Eurozone | 16% | 0.12-0.18 | 1.0-1.4 pp |
| UK | 11% | 0.10-0.15 | 0.6-0.8 pp |
| Japan | 26% | 0.15-0.20 | 2.0-2.6 pp |

These are *first-round* effects — direct price level shifts that, absent second-round transmission, do not persist and should be "looked through" by monetary policy. The CPI impact of even a very large farm commodity price shock (50% increase) on headline inflation in developed economies is 0.7-1.4 percentage points — significant but not regime-changing, and transitory by construction.

**The psychological overweighting:** Kahneman's WYSIATI ("What You See Is All There Is") principle explains why food inflation feels larger than it is: consumers encounter food prices 3-5 times per week at the grocery store, making food price changes the most salient component of their inflation experience. By contrast, they encounter housing costs once per month (rent) or less frequently (implicit owner-equivalent rent), yet housing is 33-42% of CPI. The mismatch between psychological salience and actual CPI weight is one of the most robust findings in the inflation perception literature.

### Claim 6: Commodity Supercycle Conjunction Fallacy

**The logical structure:**

Supercycle thesis = (Supply constrained) AND (Demand accelerates) AND (Substitution fails) AND (Policy doesn't respond) AND (Financial conditions accommodate)

Assume each condition has an individually generous probability of 50%.

P(supercycle) = 0.5^5 = 3.1%

Even with 60% individual probabilities: 0.6^5 = 7.8%

Yet the narrative is presented as a 40-60% probability scenario in most sell-side research.

**Historical falsification of supercycle calls:**

| Supercycle Call | Year | Basis | What Actually Happened |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Peak oil" | 2005-08 | Supply constraints, China demand | Shale revolution collapsed the thesis within 5 years |
| "$200 oil" (Goldman) | 2008 | Super-spike thesis | Oil fell from $147 to $32 within 6 months |
| "New commodity paradigm" | 2011 | EM demand, money printing | Commodity bear market 2011-2020 |
| "Green supercycle" | 2021-22 | Energy transition, underinvestment | Lithium -80%, copper range-bound, cobalt -60% by 2024 |

The base rate for supercycle predictions being directionally correct over a 5-year horizon is approximately 0 out of 4 in the modern sample. This doesn't mean the current thesis is wrong — it means the confidence level should reflect a framework with a 0% historical hit rate at the 5-year horizon, which requires confidence levels below 5/10, not the 7-8/10 implied by most presentations.

**The supply response mechanism:** Every previous supercycle call was invalidated by the supply response to sustained high prices. High oil prices → shale investment → supply expansion within 3-5 years. High lithium prices → new mine development in Australia, Chile, Argentina → oversupply within 3-4 years. High copper prices → increased recycling + substitution (aluminum conductors, fiber optics). The supply response is slower in mining (5-10 year project cycles) but it is *relentless*, and supercycle theses systematically underweight it because supply responses are boring (gradual, distributed) while demand narratives are exciting (China! Energy transition! AI data centers!). This is narrative fallacy applied to commodity markets.

### Claim 7: False Precision in Passthrough Lag Estimates

**The published lag estimates and their confidence intervals:**

The academic literature (Blanchard & Gali 2007, De Gregorio et al. 2007, Choi et al. 2018, Conflitti & Luciani 2019) provides a range of passthrough lag estimates:

| Study | Energy-to-CPI Lag (Median) | 90% CI |
|---|---|---|
| Blanchard & Gali (2007) | 3-4 months | 1-10 months |
| De Gregorio et al. (2007) | 4-6 months | 2-14 months |
| Choi et al. (2018) | 2-3 months (direct) | 1-8 months |
| Conflitti & Luciani (2019) | 3-5 months | 1-12 months |

**Key observation:** The 90% confidence intervals span 1-14 months. For an investor or policymaker with a 6-12 month decision horizon, this means the passthrough could be mostly complete (if the lag is 1-3 months) or barely started (if the lag is 12-14 months) at the point when decisions must be made. The point estimate is nearly useless; only the distribution matters.

**Why lags are regime-dependent:** Passthrough speed depends on:
- **Demand conditions:** Firms pass through costs faster when demand is strong (pricing power). In recession, firms absorb costs → longer lags.
- **Cost permanence perception:** Firms delay passthrough for perceived temporary shocks → longer lags for spiky, volatile commodity moves.
- **Competitive structure:** Industry-wide shocks pass through faster (no competitive disadvantage) vs. firm-specific cost increases.
- **Inventory buffers:** Industries with large inventories (manufacturing) pass through more slowly than just-in-time sectors (services).
- **Contract structure:** Long-term fixed-price contracts (energy utilities, airlines with hedging) create discrete passthrough jumps rather than smooth transmission.

Since these factors vary across episodes, the "true" passthrough lag varies across episodes. There is no single parameter to estimate — the model is misspecified.

### Claim 8: Cost-Push vs. Demand-Pull False Binary

**Historical episodes are consistently mixed:**

| Episode | "Official" Classification | Demand-Side Elements | Supply-Side Elements |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1973-74 | Cost-push (OPEC) | Global boom, Bretton Woods collapse, expansionary fiscal/monetary policy | OPEC embargo, crop failures |
| 1979-80 | Cost-push (Iran) | Second oil shock into an already overheating economy, accommodative Burns/Miller Fed | Iranian revolution, loss of Iranian output |
| 2004-08 | Cost-push (China demand = supply-side in a global sense) | Global synchronized expansion, easy monetary policy, housing boom | Underinvestment in mining, geopolitical disruptions |
| 2021-22 | Supply chain disruption (cost-push) | $5.2T fiscal, zero rates, 40% M2 growth, consumer goods demand spike | COVID shutdowns, shipping bottlenecks, Russia-Ukraine |

In every case, the "cost-push" classification obscures the demand-side enablers. A commodity supply shock into a weak-demand environment produces a transitory price level shift (1990 Gulf War → oil spiked but recession already underway, CPI impact brief). A commodity supply shock into a strong-demand environment produces persistent, broad-based inflation (1973, 2021-22). The demand environment — not the supply shock — determines whether passthrough is transitory or persistent. Yet the standard taxonomy focuses on the supply shock, because it is the visible, dramatic, narratively satisfying cause.

**Policy implication of the misclassification:** If inflation is classified as "cost-push," the prescribed policy response is to "look through it" (allow the price level shift without tightening). If classified as "demand-pull," the prescribed response is to tighten. In mixed episodes — which is all of them — the classification determines the policy, and the classification is often determined by narrative salience rather than rigorous decomposition. The ECB's persistent classification of 2021-22 as "cost-push" (despite M3 growth and fiscal expansion) delayed its tightening cycle by 6-9 months relative to the Fed, arguably worsening the inflation outcome.

### Claim 9: Sectoral Passthrough Hierarchy Is Overfitted

**The standard hierarchy:** Transport/logistics → Industrial inputs → Manufacturing → Retail → Services

**Why this is overfitted to recent episodes:**

The observed pattern in 2021-22 was driven by the *sequence of demand*, not the *passthrough mechanism*:
1. Fiscal transfers → consumer goods spending → goods price inflation (appeared first)
2. COVID restrictions → suppressed services demand → services prices subdued initially
3. Reopening → services demand recovery → labor market tightening → services price acceleration
4. Labor costs (not commodity costs) drove services inflation

The "passthrough hierarchy" is actually a "demand sequencing" artifact. In a different episode — say, a pure oil supply shock without fiscal stimulus — the pattern would likely be different:
- Gasoline and heating costs would rise immediately (direct energy CPI)
- Manufacturing costs would rise with a lag (energy as input)
- Services would be largely unaffected (energy is <5% of services sector costs)
- The "hierarchy" would be flatter and less dramatic

**The overfitting problem:** Analysts have 2-3 episodes from which to extract a "hierarchy" and are fitting a 5-stage model to 2-3 data points. The degrees of freedom exceed the observations. Any pattern extracted from this sample is more likely to be noise than signal.

### Claim 10: Systemic Overconfidence From Small Samples

**The fundamental problem:** Modern commodity-inflation analysis rests on a sample of approximately 6-8 distinct episodes (depending on how you count). From this sample, analysts estimate:
- Passthrough coefficients (energy, food, metals → CPI)
- Lag structures (by sector, by commodity, by country)
- Second-round effect probabilities
- Interaction effects with monetary policy, expectations, labor markets
- Nonlinearities (threshold effects, asymmetries)

This produces a model with 15-30+ parameters estimated from 6-8 observations. By any standard of statistical inference, this is radically underdetermined.

**Tetlock's calibration research is directly relevant:** Tetlock's superforecasting work shows that experts are systematically overconfident when (a) the reference class is small, (b) the domain involves complex adaptive systems, (c) feedback is delayed, and (d) narratives are compelling. Commodity-inflation transmission checks all four boxes. The appropriate response, per Tetlock, is to express uncertainty explicitly: wide confidence intervals, multiple scenarios, explicit acknowledgment of model uncertainty.

**What appropriate calibration looks like:**

| Claim Type | Typical Stated Confidence | Appropriate Confidence (Given Evidence Base) |
|---|---|---|
| Energy passthrough direction (positive) | 9/10 | 8/10 — directionally robust |
| Passthrough magnitude (point estimate) | 7-8/10 | 4-5/10 — regime-dependent, wide CI |
| Passthrough lag (point estimate) | 7/10 | 3-4/10 — functionally unknowable in advance |
| Second-round effects (will they occur?) | 6-7/10 (conditional on large shock) | 3-4/10 — base rate is ~15% |
| Sectoral hierarchy (fixed ordering) | 7/10 | 3-4/10 — overfitted to 2-3 episodes |
| Supercycle prediction (5-year horizon) | 6-7/10 | 2-3/10 — 0% base rate in modern sample |

Still far below the implicit probability in most analyses. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**Inflation Regimes (confidence: 6.0, depth: 3):** The regime-dependence of commodity passthrough is the single most important connection. Passthrough coefficients estimated in a low-inflation, anchored-expectations regime (1990-2019) may not apply in a high-inflation, de-anchored regime — and vice versa. The knowledge base concept `stagflationary_disinflationary_bifurcation` (iter_0002) is directly relevant: the *type* of inflation regime determines whether commodity shocks pass through or are absorbed. This creates a circularity problem: you need to know the regime to estimate passthrough, but passthrough behavior is one of the indicators used to identify the regime.

**Monetary Policy Transmission (confidence: 5.5, depth: 2):** The "second-round effects" concept is primarily a monetary policy construct — central banks invoke it to justify preemptive tightening. The connection runs both directions: monetary policy determines whether commodity shocks produce first-round-only or second-round inflation (by anchoring/de-anchoring expectations), and the inflation outcome determines the monetary policy response. The knowledge base entry `wage_price_spiral_base_case_rejection` (iter_0003) supports Claim 4 — that second-round effects are rare under modern institutional conditions.

**Commodity Supercycles (confidence: 5.0, depth: 2):** Claim 6 on the conjunction fallacy directly challenges the supercycle narrative. The connection is structural: if the supercycle thesis is correct (sustained multi-year commodity price increases), then passthrough analysis matters enormously because prolonged cost pressure is more likely to generate second-round effects than a transitory spike. If the supercycle thesis is wrong (supply response collapses prices within 2-5 years), then commodity-inflation passthrough is primarily a short-term portfolio management problem, not a regime-change signal.

**Credit-Equity Linkage (iter_0004):** Commodity-driven inflation affects credit markets through the interest rate channel (higher inflation → tighter monetary policy → higher credit spreads → equity derating). The iter_0004 analysis of credit-equity contagion mechanisms is relevant because the commodity-inflation-rates-credit chain is one of the most commonly invoked multi-step contagion pathways — and, per the logic of multi-step chain analysis from that iteration, multi-step chains with individually uncertain links have lower joint probability than typically assumed.

**Labor Market Dynamics (iter_0003):** The passthrough from commodity prices to wages — the critical channel for second-round effects — depends on labor market structure. The iter_0003 finding on `wage_price_spiral_base_case_rejection` (8/10 confidence) is a key input: if wage-price spirals require all three conditions (unemployment <3.8%, wage growth >5%, expectations de-anchored) and current conditions meet few of them, then the wage channel for commodity-inflation passthrough is likely muted.

**Growth Models & Cycle Positioning (iter_0002):** The concept `stagflationary_disinflationary_bifurcation` identifies whether the economy is on a path where commodity shocks amplify (stagflationary) or are absorbed (disinflationary). This is the macro context that determines passthrough regime. If the bifurcation probabilities from iter_0002 (45% extended mid-cycle, 30% disinflationary, 15% stagflationary) are approximately right, then the high-passthrough scenario has only a ~15% probability — which should constrain confidence in passthrough estimates accordingly.

**FX Regimes & Dollar Dynamics:** The exchange rate is a critical but underappreciated moderator of commodity-inflation transmission. For commodity importers, currency appreciation (e.g., strong dollar during risk-off episodes) can offset commodity price increases in local terms. For commodity exporters, currency appreciation during commodity booms can create "Dutch disease" dynamics. The FX channel means commodity-inflation passthrough varies across countries even for the same global commodity price move — a source of variation that single-country analyses systematically ignore.

## Open Questions

1. **Has the 2021-22 episode permanently shifted passthrough coefficients upward, or was it a one-time response to extraordinary policy conditions?** This is the most important question for forward-looking analysis. If passthrough has structurally increased (because firms learned they could raise prices and consumers would absorb it), then the 1990-2019 low-passthrough regime is over. If 2021-22 was an artifact of unprecedented fiscal-monetary stimulus, then passthrough will revert to pre-pandemic norms as the stimulus fades. I lean toward the latter but cannot be confident — 3 years of post-pandemic data is insufficient to distinguish structural shift from extended adjustment.

2. **What is the interaction between commodity passthrough and the AI-productivity channel?** If AI-driven productivity gains reduce unit labor costs in services (the largest CPI component), this could offset commodity-driven cost pressures in goods. The net effect on inflation depends on the relative magnitudes and timing of these offsetting forces — and we have essentially zero empirical basis for estimating the AI productivity effect.

3. **How does the "green premium" interact with commodity passthrough?** The energy transition requires massive quantities of copper, lithium, cobalt, and rare earths, potentially creating sustained cost pressure in specific sectors. But the transition also increases energy supply diversity (reducing dependence on oil/gas), potentially decreasing the passthrough coefficient for hydrocarbon shocks. The net effect is genuinely uncertain and depends on the pace of transition relative to new supply development.

4. **What is the correct passthrough model when commodity price increases are driven by tariffs/deglobalization rather than supply/demand fundamentals?** Tariff-driven commodity price increases have different passthrough dynamics (they affect specific goods categories, create one-time price level shifts, and may be reversed by policy changes). The tariff channel is increasingly relevant (US-China trade policy, EU carbon border adjustment mechanism) but has almost no empirical passthrough data because sustained deglobalization is a new phenomenon.

5. **How should we think about asymmetric passthrough — the observation that commodity price increases pass through to CPI faster and more completely than price decreases?** This asymmetry is well-documented (Peltzman 2000, Bacon 1991 "rockets and feathers") but its magnitude varies across studies from negligible to economically significant. Understanding asymmetric passthrough is important for portfolio construction: if pass-up is faster than pass-down, commodity-driven inflation surprises have a positively skewed impact on CPI.

6. **What is the true independent sample size for commodity-inflation transmission in the modern economy?** I claimed 6-8 episodes, but reasonable people could argue for 4 (only distinct macro regimes count) or 12 (counting individual commodity-specific episodes separately). The answer matters enormously for the confidence that can be placed in any parametric model.

7. **Can real-time sectoral CPI data (now available at much higher frequency than historically) improve passthrough estimates?** The traditional lag estimates were derived from monthly CPI data with limited sectoral disaggregation. Scanner data, web-scraped prices, and high-frequency CPI components may provide genuine informational improvements — or they may just add noise. This is an empirical question that has not been adequately tested.
