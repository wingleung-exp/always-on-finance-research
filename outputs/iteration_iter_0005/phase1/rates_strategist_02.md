# Commodity Price → Inflation Transmission Mechanisms — Central Bank Policy Transmission Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Central banks' "look-through" doctrine for commodity price shocks is state-contingent, not a fixed rule — and the conditions under which they abandon it are the single most important variable for rate markets.** The Fed, ECB, and BOE all officially treat first-round commodity price effects as transitory and focus on second-round effects (wages, inflation expectations). But the 2021-2023 episode proved that when commodity shocks are broad-based (energy + food + metals simultaneously), sufficiently large (>2 standard deviation moves), and coincide with already-above-target inflation, central banks will abandon the look-through and tighten aggressively against headline inflation. The reaction function shifts from "look-through unless second-round effects emerge" to "tighten until headline declines, regardless of demand conditions."

2. **The commodity-to-CPI passthrough lag structure is asymmetric across sectors and creates a policy timing trap: energy passes through in 1-3 months, food in 3-6 months, and industrial metals in 6-18 months via intermediate goods.** This staggered passthrough means that when a broad commodity shock hits, headline CPI accelerates sequentially across categories, creating the *appearance* of persistent broadening inflation even when the original shock is fading. Central banks, observing sequential category-level acceleration, interpret this as second-round effects and tighten more aggressively than the underlying shock warrants. This is the "passthrough illusion" — the lag structure creates a feedback loop between the data and the policy response.

3. **The exchange rate channel is the critical amplifier or dampener of commodity-to-inflation transmission, and it creates a fundamental asymmetry between the Fed and all other central banks.** Because commodities are priced in USD, a hawkish Fed response to commodity inflation strengthens the dollar, which partially offsets the commodity price impact on US CPI but amplifies it for every other economy. This produces a "commodity-FX doom loop" for non-US central banks: commodity prices rise → imported inflation accelerates → central bank tightens → but if the Fed tightens faster, the local currency depreciates → imported commodity inflation worsens → more tightening required. The ECB's 2022 experience is the canonical example: energy prices were already falling in EUR terms by late 2022, but EUR depreciation to parity with USD extended the inflationary impulse by 3-6 months.

4. **The credit channel of monetary policy is impaired when tightening is directed against commodity-driven inflation rather than demand-driven inflation, because rate hikes cannot reduce commodity supply constraints.** When central banks raise rates to combat demand-pull inflation, the transmission mechanism works as intended: higher rates → tighter credit → reduced demand → lower inflation. When the inflation source is a commodity supply shock, rate hikes still tighten credit and reduce demand, but this does not address the supply constraint. The result is a higher sacrifice ratio — more output lost per unit of inflation reduction. Empirically, the IMF estimates the sacrifice ratio for supply-driven inflation episodes is 1.5-2.5x higher than for demand-driven episodes. This impairment is critical because it means financial conditions must tighten *more* than in a demand-driven episode to achieve the same inflation reduction, creating larger dislocations in credit and equity markets.

5. **Second-round effects from commodity shocks are institutional, not mechanical — they depend on wage-setting structures, contract indexation, and inflation expectations anchoring, all of which vary dramatically across economies.** The key discriminant for whether a commodity price shock produces persistent inflation is whether it feeds into wage negotiations. In economies with high union density, COLA clauses, or backward-looking wage indexation (parts of the eurozone, much of EM), second-round effects are near-automatic. In economies with decentralized wage bargaining and low union density (US, UK post-Thatcher), second-round effects require inflation expectations to de-anchor first — a much higher bar. The 2021-2023 episode confirmed this: US wage growth tracked labor market tightness (Phillips Curve), not commodity prices, while eurozone negotiated wages showed a lagged but direct response to energy price levels.

6. **The financial conditions index (FCI) is a better real-time gauge of how much policy work is being done against commodity inflation than the policy rate itself, because asset price channels can amplify or offset rate changes by 100-200bp equivalent.** In 2022, the Fed raised rates 425bp, but financial conditions tightened by the equivalent of ~550-600bp because equity declines, credit spread widening, and dollar appreciation all reinforced the rate channel. Conversely, in late 2023, the Fed held rates steady but financial conditions eased by ~150bp equivalent as equities rallied, credit spreads tightened, and the dollar weakened — partially unwinding the prior tightening despite no rate cuts. For commodity-driven inflation specifically, the FCI is more informative than the policy rate because the exchange rate component directly affects import prices and hence the commodity passthrough.

7. **Central bank credibility is the most underpriced variable in the commodity-inflation transmission mechanism — when credibility is high, commodity shocks produce one-off price level shifts; when credibility is low, they produce persistent inflation regime changes.** The Fed's 2021 "transitory" miscall temporarily damaged credibility, which is why the subsequent tightening cycle was more aggressive than any Taylor Rule variant would have prescribed. The overreaction was a credibility restoration exercise. Now that 5Y5Y breakevens have re-anchored at ~2.3% (consistent with the KB's NKPC analysis), the Fed has more room to look through future commodity shocks — but only if they are isolated (single-commodity) and moderate in size. A repeat of the 2021-2022 broad-based commodity surge would immediately re-trigger the "tighten against headline" reaction function because the credibility buffer is thinner than pre-2021.

8. **The PBOC and BOJ operate with fundamentally different commodity-inflation transmission mechanisms than Western central banks, creating structural divergences that drive capital flows and FX.** The PBOC manages commodity passthrough primarily through administered pricing (fuel subsidies, strategic reserve releases, refinery guidance) rather than interest rates, meaning Chinese inflation is partially decoupled from global commodity prices. The BOJ's yield curve control regime (now loosened but still anchored by forward guidance) means that commodity-driven inflation in Japan actually *eases* real financial conditions (rising inflation with anchored nominal rates = lower real rates), which is the opposite of the intended policy response. These structural differences mean that a global commodity shock produces policy divergence rather than coordination, with the Fed/ECB tightening and the BOJ/PBOC maintaining accommodation — a divergence that strengthens the dollar, amplifies the FX channel, and makes the passthrough asymmetry (Claim 3) more extreme.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: State-Contingent Look-Through Doctrine

The "look-through" framework originates from the Bernanke-era Fed, which explicitly distinguished between headline and core PCE and targeted the latter. The theoretical justification (Blanchard-Galí, 2007) is that in a credible inflation-targeting regime, first-round commodity price effects are self-correcting: they raise the price level but not the inflation rate, because inflation expectations remain anchored and wage-setters do not treat temporary price spikes as permanent.

The 2021-2023 episode stress-tested this doctrine and found its limits:
- **June 2021:** Fed characterizes inflation as "transitory," consistent with look-through. Core PCE at 3.4%.
- **November 2021:** Fed retires "transitory" language. Headline CPI at 6.8%, with broad-based commodity contributions (energy, food, used cars/metals).
- **March 2022:** Fed begins hiking, explicitly citing headline inflation and "broadening" price pressures.
- **By December 2022:** Fed has hiked 425bp despite commodity prices (WTI, wheat, copper) already declining from mid-2022 peaks.

The key diagnostic: the Fed abandoned look-through when three conditions were met simultaneously: (a) the shock was multi-commodity (not just oil), (b) core inflation was already above target before the shock, and (c) inflation expectations began drifting (Michigan 1Y at 5.4%, 5Y at 3.1%). None of these conditions individually would have triggered abandonment — it was the conjunction that shifted the reaction function.

For the ECB, the threshold was lower: President Lagarde explicitly cited "second-round risks" from energy prices in February 2022, despite the ECB's own staff projections showing inflation returning to target by 2024. The ECB was more hawkish than its own models suggested because the political economy of energy inflation in Europe (yellow vest precedent, fuel poverty) made the look-through untenable.

### Claim 2: Staggered Passthrough and the Policy Timing Trap

Empirical passthrough lags from academic and central bank research:

- **Energy → CPI:** 1-3 months. Gasoline prices pass through to CPI almost immediately (1 month). Natural gas/electricity pass through with 2-3 month lags depending on contract structures (regulated tariffs in Europe create 3-6 month lags; deregulated US markets are faster). The BLS estimates energy's CPI weight at ~7% but its contribution to CPI volatility at ~30%.
- **Food → CPI:** 3-6 months. Agricultural commodity prices (wheat, corn, soybeans) pass through to retail food prices with 3-6 month lags reflecting processing, inventory, and contract renegotiation. The passthrough rate is approximately 0.2-0.3 (a 10% increase in agricultural commodities produces a 2-3% increase in retail food). Food's CPI weight is ~14%.
- **Industrial metals → CPI:** 6-18 months. Copper, aluminum, and steel pass through to consumer prices via intermediate goods (appliances, autos, construction materials) with extended lags. The passthrough rate is lower (~0.05-0.1) but more persistent.

The "passthrough illusion" works as follows: suppose a synchronized commodity shock hits in Month 0. By Month 3, energy CPI is peaking and starting to decelerate. But food CPI is now accelerating (responding to Month 0 agricultural prices), and the diffusion index of CPI categories showing acceleration is broadening. By Month 9, food is decelerating but industrial metals passthrough is now appearing in goods prices. A central bank observing this sequential acceleration in CPI breadth will interpret it as "inflation broadening beyond commodities" — precisely the diagnostic for second-round effects — when in reality it is the lagged first-round effect of a single shock propagating through the input-output structure.

The ECB staff recognized this dynamic ex post in their 2023 Economic Bulletin: the energy-to-food-to-core passthrough chain explained a significant portion of what appeared to be autonomous "underlying" inflation in the eurozone. The policy implication is that central banks systematically over-tighten in response to broad commodity shocks because the passthrough lag creates a breadth signal that mimics second-round effects.

### Claim 3: Exchange Rate Channel Asymmetry

The USD denomination of commodities creates a structural asymmetry in the commodity-inflation transmission:

- **For the US:** Dollar appreciation (driven by Fed tightening) partially offsets commodity passthrough. A 10% dollar appreciation reduces the USD price of commodities by approximately 3-5% (partial offset, since commodities are priced in USD but supply/demand fundamentals also matter). The Fed benefits from a self-correcting mechanism: tightening → stronger dollar → lower import prices → less commodity passthrough.

- **For the eurozone:** EUR depreciation amplifies commodity passthrough. EUR/USD fell from 1.22 (June 2021) to 0.96 (September 2022), a 21% depreciation. This added approximately 8-12pp to eurozone commodity import costs on top of the dollar-denominated price increase. The ECB was fighting both the commodity shock AND the FX amplification.

- **For EM economies:** The amplification is most extreme. Many EM central banks face the impossible trinity: they cannot simultaneously maintain exchange rate stability, capital account openness, and independent monetary policy. When the Fed tightens against commodity inflation, EM central banks must choose between (a) matching Fed hikes to defend the currency (importing contractionary policy they may not need) or (b) tolerating depreciation (importing more commodity inflation). Brazil, Mexico, and Chile all hiked well ahead of the Fed in 2021-2022, illustrating option (a). Turkey chose option (b) with severe inflationary consequences.

The "commodity-FX doom loop" was most visible in the eurozone in 2022: higher gas prices → higher inflation → ECB tightens (but less than Fed) → EUR depreciates → higher EUR-denominated gas prices → more inflation → more ECB tightening required. The TTF gas price in EUR terms peaked 3 months after the USD price peak, entirely due to the FX channel.

### Claim 4: Impaired Credit Channel During Commodity-Driven Inflation

The standard monetary policy transmission mechanism:
1. Central bank raises policy rate → 2. Banks raise deposit and lending rates → 3. Higher borrowing costs reduce investment and consumption → 4. Lower aggregate demand → 5. Inflation falls.

Steps 1-3 operate regardless of the inflation source. Step 4→5 is where the impairment occurs: when inflation is driven by commodity supply constraints, reduced demand does not directly lower commodity prices (which are set in global markets by global supply/demand). Domestic demand destruction only reduces the domestic economy's commodity consumption, which has a marginal effect on global commodity prices unless the economy is very large (the US and China are partial exceptions).

The sacrifice ratio asymmetry is documented in IMF Working Paper WP/22/221 and related research:
- **Demand-driven inflation episodes (1980-2020 sample):** Sacrifice ratio approximately 1.0-1.5 (1-1.5% cumulative output loss per 1pp inflation reduction).
- **Supply/commodity-driven inflation episodes:** Sacrifice ratio approximately 2.0-3.5, with the range reflecting the commodity's share of the consumption basket.

This connects directly to the KB's existing framework: the modified Taylor Rule with a credit spread term (Curdia-Woodford, KB confidence 7) implicitly assumes demand-driven inflation. When inflation is commodity-driven, the same 100bp of HY spread widening produces less inflation reduction per unit of output cost, meaning the "shadow Fed funds rate" equivalent overestimates the effective tightening.

### Claim 5: Institutional Determinants of Second-Round Effects

The "will commodity prices feed into wages?" question is the central bank's key diagnostic. The institutional determinants:

- **Union density and collective bargaining coverage:** Germany (collective bargaining covers ~52% of workers), France (~98% due to extension mechanisms), vs. US (~10%), UK (~23%). Higher coverage increases the probability that commodity price shocks feed into negotiated wage increases with 6-12 month lags.
- **COLA clauses:** Largely extinct in the US (significant mainly in some public sector and legacy contracts). More prevalent in parts of the eurozone (Belgium has near-automatic wage indexation). Where they exist, commodity-to-wage passthrough is mechanical.
- **Inflation expectations anchoring:** The key behavioral variable. When expectations are anchored (5Y5Y breakevens near target), workers and firms treat commodity price increases as temporary and do not embed them in multi-year contracts. When expectations drift, the commodity shock becomes self-reinforcing.

The 2021-2023 natural experiment:
- **US:** Wage growth tracked labor market tightness (unemployment fell to 3.4%), not commodity prices. The correlation between energy CPI and average hourly earnings was approximately 0.15 with a 6-month lag — statistically insignificant. Wage-price spiral conditions not met (per KB: wage_price_spiral_base_case_rejection, confidence 8).
- **Eurozone:** Negotiated wages responded to energy prices with a 9-12 month lag. German IG Metall's 2022 negotiation explicitly cited energy costs. French SMIC (minimum wage) is indexed to CPI and rose 5.3% in 2022.
- **EM:** Highly heterogeneous. Brazil (credible central bank, inflation targeting since 1999) saw limited second-round effects. Turkey (undermined central bank independence) saw full passthrough into a wage-price spiral.

### Claim 6: FCI as the Operative Policy Gauge

The Goldman Sachs FCI, Bloomberg US Financial Conditions Index, and Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index all attempt to summarize the effective stance of monetary policy by combining:
- Short-term interest rates (policy rate)
- Long-term interest rates (term premium + expectations)
- Credit spreads (HY, IG, mortgage)
- Equity prices (wealth effect)
- Exchange rate (import price effect)

For commodity-inflation transmission specifically, the FCI is superior to the policy rate because:

1. **The exchange rate component directly captures the commodity passthrough amplification/dampening.** A 10% dollar appreciation is worth roughly 25-50bp of additional tightening via reduced import prices. This is invisible in the policy rate but captured in the FCI.

2. **Equity price declines create a negative wealth effect that reduces consumption and commodity demand.** The S&P 500's 25% decline in 2022 reduced household financial wealth by ~$10T, contributing an estimated 50-75bp of tightening equivalent.

3. **Credit spread widening increases the cost of capital for commodity-intensive industries (energy, mining, agriculture), potentially reducing supply investment and creating a perverse feedback loop.** Higher credit spreads → less energy capex → tighter future energy supply → higher future energy prices. This is the "tightening paradox" for commodity inflation.

The divergence between policy rate and FCI was most dramatic in late 2023: the Fed held at 5.25-5.50% but the FCI eased by ~150bp equivalent (S&P 500 +24%, HY OAS -120bp, dollar -5%). From a commodity-inflation perspective, this FCI easing increased the passthrough of any future commodity shock because: (a) the wealth effect boosted consumption, (b) tighter credit spreads reduced the cost of energy investment (good for future supply but also signaling demand resilience), and (c) the weaker dollar amplified the USD price of commodities for the US.

### Claim 7: Credibility as the Master Variable

The theoretical framework (Barro-Gordon, 1983; Clarida-Galí-Gertler, 1999) establishes that in a credible inflation-targeting regime, commodity price shocks produce temporary price level shifts without persistent inflation effects. This is because:
- Firms set prices expecting future inflation to return to target → no persistent markup adjustment
- Workers negotiate wages based on expected future inflation ≈ target → no second-round wage effects
- Financial markets price inflation expectations near target → term premium does not embed permanent commodity inflation

The 2021 "transitory" miscall weakened this mechanism:
- Michigan 5-10Y expectations rose from 2.5% to 3.1% (June 2022)
- 5Y5Y breakevens rose from 2.0% to 2.5%
- The Fed's credibility premium (the gap between actual and Taylor-Rule-prescribed rates) turned sharply negative

The subsequent overtightening (hiking to 5.25-5.50% when a standard Taylor Rule with r* = 0.5% and balanced coefficients prescribed ~4.0-4.5%) was a credibility restoration exercise. The overtightening was rational from a repeated-game perspective: the cost of persistently above-target expectations (requiring even more tightening later) exceeds the cost of temporary over-restriction.

Current credibility state: 5Y5Y breakevens at ~2.3% suggest partial but not complete restoration. The remaining 30bp above the pre-2021 2.0% level represents a "credibility scar" — the market assigns some probability to the Fed tolerating above-2% inflation in future supply shock episodes. This means the Fed's look-through capacity for future commodity shocks is reduced relative to the pre-2021 baseline.

### Claim 8: Structural Divergence in BOJ and PBOC Transmission

**BOJ:** The BOJ's framework creates a unique commodity-inflation dynamic:
- YCC (now loosened to ~1.0% 10Y cap with flexibility) means that commodity-driven inflation does not translate into higher nominal long-term rates
- The result: rising CPI + anchored JGB yields = falling real rates = easier real financial conditions
- This is the opposite of what inflation-targeting frameworks prescribe
- The BOJ has explicitly cited commodity-driven inflation as a "positive" development helping achieve the 2% target, making it the only major central bank that *welcomes* commodity price increases
- The JPY depreciation this produces (via rate differentials) amplifies commodity import costs for Japan, creating a "virtuous" (from the BOJ's perspective) feedback loop: weaker yen → higher import prices → higher CPI → closer to 2% target

**PBOC:** China's commodity-inflation transmission is administered rather than market-driven:
- Domestic fuel prices are set by NDRC formula (adjusts every 10 working days but with caps and floors)
- Strategic Petroleum Reserve releases/purchases smooth oil price volatility
- State-owned refineries absorb price swings via mandated refining margins
- Agricultural commodity prices are managed through procurement programs and reserves
- The PBOC's interest rate tools (MLF, LPR) are not the primary instruments for managing commodity inflation; administrative measures are

The policy divergence implications for global markets:
- A global commodity shock → Fed/ECB tighten, BOJ accommodates, PBOC uses administrative tools
- This divergence widens rate differentials → USD/JPY appreciates, USD/CNY range-bound (managed), EUR/USD depends on relative tightening
- Capital flows toward USD assets (carry + safe haven) → further dollar strength → further amplification of commodity passthrough for non-US economies
- The divergence can persist for 12-24 months, creating sustained FX trends that rate markets must price

## Connections to Other Topics

**Commodity Supercycles (KB depth 2, confidence 5.0):** The supercycle framework determines the *frequency and magnitude* of commodity shocks, while this analysis covers the *transmission* of those shocks. A key interaction: if we are in the early stages of a new commodity supercycle (driven by underinvestment in extractive industries during 2015-2020 and green transition demand for metals), the look-through doctrine will face repeated stress tests. Recurrent, large commodity shocks would make it progressively harder for central banks to maintain credibility while looking through — potentially forcing a permanent shift toward headline-inflation targeting.

**Inflation Regimes (KB depth 3, confidence 6.0):** Commodity passthrough is a regime *input*, not just a variable. The 2021-2022 commodity shock was a key catalyst for the regime shift from low-and-stable to elevated-and-volatile inflation. The KB's stagflationary/disinflationary bifurcation framework (confidence 6) depends critically on whether the next commodity shock produces persistent or transitory inflation — which in turn depends on the institutional and credibility factors analyzed here. The bifurcation probability weights (15% stagflationary) should be revised upward if commodity passthrough mechanisms are strengthening.

**Labor Market Dynamics (KB depth 2, confidence 7.0):** The second-round effects analysis (Claim 5) directly connects to the KB's wage_price_spiral_base_case_rejection (confidence 8) and phillips_curve_regime_dependence (confidence 6). The key insight from this analysis: commodity shocks can produce wage acceleration without a wage-price spiral — the distinction matters. The US saw 5%+ wage growth in 2022 driven by labor market tightness coinciding with commodity inflation, but no spiral because institutional conditions prevented self-reinforcing dynamics. This validates the KB's conjunctive conditions framework.

**Credit-Equity Linkage (KB topic from iter_0004):** The "tightening paradox" (Claim 4) connects to the modified Taylor Rule with credit spread term (KB confidence 7). When central banks tighten against commodity inflation, credit spreads widen more than in demand-driven episodes because the fundamental outlook for commodity-consuming firms deteriorates (higher input costs + weaker demand). This means the credit spread term in the Taylor Rule provides *less* of an automatic stabilizer during commodity-driven tightening — the Fed's implicit "100bp HY widening ≈ 25-50bp tightening equivalent" underestimates the output cost because the widening reflects both demand destruction AND supply-side margin compression.

**Risk Appetite Regimes (KB depth 1, confidence 7.9):** Commodity shocks can trigger regime transitions in the five-regime model. The 2022 commodity shock was a key driver of the Regime 1→Regime 3 (rapid transition, skipping Regime 2) move. The transmission path was: commodity shock → central bank hawkishness → rising term premium (supply concerns) → credit-equity correlation spike → risk-off regime. The yield-curve-mediated contagion sequence (from iter_0004 rates_strategist_01 analysis) applies with a twist: commodity-driven tightening produces bear flattening (front end rises on policy expectations) before bear steepening (term premium rises on inflation uncertainty), whereas demand-driven tightening produces the reverse sequence.

**Growth Models & Cycle Positioning (KB depth 2):** The fiscal_deficit_kalecki_channel (confidence 9) interacts with commodity passthrough in an underappreciated way. Fiscal deficits sustain demand (Kalecki profit channel) even as commodity prices rise, which means commodity passthrough may be *higher* in a fiscally-expansive environment because demand does not decline sufficiently to offset the price-level shock. The "insulation" provided by fiscal deficits against recession also insulates against the demand-destruction channel that would normally moderate commodity-driven inflation — potentially making the central bank's job harder and the sacrifice ratio even higher.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the current state of energy price passthrough by sector in the US and eurozone?** The 2022-2023 commodity shock has largely unwound in price-level terms, but the passthrough to services prices (restaurant meals, transportation, healthcare) may still be working through with 12-18 month lags. If services inflation is partially a lagged commodity passthrough effect rather than autonomous demand-driven inflation, the policy implication is significant: further tightening is addressing a problem that will self-correct.

2. **Has the 2022-2023 experience permanently shifted central bank reaction functions, or will the look-through doctrine reassert itself?** Specifically, will the Fed (under Chair Powell or a successor) respond to the next commodity shock with 2008-style look-through or 2022-style headline-targeting? The answer depends on the starting inflation level, but there may be a permanent hawkish bias embedded by the "transitory" reputational damage.

3. **How does the green energy transition change commodity passthrough dynamics?** Electrification reduces oil passthrough but increases copper, lithium, and rare earth passthrough. The net effect on CPI is ambiguous and depends on the relative price elasticities. The transition period (2025-2040) may feature *higher* commodity inflation volatility as new demand sources (EV batteries, grid infrastructure) interact with constrained supply in minerals that have long investment lead times (7-15 years for new mines).

4. **What is the passthrough rate from commodity prices to core PCE (the Fed's target variable) under current conditions?** The standard estimate is ~0.1-0.15 (a 10% broad commodity index increase raises core PCE by 1.0-1.5pp with a 6-12 month lag). But this estimate is derived from a low-inflation sample period and may be non-linear — the passthrough rate may be higher when starting from above-target inflation (firms are more willing to pass through input costs when competitors are also raising prices).

5. **Can the PBOC's administered pricing model survive a large enough commodity shock, or would it break down?** China's strategic reserves, subsidies, and SOE loss absorption have limits. A sustained doubling of oil prices (e.g., from a Middle East supply disruption) would strain even China's administrative capacity, potentially forcing a more market-driven passthrough that would amplify global inflation.

6. **How should central banks treat commodity-driven inflation that is partially policy-induced (e.g., carbon taxes, emissions trading schemes)?** This is a growing component of energy costs. If the inflation is a deliberate policy choice (carbon pricing), should monetary policy look through it? The ECB has signaled yes in principle, but the political economy may not permit it if headline CPI is elevated.

7. **What is the interaction between commodity passthrough and the fiscal stance?** The KB identifies the Kalecki fiscal channel as dominant. If fiscal deficits sustain demand during a commodity shock, does the higher demand prevent the natural demand-destruction mechanism from moderating passthrough? This would imply that the sacrifice ratio is endogenous to fiscal policy — a point not captured in standard Taylor Rule frameworks.

8. **How do commodity futures market structure changes (increased financialization, algorithmic trading, reduced speculative position limits) affect the speed and magnitude of passthrough?** If commodity markets are more volatile due to financial factors, the commodity-to-CPI passthrough may be faster and more overshooting than in historical samples, reducing central banks' time to assess whether a shock is transitory.
