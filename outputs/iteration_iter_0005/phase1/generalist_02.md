# Commodity Price → Inflation Transmission Mechanisms — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Commodity-to-CPI passthrough rates have structurally declined since the 1970s, but the decline is non-monotonic and regime-dependent.** The passthrough coefficient from a 10% oil price shock to headline CPI has fallen from ~0.6-0.8pp in 1973-74 to ~0.1-0.3pp in 2010-2022, but the 2021-2022 episode demonstrated that passthrough can re-accelerate when supply shocks coincide with excess aggregate demand and accommodative monetary policy.

2. **The historical base rate for commodity shocks producing sustained core inflation (second-round effects) is approximately 2-out-of-7 major episodes since 1970 — roughly 30%.** The 1973-74 and 1979-80 episodes produced durable wage-price spirals; the 1990, 2008, 2011, and 2014-15 episodes did not. The 2021-22 episode is a contested intermediate case. The key discriminant is whether the shock arrives into an economy with already-tight labor markets and accommodative policy.

3. **Energy and food commodity shocks follow fundamentally different transmission channels with different lag structures.** Energy passthrough operates through a cost-push channel with 2-6 month lags to CPI (gasoline near-instantaneous, utilities 3-6 months, airline fares 1-3 months), while food commodity passthrough operates through a margin-compression channel with 6-18 month lags (farm gate → processor → wholesale → retail), and is heavily mediated by retail industry concentration and private-label penetration.

4. **The sequencing of asset class responses to commodity-driven inflation shocks follows a historically consistent pattern: commodities lead → breakevens widen → front-end rates reprice → curve flattens → equities re-rate → credit spreads widen.** This sequence has held across 5 of the 7 major commodity inflation episodes since 1970, with the main exceptions being episodes where central banks were perceived to be behind the curve, which compressed the lag between breakeven widening and equity de-rating.

5. **The most relevant historical analogues for the current (2025-2026) commodity-inflation transmission environment are: (a) 1973-74 OPEC embargo, (b) 1979-80 second oil shock, (c) 2007-2008 commodity supercycle peak, (d) 2010-2011 Arab Spring / QE2 commodity spike, and (e) 2021-2022 post-COVID supply shock.** These span the full range of outcomes from stagflationary catastrophe to transitory pass-through, and the structural differences between them identify the key variables that determine which outcome materializes.

6. **The FX channel is the most underappreciated transmission mechanism in commodity-to-inflation analysis.** A simultaneous commodity price shock + USD depreciation (as in 1973-74, 2007-08, 2021-22) roughly doubles the effective passthrough rate for a commodity-importing nation relative to a commodity shock alone. Conversely, a commodity shock accompanied by USD strength (as in 2014-15) can offset a significant fraction of the passthrough for the US while amplifying it in EM importers.

7. **The structural shift from goods-heavy to services-heavy economies in developed markets has not eliminated commodity inflation transmission — it has changed the channel from direct input costs to indirect margin/expectations effects.** Services inflation responds to commodity shocks with a 6-18 month lag via the wage-rent-expectations channel, not the direct input cost channel. This lag creates a policy trap: by the time commodity shocks appear in core services inflation, the original commodity price move may have reversed, making it appear that services inflation is autonomous when it is actually a delayed echo.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Declining but regime-dependent passthrough

**Analogue Identification & Decomposition:**

| Episode | Oil Shock Magnitude | CPI Impact (12m) | Passthrough Coefficient | Labor Market | Policy Stance |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------|--------------|---------------|
| 1973-74 | +300% (OPEC embargo) | +12.3% headline | ~0.7-0.8pp per 10% oil | Tight (3.4% UE) | Accommodative (Burns Fed) |
| 1979-80 | +150% (Iranian Revolution) | +14.8% headline | ~0.6-0.7pp per 10% oil | Mixed (5.8% UE) | Initially accommodative → Volcker tightening |
| 1990 | +100% (Gulf War) | +6.1% headline | ~0.3-0.4pp per 10% oil | Loosening (5.9% UE) | Neutral |
| 2007-08 | +100% (supercycle peak) | +5.6% headline | ~0.2-0.3pp per 10% oil | Tight-then-collapsing | Easing (GFC response) |
| 2010-11 | +40% (Arab Spring/QE2) | +3.9% headline | ~0.2-0.3pp per 10% oil | Slack (9.0% UE) | QE2 in effect |
| 2014-15 | -60% (shale supply glut) | −0.2% headline | ~0.1-0.2pp per 10% oil | Tightening (5.5% UE) | Approaching liftoff |
| 2021-22 | +80% (post-COVID) | +9.1% headline | ~0.4-0.5pp per 10% oil | Extremely tight (3.5% UE) | Ultra-accommodative → rapid tightening |

The passthrough coefficient is not a stable parameter — it is regime-dependent. The structural decline from the 1970s reflects reduced energy intensity (US energy consumption per unit of GDP fell ~50% from 1975-2020), de-unionization (reducing second-round wage indexation), central bank credibility (anchoring inflation expectations), and globalization (expanding the supply response). However, the 2021-22 episode showed that when multiple structural tailwinds reverse simultaneously — supply chains disrupted, labor markets tight, fiscal stimulus elevated, monetary policy accommodative — passthrough can revert toward 1970s levels.

**Key finding:** The base rate across 7 episodes gives a mean passthrough of ~0.3-0.4pp per 10% oil price increase, but with a bimodal distribution: ~0.1-0.3pp when anchoring conditions hold, ~0.5-0.8pp when they don't.

### Claim 2: Second-round effects base rate of ~30%

Second-round effects — where commodity price shocks feed into wages, rents, and services prices creating self-sustaining inflation — have occurred in approximately 2 of 7 major episodes (1973-74, 1979-80), with 2021-22 as a partial/contested case.

**Episodes with durable second-round effects:**
- **1973-74:** Cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) clauses covered ~60% of union workers. Wage growth accelerated from 6.2% to 9.4%. CPI remained above 5% for 40 consecutive months. Inflation expectations (Michigan survey) ratcheted from 4% to 12%.
- **1979-80:** Despite Volcker's appointment, backward-looking wage indexation and embedded expectations kept core CPI above 8% for 18+ months after the oil shock peaked. Wage-price spiral was only broken by engineering a severe recession (10.8% unemployment).

**Episodes without durable second-round effects:**
- **1990:** Gulf War oil spike reversed within 6 months; recession crushed demand; core CPI barely moved.
- **2007-08:** Despite a massive commodity supercycle peak, collapsing credit and housing markets overwhelmed any passthrough; core CPI peaked at 2.5% and fell rapidly.
- **2010-11:** Large labor market slack (9% UE) and anchored expectations prevented any wage-price transmission. Core CPI rose modestly from 0.6% to 2.3% then stabilized.
- **2014-15 (disinflation):** The oil collapse tested the symmetric hypothesis; core CPI barely budged, suggesting asymmetric passthrough.

**The 2021-22 contested case:** Headline CPI reached 9.1%, core CPI 6.6%. Wage growth accelerated to 5.5%+ (Atlanta Fed Wage Tracker). However, the mechanism was not pure commodity-to-wage — it was a multi-causal shock (fiscal transfers + supply chains + labor supply withdrawal + commodity prices simultaneously). The disinflation to ~3% by late 2023 without a recession suggests second-round effects were present but not fully entrenched — a partial analogue rather than a clean repeat of 1973-74.

**Discriminant variables for second-round effects:**
1. Union density / COLA coverage (1970s: ~35% of workers covered; 2020s: ~6%)
2. Inflation expectations anchoring (University of Michigan long-run expectations)
3. Labor market tightness at time of shock (unemployment rate, quits rate)
4. Monetary policy stance and credibility (real rate gap)
5. Fiscal policy co-movement (pro-cyclical fiscal expansion amplifies)

### Claim 3: Energy vs. food transmission channels

**Energy passthrough (documented across all 7 episodes):**
- **Gasoline:** Near-instantaneous (rockets-and-feathers asymmetry documented by Borenstein et al.). CPI gasoline component moves within 2-4 weeks of crude. Asymmetric: rises faster than it falls.
- **Electricity/utilities:** 3-6 month lag due to regulated rate structures. Natural gas passthrough varies by contract structure (Henry Hub spot vs. fixed-rate residential).
- **Airlines/transport:** 1-3 month lag via fuel surcharges and hedging book roll.
- **Petrochemicals → manufactured goods:** 6-12 month lag via plastics, fertilizers, packaging.

**Food commodity passthrough (documented across 1973-74, 2007-08, 2010-11, 2021-22):**
- **Farm gate → retail lag:** 6-18 months. The 2007-08 wheat/corn price doubling showed up in US food CPI with a ~9 month median lag.
- **Margin compression buffer:** Retail grocers (Walmart, Kroger) absorb wholesale cost increases for 3-6 months before repricing, depending on competitive dynamics and private label share.
- **Fattening period:** Livestock cycles add 12-18 month lags for meat — feed cost increases take a full production cycle to transmit.
- **Developing vs. developed market divergence:** Food constitutes ~15% of US CPI but 30-50% in EM CPI baskets. Passthrough to EM inflation is 2-3x the passthrough in DM.

### Claim 4: Asset class sequencing

Across the 5 episodes where the sequence was clearly observable (1973-74, 1979-80, 2007-08, 2010-11, 2021-22):

**Typical sequence and timing:**
1. **Commodity prices surge** (T=0)
2. **Breakeven inflation rates widen** (T+1 to T+3 months) — TIPS breakevens reprice as market incorporates passthrough expectations
3. **Front-end rates reprice** (T+2 to T+6 months) — markets price Fed reaction function
4. **Yield curve flattens/inverts** (T+3 to T+9 months) — front-end rises on policy tightening expectations, long-end partially anchored by credibility
5. **Equity multiples compress** (T+4 to T+12 months) — discount rate rises and margin compression hit simultaneously
6. **Credit spreads widen** (T+6 to T+18 months) — weakest credits face margin squeeze + higher funding costs

**Exceptions to the sequence:**
- **1973-74:** Equity de-rating began almost simultaneously with the oil shock because the embargo was a discrete, immediately visible event. The S&P 500 fell ~45% from Jan 1973 to Oct 1974.
- **2007-08:** The credit spread widening preceded and overwhelmed the commodity inflation signal — the GFC credit crisis was the dominant force.

**Key insight from the analogue decomposition:** The lag between commodity price move and equity/credit impact gives investors a window — historically 3-9 months — to reposition. This window is shorter when central banks are perceived as behind the curve.

### Claim 5: Five most relevant analogues for 2025-26

| Analogue | Similarity Score | Key Similarity | Key Difference |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|----------------|
| 1973-74 OPEC Embargo | 5/10 | Supply shock, fiscal expansion, geopolitical driver | COLA coverage, gold standard collapse, energy intensity 3x higher |
| 1979-80 Second Oil Shock | 4/10 | Embedded inflation psychology, Fed credibility question | Starting point was already high inflation; Volcker had no precedent |
| 2007-08 Supercycle Peak | 6/10 | Synchronized global demand, China-driven commodity demand, EM growth | No concurrent financial crisis currently; housing not in bubble |
| 2010-11 Arab Spring/QE2 | 7/10 | QE-driven liquidity, geopolitical supply risk, EM-DM divergence | Much more labor market slack then; expectations firmly anchored |
| 2021-22 Post-COVID | 8/10 | Multi-factor supply shock, fiscal-monetary co-stimulus, tight labor, services inflation lag | COVID supply chain disruption was unique; fiscal impulse was one-time |

The 2021-22 episode is the closest analogue but with important caveats: the post-COVID shock was genuinely multi-causal in ways that make isolating the commodity transmission channel difficult. The 2010-11 episode may be more useful for isolating the commodity-specific channel because the demand/supply shock was more commodity-focused.

### Claim 6: FX channel amplification

**Evidence across analogues:**

- **1973-74:** USD depreciated ~20% (DXY equivalent) from 1971-73 as Bretton Woods collapsed. This amplified import prices on top of the oil shock. Double whammy.
- **2007-08:** DXY fell from ~85 to ~71 (March 2008) as the Fed cut rates. Commodity prices in USD terms surged partly because of USD weakness. For the US, the FX channel added roughly 1.5-2pp to import price inflation.
- **2010-11:** QE2 weakened the dollar; EM commodity importers (India, Turkey) faced amplified passthrough — Indian WPI food inflation hit 18%.
- **2014-15:** DXY surged from ~80 to ~100. Despite the oil collapse being driven by supply, the strong dollar reduced the disinflationary impulse for non-US commodity importers (Europe, Japan). For the US, strong dollar suppressed non-energy import prices, acting as a buffer.
- **2021-22:** USD was initially stable (DXY ~90-93) during the commodity surge, then strengthened sharply (DXY ~93 to ~114 by Sep 2022). This strong dollar partially mitigated US commodity passthrough in the later stages while amplifying inflation in Europe and EM.

**Quantification:** A rough rule of thumb across analogues: a 10% USD depreciation coinciding with a commodity shock adds ~0.5-1.0pp to US headline CPI over 12 months, primarily through the import price channel. Conversely, 10% USD appreciation offsets roughly 0.3-0.5pp.

### Claim 7: The services lag channel

The shift from goods-intensive to services-intensive inflation transmission is observable by comparing the 1970s episodes with post-2000 episodes:

- **1970s:** Direct energy input costs were 8-10% of GDP. Oil → CPI transmission was fast and direct. Manufacturing sector was ~25% of GDP.
- **2020s:** Direct energy input costs are ~3-4% of GDP. Manufacturing is ~11% of GDP. But services are ~70% of GDP.

**The indirect channel operates through:**
1. **Rent/OER:** Energy costs → heating/cooling → operating costs → rent adjustments (12-18 month lag due to lease structures).
2. **Wages:** Commodity price shock → headline CPI rise → inflation expectations rise → wage demands rise → services prices rise (6-12 month lag).
3. **Transport/logistics:** Fuel costs → delivery costs → service provider cost base → services pricing (3-6 month lag).

**The 2021-23 episode provided a clear case study:** Goods inflation (heavily commodity-driven) peaked in Q1 2022. Services inflation (ex-shelter) peaked in Q3 2022 — a ~6 month lag. Shelter inflation (OER) peaked in Q1 2023 — a ~12 month lag. This created the "last mile" disinflation problem: by the time core services inflation was peaking, commodity prices had already fallen, making it appear that services inflation was autonomous.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Direct Connections (Within Knowledge Base)

- **Commodity Supercycles (asset_class_dynamics):** The transmission analysis is downstream of the commodity supercycle framework. Whether the current commodity environment constitutes a new supercycle (driven by underinvestment + green transition demand) or a cyclical rally fundamentally determines the persistence of the commodity shock and therefore the transmission dynamics. A supercycle produces repeated and sustained passthrough; a cyclical move produces one-time level shifts.

- **Inflation Regimes (macro_frameworks):** The passthrough coefficient is not a constant — it is a function of the prevailing inflation regime. In a low-and-anchored regime (2010-19), passthrough is minimal. In a transitioning/de-anchoring regime (2021-22), passthrough amplifies. This creates a reflexive loop: large commodity shocks can shift the inflation regime, which then amplifies the passthrough of the next shock.

- **Monetary Policy Transmission (macro_frameworks):** The central bank reaction function determines whether first-round commodity passthrough becomes second-round core inflation. The historical analogue evidence is clear: pre-emptive tightening (1990, 2010-11) suppresses second-round effects; delayed tightening (1973-74, 2021-22) permits them. This connects directly to the current debate about the Fed's reaction function in a world with elevated government debt and fiscal dominance concerns.

- **Stagflationary/Disinflationary Bifurcation (existing KB concept):** The commodity transmission mechanism is a key swing variable in the stagflation vs. disinflation bifurcation already identified in iter_0002. If commodity passthrough is high and persistent → stagflationary path (positive stock-bond correlation). If passthrough is low and transitory → disinflation path (negative stock-bond correlation, bonds hedge). The historical analogue base rate (30% chance of sustained second-round effects) can directly inform the probability weights on these scenarios.

- **Commodity-EM Labor Procyclicality (existing KB concept from iter_0003):** The EM labor market amplification mechanism documented in the KB is the flip side of the DM commodity inflation transmission story. Commodity price surges that create inflationary pressure in commodity importers simultaneously create overheating and REER appreciation in commodity exporters. This is a cross-asset connection that creates divergent trade expressions.

### Indirect Connections

- **Credit-Equity Linkage (iter_0004):** Commodity-driven margin compression can be an early trigger for credit deterioration in energy-intensive sectors, which then feeds into the credit-equity contagion channels documented in the previous iteration.

- **Risk Appetite Regimes (iter_0001):** Commodity inflation shocks historically coincide with regime shifts in risk appetite — the shift from risk-on to risk-off often occurs with a lag after the commodity shock, following the asset class sequencing described in Claim 4.

- **Yield Curve Dynamics (credit_rates):** The curve flattening/inversion response to commodity shocks is a key signpost. The 1973-74 and 1979-80 episodes both produced significant curve inversions as the Fed tightened; the 2010-11 episode did not because the Fed remained on hold.

- **Deglobalization (structural_themes):** The secular trend toward deglobalization/reshoring structurally increases commodity passthrough by: (a) reducing the supply-response elasticity that damped passthrough in the 2000s, (b) increasing transport cost sensitivity, and (c) reducing the disinflationary offset from cheap imported goods.

## Open Questions

1. **Has the 2021-22 episode permanently re-calibrated passthrough coefficients, or was it a one-time deviation?** The base rate analysis treats it as one data point, but if it reflects a structural break (de-anchoring of expectations, deglobalization, reduced energy supply elasticity), the forward-looking passthrough may be permanently higher than the 2010-19 period.

2. **What is the passthrough coefficient for non-energy, non-food commodity shocks (metals, lumber, cotton)?** The historical analogue evidence is dominated by oil and food episodes. Industrial metals passthrough is less well-documented and may follow a different lag structure (operating through capex costs and durable goods prices rather than headline CPI).

3. **How does the green energy transition change the commodity-inflation transmission mechanism?** Electrification reduces oil passthrough but potentially increases copper/lithium/rare earth passthrough. There is no historical analogue for a commodity mix shift of this nature occurring simultaneously with a transmission channel change.

4. **What is the role of fiscal policy in amplifying or dampening commodity passthrough?** The 2021-22 episode was unique in combining a commodity shock with massive fiscal transfers. Historical analogues suggest that fiscal expansion amplifies second-round effects (1973-74 had expansionary fiscal), but the sample is too small for confident base rate estimation of this interaction.

5. **How do algorithmic trading and real-time data change the asset class sequencing lags?** The 3-9 month window between commodity shock and equity/credit repricing was established in an era of slower information transmission. ETF flows, CPI nowcasting, and algorithmic momentum strategies may have compressed these lags, reducing the repositioning window.

6. **What is the threshold level of commodity price increase that triggers second-round effects?** The analogue evidence suggests a possible threshold effect — shocks above ~40-50% in oil seem more likely to produce second-round effects than smaller shocks — but the sample is too small to estimate this threshold with precision.

7. **How do inventory management changes (just-in-time vs. just-in-case) alter the passthrough lag structure?** The post-COVID shift toward higher inventory buffers may have changed the lag between commodity price moves and final goods prices. This is genuinely new — there is no historical analogue for a widespread inventory strategy shift coinciding with commodity price volatility.
