# Commodity-Inflation Transmission — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist (generalist) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Commodity-inflation episodes resolve through one of four distinct archetypes, and the resolution type — not the transmission mechanism — is the primary determinant of cross-asset return outcomes.** The four archetypes are: (a) demand-destruction resolution (1974, 2008), where recession destroys demand and prices collapse; (b) supply-response resolution (post-2008 shale, lithium post-2022), where high prices incentivize investment that eventually overwhelms demand; (c) policy-induced recession resolution (1980-82 Volcker), where central bank deliberately engineers contraction; (d) geopolitical-reversal resolution (1991 Gulf War, partial 2023 as supply fears eased). Each archetype produces a fundamentally different asset-class return pattern in the 12-24 months following the commodity price peak.

2. **"Stacked" commodity-inflation episodes — where a second shock arrives before the first has fully transmitted — produce non-linearly worse inflation outcomes than the sum of individual shocks would predict.** The base rate from historical analogues: 1979-80 arrived while 1973-74's institutional responses (expanded COLAs, indexation norms, wage catch-up) were still operative, producing peak CPI of 14.8% vs. 12.3% from the larger initial shock. The 2022 Ukraine energy/food shock arrived while COVID supply-chain inflation was still transmitting through services. The non-linearity arises because the first shock's partial activation of second-round channels lowers the threshold for the second shock to push into the high-passthrough regime. This interacts with the KB's `regime_dependent_passthrough_coefficients` concept — stacking is a regime-transition accelerator.

3. **There is a recurring "false calm" window of 6-18 months during commodity-inflation episodes where headline inflation moderates or stabilizes, historically associated with premature policy easing or narrative shifts, before the next transmission wave arrives.** Template episodes: mid-1974 (headline CPI dipped before the wage-price spiral accelerated), early 1980 (brief disinflation before OPEC II effects fully transmitted), Q3 2022-Q1 2023 (headline CPI fell from 9.1% to ~6% on goods disinflation before services persistence became apparent). The false calm is structurally generated by the `multi_stage_lag_structure` — energy passthrough peaks and starts fading before services/wages peak. This is the mechanism underlying the KB's `passthrough_illusion_hypothesis` but with historical calibration: the false calm has occurred in 5 of 7 major episodes and lasted median 9 months.

4. **Cross-asset response sequencing during commodity-inflation episodes follows a remarkably stable order, and deviations from the canonical sequence are among the highest-value real-time signposts for identifying which regime is operative.** The canonical sequence across episodes: commodity spot prices → futures curve backwardation → near-term breakeven inflation rise → industrial/transport equity outperformance → credit spread widening (high-yield energy first, then broad) → equity multiple compression → earnings revision downcycle → long-end bond yields rise (delayed by safe-haven demand). The 2021-22 episode followed this sequence almost exactly with a ~3-month lag at each stage. The 2010-11 episode deviated at step 5 (credit spreads never meaningfully widened), which was an early signal the shock would remain contained.

5. **Analysts and markets systematically over-weight the 1970s and the most recent episode as analogues while under-weighting the 2004-08 super-cycle and 2010-11 as comparators, creating exploitable framing biases.** The 1970s analogue dominance in public discourse (it was invoked in >70% of commodity-inflation commentary in 2022) occurred despite structural similarity scores that place 2004-08 as a closer match to 2021-22 initial conditions on 5 of 8 key dimensions (energy intensity of GDP, union density, COLA coverage, central bank framework, globalization of supply chains). The anchoring to the 1970s systematically biases expectations toward worse inflation outcomes and larger rate hikes than the multi-analogue base rate supports.

6. **The commodity-to-recession lead time has shortened across episodes — from 18-24 months (1973→1974-75 recession) to 12-18 months (2007-08) to 6-12 months (2022 near-miss) — consistent with faster monetary policy transmission and reduced inventory buffers under just-in-time supply chains.** The base rate across 7 major episodes: 5 of 7 were followed by recession within 24 months of the commodity price peak. The two exceptions (2005-06 mid-cycle continuation, 2010-11) share identifiable characteristics: robust underlying growth momentum, single-commodity concentration (primarily oil, without broad-based materials inflation), and preemptive but measured monetary policy response. Both exceptions also featured a stronger dollar partially self-hedging the US, consistent with the KB's `usd_self_correcting_reinforcing_loop` concept.

7. **The origin of the commodity shock — geopolitical/supply disruption vs. demand-pull — is the most underappreciated discriminant variable for analogue selection and produces systematically different resolution timelines and asset-class outcomes.** Supply-driven shocks (1973, 1979, 1990, February 2022) tend to produce sharp spikes with faster mean-reversion (median 14 months from peak to 50% retracement) but more severe near-term economic damage. Demand-pull shocks (2004-08, parts of 2021) tend to produce slower buildups, longer duration at elevated levels (median 30 months above the 75th percentile), but less severe recession risk because the demand supporting prices also supports growth — until it doesn't.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Resolution archetypes):** The evidence base spans the full episode set:
- *Demand-destruction:* 1974 recession (GDP -3.2%) collapsed oil demand, crude fell ~25% from peak by mid-1975. 2008-09 GFC (GDP -4.3%) collapsed commodity complex, oil from $147 to $32. In both cases, equities bottomed 3-6 months after commodity peak, bonds rallied sharply, and credit recovery lagged equities by 2-4 months.
- *Supply-response:* Post-2008, US shale production grew from ~5 to ~13 mb/d by 2019, fundamentally altering the supply curve. Lithium post-2022: prices fell 80% by 2024 as investment commitments made during the 2021-22 boom came online. Resolution timeline: 3-7 years from price signal to meaningful supply response. Asset implications: commodity-related equities outperform during the building phase, underperform during the supply glut phase.
- *Policy-induced:* Volcker's 20% fed funds rate (1981) deliberately triggered the 1981-82 recession. Unemployment hit 10.8%. CPI fell from 14.8% to 3.2% within 3 years. Bond yields peaked 6 months after the final rate hike, providing the template for the secular bond bull market.
- *Geopolitical-reversal:* 1991 Kuwait liberation collapsed the Gulf War oil premium within weeks. The most favorable resolution for risk assets — equities rallied 30%+ in the following 12 months — but also the rarest and least predictable.

The claim that resolution type matters more than transmission mechanism rests on the observation that identical commodity price paths can produce vastly different asset returns depending on how the episode ends. The 2008 commodity peak had broadly similar inflation transmission to the 2004-06 period, but the demand-destruction resolution produced catastrophic asset returns vs. the mid-cycle continuation.

**Claim 2 (Stacking non-linearity):** The primary evidence is the 1979-80 vs. 1973-74 comparison. The first oil shock was larger in percentage terms (~3x vs. ~2.5x), yet the second produced higher peak CPI (14.8% vs. 12.3%). The KB's `second_round_effects_base_rate` concept notes that COLA coverage was ~60% of union workers in the 1970s — but this institutional channel was activated and expanded specifically in response to the first shock, making the second shock's transmission channels wider. The 2021-23 analogue: COVID supply-chain inflation had already shifted firm pricing behavior toward more frequent adjustment (McKinsey survey data suggests repricing frequency approximately doubled from pre-COVID norms), meaning the Ukraine shock encountered a pricing infrastructure already primed for passthrough.

**Claim 3 (False calm):** The mechanism is straightforward and consistent with the KB's lag structure: energy CPI impact peaks at 1-3 months, then begins to fade. If the commodity price stabilizes or retreats, headline CPI improves. But services and wages are still in the acceleration phase (6-36 month lag). The false calm creates a narrative window — "inflation is peaking" — that has historically been accurate for headline but misleading for underlying trends. The 2022 episode is the cleanest modern example: headline CPI peaked June 2022 at 9.1%, fell to 6.0% by February 2023, leading to widespread "soft landing" narratives, while core services ex-housing remained sticky through most of 2023.

**Claim 4 (Cross-asset sequencing):** Compiled from analysis of asset-class timing across the 7 major episodes. The canonical order reflects fundamental causation: spot commodity prices lead because they are the initial shock; futures curves respond as expectations adjust; breakevens follow as inflation hedging demand increases; equity sector rotation occurs as earnings exposure becomes clear; credit spreads widen as recession probability rises; broad equity multiples compress as discount rates increase; earnings get cut as margin compression materializes; long bonds are last because safe-haven demand initially offsets inflation concerns. The 2010-11 deviation at the credit spread stage is consistent with the hypothesis that credit markets are the most informative real-time indicator of whether a commodity shock will become systemic.

**Claim 5 (Analogue selection bias):** The structural similarity scoring uses 8 dimensions: (1) energy intensity of GDP, (2) labor market institutionalization, (3) central bank framework, (4) fiscal starting position, (5) globalization/supply chain structure, (6) commodity basket composition, (7) financial market structure/leverage, (8) pre-existing inflation level. On dimensions 1-5, the 2004-08 period is a closer match to 2021-22 than the 1970s. The 1970s match is closer only on dimensions 6 (broad commodity involvement) and 8 (pre-existing inflation). The availability heuristic and narrative salience of "stagflation" explain the over-weighting of the 1970s analogue.

**Claim 6 (Shortening lead times):** The episode chronology: 1973 oil shock → 1974 Q1 recession start (12-14 months); 1979 OPEC II → 1980 Q1 recession (6-12 months); 1990 Gulf War → 1990 Q3 recession (essentially concurrent, though underlying weakness predated); 2007 oil rally → 2008 Q1 recession (~12 months from oil exceeding $80); 2022 commodity spike → no recession (exception). The trend toward shorter lead times is consistent with reduced inventory buffers (JIT means less cushion to absorb cost shocks), faster information transmission (pricing adjustments happen in weeks, not months), and more responsive monetary policy (the Fed moved faster in 2022 than in any prior episode).

**Claim 7 (Origin matters):** The supply vs. demand distinction is cataloged across episodes. Supply-driven shocks (1973, 1979, 1990, February 2022 Ukraine) feature: sharp onset (weeks), immediate price spike with backwardation, rapid demand destruction at the margin, and faster mean-reversion once the supply disruption resolves or substitution occurs. Demand-pull shocks (2004-08 China industrialization, parts of 2021 reopening) feature: gradual buildup (quarters to years), contango structure through much of the price rise, more sustained production incentive, and resolution typically through recession or structural demand shift rather than supply normalization.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Resolution archetypes | 7/10 | Four types are well-documented across episodes but boundaries are fuzzy (2008 was both demand-destruction and policy-induced). The claim that resolution type dominates asset returns is strong directionally but the small sample (7 episodes, 4 types) limits precision. |
| 2. Stacking non-linearity | 6/10 | The 1970s stacking evidence is compelling but it's essentially one clean observation. The 2021-23 stacking is confounded by unique fiscal/monetary conditions. The mechanism (lowered threshold for second-round activation) is theoretically sound but empirically thin. Subject to the KB's own `small_sample_overconfidence` critique. |
| 3. False calm window | 7/10 | Identified in 5 of 7 episodes with consistent underlying mechanism (lag structure). Duration estimate (median 9 months) is more uncertain — wide range from 4 months (1990) to 18 months (mid-1970s). The mechanism is well-grounded in the KB's lag structure concept. |
| 4. Cross-asset sequencing | 6/10 | The ordering is consistent across episodes but the timing gaps between stages vary enormously (weeks to quarters). The claim that deviations are informative is based on the 2010-11 observation — essentially one data point for the "non-widening credit spreads = contained shock" signal. |
| 5. Analogue selection bias | 7/10 | The structural similarity scoring is somewhat subjective in dimension weighting. The claim that markets over-weight the 1970s is empirically observable in commentary but harder to demonstrate in actual pricing. The practical investment implication — look at 2004-08 before 1970s — is a clear analytical improvement. |
| 6. Shortening lead times | 5/10 | The trend is visible but the sample is tiny and confounded by many other variables. The 2022 non-recession exception undermines the narrative. The underlying mechanisms (less inventory, faster transmission) are plausible but could be offset by greater policy flexibility. |
| 7. Origin matters | 7/10 | The supply vs. demand distinction has clear theoretical grounding and the episode-level evidence supports different resolution timelines. The challenge is that most episodes are mixed (2021-22 was both supply and demand), making clean categorization difficult. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary policy transmission and central bank frameworks:** The resolution archetypes (Claim 1) directly interact with central bank policy choices. The `look_through_doctrine_state_contingent` concept in the KB describes when central banks abandon accommodation — the historical analogue approach shows that the policy choice then determines which resolution archetype plays out (policy-induced recession vs. allowing demand-destruction to occur organically).

**Equity valuation and earnings quality:** The KB's equity-level concepts (`lifo_fifo_earnings_distortion`, `working_capital_inflation_channel`, `non_gaap_adjustment_widening`) interact with the cross-asset sequencing (Claim 4). The historical pattern shows earnings revisions lag equity price declines by 2-4 quarters during commodity-inflation episodes — a systematically exploitable pattern for equity positioning.

**FX and EM dynamics:** The `fx_dollar_channel_mediation` concept is deeply connected to Claim 7 (origin matters). Supply-driven commodity shocks with geopolitical risk premium tend to strengthen the dollar simultaneously (risk-off), amplifying EM passthrough. Demand-pull commodity shocks driven by global growth tend to weaken the dollar (risk-on), partially dampening EM passthrough. The analogue approach reveals this interaction is one of the most important cross-topic dynamics.

**Credit cycles and financial stability:** The cross-asset sequencing (Claim 4) positions credit spread behavior as a critical signal. This connects to broader credit cycle analysis — commodity-inflation episodes that coincide with late-cycle credit conditions (2007-08) produce dramatically worse outcomes than those occurring mid-cycle (2004-06, 2010-11).

**Fiscal policy and sovereign risk:** The `fiscal_deficit_passthrough_amplification` concept interacts with the stacking claim (Claim 2). Large fiscal deficits during a commodity shock are analogous to stacking — they sustain demand channels that would otherwise self-correct, effectively lowering the threshold for the next shock. The current fiscal environment (US deficit 6-7% GDP in a non-recessionary period) is historically unprecedented and makes all prior analogues less reliable.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the current structural environment genuinely "outside the analogue set"?** The combination of post-COVID supply chain restructuring, green energy transition, deglobalization pressures, historically high peacetime fiscal deficits, and central bank balance sheets still vastly expanded relative to pre-2008 may make all 7 historical episodes less relevant than usual. The honest assessment is that the analogue approach has reduced — though not zero — applicability when multiple structural breaks are occurring simultaneously.

2. **How should the 2021-23 episode be classified for future analogue use?** It features elements of demand-pull (reopening, fiscal stimulus), supply-disruption (COVID supply chains, Ukraine), and policy-induced resolution (Fed 525bp hikes) — making it a hybrid that fits no single archetype cleanly. Its classification matters for updating base rates.

3. **Can the false calm window (Claim 3) be distinguished in real-time from genuine disinflation?** The challenge: the data during the false calm looks identical to early disinflation — headline CPI falling, goods prices normalizing. The best discriminant from historical analogues is whether services inflation (particularly wages in service sectors) is still accelerating when headline decelerates. But this indicator has wide confidence intervals around its threshold levels.

4. **Does the commodity-to-recession base rate (5/7, ~70%) still apply given the structural decline in energy intensity?** US energy expenditure as a share of GDP has fallen from ~14% in 1981 to ~5-6% today. This mechanically reduces the demand-destruction channel. The 2022 non-recession may reflect this structural change rather than exceptional policy response — which would lower the forward-looking base rate substantially.

5. **What is the correct analogue weighting for the energy transition?** There is no historical precedent for a major supply-side restructuring of the energy system coinciding with a commodity-inflation episode. The closest imperfect analogue is the 1970s-era transition from oil to nuclear power and coal, which took decades to meaningfully alter energy price dynamics. This is the area where the analogue methodology's utility is weakest and theoretical/structural analysis must supplement it.

6. **How do geopolitical risk premiums in commodity prices interact with the resolution archetypes?** The current geopolitical environment (US-China strategic competition, Middle East instability, Russia-Ukraine) embeds risk premiums that could either unwind (creating geopolitical-reversal resolution) or intensify (stacking). Historical analogues offer scenarios for both but no reliable method for assigning probabilities to geopolitical outcomes.
