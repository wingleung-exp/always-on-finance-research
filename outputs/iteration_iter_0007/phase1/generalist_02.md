### Claim 7: Japan's Demographic Bid as Cautionary Analogue

Japan's life insurance companies (Nippon Life, Dai-ichi, etc.) maintained massive demand for JGBs and corporate credit through the 1990s, structurally compressing spreads below levels justified by fundamentals. This is the closest historical analogue to the current "demographic bid" — aging pension and insurance assets creating persistent demand for fixed-income yield.

The timeline: Japan's life insurers maintained their bid through the 1997 Asian crisis, the 1998 LTCM crisis, and the 2001-02 global recession. Spreads remained compressed. Then in 2003, Resona Bank — a mid-tier institution — required a government bailout. The shock was modest in absolute terms but destroyed confidence in the spread compression thesis. Japanese credit spreads widened 100-200bp in weeks as the "structural bid" thesis evaporated. The lesson: structural demand creates resilience to small shocks but not to the shock that invalidates the premise of the structural demand itself.

**Application to current US environment**: The demographic bid from US pension funds, insurance companies, and HNW retail investors ($200B+ in semi-liquid vehicles) is sustaining credit spread compression similar to Japan's life insurer bid. The vulnerability is the same: a single event that causes this cohort to question the safety of their allocation — a large private credit fund gating redemptions, a series of unexpected defaults in investment-grade-rated private credit — could trigger a coordinated withdrawal that overwhelms the underlying illiquidity.
