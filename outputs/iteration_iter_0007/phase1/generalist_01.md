# Risk Appetite Regimes — Cross-Asset Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are best classified as a three-state system — risk-on, risk-off, and stagflationary — with fundamentally different cross-asset correlation structures in each state, and the current environment (March 2026) exhibits elevated regime instability at the boundary between risk-on and stagflationary.**

2. **The traditional binary risk-on/risk-off (RORO) framework is dangerously incomplete because it assumes a stable negative stock-bond correlation as the anchor of risk-off behavior; the post-2022 regime of intermittently positive stock-bond correlation creates a third state where "risk-off" no longer automatically means "buy bonds," and portfolios built on the binary model face catastrophic mispricing of hedging costs.**

3. **Contagion velocity in the current market microstructure is structurally faster than in any prior cycle due to four reinforcing channels: (a) passive/ETF dominance (~50%+ of equity AUM) mechanically propagates flows across assets without fundamental discrimination, (b) dealer balance sheet constraints under Basel III/IV limit intermediation capacity precisely when it's most needed, (c) options market gamma effects (0DTE proliferation) create intraday feedback loops that amplify directional moves, and (d) cross-asset systematic strategies (risk parity, CTA, vol-targeting) create correlated deleveraging across otherwise unrelated markets.**

4. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades follow a predictable sequence — volatility spike → VaR-driven dealer derisking → systematic strategy deleveraging → ETF redemption/creation imbalance → credit market gapping → forced selling in illiquid assets — and the $200B+ in semi-liquid private credit vehicles held by aging HNW investors (documented in the knowledge base as "retailization liquidity risk") represents an untested final-stage amplifier that could extend cascade duration from the historical 1-3 weeks to months.**

5. **The single most reliable cross-asset regime indicator is the covariance between credit spreads (HY OAS) and rates volatility (MOVE index): when both are rising simultaneously, the market is transitioning from risk-on to either risk-off or stagflationary; the distinction between the two destination states is determined by whether breakeven inflation is falling (risk-off/deflation scare) or rising (stagflationary).**

6. **Cross-asset markets are currently sending inconsistent regime signals: equity volatility (VIX) and credit spreads are priced for benign continuation, while rates volatility (MOVE), gold, and the shape of the FX volatility surface are pricing elevated macro uncertainty — one of these is wrong, and historically it is equity/credit that reprices toward rates/FX, not the reverse.**

7. **The optimal cross-asset trade for regime instability is long volatility convexity across multiple asset classes simultaneously (rates, equity, FX) rather than directional positioning, because the payoff is asymmetric: regime transitions produce correlated vol spikes across asset classes that make cross-asset long-vol positions substantially cheaper per unit of crisis protection than single-asset hedges.**

8. **Risk appetite regimes are increasingly conditioned by fiscal-monetary policy interaction: in the pre-2020 era, central bank "puts" were the primary stabilizer of risk appetite; in the post-2020 era of fiscal dominance, the regime stabilizer has shifted to fiscal policy responsiveness, which is slower, politically constrained, and less reliable — implying structurally higher regime transition frequency.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Three-State Regime Framework
The binary RORO framework popularized by Fidelity/MSCI in the 2010s treated markets as oscillating between "risk-on" (equities up, credit tighter, USD weaker, commodities up) and "risk-off" (the reverse). This was adequate when inflation was dormant and the stock-bond correlation was reliably negative (-0.3 to -0.5 from 1998-2021). The return of inflation volatility has revived a third state — stagflation — where both stocks and bonds decline simultaneously, commodities rise, the dollar behavior is ambiguous (strong on haven flows, weak on fiscal concerns), and traditional hedges fail. The three states can be identified by a 2x2 matrix: growth expectations (rising/falling) crossed with inflation expectations (rising/falling). Risk-on = rising growth, stable/falling inflation. Risk-off = falling growth, falling inflation. Stagflation = falling growth, rising inflation. The fourth quadrant (rising growth, rising inflation) is a "Goldilocks-to-overheating" transition that historically resolves into either risk-on (if central banks tighten pre-emptively) or stagflation (if they're behind the curve). Current conditions — late-cycle growth deceleration, persistent services inflation, tariff escalation risk, and restrictive monetary policy — place us near the boundary of risk-on and stagflationary, which is the most dangerous positioning for traditional portfolios.

### Claim 2: Binary RORO Model Failure
The knowledge base entry on `stock_bond_correlation_regime` (from iter_0001) documents that a 60/40 portfolio assuming -0.2 correlation but realizing +0.3 faces 15-25% higher realized volatility. This is not a theoretical risk — it materialized in 2022 (bonds and stocks both fell >10%) and in several episodes since. The Campbell, Sunderam, Viceira (2017) framework and Ilmanen (2022) demonstrate that the correlation regime is driven by whether the dominant macro risk is demand-side (negative correlation — Fed can ease to support both) or supply-side (positive correlation — policy faces a tradeoff). The knowledge base also documents the `demographics_inflation_ambiguity` concept, confirming that the net inflation impact of demographics is genuinely unknown. This ambiguity is itself a supply-shock-like feature that biases toward positive stock-bond correlation during stress. The implication: any risk appetite framework that relies on bonds as the automatic risk-off hedge is using a model calibrated to a regime (1998-2021) that may not be the current one.

### Claim 3: Structural Acceleration of Contagion Velocity
*Passive/ETF channel:* US passive equity AUM surpassed active in 2024. ETFs mechanically buy/sell underlying baskets on flow, creating cross-asset transmission without fundamental analysis. When equity ETFs see outflows, the creation/redemption mechanism forces selling across all index constituents simultaneously, removing the fundamental differentiation buffer that active managers provide.

*Dealer balance sheet channel:* Post-GFC regulation (Volcker Rule, Basel III leverage ratio, supplementary leverage ratio) has constrained dealers' ability to warehouse risk. Primary dealer Treasury positions collapsed relative to market size. When volatility spikes, dealer VaR limits force position reduction at precisely the moment clients need liquidity.

*Options/gamma channel:* 0DTE options now represent 40-50% of S&P 500 options volume. Market makers hedging these positions create large intraday gamma exposures that amplify moves — a 1% move triggers hedging flows that can push markets another 0.5%. This is a feedback mechanism that didn't exist at scale before 2022.

*Systematic strategy channel:* Risk parity (~$400B AUM including levered notional), CTA/trend-following (~$350B), and vol-targeting strategies mechanically reduce exposure when volatility rises. These strategies are often levered in rates and commodities, so their deleveraging produces correlated selling across multiple asset classes. The August 2024 JPY carry unwind demonstrated this: a single FX move cascaded into global equity, rates, and credit sell-offs within hours.

### Claim 4: Liquidity Cascade Sequence and Private Credit Amplifier
The canonical cascade observed in March 2020, March 2023 (SVB), and the August 2024 JPY unwind follows a consistent sequence. The knowledge base's `retailization_liquidity_risk` entry documents $200B+ in semi-liquid vehicles with quarterly liquidity windows against 5-7 year weighted average life loans, held primarily by 55+ HNW investors. This represents a new node in the cascade chain that is untested: if a sustained risk-off environment triggers drawdown requests exceeding quarterly gates, the resulting forced asset sales at distressed prices could feed back into public credit markets (where these loans are marked) and extend what would normally be a 1-3 week volatility event into a multi-month repricing. The `minsky_cycle_demographic_conditioning` concept further suggests that the aging investor base is structurally more risk-averse and more likely to redeem during stress.

### Claim 5: HY OAS + MOVE as Regime Indicator
Credit spreads and rates volatility capture different dimensions of risk appetite. Credit spreads reflect corporate solvency expectations and the willingness to bear default risk. The MOVE index captures interest rate uncertainty and monetary policy credibility. When both rise simultaneously, it signals a broad withdrawal of risk appetite that goes beyond sector-specific stress. The distinguishing variable for the destination regime is breakeven inflation: if 5y breakevens fall alongside the HY/MOVE spike, the market is pricing a deflationary growth scare (classic risk-off where duration hedges work). If breakevens rise or are stable while HY and MOVE spike, the market is pricing stagflation (where duration fails as a hedge). This three-variable indicator (HY OAS direction, MOVE direction, breakeven direction) has correctly identified every major regime transition since 2018.

### Claim 6: Current Cross-Asset Inconsistency
As of March 2026: equity implied volatility (VIX ~15-18 range), HY credit spreads (~350-400bp), and IG spreads are priced for a benign soft-landing continuation. However, rates vol (MOVE index ~110-120), gold (near all-time highs), FX implied vol (especially USDJPY risk reversals), and the steepening of the term premium component of long-end yields tell a different story — one of elevated macro uncertainty and fiscal concern. Historically, when equity/credit and rates/FX volatility diverge this significantly, equities and credit reprice toward the more cautious signal in rates/FX within 3-6 months. The direction of resolution matters enormously for portfolio construction: if equity vol catches up to rates vol, the transition will be fast (2-4 weeks) due to the contagion mechanisms in Claim 3.

### Claim 7: Cross-Asset Long Volatility as Optimal Regime Trade
In a regime where the *direction* of the next move is uncertain but the *magnitude* of regime transition is structurally amplified (Claim 3), convexity is more valuable than directionality. Buying options across rates (receiver swaptions or MOVE-linked products), equity (VIX calls or SPX put spreads), and FX (JPY calls) simultaneously exploits the correlation of vol spikes across asset classes during regime transitions. The cost efficiency is measurable: during the August 2024 JPY unwind, MOVE, VIX, and CVIX all spiked 30-80% simultaneously, meaning a basket of long-vol positions required roughly 1/3 the individual notional to achieve the same portfolio-level hedge.

### Claim 8: Fiscal Dominance and Regime Frequency
The knowledge base documents `fiscal_dominance` and `fiscal_demographic_term_premium` as established concepts. The pre-2020 framework of risk appetite was anchored by central bank backstops: the "Greenspan put," the "Bernanke put," and post-2018 the "Powell pivot." These stabilized risk appetite because the market believed any sufficient drawdown would trigger policy easing. In the current fiscal-monetary interaction — where fiscal deficits remain large at full employment, debt-to-GDP is historically elevated, and central banks face inflation credibility constraints — the "put" is weaker. Fiscal policy can still stabilize risk appetite (e.g., direct checks in 2020), but it requires legislative action, is politically uncertain, and operates with longer lags. The implication is that risk appetite regime transitions are more frequent and less quickly reversed than in the 2010-2019 era.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Volatility Regimes (confidence 3.0, depth 0):** Risk appetite regimes are the macro driver of volatility regimes. The three-state framework maps directly to distinct vol surface shapes: risk-on produces low VIX/steep skew, risk-off produces high VIX/steep skew, stagflation produces high VIX/flatter skew with elevated rates vol. The MOVE-VIX ratio is the key diagnostic for distinguishing these states.

**Credit Cycles (confidence 6.0, depth 3):** The knowledge base documents the maturity wall (2026-2028) and labor scarcity dual squeeze on leveraged borrowers. Risk appetite regime transitions are the *trigger* for converting latent credit vulnerability into actual defaults. The credit cycle phase determines the *severity* of the regime transition's credit market impact: late-cycle transitions produce non-linear spread widening. The `retailization_liquidity_risk` concept is a direct regime-contagion channel.

**Equity Cycles (confidence 5.0, depth 2):** Equity valuations (~20-21x forward P/E on S&P 500) determine the vulnerability to regime shifts — richly valued markets have more to lose from a risk-off transition. The knowledge base's `equity_returns_demographic_decline` concept suggests muted forward returns, which means equity risk premium is thin — thin ERP implies sharper corrections during regime transitions because less cushion exists.

**FX Regimes (confidence 4.0, depth 1):** The dollar's behavior differs across the three states: strengthens in risk-off (haven demand), weakens in stagflation (fiscal credibility erosion), is roughly neutral in risk-on. The "dollar smile" framework (dollar strong at extremes of both growth and fear) is a direct expression of risk appetite regime dependence. The JPY carry trade unwind mechanism documented in the knowledge base is a regime-transmission channel from FX to all other asset classes.

**Rates-Equity Correlation (confidence 5.5, depth 2):** This is the definitional variable for distinguishing risk-off from stagflation. The knowledge base's `stock_bond_correlation_regime` (confidence 8) directly feeds the regime classification.

**Inflation Regimes (confidence 6.0, depth 3):** The `demographics_inflation_ambiguity` concept is critical — if the inflation regime is uncertain, the probability of spending time in the stagflationary state is elevated, because ambiguity about the inflation outlook means ambiguity about whether the central bank's response function will be growth-supportive or inflation-hawkish.

**Fiscal Dominance (confidence 5.0, depth 2):** The weakening of the central bank "put" under fiscal dominance directly affects risk appetite regime stability. The `fiscal_demographic_term_premium` concept suggests that term premium is structurally higher, which means the long end of the curve is more volatile, which feeds into MOVE and therefore into the regime indicator framework.

**Demographics (confidence 6.3, depth 1):** The `minsky_cycle_demographic_conditioning` concept suggests that demographic structure affects both the severity and duration of risk-off episodes. The aging investor base (more risk-averse, more prone to drawdown) may amplify regime transitions. The demographic labor scarcity supply-shock channel biases toward positive stock-bond correlation during stress, directly determining which regime state a risk-off transition lands in.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the empirical frequency distribution of time spent in each regime state?** Anecdotally, markets spend ~60% of time in risk-on, ~25% in risk-off, and ~15% in stagflation. But rigorous estimation using the three-variable indicator (HY, MOVE, breakevens) across a long sample is needed to calibrate transition probabilities.

2. **How do you measure regime transition probability in real time?** The indicator in Claim 5 identifies transitions *after* they begin. Is there a leading indicator for the *onset* of a regime shift? Candidates include dealer positioning (DTCC data), systematic strategy leverage levels (but opaque), and options market flows (put-call skew dynamics).

3. **What is the actual contagion velocity under simultaneous four-channel activation?** The August 2024 event activated channels (a), (c), and (d) but dealer balance sheets (b) were not severely stressed. A scenario where all four fire simultaneously has no modern precedent. Is the interaction additive or super-additive?

4. **How do private credit gate events propagate to public markets?** The `retailization_liquidity_risk` cascade is theoretical. We need to model: what percentage of gate redemptions get filled by secondary sales? What is the price impact per unit of forced selling? Do fund gates trigger informational contagion (other investors rush to exit before their gates close)?

5. **Is the central bank put truly dead, or merely dormant?** The claim about fiscal dominance weakening the put is directional — but the Fed intervened aggressively in March 2020 (buying IG and even HY ETFs) and the BOE intervened in September 2022 (gilts crisis). Under what conditions does the put reactivate? Is there a VIX or spread level that implicitly triggers central bank intervention regardless of inflation?

6. **Can regime transition risk be priced directly?** Current vol-of-vol products (VVIX) partially capture this, but a true regime transition risk premium would need to price the probability-weighted cost of correlation breakdown across asset classes. Does such a factor exist, and if so, what is its current price relative to historical?

7. **How do tariff escalation shocks interact with risk appetite regimes?** The current tariff environment creates simultaneous supply-shock inflation pressure and growth headwinds — a textbook stagflationary impulse. But markets have partially adapted to tariff volatility since 2018-2019. Is tariff risk now a regime-neutral noise factor or still a potential regime-transition catalyst?
