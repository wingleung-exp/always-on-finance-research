# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are best understood as equilibrium states of a three-variable system: the output gap, the policy rate gap (r minus r\*), and the term structure of inflation expectations.** No single variable suffices. The output gap captures the real-economy fundamentals supporting earnings and credit quality; the policy rate gap captures the degree of monetary accommodation fueling leverage and search-for-yield; and the term structure of inflation expectations (short-run vs. long-run) identifies whether the prevailing shock is demand-side (symmetric movement) or supply-side (divergent movement), which determines the central bank's likely reaction function and hence the stability of the current regime.

2. **Regime transitions are triggered when central bank reaction functions cross thresholds in a Taylor Rule framework — specifically, when the implied Taylor Rule rate diverges from the actual policy rate by more than ~100-150bp in either direction for more than two quarters.** When the Taylor Rule prescribes significantly higher rates than prevailing policy ("behind the curve"), the resulting excess accommodation inflates risk appetite beyond what fundamentals justify, building fragility. When the Taylor Rule prescribes significantly lower rates ("over-tightened"), the excess restriction erodes the cash-flow foundations of risk-on positions. The 2021-2022 transition illustrates the first case; the late-2018 episode illustrates the second.

3. **Contagion velocity in risk-off transitions is a function of the monetary policy regime's credibility, measurable through the anchoring of long-run inflation expectations.** When expectations are well-anchored (5y5y breakevens within 25bp of target), central banks retain the option to cut rates aggressively in response to financial stress without unmooring expectations — the "put" is credible and contagion is truncated. When anchoring weakens (as in 2022), central banks face a Phillips Curve trade-off that constrains their ability to backstop markets, and contagion runs further because the circuit-breaker is disabled.

4. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades are fundamentally a coordination failure analyzable through New Keynesian models with strategic complementarities in funding markets.** Individual agents' decisions to withdraw liquidity are optimal given their expectation that others will do the same (Diamond-Dybvig logic extended to wholesale funding). The central bank's role as lender of last resort is precisely the public provision of the coordination mechanism that the private market fails to supply — this is why standing facilities and swap lines are not ad hoc interventions but structural solutions to a well-defined market failure.

5. **The current risk appetite regime (Q1 2026) is best characterized as a "conditional risk-on" — risk-on sustained by the expectation of continued monetary easing, but conditional on inflation continuing to converge toward target.** The conditionality is the distinguishing feature. Unlike the unconditional risk-on of 2020-2021 (when the Fed committed to average inflation targeting and pinned rates at zero regardless of data) or the unconditional risk-off of H2 2022 (when the Fed was deterministically tightening), the current regime is data-dependent in a meaningful sense: a single quarter of re-accelerating core inflation would invalidate the easing narrative and trigger a regime shift.

6. **Cross-asset contagion patterns differ systematically depending on whether the risk-off trigger is a demand shock, a supply shock, or a financial friction shock, and failure to classify the shock type leads to incorrect hedging.** Demand shocks (recession fears) produce positive stock-bond correlation and flight-to-quality into duration — the traditional 60/40 hedge works. Supply shocks (inflation surges) produce negative stock-bond correlation — duration amplifies equity losses rather than hedging them. Financial friction shocks (liquidity cascades, margin spirals) initially produce positive stock-bond correlation (everything sells) before the central bank intervention re-establishes negative correlation — the timing of central bank response determines whether the 60/40 portfolio survives.

7. **The fiscal impulse has become the dominant swing variable for risk appetite regime durability in the current cycle, operating through the IS curve channel rather than the traditional monetary transmission mechanism.** With US structural deficits running ~6% of GDP near full employment, the fiscal stance is substituting for monetary accommodation in supporting aggregate demand and therefore corporate earnings. The risk appetite regime is increasingly "fiscal-dependent" rather than "monetary-dependent," which introduces a new class of political risk (fiscal consolidation, debt ceiling episodes, tariff shocks) into what was traditionally a central-bank-watching exercise.

8. **The natural rate of interest (r\*) is itself regime-dependent, creating a reflexivity problem for risk appetite assessment: accommodative financial conditions raise r\* by boosting investment and potential growth, while restrictive conditions depress r\* — meaning the central bank is partly chasing a moving target.** This is the "hysteresis in r\*" hypothesis, supported by the observation that r\* estimates have risen modestly since 2023 as AI-related capital expenditure has increased. The practical implication is that the neutral rate is higher in risk-on regimes and lower in risk-off regimes, producing self-reinforcing dynamics in both directions.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 — Three-Variable System

The inadequacy of single-variable regime identification is demonstrated by numerous counterexamples. The output gap alone fails because risk appetite can remain elevated with a negative output gap if monetary accommodation is extreme (2009-2010 equities rallied 65% despite deeply negative output gaps). The policy rate gap alone fails because accommodation can persist without generating risk-on if output gaps are sufficiently negative and credit demand is weak (Japan, 1999-2012). The inflation expectations term structure alone fails as a regime identifier in isolation but is essential for classifying the shock type and therefore predicting the policy response. Woodford (2003, *Interest and Prices*) provides the theoretical backbone: in the canonical New Keynesian three-equation system (IS curve, Phillips Curve, Taylor Rule), the equilibrium in asset markets depends on the joint state of all three equations, not any one in isolation. Adrian, Boyarchenko, and Giannone (2019, "Vulnerable Growth") provide the empirical framework showing that financial conditions — which proxy for risk appetite — predict the entire distribution of future GDP growth, not just the mean, confirming the regime-dependence of the output-risk nexus.

### Claim 2 — Taylor Rule Divergence as Trigger

The Taylor Rule prescribes: r = r\* + π + 0.5(π − π\*) + 0.5(y − y\*). When the actual policy rate deviates substantially from this prescription, financial conditions will eventually force a correction — either the central bank adjusts, or market-generated tightening/easing substitutes. In early 2022, a standard Taylor Rule with r\* = 0.5%, π = 5.5% (core PCE), π\* = 2%, and y − y\* ≈ +1% prescribed a rate of approximately 6.5-7.0%, while the actual FFR was 0.0-0.25% — a gap of ~650bp. This extraordinary divergence corresponded to the peak of the post-pandemic risk-on regime and the maximum overstretch of equity valuations, credit spread compression, and speculative leverage. Conversely, by late 2018, the Taylor Rule with the then-prevailing inputs (core PCE ~2%, output gap closing, r\* ~0.5%) prescribed approximately 2.5-3.0%, while the FFR was 2.25-2.50% — close to but arguably slightly above Taylor. The small overshoot, combined with QT draining reserves, was sufficient to trigger the Q4 2018 risk-off episode, illustrating the asymmetric sensitivity: small overshoots toward restriction trigger risk-off faster than equivalent undershoots because of the nonlinearity of deleveraging dynamics.

### Claim 3 — Credibility and Contagion Velocity

The mechanism operates through the expectations channel of the New Keynesian Phillips Curve: π = βE[π_{t+1}] + κ(y − y\*). When E[π_{t+1}] is anchored, the central bank can cut rates in response to a negative demand shock or financial stress without fearing a de-anchoring of expectations — the real rate falls, the IS curve shifts, output stabilizes, and the risk-off event is truncated. This is precisely what occurred in March 2020: the Fed cut 150bp in two weeks and launched unlimited QE, and long-run breakevens barely flinched (5y5y stayed within 1.5-2.0%), confirming anchoring. Contagion from rates to credit to equities was truncated within approximately 3 weeks. In contrast, during the 2022 risk-off, the Fed could not cut despite equity drawdowns of 25%+ because inflation expectations had partially de-anchored (5y5y breakevens hit 2.6%, Michigan 5-10y expectations rose to 3.1%). The circuit-breaker was disabled, and the risk-off regime persisted for approximately 10 months. The cross-country evidence supports this: ECB credibility erosion during the 2011-2012 sovereign crisis extended the European risk-off episode far longer than the US equivalent, because the ECB faced binding inflation-credibility constraints on its response until Draghi's "whatever it takes" effectively re-anchored expectations.

### Claim 4 — Coordination Failure in Funding Markets

The wholesale funding market (repo, commercial paper, FX swaps) exhibits the classic coordination failure structure formalized by Morris and Shin (2004) in their global games framework applied to funding liquidity. Each lender's decision to roll over short-term funding to a borrower depends on their expectation of whether other lenders will do the same. If expectations coordinate on "roll," the borrower is solvent and the lender's decision is validated. If expectations coordinate on "withdraw," the borrower faces a liquidity crisis that may cause insolvency, validating the withdrawal. The New Keynesian insight is that this coordination failure is not inherent to the structure of private markets — it is a market failure amenable to policy intervention. The lender of last resort provides the outside option that shifts the equilibrium from "withdraw" to "roll" by guaranteeing that funding will be available even if other private lenders withdraw. The Fed's standing repo facility (SRF), swap lines with foreign central banks, and discount window are institutional expressions of this logic. The practical question — raised in the rates strategist's analysis — of whether the SRF suffers from stigma that limits its effectiveness is precisely the question of whether the coordination mechanism functions as designed.

### Claim 5 — Conditional Risk-On (Q1 2026)

The current regime is best defined by what would break it. As of early 2026, the Fed has likely eased 75-125bp from the 2023-2024 peak, bringing the FFR corridor to roughly 3.75-4.25%. Core PCE has likely decelerated to the 2.3-2.6% range, with the market pricing continued convergence toward 2.0% over the forecast horizon. The real FFR (~1.5%) sits near the upper bound of Laubach-Williams r\* estimates (~1.0-1.5%), implying a neutral-to-modestly-restrictive stance. Financial conditions have eased from 2022-2023 tightness but are not accommodative by historical standards. The risk-on positioning (compressed credit spreads, elevated equity multiples, low realized vol) is sustainable only if the easing cycle continues, which requires inflation to keep falling. The regime is therefore conditional: one standard deviation of upside inflation surprise (core PCE re-accelerating to 3.0%+) would force the Fed to pause or reverse course, invalidating the forward curve and triggering a repricing of risk premia across asset classes. The probability I assign to the conditional being sustained (inflation continues to converge) is approximately 55-60%, making this a moderate-confidence risk-on with a fat left tail.

### Claim 6 — Shock Classification and Hedging

The New Keynesian framework distinguishes demand shocks (shift the IS curve), supply shocks (shift the Phillips Curve), and financial shocks (shift the financial conditions/credit channel) as analytically distinct with distinct asset price implications. Blanchard (2021) and Benigno et al. (2022) emphasize that the stock-bond correlation regime depends on the dominant shock type: demand-shock-dominant environments produce negative stock-bond correlation (stocks and bonds hedge each other); supply-shock-dominant environments produce positive stock-bond correlation (they move together, destroying diversification). The post-pandemic period saw a transition from demand-dominant (2020, negative correlation) to supply-dominant (2022, positive correlation) and now potentially back toward demand-dominant or mixed (2025-2026, as supply chains have normalized but demand uncertainty persists). Empirically, the stock-bond correlation estimated from 60-day rolling windows shifted from approximately -0.3 in 2019 to +0.4 in 2022 and has oscillated between -0.1 and +0.2 since mid-2024 — consistent with a mixed-shock environment where neither demand nor supply dominates clearly.

### Claim 7 — Fiscal Impulse as Swing Variable

The fiscal impulse (change in the cyclically-adjusted primary balance) has been the dominant driver of aggregate demand dynamics since 2020, dwarfing the monetary channel. The CARES Act, ARP, IRA, and CHIPS Act injected cumulative fiscal stimulus of approximately 25% of GDP over 2020-2023, producing the demand-driven component of inflation and sustaining corporate earnings growth well beyond what monetary policy alone would have supported. Even as the Fed tightened, the fiscal stance remained expansionary — structural deficits of 5-7% of GDP at low unemployment are historically unprecedented outside wartime. This IS curve shift has raised the equilibrium interest rate, meaning that even "restrictive" monetary policy has been partially offset by fiscal accommodation. For risk appetite, the implication is that the traditional framework of watching the Fed for regime signals is insufficient: the fiscal trajectory is at least as important. The Taylor Rule must be conditioned on the fiscal stance — a point made by Leeper (1991) in the fiscal theory of the price level and operationalized by Bianchi and Melosi (2017) in regime-switching models of fiscal-monetary interaction.

### Claim 8 — Hysteresis in r\*

The standard Laubach-Williams (2003) framework treats r\* as a slow-moving function of trend growth and the discount rate, implicitly assuming exogeneity to the business cycle. But recent evidence suggests feedback effects: accommodative financial conditions boost investment (especially in technology and AI-related capex, which has run 25-30% above trend since 2023), raising potential output growth and therefore r\*. Conversely, prolonged restrictive conditions depress investment and reduce potential growth — secular stagnation dynamics (Summers, 2014). This creates a reflexivity problem: the central bank estimating r\* uses data generated under the current policy regime, but the act of easing or tightening shifts the r\* it is trying to target. Holston, Laubach, and Williams (2017) acknowledge wide confidence intervals around r\* estimates (±150bp), which are large enough to encompass the regime-dependence effect. The practical consequence for risk appetite: if r\* is higher in risk-on environments (because risk-on boosts investment and growth), then more accommodation is needed to sustain risk-on than a static r\* estimate would suggest — the central bank must "run to stand still," and the risk-on regime becomes increasingly fragile as r\* drifts up.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Volatility regimes (confidence: 3.0):** The NK framework predicts that volatility regimes are downstream of the monetary policy regime. Low-volatility regimes correspond to periods when the policy rate is near or below r\* and the Taylor Rule is accommodated — volatility is endogenously suppressed by accommodative financial conditions reducing the dispersion of outcomes. The transition to high-volatility regimes occurs when the Taylor Rule gap closes or inverts. The shock classification framework (Claim 6) adds that the *type* of volatility regime depends on the dominant shock: demand-shock vol is hedgeable with duration; supply-shock vol is not.

- **Credit cycles (confidence: 6.0):** Risk appetite regimes and credit cycles are two perspectives on the same underlying dynamic. The NK "financial accelerator" (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist, 1999) formalizes how risk appetite translates into credit conditions: rising asset prices → improved collateral values → relaxed borrowing constraints → expanded credit → further asset price appreciation. The credit impulse (emphasized by the Post-Keynesian analysis) can be interpreted within the NK framework as the change in the financial accelerator multiplier. The connection is bidirectional: credit cycle dynamics determine the amplitude of risk appetite regimes, while the monetary policy stance — the Taylor Rule gap — determines the timing of credit cycle turns.

- **Equity cycles (confidence: 5.0):** Equity valuations depend on the discount rate (r\* + equity risk premium) and expected earnings growth. Risk appetite regimes affect both: risk-on compresses the equity risk premium and elevates earnings growth expectations (via accommodative financial conditions and fiscal stimulus); risk-off reverses both. The P/E expansion of 2023-2024 is consistent with a transition from restrictive to neutral monetary policy compressing risk premia, but the continuation requires sustained easing — the conditionality of Claim 5.

- **FX regimes (confidence: 4.0):** Under Mundell-Fleming, risk appetite regime shifts propagate through capital flows and exchange rates. Dollar risk appetite is particularly important: risk-on globally tends to weaken the dollar (capital flows to higher-yielding EM and risk assets), while risk-off strengthens the dollar (safe-haven flows, unwinding of dollar-funded carry trades). The fiscal-dominance dynamic (Claim 7) adds a new channel: if US fiscal deficits undermine long-run dollar confidence while simultaneously supporting short-run risk appetite, the traditional risk-on = weak dollar relationship may be amplified by fiscal concerns in ways that create nonlinear dollar dynamics.

- **Monetary policy transmission:** This is the foundational connection. Risk appetite regimes are the asset market manifestation of monetary transmission. The "risk-taking channel" (Borio & Zhu, 2012) and the "financial conditions channel" are the pathways through which the Taylor Rule gap translates into cross-asset risk premia. The current bifurcation between the front-end (monetary-policy-driven) and back-end (fiscal-supply-driven) of the yield curve — identified by the rates strategist — complicates this transmission by introducing a maturity-dependent risk appetite signal.

- **Inflation dynamics and the Phillips Curve:** The stock-bond correlation regime (Claim 6) is directly governed by which leg of the Phillips Curve trade-off is dominant. When the output gap is the primary driver of inflation (standard demand-pull), risk appetite follows the simple business cycle narrative. When supply shocks dominate, the central bank faces an impossible trinity of stabilizing output, inflation, and financial conditions simultaneously — risk appetite becomes unstable because the policy reaction function is itself uncertain.

## Open Questions

1. **How should the Taylor Rule be modified to incorporate financial conditions directly, and would a "financial stability Taylor Rule" (adding a financial conditions index term) improve regime identification?** Curdia and Woodford (2010) explore augmented Taylor Rules but find ambiguous welfare implications. The practical question is whether a rule that responds to credit spreads or leverage ratios would have performed better at identifying the 2021 overstretch or the 2018 over-tightening.

2. **Is the current "conditional risk-on" regime more fragile than historical analogs because the conditioning variable (inflation) has fatter tails than in the pre-2020 era?** If the distribution of inflation outcomes has permanently widened — due to supply chain fragility, geopolitical commodity risk, or de-anchored expectations — then the probability of the condition breaking is structurally higher, and the risk premium currently embedded in markets is insufficient.

3. **What is the interaction between AI-driven productivity growth and risk appetite regimes?** A genuine positive supply shock (rightward shift of the Phillips Curve) would allow the central bank to ease without inflation cost, sustaining risk-on. But if the productivity gains are concentrated in a narrow sector (tech) while the broader economy faces labor displacement and adjustment costs, the aggregate Phillips Curve may not shift as favorably as the sector-specific data suggests.

4. **How does the Post-Keynesian emphasis on credit impulse relate to the NK Taylor Rule gap framework?** Are these complementary (credit impulse is the transmission mechanism through which the Taylor Rule gap affects the real economy) or competing (credit dynamics are autonomous from the policy rate)? The Minskyan critique — that endogenous credit dynamics can override the central bank's intentions — represents a genuine challenge to the NK framework's assumption that the central bank controls financial conditions through the policy rate.

5. **Can the fiscal impulse sustain a risk-on regime indefinitely in a non-Ricardian world, or is there a debt-to-GDP threshold beyond which fiscal expansion itself becomes a source of risk-off?** The New Keynesian framework assumes Ricardian equivalence as a benchmark but acknowledges its empirical failure. If agents are not Ricardian, fiscal deficits stimulate demand and support risk appetite, but at some point the stock of debt may raise sovereign credit risk premia, tightening financial conditions — the "Reinhart-Rogoff zone." Where that threshold lies for a reserve currency issuer remains deeply uncertain.

6. **How should the regime identification framework handle the possibility that r\* has risen above consensus estimates (to ~2.0% or higher)?** If r\* is 2.0% and the real FFR is 1.5%, the stance is actually accommodative despite appearing neutral — the conditional risk-on assessment would need to be upgraded to something closer to unconditional risk-on. This is not merely a measurement problem; it changes the actionable portfolio implications.
