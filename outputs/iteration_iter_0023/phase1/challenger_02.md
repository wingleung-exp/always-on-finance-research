# Inflation Regime Identification & Dynamics — Behavioral Finance Critic & Historical Falsifier Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The accumulated KB exhibits systematic anchoring to the 2021-2023 inflation episode, distorting base rate assessments for regime persistence and transition probabilities.** The repeated framing of current conditions as "razor-thin buffer" and "thinner than any point since the late 1990s" is an availability-driven narrative, not a calibrated probabilistic statement.

2. **The Coibion-Gorodnichenko de-anchoring threshold (sustained >75-100bp survey-market divergence for >6 months) is treated as a structural constant when it is an empirical estimate from a specific sample period, with limited out-of-sample validation and no established track record of real-time predictive accuracy.**

3. **The scenario probability distribution (45% mid-cycle / 30% disinflationary / 15% stagflationary / 10% re-acceleration) exhibits classic overconfidence in point estimates for a four-way bifurcation that has no meaningful base rate in the historical record.** Assigning probabilities summing precisely to 100% to four named scenarios creates an illusion of exhaustiveness and precision.

4. **Services inflation persistence is likely overweighted due to recency bias from the post-COVID period.** Historical base rates show that services inflation mean-reverts within 18-30 months of goods disinflation completing in 5 of 7 post-1960 episodes, though the lag structure is variable enough to frustrate timing.

5. **The "biofuel mandate inflation ratchet" concept, while mechanically correct, is subject to the narrative fallacy — it converts a modest structural price floor in one commodity into a sweeping claim about fiscal dominance.** Corn ethanol's contribution to headline CPI is approximately 0.05-0.12pp, insufficient to constitute a "regime" driver on its own.

6. **The wage-price spiral risk assessment in the KB is underspecified: the term "spiral" implies a self-reinforcing feedback loop, but the historical record shows that true wage-price spirals (where real wage growth exceeds productivity growth AND firms have pricing power to pass through costs AND inflation expectations ratchet upward iteratively) have only occurred twice in the US post-war period (1966-1969 and 1973-1975).** Current conditions fail at least one of the three necessary conditions.

7. **Breakeven inflation as a regime signal is contaminated by liquidity premia, institutional demand shifts (pension LDI), and inflation risk premia — treating the 1.8-2.5% "anchored band" as stable implicitly assumes these non-expectation components are stationary, which they demonstrably are not.**

8. **The Taylor Rule coefficient framework (φ\_π as "institutional lynchpin") commits the fallacy of misplaced concreteness: it treats a reduced-form parameter estimated from revealed behavior as a stable structural parameter, when in fact it is regime-dependent, asymmetric across hiking/cutting cycles, and changes with the composition of the FOMC.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1 — Anchoring to 2021-2023
The KB's evidence base is overwhelmingly drawn from the 2021-2023 inflation episode. The phrase "thinner than any point since the late 1990s" for the anchoring buffer appears in multiple concept files almost verbatim — a sign of echo-chamber reinforcement across agents rather than independent verification. The actual historical record shows that breakeven inflation has been near the upper bound of the "anchored" band multiple times (2005-2006: 5Y BEI ~2.4-2.6%; 2011-2012: ~2.2-2.5% following QE2; 2018: ~2.1-2.3%) without triggering de-anchoring. The current level is not historically unique. The emotional salience of the 2021-2023 spike (availability bias) makes current levels *feel* more precarious than they are relative to the base rate.

### Claim 2 — Coibion-Gorodnichenko Threshold
The Coibion-Gorodnichenko (2015) framework was estimated using pre-2020 data. The threshold has never been "tested" in real-time as a predictive tool — we have exactly one near-miss (2022) in the post-estimation sample. A confidence of 8.0 assigned to a concept whose core discriminating parameter has N=0 successful real-time predictions is a textbook calibration failure. The appropriate confidence for "we know where the threshold is" is closer to 4-5, reflecting genuine uncertainty about whether the parameter is stable.

### Claim 3 — Scenario Probability Overconfidence
Tetlock's superforecasting research demonstrates that four-way geopolitical/macro scenario forecasts at 12+ month horizons typically have calibration scores (Brier) of 0.3-0.4 at best, meaning forecasters are right about the dominant scenario roughly 40-50% of the time. The KB assigns 45% to the modal outcome — this is reasonable as a point estimate but the precision is spurious. More importantly, the scenarios are not mutually exclusive or collectively exhaustive: a "mild stagflation" that doesn't neatly fit the 15% stagflationary bucket is entirely plausible, and the framework provides no probability mass for surprise scenarios (geopolitical shock, financial accident, pandemic recurrence). This is the conjunction fallacy at the macro level: the four named scenarios feel more probable than "something else entirely."

### Claim 4 — Services Inflation Persistence
The post-COVID services inflation spike is driven by well-identified components: shelter/OER (lag from 2021-2022 home price appreciation, now resolving), auto insurance (catch-up from total loss cost inflation, one-time level adjustment), and healthcare (lagged Medicare reimbursement adjustments). Historical episodes of services inflation >4% that were followed by goods disinflation:
- **1991-1993**: Services CPI fell from 5.2% to 2.9% within 24 months of goods disinflation
- **2001-2003**: Services from 3.6% to 2.7% within 18 months
- **2008-2010**: Services from 3.1% to 1.2% within 20 months
- **Counter-example (1970s)**: Services inflation *accelerated* during goods disinflation in 1975-1976 — but this required the specific institutional backdrop of cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs) covering >60% of the workforce, vs ~10-12% today

The recency bias risk: because services inflation has been sticky in 2023-2025, analysts extrapolate persistence beyond what the component-level resolution dynamics support.

### Claim 5 — Biofuel Mandate Inflation Ratchet
The RFS does allocate ~36% of corn to ethanol. But corn's weight in CPI is negligible directly (~0.02% via corn products) and modest indirectly (~0.15-0.25% via feed costs → meat/dairy/eggs). A $3.50-4.00/bushel floor vs a counterfactual of $2.50-3.00/bushel without the mandate translates to roughly $8-12B in annual transfers — meaningful for farm economics but a rounding error in an $28T economy. The narrative fallacy converts this mechanically correct but quantitatively minor point into a structural inflation story via the rhetorical bridge of "fiscal dominance" and "political economy." The concept's confidence of 6.0 is appropriate for the mechanical claim but too high for the "inflation regime" inference drawn from it.

### Claim 6 — Wage-Price Spiral Historical Base Rate
The term "spiral" is deployed loosely in the KB. The strict definition requires:
1. Real wages growing faster than labor productivity
2. Firms passing through wage costs into prices (pricing power)
3. Workers demanding higher wages in response to higher prices (expectations feedback)

This triple conjunction has occurred in only 2 of ~15 inflation episodes in the US since 1948. Current conditions (as of early 2026): unit labor cost growth ~3-4%, productivity growth ~1.5-2%, but labor's share of income has *declined* from its 2020-2021 peak, and union density is ~10% (vs ~25-30% in the 1970s). The institutional prerequisite (wage-setting mechanisms with automatic CPI escalators) is largely absent. Stating "wage_price_spiral_risk" as a subtopic implicitly assigns it more probability mass than the base rate warrants — a conjunction fallacy (the specific scenario of a wage-price spiral is assigned greater concern than the more general scenario of "above-target inflation persisting for various reasons").

### Claim 7 — Breakeven Contamination
D'Amico, Kim, and Wei (2018) decompose TIPS breakevens into expected inflation, inflation risk premium, and liquidity premium. The liquidity premium alone has ranged from -80bp to +40bp since 2004. When the KB treats 2.3-2.5% BEI as evidence of "anchored expectations at the upper bound," it is implicitly assuming the liquidity premium is at its long-run average — but post-QT, with the Fed reducing its TIPS holdings, the liquidity premium has likely widened by 15-30bp, meaning *true* expected inflation may be 2.0-2.2%, well within the comfort zone. Alternatively, inflation risk premia may have risen due to the 2021-2023 experience (rational Bayesian updating), making the BEI *higher* than expected inflation. Either way, the raw BEI number is a noisy signal, and building a "razor-thin buffer" narrative on it is anchoring to a contaminated measure.

### Claim 8 — Taylor Rule Coefficient Instability
The estimated φ\_π varies dramatically by:
- **Estimation period**: Clarida-Galí-Gertler (2000) found φ\_π ≈ 0.83 for 1960-1979, φ\_π ≈ 2.15 for 1979-1996. Orphanides (2001) found that using *real-time* data (what the Fed actually knew) gave φ\_π ≈ 1.5 even for the Burns-era Fed — meaning the "mistake" narrative is partly an artifact of data revisions.
- **Cycle position**: The Fed's revealed φ\_π during hiking cycles is systematically higher than during cutting cycles. In 2001, the Fed cut despite 2.6% core CPI; in 2020, it cut despite incipient inflation signals. The KB acknowledges this but still treats φ\_π as a stable "lynchpin."
- **FOMC composition**: A dovish rotation (3-4 new governors) could shift effective φ\_π by 0.3-0.5 within a year.

The concept's 8.0 confidence is appropriate for the *theoretical* claim that φ\_π matters, but inappropriate for the *empirical* claim that we know its current value or can predict its trajectory.

## Open Questions

1. **Can we construct a real-time de-anchoring indicator that outperforms the naive BEI signal?** The Coibion-Gorodnichenko threshold needs to be decomposed into components that are independently measurable: survey expectations (multiple surveys, not just Michigan), market-based expectations (BEI adjusted for liquidity and risk premia), business pricing intentions (NFIB, ISM prices paid), and wage-setting behavior (ECI vs AHE divergence). No single indicator has sufficient signal-to-noise.

2. **What is the actual false positive rate for "last mile" inflation persistence calls?** In prior episodes (1996, 2007, 2012, 2019), analysts warned about persistent above-target inflation that subsequently resolved. If we could estimate the base rate of "last mile persistence" vs "last mile resolution," it would sharply improve calibration of current claims.

3. **Is the Phillips Curve slope (0.1-0.5 in the KB) itself a stable structural parameter, or is it endogenous to the expectations anchoring state?** If the slope is endogenous (Ball, Mankiw, & Romer, 1988), then the entire "flat curve under anchored expectations" framework is tautological — we observe a flat curve *because* expectations are anchored, and we infer expectations are anchored *because* the curve is flat. This circularity needs to be broken with independent identification.

4. **How should we weight survey-based vs market-based expectations when they diverge?** Michigan 5-10Y at 3.0-3.2% vs BEI at 2.3-2.5% is a ~60-70bp gap. Is this within the normal noise band? The KB flags the Coibion-Gorodnichenko >75-100bp threshold for *sustained* divergence — but we may be approaching it, and the direction matters (surveys higher than markets suggests households are less anchored than institutions).

5. **What is the probability that the *next* inflation regime shift will be driven by a mechanism not currently in the KB's taxonomy?** Historical regime shifts have been triggered by oil embargoes (1973), Volcker's appointment (1979), China's WTO accession (2001), COVID supply disruptions (2021). Each was outside the then-prevailing analytical framework. This is the deepest form of overconfidence — the confidence that our *list of risks* is complete.

6. **How should we formally incorporate model uncertainty into the scenario probabilities?** The current 45/30/15/10 split treats the model as given. A Bayesian model averaging approach that assigns probability to "my model is wrong" would widen the confidence intervals and, critically, would raise the probability of tail outcomes that the current framework compresses.

7. **Is "structural vs cyclical inflation decomposition methodology" (identified as a knowledge gap) actually achievable in real-time, or is it only identifiable ex post?** The Beveridge-Nelson decomposition, HP filter, and UC models all produce estimates that are revised substantially as new data arrives. If structural vs cyclical decomposition is inherently an ex-post exercise, then framing it as an actionable knowledge gap may itself be overconfident about what's knowable.
