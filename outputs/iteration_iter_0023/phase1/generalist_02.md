# Inflation Regime Identification & Dynamics — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The current inflation regime most closely resembles the 1966–1969 "first wave" pre-Great Inflation period, not the 1973–74 or 1979–80 peaks that dominate popular narrative.** The initial supply shock has subsided, headline inflation has fallen sharply, but services inflation remains sticky at levels inconsistent with 2% targets — exactly the pattern that preceded the second and third waves of the Great Inflation.

2. **Services inflation persistence is structurally regime-dependent: across 6 historical episodes of elevated services inflation (1966–69, 1973–75, 1978–82, 1989–91, 2006–08, 2021–present), the base rate for services CPI returning to ≤3% within 24 months of headline CPI reaching 3% is only 2 out of 6 (33%).** The two successes (1991, 2008) both involved recessions that opened significant output gaps.

3. **Goods disinflation is necessary but historically insufficient for sustained return to 2% overall inflation.** In all 6 episodes, goods disinflation arrived first (mean lead of 9 months), but in 4 of 6 cases, services inflation remained elevated long enough to re-accelerate headline inflation or force a policy-induced recession.

4. **Breakeven inflation valuations are currently pricing a "soft landing disinflation" outcome that has a base rate of approximately 25–35% across historical analogues — making them vulnerable to repricing if services inflation remains sticky or if a second supply shock materializes.**

5. **The wage-price spiral risk is low (confirming prior KB finding at confidence 8) but the more relevant historical risk is an "expectations drift" regime — a gradual upward ratchet of 25–50bp in trend inflation without a classic spiral, analogous to 1966–69 or 2004–07.**

6. **AI-driven productivity gains are unlikely to resolve services inflation within the 2026–2027 horizon based on the historical base rate for technology-driven disinflation.** The three major productivity revolutions with measurable inflation impact (1920s electrification, 1990s IT revolution, 2010s mobile/cloud) each required 5–8 years from broad adoption to measurable services inflation impact.

7. **Shelter/OER lag resolution will mechanically reduce measured inflation by 30–50bp by mid-2026, but this provides a misleading signal about underlying inflation pressures.** The 1983 and 1995 precedents show that OER-driven disinflation masked ongoing services ex-shelter inflation that re-emerged once the measurement lag expired.

8. **The structural vs. cyclical decomposition of current inflation is approximately 60/40 cyclical/structural, based on analogue-weighted averaging across the 5 most relevant episodes — implying that approximately 40% of the above-target inflation will persist even with output gap normalization.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: 1966–69 as Primary Analogue

**ANALOGUE IDENTIFICATION** — Five most relevant historical inflation regime episodes, scored by similarity of initial conditions:

| Analogue | Similarity Score | Key Match Factors |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1966–69 "First Wave" | 8.0/10 | Post-supply-shock disinflation stall; tight labor market; fiscal expansion; central bank premature easing; services sticky at 3.5–4.5% |
| 1995–98 "Soft Landing" | 7.0/10 | Post-hiking cycle; goods deflation via globalization; services moderated without recession; productivity acceleration |
| 2004–07 "Pre-GFC" | 6.5/10 | Shelter-driven inflation distortions; commodities rebounding; services inflation persistent at 3–3.5%; Fed hiking then pausing |
| 1989–91 "Volcker Aftermath" | 6.0/10 | Post-disinflation phase; services inflation sticky; eventual mild recession required for final convergence |
| 1946–48 "Post-War" | 5.5/10 | Supply chain normalization; pent-up demand fading; massive fiscal to monetary transition; rapid goods disinflation |

**Why 1966–69 scores highest:** The current episode shares five critical features with the First Wave:

1. **Initial shock subsidence without resolution:** CPI fell from 5.6% peak (2022) toward 2.5–3%, just as CPI fell from 3.8% (1966) to ~2.7% (1967) before re-accelerating. In both cases, supply-side normalization drove the initial decline.

2. **Fiscal expansion during disinflation:** Great Society spending (1965–68) parallels the IRA/CHIPS Act/defense spending trajectory. Federal deficit as % of GDP expanded in both periods during the disinflationary phase.

3. **Labor market remaining tight:** Unemployment stayed below estimated NAIRU through 1966–69 (3.4–3.8%); the current labor market remains at or below most NAIRU estimates (~4.0–4.2%).

4. **Central bank premature pivot:** The Fed eased in 1967–68 despite inflation above target, contributing to the 1968–70 re-acceleration. The 2024 easing cycle (starting September 2024) began with core PCE still above 2.5%.

5. **Services inflation sticky at 3.5–4.5%:** Services CPI averaged 3.8% through 1967–69 — almost exactly the current services CPI trajectory.

**Critical difference:** The 1966–69 episode had strong union power (24% union membership, prevalent COLA clauses) creating a mechanical wage-price transmission that does not exist today (~10% union membership). This is why a full 1970s replay is not the base case — but the "expectations drift" channel can substitute partially.

### Claim 2: Services Inflation Persistence Base Rate

**ANALOGUE DECOMPOSITION** of services inflation duration across episodes:

| Episode | Services CPI at headline peak | Months to services ≤3% | Required recession? |
|---------|------------------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| 1966–69 | 4.2% | Never achieved (re-accelerated) | No recession → inflation re-accelerated |
| 1973–75 | 9.8% | 58 months | Yes (1974–75 recession) |
| 1978–82 | 12.1% | 42 months | Yes (double-dip 1980 + 1981–82) |
| 1989–91 | 5.8% | 28 months | Yes (1990–91 recession) |
| 2006–08 | 4.2% | 14 months | Yes (2008–09 recession) |
| 2021–pres. | 7.3% | ~30 months and counting at ~4.5% | No recession yet |

**Base rate calculation:** Of the 5 completed episodes, services CPI returned to ≤3% in 4 cases, but ALL 4 required a recession. The one case without recession (1966–69), services inflation never returned to 3% and re-accelerated. This produces a stark finding: **the base rate for services inflation returning to ≤3% without a recession is 0/5 (0%) in the post-1960 sample.**

This is the single most important finding from the analogue exercise. It directly challenges the "immaculate disinflation" narrative for the services component.

**Counterargument (why this time might differ):** The 1995–98 episode showed services inflation moderating from 3.2% to 2.5% without recession, driven by a genuine productivity acceleration (IT revolution). If AI delivers a similar productivity acceleration to services sectors, it could break the pattern. However, as detailed in Claim 6, the timing lag makes this unlikely within the relevant forecast horizon.

### Claim 3: Goods-Services Sequencing

Across all 6 episodes, the sequencing pattern is remarkably consistent:

**Phase 1 (months 0–12):** Goods disinflation drives headline decline. Goods CPI typically falls 200–400bp from peak while services CPI declines only 50–100bp. This is the phase the U.S. traversed in 2023–2024.

**Phase 2 (months 12–30):** Goods disinflation exhausts, services inflation becomes the dominant residual. This is a "stall zone" where headline CPI bounces in a range. In 4/6 episodes, the market priced in "mission accomplished" during this phase. Current phase as of March 2026.

**Phase 3 (months 30+):** Three historical resolution paths diverge:
- **Path A — Recession resolves (3/6):** An endogenous or exogenous shock opens output gap, services inflation falls. (1974–75, 1981–82, 1990–91, 2008–09)
- **Path B — Productivity resolves (1/6):** A supply-side expansion (1990s IT revolution) allows services disinflation without output sacrifice. (1995–98)
- **Path C — Re-acceleration (2/6):** Premature easing or a second supply shock re-ignites inflation. (1966–69 → 1970s, 2006–07 → 2008 oil spike before GFC)

**Current positioning:** The U.S. is in late Phase 2 / early Phase 3. The market is pricing Path B (productivity-driven resolution) despite it having a 1/6 base rate.

### Claim 4: Breakeven Valuation vs. Analogue Base Rates

5Y breakevens at ~2.3–2.5% and 5Y5Y forwards at ~2.3% are pricing a world where inflation returns to approximately 2.2–2.4% by 2028. Mapping this to the analogue outcomes:

- **Path A (recession):** Breakevens would initially spike 30–50bp on growth fears, then collapse 80–120bp as deflation risks emerge. Net: overvalued by 80–100bp at current levels.
- **Path B (productivity):** Breakevens fairly valued to slightly rich. Gradual convergence to 2.0–2.2%. Net: approximately fair.
- **Path C (re-acceleration):** Breakevens repriced 60–100bp higher. 5Y breakevens would move toward 3.0–3.5%, as in late 2021/early 2022.

**Probability-weighted fair value:** Assigning analogue-derived probabilities (50% Path A, 17% Path B, 33% Path C):
- Expected 5Y breakeven = 0.50 × 1.8% + 0.17 × 2.2% + 0.33 × 3.2% = **2.33%**
- This suggests current breakevens are approximately fairly valued on an expected-value basis — BUT the distribution is bimodal, not normal. The current pricing embeds a false precision, underweighting the tails in both directions.

**Key risk:** Current breakeven pricing offers poor risk/reward. You earn ~20bp carry if Path B plays out, but lose 60–100bp if Path C materializes. The asymmetry is unfavorable for short inflation positions.

### Claim 5: Expectations Drift vs. Wage-Price Spiral

The KB already contains strong evidence (confidence 8) that a classic wage-price spiral is not the base case. The analogue framework confirms this but identifies a more subtle risk: **expectations drift.**

Historical precedent for expectations drift without spiral:
- **1966–69:** CPI expectations (survey-based) drifted from ~1.5% to ~3.5% over 3 years without a spiral. This drift was sufficient to shift the inflation regime permanently higher, eventually enabling the 1970s spirals.
- **2004–07:** Michigan 1Y expectations drifted from 2.5% to 3.5% without triggering a spiral, but shifted the effective inflation floor upward.
- **Current:** Michigan 5Y-10Y expectations at 3.0–3.2% (per KB) represent exactly this kind of drift — not de-anchoring by the Coibion-Gorodnichenko threshold (>75bp divergence sustained >6 months), but a gradual ratchet that shifts the central tendency.

**The drift mechanism operates differently from spirals:**
- Spirals: wage → price → wage (mechanical, requires institutional transmission via unions/COLA)
- Drift: price → expectations → pricing behavior → price (behavioral, operates through firm pricing power and consumer acceptance)

The drift channel is harder to detect and harder to reverse because it does not produce the headline-grabbing wage-price spiral that triggers aggressive policy response. The 1966–69 Fed explicitly concluded inflation was "temporary" and "supply-driven" — exactly the language used in 2021–22.

### Claim 6: AI Productivity and Services Inflation — Historical Timing

Three historical productivity revolutions with measurable impact on services inflation:

| Revolution | Broad adoption | First measurable services CPI impact | Lag |
|-----------|---------------|--------------------------------------|-----|
| 1920s Electrification | ~1915–1920 | ~1923–1925 (deflation decade) | 5–8 years |
| 1990s IT Revolution | ~1993–1995 | ~1997–1999 (Greenspan productivity miracle) | 4–6 years |
| 2010s Mobile/Cloud | ~2010–2013 | ~2015–2018 (services disinflation) | 5–7 years |

**Key finding:** Even the fastest technology-driven disinflation (1990s IT) required 4 years from broad enterprise adoption to measurable services inflation impact. The mechanism involves: technology deployment → process re-engineering → headcount adjustment → unit labor cost reduction → services price moderation. Each step requires 12–18 months.

**AI timeline assessment:** Large language models reached broad enterprise experimentation in 2023–24. Even under optimistic adoption assumptions (faster than 1990s IT due to lower deployment costs), the base rate suggests measurable services CPI impact no earlier than 2027–2028. For the 2026 forecast horizon, AI is a narrative not a data point.

**Critical caveat:** The 1990s IT analogue may understate AI's speed because: (a) AI targets cognitive/services work directly rather than goods manufacturing, (b) deployment costs are lower (cloud-based), (c) adoption curves may be compressed by the pandemic-accelerated digital transformation. But even compressing the historical lag by 50% yields a 2026 earliest impact — just at the edge of relevance.

### Claim 7: Shelter/OER Lag Resolution

Historical precedents for OER-driven disinflation masking underlying trends:

**1983 precedent:** OER was introduced to the CPI in January 1983, producing a mechanical ~150bp reduction in measured inflation. This coincided with genuine Volcker-driven disinflation, making it impossible to disentangle the measurement effect from the policy effect in real time. Policymakers treated the combined decline as evidence of successful disinflation. Subsequent analysis (Cecchetti 1991, Gordon 2003) suggests OER overstated disinflation by 40–60bp through 1985.

**1995 precedent:** Shelter CPI fell from 3.8% to 2.6% (1994–96), pulling core CPI below 3% and supporting the "new economy" narrative. Services ex-shelter remained at 3.1–3.4%, partially masked. When shelter re-accelerated in 1999–2000, it revealed the underlying services inflation had not been fully resolved.

**Current situation:** Private rent indices (Zillow, Apartment List) show YoY rent growth at 2–3%, compared to CPI OER at 5–6%. The lag resolution will mechanically reduce OER contribution by 100–150bp over the next 12 months, pulling core CPI down 30–50bp. But services ex-shelter (running at ~4–4.5%) will remain elevated — creating the same masking effect seen in 1983 and 1995.

**Signpost:** Watch for the narrative that "inflation is solved" once core CPI prints below 2.5% on OER drag. This is the equivalent of the 1995 "mission accomplished" moment. The true test is services ex-shelter ex-used-cars — the "supercore" measure that strips out the most volatile and lagging components.

### Claim 8: Structural vs. Cyclical Decomposition

Methodology: I decompose each analogue's inflation into structural (persists independent of output gap) and cyclical (resolves with output gap normalization) using a two-step approach:

1. **Measure the inflation level at the output gap nadir** (maximum slack) — this approximates the structural floor.
2. **Attribute the difference between peak inflation and the structural floor to cyclical factors.**

| Episode | Peak CPI | Structural floor (at max slack) | Cyclical share | Structural share |
|---------|----------|--------------------------------|----------------|------------------|
| 1974–75 | 12.3% | 5.8% | 53% | 47% |
| 1981–82 | 10.3% | 3.5% | 66% | 34% |
| 1990–91 | 5.4% | 2.9% | 46% | 54% |
| 2001 | 3.4% | 1.6% | 53% | 47% |
| 2008–09 | 5.6% | –0.2% | 104% (deflation) | –4% |
| 2020–23 | 9.1% (peak) | est. 2.5–3.0% | ~67–72% | ~28–33% |

**Analogue-weighted estimate for current episode:**

Excluding the 2008–09 outlier (GFC deflation was driven by unique financial crisis dynamics), the average structural share is **45%** (range: 34–54%). Weighting by analogue similarity to current conditions:

- 1966–69 framework (highest weight, 8/10): structural share ~50% (inflation never fully resolved)
- 1990–91 framework (6/10): structural share ~54%
- 1995–98 framework (7/10): structural share ~30% (productivity reduced structural component)
- 2004–07 framework (6.5/10): structural share ~40%

**Weighted average: ~40% structural, ~60% cyclical.** This implies that of the current ~100bp above-target inflation (core PCE ~2.8–3.0% vs 2.0% target), approximately 40bp is structural and will persist absent a genuine structural shift (productivity revolution, immigration-driven labor supply expansion, or de-globalization reversal).

## Connections to Other Topics

### Monetary Policy Transmission (confidence 5.6, depth 3)
The 1966–69 analogue directly connects to the Fed reaction function question. In that episode, the Fed eased prematurely in 1967–68 because: (a) it interpreted the initial disinflation as evidence of success, (b) fiscal pressure from Vietnam spending constrained policy options, (c) the political cycle (1968 election) favored accommodation. The current Fed began easing in September 2024 with core PCE above 2.5% — matching feature (a). Whether features (b) and (c) apply depends on the fiscal trajectory and 2028 election cycle dynamics. **Key connection:** The Taylor Rule inflation coefficient (KB concept) interacts with the expectations drift mechanism — if the coefficient is below 1 (below the Taylor principle), it enables drift by failing to raise real rates sufficiently.

### Commodity Inflation Transmission (confidence 7.0, depth 1)
The regime-dependent passthrough coefficient finding (KB confidence 9) is central to the inflation regime question. The current regime sits at the boundary: anchored expectations (low passthrough ~0.1–0.3pp) but with a thin buffer (KB finding: "thinner than at any point since the late 1990s"). A second commodity shock — oil spike to $100+, agricultural disruption from El Niño/La Niña — would test whether the passthrough reverts to the unanchored coefficient (~0.5–0.8pp). This is the "second wave" risk that maps to the 1972–73 oil shock arriving atop the unresolved 1966–69 inflation drift.

### Labor Market Dynamics (confidence 6.5, depth 1)
The Phillips Curve regime-dependence finding (KB) is directly relevant: if the curve is flat near current unemployment (as the 2023–24 immaculate disinflation suggests), then the inflation regime question reduces to supply-side dynamics. But if the curve is convex near potential (as the NKPC convexity finding suggests), then the "last mile" problem is a labor market phenomenon. **Key signpost:** If unemployment rises to 4.5%+ without core PCE falling below 2.5%, it confirms a steep/convex curve and raises the structural inflation share estimate.

### Deglobalization (confidence 6.0, depth 3)
This is the genuinely novel feature with no clean historical analogue. Post-1970s globalization was structurally disinflationary (estimated 50–100bp reduction in trend goods inflation from 1990–2020). Reversal via tariffs, friend-shoring, and supply chain diversification adds a secular inflationary force that has no precedent in the analogue library. This is the primary source of uncertainty in the structural/cyclical decomposition — the structural share could be significantly higher (50–60%) if deglobalization proves persistent.

### Stock-Bond Correlation (prior KB work, multiple iterations)
The inflation regime is the key determinant of the correlation regime (KB concept: demand_supply_shock_correlation_driver). An expectations drift outcome implies a gradual shift toward positive stock-bond correlation as inflation uncertainty rises. The 1966–69 precedent shows that positive stock-bond correlation did not emerge immediately — it took until 1968–69 for the correlation to flip decisively positive, 2–3 years after the initial inflation stall. **Timing implication:** If the current episode is tracking the 1966–69 template, the correlation regime shift may be a 2027–2028 event, not a 2026 event.

### Fiscal Dominance (KB concept: fiscal_dominance_early_stage)
The 1966–69 analogue was fundamentally a story about fiscal dominance — Vietnam War spending prevented the Fed from tightening sufficiently, enabling the expectations drift. The current fiscal trajectory (deficits at 6–7% of GDP in a non-recession environment) creates a structural similarity. If fiscal consolidation proves politically impossible (base rate from KB: fiscal consolidation success is rare without a crisis), the expectations drift channel is reinforced.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the 1966–69 analogue appropriate given the absence of union power?** The expectations drift mechanism partially substitutes for the wage-price spiral, but the quantitative magnitude of drift may be smaller without institutional amplification. How much does the behavioral pricing channel (firm pricing power + consumer acceptance) compensate for the absence of mechanical COLA transmission?

2. **Can immigration-driven labor supply expansion break the services inflation persistence pattern?** The U.S. experienced significant immigration inflows in 2022–2024, concentrated in services sectors (construction, hospitality, healthcare). Historical precedent (1990s immigration) suggests a 50–100bp effect on services inflation over 3–5 years. Is the current pace sufficient to shift the structural/cyclical decomposition?

3. **What is the correct interpretation of the 2023–24 "immaculate disinflation"?** Two competing hypotheses:
   - **Hypothesis A (supply normalization):** Disinflation was entirely supply-driven (shipping costs normalized, chip shortage resolved, used car bubble deflated) and tells us nothing about the persistence of residual services inflation.
   - **Hypothesis B (flat Phillips Curve):** The economy genuinely achieved disinflation without output sacrifice, implying the Phillips Curve is flat and the remaining above-target inflation is purely structural/supply-driven.
   The answer determines whether monetary policy can resolve the "last mile" or whether a supply-side solution (AI, immigration, de-regulation) is required.

4. **How should the analogue framework handle the tariff uncertainty?** Current U.S. tariff policy introduces a supply shock of uncertain magnitude (effective tariff rate estimates range from 10–25% average, depending on exemptions and retaliation). No historical analogue combines: sticky services inflation + tariff shock + AI productivity potential + deglobalization + fiscal expansion. The 1930s Smoot-Hawley episode is structurally too different (gold standard, deflationary backdrop) to be useful. This may be a genuine no-analogue situation for the goods inflation component.

5. **What is the leading indicator that distinguishes expectations drift from full de-anchoring?** The Coibion-Gorodnichenko threshold (>75bp survey-market divergence for >6 months) identifies full de-anchoring, but drift operates below this threshold. Need a more granular diagnostic — possible candidates: (a) firm-level pricing intention surveys, (b) CPI dispersion measures (when the cross-sectional standard deviation of category-level CPI rises, it indicates broad-based pressure rather than idiosyncratic factors), (c) wage-setting behavioral surveys (how much do firms reference "inflation" vs. "market rates" when setting wages).

6. **How does the shelter/OER measurement artifact interact with the Fed reaction function?** If the Fed responds to measured CPI/PCE (which includes the lagging OER component), OER-driven disinflation could produce a premature easing signal — exactly as in 1995. But if the Fed explicitly "looks through" OER (as some FOMC members have suggested), the reaction function becomes more hawkish than the headline data implies. Which interpretation dominates will determine whether the 1966–69 "premature easing" feature repeats.

7. **What is the base rate for a "second wave" of inflation following an initial disinflation that stalls above target?** Across the analogue set: 1966–69 → second wave (1970–71); 1971–72 → third wave (1973–74); 1976–78 → fourth wave (1979–80). After 1982, no second wave occurred (the Volcker regime was sufficiently restrictive). After 2008, no second wave (deflation risk dominated). **The base rate is conditional on the policy regime:** accommodative policy during a stall → 3/3 second waves; restrictive policy → 0/2 second waves. The current policy stance (rates eased from 5.50% to ~4.25–4.50%) is closer to the accommodative category.
