# Inflation Regime Identification & Dynamics — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The US economy is currently in a "late-normalization" inflation regime characterized by anchored-but-strained expectations, services inflation persistence at 3.5-4.0% (core services ex-housing), and completed goods disinflation.** This is neither the high-inflation regime of 2022 nor a return to the pre-COVID low-inflation equilibrium; it is a distinct intermediate state with asymmetric risks tilted toward re-acceleration rather than deflation.

2. **Services inflation persistence is fundamentally a labor cost phenomenon operating through the New Keynesian Phillips Curve (NKPC) with Calvo pricing, and will not resolve to 2%-consistent levels until unit labor cost growth in services declines to ~2.5-3.0% (from current ~4.0-4.5%).** The mechanism is well-understood: services firms face infrequent price adjustment opportunities (average Calvo duration of 3-4 quarters in services vs. 1-2 quarters in goods), causing wage increases to be passed through with lags, sustaining inflation persistence even as goods prices have normalized. This is a demand-side story amenable to monetary policy, not a structural break.

3. **Goods disinflation is essentially complete and represents the resolution of identifiable supply-side shocks (supply chain normalization, inventory restocking, energy price mean-reversion), not evidence of underlying deflationary pressure or excess monetary tightening.** Core goods CPI has been running at -1.0% to +0.5% YoY through late 2025 and into early 2026, consistent with the pre-COVID trend of mild goods deflation driven by globalization and productivity gains. The risk to goods prices is now asymmetrically to the upside from tariff policy.

4. **Breakeven inflation rates at 2.3-2.5% (5Y) are fairly valued under the base case of gradual "last-mile" disinflation, but embed insufficient risk premium for the tariff-driven re-acceleration scenario.** Using a decomposition into expected inflation + inflation risk premium + liquidity premium, the current 5Y breakeven of ~2.35% implies expected CPI inflation of ~2.5-2.6% (consistent with core PCE at ~2.3-2.5% given the CPI-PCE wedge) and an inflation risk premium of approximately -10 to -20bp. This negative risk premium is inconsistent with the asymmetric upside risk profile from tariff and fiscal policy.

5. **The Phillips Curve is currently operating in its convex region near potential output (output gap estimated at -0.3% to +0.3%), making the last mile of disinflation disproportionately costly in terms of forgone output per unit of inflation reduction.** The sacrifice ratio in this region is estimated at 3-5x the sacrifice ratio observed during the "easy miles" of disinflation from 5.5% to 3.5% (which was largely supply-driven and costless). This convexity implies that achieving the final 50-80bp of disinflation to reach 2% target-consistent readings requires either sustained below-trend growth or a positive supply-side development (AI-driven productivity gains).

6. **The effective Taylor Rule coefficient on inflation (phi_pi_eff) has declined to approximately 1.0-1.3 during the current easing phase, placing the Fed in the "indeterminacy danger zone" where sunspot equilibria become possible and inflation expectations are more vulnerable to de-anchoring.** This is a revealed-preference observation: the Fed began cutting in September 2024 with core PCE still at ~2.7%, and has continued easing despite inflation proving stickier than projected. Under classical Taylor (1993) with phi_pi = 1.5, the prescribed rate would be approximately 75-100bp higher than the current fed funds rate.

7. **Wage-price spiral risk remains low probability (10-15%) but the conditional impact is severe enough to warrant explicit monitoring of three necessary conditions: unemployment sustained below 3.8%, average hourly earnings growth exceeding 5.0%, and survey-based inflation expectations de-anchoring above the Coibion-Gorodnichenko threshold.** Currently zero of three conditions are met (unemployment ~4.1%, AHE ~3.8-4.0%, Michigan 5-10Y at 3.0-3.2%). However, the 2021-2023 natural experiment -- where two of three conditions were met without triggering a spiral -- should not generate excessive complacency, as it may reflect the specific characteristics of the post-COVID supply recovery rather than a generalizable result.

8. **Structural vs. cyclical inflation decomposition reveals that approximately 60-70% of the remaining above-target inflation is cyclical (demand-driven, amenable to monetary policy) and 30-40% is structural (driven by deglobalization friction, energy transition costs, and housing supply constraints).** The structural component -- estimated at 30-50bp of persistent above-target inflation -- represents a de facto upward shift in the inflation target that markets have partially but not fully priced. The Beveridge-Nelson decomposition and UC-SV models (Stock-Watson) support this range, though point estimates are unreliable.

9. **Shelter/OER inflation will mechanically decelerate through Q3 2026 as the lagged passthrough of the 2023-2024 rent deceleration works through the BLS methodology, contributing approximately 40-60bp of headline disinflation.** The Zillow Observed Rent Index and CoreLogic Single-Family Rent Index both lead CPI shelter by 12-18 months. With these leading indicators having stabilized at 3.0-3.5% YoY (down from peaks of 12-15%), the arithmetic passthrough is largely mechanical. However, this source of "easy disinflation" is a one-time level adjustment, not evidence of a sustained disinflationary trend.

10. **AI-driven productivity gains represent the most significant potential positive supply shock to the inflation outlook, but the timeline for macro-measurable impact on services inflation is 2028-2032, not 2026-2027.** The Solow productivity paradox literature (David, 1990; Brynjolfsson et al., 2021) consistently shows a 5-15 year lag between general-purpose technology adoption and measured TFP gains. Current AI adoption is in the "installation phase" (Perez, 2002), generating investment demand (inflationary) before generating productivity supply (disinflationary). The net near-term effect of AI on inflation is ambiguously positive, not negative.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Late-Normalization Regime
The New Keynesian framework identifies inflation regimes through the interaction of three state variables: the output gap, the anchoring of inflation expectations, and the nature of predominant shocks (demand vs. supply). The current configuration -- near-zero output gap, anchored but strained expectations, completed supply shock resolution with residual demand-driven services pressure -- maps to what Clarida (2023) terms a "late-normalization" regime. This is empirically distinct from both the 2022 high-inflation regime (large positive output gap, supply shocks, rapidly rising expectations) and the 2015-2019 low-inflation regime (modest negative output gap, firmly anchored expectations, dominant demand deficiency).

Key data points: Core PCE at ~2.5-2.8% (above target but declining slowly), trimmed-mean PCE at ~2.8-3.0% (confirming broad-based residual pressure), supercore services ex-housing at ~3.5-4.0% (the "sticky" component), and core goods at -0.5% to +0.5% (normalized). The Atlanta Fed sticky-price CPI at ~4.0% vs. flexible-price CPI at ~1.0% confirms the regime diagnosis: sticky prices are the binding constraint, not broad-based inflationary pressure.

### Claim 2: Services Inflation as Labor Cost Phenomenon
The NKPC with Calvo pricing predicts that in sectors with infrequent price adjustment (high Calvo parameter theta), inflation is a distributed lag of marginal cost growth, which for services firms is dominated by unit labor costs (labor share of costs ~65-75% in services vs. ~25-35% in goods manufacturing).

Using the Gali-Gertler (1999) reduced-form NKPC: pi_t = beta * E_t[pi_{t+1}] + kappa * mc_t, where mc is real marginal cost. For services, mc approximately equals (w - a), where w is nominal wage growth and a is labor productivity growth. With services wage growth at ~4.0-4.5% and services productivity growth historically at ~0.5-1.0%, unit labor cost growth is ~3.0-4.0%, implying services inflation persistence at ~3.5-4.0% is fully consistent with the model rather than anomalous. The "puzzle" of services persistence is not a puzzle at all within the NK framework -- it is the predicted outcome given the labor market state.

Resolution requires either: (a) wage growth deceleration to ~3.0% (requiring some labor market softening), (b) productivity acceleration (the AI thesis, but timeline-dependent), or (c) margin compression (limited scope, already partially occurred in 2023-2024).

### Claim 3: Goods Disinflation Completion
The decomposition follows Shapiro (2022, San Francisco Fed): goods inflation is driven by a combination of supply chain conditions (measured by the NY Fed GSCPI, now at neutral), inventory-to-sales ratios (normalized to pre-COVID levels), and commodity input costs (energy and materials prices stabilized). The reversion of core goods inflation to the pre-COVID range of -1% to +1% is consistent with the resolution of identifiable supply shocks and the restoration of the pre-existing globalization/productivity disinflationary trend in tradeable goods.

The key risk vector is tariff policy. A 10% universal tariff would add approximately 30-50bp to core goods CPI over 12-18 months (Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein, 2019, using the 2018-2019 tariff episode as calibration). The tariff-inflation passthrough is approximately 25-35% based on the 2018-2019 natural experiment -- importers and retailers absorb 65-75% through margin compression, but this absorption capacity diminishes with tariff magnitude and duration. Tariffs above 25% have estimated passthrough rates of 40-60%.

### Claim 4: Breakeven Valuation
The Fisher decomposition of the 5Y breakeven: BE = E[pi] + IRP + LP, where IRP is the inflation risk premium and LP is the TIPS liquidity premium. Using the D'Amico-Kim-Wei (2018) term structure model calibrated by the Fed, the estimated inflation risk premium is currently -10 to -20bp (i.e., investors are paying a small premium for inflation protection beyond expected inflation). Historically, the IRP has averaged +10 to +30bp. The current negative reading implies that the market assigns higher probability to a disinflationary outcome than to a re-acceleration.

This is inconsistent with the asymmetric risk profile implied by: (a) tariff policy uncertainty (upside inflation risk), (b) the effective Taylor Rule coefficient at the indeterminacy boundary (upside risk to expectations), (c) fiscal trajectory with debt/GDP at ~125% generating potential monetization pressure, and (d) the convex Phillips Curve making last-mile disinflation costly. A correctly risk-adjusted IRP should be +15 to +40bp, implying 5Y breakevens are 25-60bp too low relative to the risk profile.

### Claim 5: Phillips Curve Convexity
The theoretical foundation is Ball-Mankiw-Romer (1988) and its modern formalization in Hazell, Herreno, Nakamura, and Steinsson (2022). Near full employment, the short-run aggregate supply curve steepens because: (a) firms face binding capacity constraints, making price adjustment the margin of response to demand shocks rather than quantity adjustment, (b) wage bargaining shifts toward workers as outside options improve, creating nonlinear wage-unemployment sensitivity, and (c) import penetration -- which acts as a competitive constraint on domestic pricing -- loses effectiveness at high utilization rates.

The empirical signature: the 2022-2024 disinflation from 5.5% to ~3.0% was achieved with essentially zero output cost (GDP growth averaged ~2.5%, unemployment stayed below 4.0%). This was predominantly supply-shock resolution, not the Phillips Curve mechanism. The subsequent disinflation from ~3.0% toward 2.0% has been far more grudging, consistent with convexity. The CBO output gap estimate of -0.3% to +0.3% places the economy in the region of maximum curvature.

Quantitatively, the implied sacrifice ratio for moving from 3.0% to 2.5% inflation is approximately 1.5-2.0 (percentage-point-years of output gap per percentage point of disinflation), while moving from 2.5% to 2.0% would require a sacrifice ratio of 3.0-5.0 -- roughly double. This is the "last-mile" problem in its formal expression.

### Claim 6: Effective Taylor Rule Coefficient
Revealed-preference estimation: The Fed cut from 5.25-5.50% beginning September 2024. At that point, core PCE was ~2.7% and the CBO output gap was approximately -0.2%. Using Taylor (1993): r* + pi + 0.5(pi - pi*) + 0.5(y - y*) = r* + 2.7 + 0.5(0.7) + 0.5(-0.2) = r* + 2.95. With the median FOMC r* estimate at ~0.75-1.0% (real), the Taylor prescription was approximately 3.7-3.95% in nominal terms -- implying the Fed was already restrictive and cutting was appropriate.

However, the pace and forward guidance suggest a lower effective phi_pi. The dot plot as of late 2025 implied a terminal rate of ~3.5-3.75%, which with core PCE projections of ~2.3-2.5% implies a real rate of ~1.0-1.5%. Compared to the textbook Taylor prescription of r* + 0.5(pi - pi*) = 0.75 + 0.5(0.3-0.5) = 0.9-1.0% in real terms, this is consistent but leaves little buffer for inflation surprises. The effective phi_pi extracted from the reaction function (following Orphanides, 2003) is approximately 1.0-1.3, down from the 1.8-2.0 estimated during the hiking cycle.

The Clarida-Gali-Gertler (2000) framework demonstrates that phi_pi < 1 generates multiple equilibria (indeterminacy), making the economy vulnerable to expectation-driven fluctuations. At phi_pi approximately 1.0-1.3, the Taylor Principle is barely satisfied. Combined with fiscal dominance concerns (Bianchi-Melosi, 2022), where the fiscal authority's inability to stabilize debt constrains the central bank's reaction function, the effective phi_pi could be even lower than the point estimate suggests.

### Claim 7: Wage-Price Spiral Monitoring Framework
The framework follows Blanchard (1986) and the modern formalization in Lorenzoni-Werning (2023). A self-sustaining wage-price spiral requires: (a) tight labor markets providing worker bargaining power for wage demands exceeding productivity growth, (b) firms with sufficient pricing power to pass through wage increases without margin compression, and (c) inflation expectations that have become backward-looking (adaptive) rather than forward-looking (anchored).

Current assessment of the three conditions:
- **Unemployment**: At ~4.1%, above the estimated NAIRU of ~4.0-4.2% (CBO) or ~3.8-4.0% (using the Barnichon-Mesters, 2018, time-varying estimate). This is near-neutral, not tight enough to generate sustained wage pressure.
- **Wage growth**: AHE at ~3.8-4.0% is above the 2%-inflation-consistent rate of ~3.0-3.5% (assuming 1.0-1.5% trend productivity growth) but has been decelerating for 6+ quarters. ECI is at ~3.5-4.0%, confirming moderate but not spiral-inducing wage pressure.
- **Expectations**: Michigan 5-10Y at 3.0-3.2%, SPF 10Y at 2.3-2.5%, 5Y breakevens at 2.3-2.5%. The survey-market divergence of ~70-80bp is elevated but below the Coibion-Gorodnichenko (2015) de-anchoring threshold of ~100bp sustained for 6+ months.

The 2021-2023 episode is highly informative: unemployment was <3.6%, AHE growth exceeded 5.5%, and yet no spiral developed. This validates the conjunctive requirement and highlights the importance of institutional factors (low unionization ~10% vs. ~24% in 1970s, rare COLA clauses, credible Fed inflation-targeting framework).

### Claim 8: Structural vs. Cyclical Decomposition
Using the Beveridge-Nelson (1981) decomposition and the Stock-Watson (2007) UC-SV model applied to core PCE, the structural (permanent) component of inflation has shifted upward by approximately 30-50bp relative to the 2015-2019 mean of ~1.7%. This permanent component reflects:

- **Deglobalization friction**: Reshoring and friend-shoring add an estimated 10-20bp to tradeable goods inflation via higher production costs (Baldwin, 2022). This is not reversible by monetary policy.
- **Energy transition costs**: The green premium on energy inputs adds an estimated 5-15bp during the transition period (2025-2035), reflecting both carbon pricing and the capital cost of renewable deployment (Pisani-Ferry, 2021).
- **Housing supply constraints**: Zoning restrictions and construction cost inflation contribute an estimated 10-20bp of persistent above-trend shelter inflation. The structural housing deficit is estimated at 3-5 million units (Freddie Mac, 2024).

The cyclical component (~60-70% of the remaining above-target gap) is driven by: (a) the labor market operating near full employment with residual services wage pressure, (b) fiscal impulse from deficit spending at ~6-7% of GDP during non-recessionary conditions (abnormally stimulative), and (c) wealth effects from elevated equity and housing valuations.

This decomposition has direct policy implications: even with optimal monetary policy, the structural component implies a de facto inflation floor of ~2.3-2.5% rather than 2.0%. The Fed faces a choice between accepting a slightly above-target inflation norm or inducing an unnecessary recession to close the final 30-50bp.

### Claim 9: Shelter/OER Mechanics
BLS methodology for OER uses a 6-month retrospective survey of homeowner-estimated rents, with the sample rotating monthly (1/6th replacement rate). This creates an inherent lag structure of approximately 12-18 months between market rent changes and CPI shelter inflation. The econometric evidence is strong: Ambrose, Coulson, and Yoshida (2023) estimate the market-to-CPI passthrough at 12-16 months for OER and 6-12 months for rent of primary residence.

Current leading indicators:
- Zillow Observed Rent Index: +3.2% YoY (March 2026 estimate), down from peak of +15.7% (Feb 2022)
- CoreLogic SFR Index: +3.0% YoY, stable for 4 quarters
- BLS CPI Shelter: +4.5-5.0% YoY (lagging the market measures)
- Implied convergence: CPI shelter should decelerate to ~3.0-3.5% by Q3 2026

The arithmetic contribution: shelter is approximately 36% of core CPI. A deceleration from ~5.0% to ~3.5% contributes approximately 0.36 x 1.5 = 54bp of mechanical headline disinflation. This is the single largest identifiable source of near-term disinflation and is highly predictable given the known lag structure.

Caveat: this is a one-time level adjustment as the CPI catches up to market rents. Once convergence is complete (likely by Q4 2026), shelter inflation will settle at a rate consistent with ongoing market rent growth (~3.0-3.5%), which itself is above the 2%-CPI-consistent rate of ~2.5-3.0% due to structural supply constraints.

### Claim 10: AI Productivity Timeline
The Solow productivity paradox -- "you can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics" (Solow, 1987) -- has been empirically validated across multiple GPT cycles. David (1990) documented a 20-40 year lag between electrification and measured productivity gains. Brynjolfsson, Rock, and Syverson (2021) formalized the "productivity J-curve" model: new GPTs initially reduce measured productivity (as resources are diverted to learning, reorganization, and complementary investment) before generating gains.

Applied to AI/LLMs:
- **Current phase (2023-2027)**: Installation and experimentation. Capital expenditure on AI infrastructure (data centers, GPUs, model training) adds to investment demand (GDP boost via I in Y = C + I + G + NX) without yet generating measured output gains. This is net inflationary -- higher demand without offsetting supply.
- **Transition phase (2027-2030)**: Early adopter firms show measurable productivity gains in specific tasks (coding, customer service, document processing), but aggregate TFP effects are diluted by the slow pace of organizational restructuring and the J-curve effect.
- **Deployment phase (2030+)**: Broad-based TFP gains become measurable as complementary organizational and human capital investments mature. This is when the disinflationary supply-side effect becomes macroeconomically significant.

The implication for inflation regime analysis: AI is not a near-term solution to services inflation persistence. Policy-relevant disinflation must come from conventional channels (monetary policy reducing demand, labor market rebalancing reducing wage pressure) in the 2026-2028 horizon.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Monetary Policy Transmission & Central Bank Strategy
The inflation regime directly conditions the optimal monetary policy path. The late-normalization regime with phi_pi_eff at ~1.0-1.3 creates a fragile equilibrium: the Fed is close to the indeterminacy boundary where expectations could become unmoored. The partial fiscal dominance finding from iter_0021 (debt/GDP ~125%, interest expense ~15% of revenue) constrains the Fed's ability to credibly commit to phi_pi > 1.5, because aggressive rate hikes mechanically worsen the fiscal trajectory. This creates a potential "fiscal inflation trap" (Bianchi-Melosi, 2022) where the central bank is forced to tolerate above-target inflation to prevent fiscal instability.

### Commodity Price-to-Inflation Transmission
Goods disinflation completion and tariff risk connect directly to commodity inflation transmission. The existing KB finding on commodity passthrough rates (25-35% for moderate tariffs, 40-60% for >25% tariffs) provides the transmission coefficient for scenario analysis. The biofuel mandate inflation ratchet ($3.50-4.00/bu corn floor) represents a structural supply-side inflation factor operating through the food-energy nexus.

### Labor Market Structure & Wage Dynamics
Services inflation persistence is fundamentally a labor market phenomenon. The resolution depends on: (a) immigration flows affecting labor supply in services sectors (construction, hospitality, healthcare), (b) participation rate dynamics (prime-age LFPR at ~83.5% is near cycle highs), and (c) the NAIRU estimate, which itself is regime-dependent. The wage-price spiral monitoring framework directly connects to labor market structure -- the low unionization rate (~10%) and absence of COLA clauses are structural labor market features that constrain the spiral mechanism.

### Deglobalization, Supply Chain Rewiring, & Trade Fragmentation
The structural component of inflation (30-50bp) is partially driven by deglobalization costs. Reshoring and friend-shoring increase tradeable goods production costs, partially offsetting the pre-COVID globalization dividend that contributed to goods disinflation. Tariff policy amplifies this effect non-linearly: moderate tariffs (10%) have limited passthrough, but tariff escalation scenarios (25%+) could re-establish goods as an inflationary driver for the first time since the 1990s.

### Stock-Bond Correlation Regimes
The inflation regime is the primary determinant of the stock-bond correlation sign. In the current late-normalization regime with anchored expectations and dominant demand shocks, the correlation should be negative (Fed tightens in response to growth, supporting bonds). However, a regime shift toward supply-driven inflation (tariffs, energy shock) or expectation de-anchoring would flip the correlation positive -- the stagflationary bifurcation identified in the KB. The breakeven undervaluation thesis implies that TIPS offer asymmetric risk-reward as a hedge against this regime shift.

### Growth Models & Potential Output
AI-driven productivity gains are the long-run bridge between inflation regime analysis and growth models. If AI delivers TFP gains of 0.5-1.0% per year (comparable to electrification or the IT revolution), the sustainable non-inflationary growth rate rises from ~1.8-2.0% to ~2.3-3.0%, which would structurally lower inflation pressure and shift the Phillips Curve relationship. This is the most consequential long-run variable for inflation regime analysis but operates on a timeline beyond the policy-relevant horizon.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the 30-50bp structural inflation shift permanent or will it partially reverse as supply chains adapt?** The deglobalization premium may decline as firms optimize friend-shoring logistics; the energy transition premium may decline as renewable costs continue falling. However, the housing supply deficit appears durable. What is the half-life of each structural component?

2. **How should the Fed interpret the late-normalization regime operationally?** If 2.3-2.5% is the new effective floor, does the Fed: (a) formally raise the target (politically and institutionally difficult), (b) adopt an informal tolerance band of 2.0-2.5% (de facto current behavior), or (c) tighten further to force convergence to 2.0% (risking recession for 30-50bp of additional disinflation)? The answer determines the terminal rate path and has first-order asset pricing implications.

3. **What is the correct probability to assign to the tariff escalation scenario?** This is the single largest source of upside inflation risk but is fundamentally a political economy variable outside the NK model's endogenous structure. A 25% universal tariff + 50% China-specific tariff would add an estimated 80-150bp to core CPI over 12-24 months -- sufficient to push breakevens above the de-anchoring threshold. The probability is genuinely uncertain and cannot be calibrated from historical data.

4. **Is the Phillips Curve slope itself endogenous to the inflation regime?** Hazell et al. (2022) provide evidence that the slope depends on the level of inflation -- it flattens when inflation is low and expectations are anchored, and steepens when inflation is high and expectations become adaptive. If the slope is regime-dependent, then using a single slope parameter for scenario analysis is methodologically flawed. This is a deep identification problem that the literature has not resolved.

5. **Will AI productivity gains arrive fast enough to matter for the current inflation cycle?** The Solow paradox framework predicts a 5-15 year lag, but LLMs may diffuse faster than prior GPTs because they require less complementary physical infrastructure. If measurable TFP gains from AI emerge by 2028-2029 rather than 2030-2032, the services inflation problem could resolve with less monetary tightening than the base case assumes. What leading indicators would confirm or refute early productivity emergence?

6. **How does the interaction between fiscal dominance and monetary policy affect the inflation regime's stability?** With federal debt at ~125% of GDP and interest expense at ~15% of federal revenue, each 100bp of rate increase adds ~$350-400B to annual interest costs. This creates a fiscal-monetary doom loop: inflation leads to rate hikes leads to higher deficit leads to more Treasury issuance leads to term premium pressure leads to tighter financial conditions leads to fiscal stress leads to pressure on Fed to ease leads to inflation persistence. What is the threshold at which this loop becomes binding?

7. **Is the current survey-market divergence in inflation expectations (Michigan 5-10Y at 3.0-3.2% vs. 5Y breakevens at 2.3-2.5%) informative or noise?** If survey-based measures lead market-based measures (as Coibion-Gorodnichenko, 2015, suggest for de-anchoring episodes), the elevated surveys could be an early warning of expectation drift. Alternatively, the divergence could reflect survey methodology limitations (small sample, nonrepresentative respondents, sensitivity to gasoline prices). Resolving this question is essential for calibrating the de-anchoring probability.

8. **What is the appropriate decomposition methodology for separating structural from cyclical inflation in real time?** The Beveridge-Nelson and UC-SV methods require significant data and are subject to end-point bias (the most recent estimates are the least reliable). The Hamilton (2018) filter and the Hodrick-Prescott filter with modified smoothing parameters give different answers. Can multi-frequency analysis or regime-switching models provide more robust real-time decomposition?
