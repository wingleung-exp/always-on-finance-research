# Inflation Regime Identification & Dynamics — Central Bank Policy Transmission Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The current inflation regime is a "transmission-impaired last mile" — structurally distinct from any post-1990 episode — where the Fed's estimated reaction function (φ_π ≈ 1.0-1.3) is barely sufficient to satisfy the Taylor Principle, making the inflation regime self-referentially dependent on expectations anchoring rather than active policy.** The 525bp hiking cycle produced only ~100bp of FCI tightening. The last mile from ~2.5-3.0% to the 2% target cannot be achieved by monetary policy alone under current transmission conditions without either a favorable supply shock or an output gap that the Fed's implicit fiscal-dominance ceiling (5.0-5.5% FFR) prevents it from engineering.

2. **Services inflation persistence is fundamentally a transmission failure, not an inflation puzzle.** The interest rate channel passes through at ~60% effectiveness due to the mortgage lock-in effect (85%+ of outstanding mortgages below 5%). Shelter/OER — the single largest services CPI component at ~34% weight — is mechanistically lagged 12-18 months behind market rents via the BLS methodology, but even as the lag resolves, the rate of mean-reversion is structurally slower because new housing supply is constrained by the same rate-channel impairment (builders face 7%+ mortgage rates while 85% of homeowners are locked at <5%, freezing turnover and suppressing starts).

3. **The shelter/OER lag will resolve asymmetrically: providing 60-80bp of CPI relief by Q3-Q4 2026, but not completing the journey to pre-pandemic norms.** The Zillow Observed Rent Index (ZORI) year-over-year decelerated from ~16% peak (2022) to ~3.0-3.5% by late 2025. The BLS CPI shelter component lags this by 12-18 months. Mechanical pass-through projects shelter CPI declining from ~5.0% to ~3.5-4.0% by late 2026 — helpful but insufficient to drive headline to 2.0% target. Post-pandemic steady-state shelter inflation is structurally higher (~3.5-4.0% vs. pre-pandemic ~3.0%) due to persistent housing supply deficit (3.5-5.5 million units by various estimates) and the mortgage lock-in freezing existing inventory.

4. **Goods disinflation has largely exhausted its disinflationary contribution, and the next marginal move is toward re-inflation from deglobalization and tariff pass-through.** Goods CPI went from +7.0% (2022 peak) to approximately -1% to 0% (2025), representing the normalization of supply chains and inventory cycles. This ~7-8pp swing was the primary driver of the "immaculate disinflation" narrative. Further goods deflation requires either: (a) renewed China export deflation (unlikely given CNY depreciation pressures and industrial overcapacity policy shifts), or (b) consumer demand destruction. Meanwhile, tariff escalation (effective rate now ~15-20% vs. ~3% pre-2018) creates an asymmetric upside skew to goods prices with 0.3-0.8pp direct CPI impact depending on pass-through assumptions and scope.

5. **The breakeven inflation market is mispriced in the belly of the curve (2Y-5Y) relative to the structural inflation regime.** 5Y breakevens at ~2.3-2.5% price a successful return to 2% target with modest risk premium. This is inconsistent with: (a) the transmission impairment documented above, (b) structural goods re-inflation from deglobalization, (c) services stickiness from housing supply constraints, and (d) the Fed's revealed preference ceiling of ~5.0-5.5% FFR. Fair value for 5Y breakevens under these conditions is ~2.6-3.0%, implying 15-50bp of cheapening. By contrast, 5Y5Y forward breakevens at ~2.3% are approximately fair, reflecting long-run credibility that remains intact.

6. **Wage-price spiral risk is low (<10% probability) but the conditions that would trigger one are precisely the conditions under which the transmission mechanism cannot respond.** A wage-price spiral requires the conjunction of: (a) inflation expectations de-anchoring (sustained 5Y breakeven >3.0% AND Michigan 5-10Y >4.0%), (b) unit labor costs growing >4% persistently, (c) pricing power sufficient for firms to pass through cost increases, AND (d) accommodative monetary policy (φ_π < 1.0). Currently, (a) is not breached, (b) is borderline at ~3.0-3.5%, (c) is mixed (goods weak, services strong), and (d) is narrowly satisfied. The critical vulnerability: if fiscal dominance constrains the FFR ceiling at ~5.0-5.5%, and an exogenous supply shock pushes inflation to 4-5%, the Fed may be unable to satisfy the Taylor Principle, generating the precise conditions for spiral dynamics.

7. **The structural vs. cyclical inflation decomposition reveals that ~60-70% of remaining above-target inflation is structural (supply-side), not cyclical (demand-side), rendering monetary policy's toolkit fundamentally mismatched to the problem.** Structural components include: housing supply deficit (~0.5-0.8pp contribution), deglobalization/tariff regime (~0.3-0.5pp), healthcare/insurance cost dynamics (~0.2-0.3pp), and green transition energy price floors (~0.1-0.2pp). Cyclical components include: remaining labor market tightness (~0.2-0.3pp) and residual pandemic fiscal impulse (~0.1-0.2pp). Monetary policy effectively targets only the cyclical component (0.3-0.5pp of the ~0.8-1.5pp above-target gap).

8. **AI-driven productivity gains will NOT meaningfully lower services inflation within the Fed's policy-relevant horizon (2-3 years), but WILL create a false sense of disinflationary security that leads to premature easing.** The AI-to-aggregate-productivity transmission lag is 5-15 years (per KB concept ai_productivity_transmission_lag, confidence 8.0). The services sector — healthcare, education, government, housing services — has the lowest AI penetration rates and the strongest regulatory/institutional barriers to adoption. Yet forward guidance and dot-plot construction already implicitly embed AI-optimism via lower r* estimates. This creates a specific mis-calibration risk: the Fed eases on AI-disinflation hopes → financial conditions loosen → asset prices rally → wealth effect stimulates demand → last-mile inflation proves stickier → credibility erosion.

9. **Global monetary policy divergence is creating a dollar-strength channel that partially offsets US inflation but exports it to EM economies, generating a feedback loop that eventually constrains US policy.** The Fed is at ~4.5-5.0% while the ECB is at ~3.0-3.5%, BoJ at ~0.25-0.50%, and PBoC at ~3.0-3.5%. This 100-200bp differential supports USD strength, which: (a) reduces US import prices by ~0.3-0.5pp (disinflationary for US), (b) increases EM food and energy costs (inflationary for EM, per food_inflation_em_instability concept), (c) forces EM central banks to maintain restrictive policy even as growth weakens, and (d) eventually creates financial stability risks that boomerang via capital flow reversals and Fed financial stability mandate activation.

10. **The Fed's reaction function has a revealed asymmetry that biases the long-run inflation outcome above target: the pain threshold for tightening (unemployment >4.5-5.0%) is lower than the gain threshold for easing (inflation <2.5%).** This means the Fed will cut before inflation returns to target but will not hike until inflation is sustainably above 3.0-3.5%. The implied steady-state outcome is inflation fluctuating in a 2.3-3.0% band — a de facto regime shift from the 1.5-2.5% band of the pre-pandemic era — that the Fed accommodates via moving goalposts (flexible average inflation targeting → "broadly consistent with 2%").

11. **The breakeven term structure slope is the single most informative real-time indicator of inflation regime transitions, and it is currently signaling a regime that markets are not pricing.** An inverted breakeven term structure (short > long) signals transitory passthrough. A flat-to-upward-sloping term structure signals persistent passthrough and second-round effects risk. The current configuration — slightly inverted at the very front end but flattening through the belly — is consistent with a "bumpy last mile" regime where near-term disinflation stalls and medium-term inflation settles above target. This is inconsistent with the market's pricing of a clean return to 2%.

12. **The BoJ normalization is an underpriced exogenous shock to the global inflation regime framework.** If the BoJ continues normalizing (25-50bp by end-2026), Japanese investors — holding ~$1.1T in US Treasuries and ~$0.5T in European bonds — will repatriate capital as domestic yields become competitive. This creates term premium pressure in UST markets independent of Fed policy, tightening financial conditions through a channel the Fed does not control. Simultaneously, JPY appreciation would end the yen carry trade, which has been a significant source of global liquidity and financial conditions easing.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Transmission-Impaired Last Mile
The evidence base for broken transmission is now among the strongest in the KB (confidence 7.33 and rising). The Goldman Sachs FCI eased ~100bp despite the most aggressive hiking cycle since Volcker. The channel-by-channel diagnostic (from KB framework) shows: interest rate channel at 60% (mortgage lock-in), credit channel at 80% (private credit substitution), asset price channel at **negative** effectiveness (AI rally added $10-15T market cap producing $300-750B in wealth effects that exceeded the rate tightening impulse), exchange rate channel at 50%, expectations channel at 50-70%. The Taylor coefficient φ_π at 1.0-1.3 (per KB concept, confidence 8.0 from iter_0019) barely satisfies the Taylor Principle. The fiscal dominance constraint (per fiscal_dominance_five_signal_sequence, 2.0-2.5 of 5 signals triggered) creates an implicit rate ceiling. These converge to a regime where the Fed cannot generate sufficient restriction to close the last-mile gap through conventional channels.

### Claim 2: Services Inflation as Transmission Failure
The standard narrative treats services inflation persistence as a "sticky prices" puzzle. The transmission lens reveals it is a consequence of impaired channels. The interest rate channel's 60% pass-through means the effective policy rate for housing decisions is ~3.0-3.5% equivalent, not the 5.0-5.5% FFR. The labor market channel (wage → services price) is operating at moderate effectiveness but is offset by the wealth channel: the $10-15T in AI-driven equity gains has sustained consumer spending on services (restaurants, travel, healthcare) even as rate-sensitive sectors like autos showed some cooling. Housing services specifically are supply-constrained: the National Association of Realtors estimates 3.5-5.5M unit deficit, and the lock-in effect means existing homeowners face a 3-4pp rate penalty for moving, suppressing listing volumes by ~25-35% from pre-pandemic norms.

### Claim 3: Shelter/OER Lag Resolution
The BLS shelter methodology uses the rental equivalence approach with a 6-month reference period and geometric weighting that creates a 12-18 month lag to market rents. The Zillow and CoreLogic indices showed rent deceleration beginning in mid-2023, which is now passing through to CPI shelter with a lag. However, the "steady state" to which shelter inflation converges is not the pre-pandemic 3.0% but rather 3.5-4.0%, reflecting: (a) structural housing undersupply constraining vacancy rates at ~6.5% vs. equilibrium ~7.0-7.5%, (b) construction cost inflation from labor shortages and regulatory burden (permitting timelines extended ~30% since 2019), (c) insurance cost escalation (homeowners insurance +12-15% y/y in 2024-25) passing through to rent equivalence. The asymmetric resolution means shelter provides relief from 5.0% to ~3.5-4.0%, not from 5.0% to 3.0%.

### Claim 4: Goods Disinflation Exhaustion
Goods CPI normalization was driven by three one-time factors: (a) supply chain normalization (Global Supply Chain Pressure Index returned to zero by mid-2023), (b) inventory cycle (retail inventories surged in 2022-23 as orders placed during shortages arrived), (c) China export deflation (PPI -3 to -5% in 2023-24 transmitted via import prices). All three are exhausted or reversing. The tariff regime represents a structural shift: the effective tariff rate has risen from ~3% (pre-2018) to ~15-20% across multiple administrations, with potential further escalation to 25-40% under announced but partially-implemented plans. Historical pass-through of tariffs to consumer prices is 0.3-0.5x within 12-18 months (Amiti, Redding, Weinstein 2019; Fajgelbaum et al. 2020). At 25% tariff on $400-500B of Chinese goods plus 10-25% on broader imports, direct CPI impact is 0.3-0.8pp. The deglobalization/friend-shoring trend adds ~0.2-0.4pp structurally as production shifts from lowest-cost to geopolitically-aligned locations.

### Claim 5: Breakeven Mispricing
5Y breakevens at 2.3-2.5% imply CPI averaging ~2.3-2.5% over the next 5 years. For this to be realized, headline CPI must decline from current ~2.8-3.2% to ~2.0-2.2% within 2 years and remain there. This requires: (a) shelter contributing -0.5 to -0.8pp relief (plausible but partial — see Claim 3), (b) goods remaining flat-to-deflationary (increasingly unlikely — see Claim 4), (c) services ex-shelter decelerating (requires labor market loosening that the Fed's asymmetric reaction function may not produce — see Claim 10). The risk-neutral probability embedded in breakevens assigns ~70-75% to this benign outcome. My transmission-informed estimate is ~35-45%. The 5Y breakeven should carry a structural risk premium of ~20-40bp above target expectations, implying fair value of 2.6-3.0%.

### Claim 6: Wage-Price Spiral Conjunctive Conditions
The KB concept wage_price_spiral_conjunctive_conditions identifies four necessary conditions. The critical analytical point is that these conditions are conjunctive (all must hold), not disjunctive. The base probability is therefore the product: P(de-anchoring) × P(ULC > 4%) × P(pricing power) × P(accommodative policy). Current estimates: 0.15 × 0.30 × 0.40 × 0.50 ≈ 0.009. Even with generous uncertainty bands (doubling each), the probability remains <10%. However, the tail risk is non-negligible because fiscal dominance creates a specific scenario where P(accommodative policy) jumps to ~0.80: if the FFR is constrained at 5.0-5.5% and a supply shock pushes inflation to 4-5%, the effective φ_π drops below 1.0, meeting the accommodation condition. This is the "fiscal dominance spiral" scenario — low probability, high consequence.

### Claim 7: Structural vs. Cyclical Decomposition
Decomposition methodology uses the Shapiro (2022) approach (classifying PCE components as supply- vs. demand-driven based on the sign of quantity and price co-movement), supplemented by the Bernanke-Blanchard (2023) framework that decomposes inflation into labor market, supply chain, and energy contributions. Both approaches converge on the finding that demand-driven inflation peaked in mid-2022 and has largely normalized, while supply-driven inflation remains elevated due to structural factors. The specific contributions I attribute: housing supply deficit creates a persistent wedge of ~0.5-0.8pp above the 2% target through constrained vacancy rates and construction shortfalls; deglobalization/tariffs add ~0.3-0.5pp through higher import prices and supply chain restructuring costs; healthcare cost dynamics contribute ~0.2-0.3pp as insurer pricing reflects 2021-23 utilization surges with 18-24 month lags; green transition adds ~0.1-0.2pp through energy price floors (carbon pricing, renewable mandates displacing cheapest fossil fuels). Cyclical components (labor market tightness ~0.2-0.3pp, residual fiscal ~0.1-0.2pp) total only 0.3-0.5pp and are the only portion directly addressable by monetary policy.

### Claim 8: AI Productivity Mirage
The KB documents the AI-to-aggregate-productivity transmission lag at 5-15 years with high confidence (8.0). The services sector specifically faces the longest transmission lags because: (a) healthcare — the largest services sector — is regulated, liability-constrained, and requires clinical validation pipelines that add 3-7 years to any productivity-enhancing technology deployment; (b) education is institutionally conservative with tenure-based labor structures resistant to headcount reduction; (c) government services face procurement and union constraints; (d) housing services productivity is physically bounded (can't make existing apartments larger via AI). The risk I identify is not that AI won't eventually be disinflationary — it likely will at the 10-15 year horizon — but that the Fed incorporates AI-optimism into its r* estimates and forward guidance prematurely. The March 2024 SEP already showed a downward drift in long-run rate estimates that partially reflects AI productivity assumptions. If the Fed eases based on AI-optimism, financial conditions loosen through the equity channel (the one channel that is already operating in reverse), generating a pro-cyclical wealth effect that sustains demand and makes the last mile harder.

### Claim 9: Global Divergence Feedback
The policy rate differentials are well-documented. The transmission-specific insight is that global divergence creates a dollar strength channel that partially substitutes for domestic tightening (import price deflation) but at the cost of exporting inflation to EM economies. Per KB concept food_inflation_em_instability (confidence 8.0), food comprises 30-50% of EM CPI with 70-90% pass-through in the most vulnerable economies. USD strength → EM currency weakness → food/energy inflation → political pressure → policy error → further depreciation. This eventually boomerangs to the US through: (a) financial stability risks if EM distress triggers capital flow reversals, (b) reduced EM demand for US exports, (c) Fed activation of the financial stability mandate (per modified_taylor_rule_credit_term KB concept) which creates dovish pressure independent of the inflation mandate. The BoJ normalization (Claim 12) adds a specific vector: if Japanese investors repatriate even 10-15% of their ~$1.6T in foreign bond holdings, the term premium impact on USTs would be 15-30bp — equivalent to the entire estimated QT term premium effect.

### Claim 10: Asymmetric Reaction Function
The revealed preference evidence: The Fed began signaling rate cuts in late 2023 with core PCE still at ~3.2%, unemployment at ~3.7%, and growth above trend. Compare with the tightening trigger: the Fed delayed hiking until March 2022 with core PCE at ~5.2% and unemployment at ~3.8%. The asymmetry is ~200bp: the easing trigger engages at ~100bp above target while the tightening trigger only engages at ~300bp above target. This is consistent with the institutional incentives: the political cost of recession (direct, visible, attributed to the Fed) exceeds the political cost of moderately above-target inflation (diffuse, partially attributable to supply factors). The steady-state implication: over multi-year cycles, inflation will spend more time above 2.5% than below 1.5%, producing a long-run average of ~2.3-2.8% rather than the target 2.0%. This is a de facto regime shift that the FOMC will not acknowledge but will accommodate through language evolution ("broadly consistent with our mandate" → "substantial progress toward 2%").

### Claim 11: Breakeven Term Structure as Regime Indicator
The breakeven term structure shape encodes market-implied beliefs about inflation persistence. An inverted structure (1Y BE > 5Y BE) indicates markets expect current inflation to be transitory. A flat or upward-sloping structure indicates markets expect current inflation to persist or intensify. The current configuration — slight inversion at the very front end (1Y BE at ~2.5-2.8%, reflecting near-term shelter resolution) but flat through 2Y-5Y (~2.3-2.5%) — is internally inconsistent. If shelter resolution provides genuine relief, the term structure should be more steeply inverted (1Y < 2Y < 5Y by 20-30bp). The flatness through the belly signals that the market is pricing shelter relief as a level shift, not a trend — i.e., inflation falls from 3% to 2.5% but doesn't continue to 2.0%. This is consistent with my structural decomposition (Claim 7) but inconsistent with the level of 5Y breakevens (Claim 5), suggesting either the term structure or the level is mispriced.

### Claim 12: BoJ Normalization as Exogenous Shock
Japanese investors hold ~$1.1T in US Treasuries (largest foreign holder) and ~$0.5T in European bonds. The BoJ has begun normalizing from -0.1% to ~0.25-0.50%, with market pricing suggesting 50-75bp terminal by end-2026. At current JGB yields (~0.8-1.0% for 10Y), the hedged return on USTs for Japanese investors is approximately zero or negative after FX hedging costs (~4.5-5.0% for 3M basis swap). Repatriation has been gradual (~$50-80B in 2024-25) but would accelerate if: (a) JGB 10Y reaches ~1.5%, providing competitive domestic alternative, (b) BoJ formally abandons YCC successor framework, or (c) JPY appreciates past ~140/USD, generating FX losses on unhedged positions. A coordinated repatriation of 10-15% of foreign holdings (~$160-240B) over 12 months would add ~15-30bp of term premium to USTs, tightening US financial conditions through a channel completely outside Fed control and potentially exacerbating the "last mile" problem by slowing growth without reducing inflation.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Monetary Policy (directly connected, KB confidence 5.6)
This analysis is fundamentally an assessment of why the monetary policy transmission mechanism is failing to deliver the final leg of disinflation. The key bridge concepts: broken_monetary_transmission, extended_monetary_transmission_lags, and the fiscal dominance constraint all directly connect the inflation regime to the monetary policy topic. **Specific new connection**: the asymmetric reaction function (Claim 10) implies that monetary policy itself is a source of inflationary bias, not just a tool to fight inflation — a recursive relationship that the monetary_policy topic should incorporate.

### Commodity Inflation Transmission (KB confidence 7.0)
Goods disinflation exhaustion (Claim 4) directly connects to commodity supercycle dynamics (iter_0022). If we are in the early stages of a commodity supercycle (as iter_0022 suggests is plausible), the goods disinflation that powered the 2022-2024 immaculate disinflation becomes goods RE-inflation — a regime change for which breakeven markets are unprepared. The biofuel_mandate_inflation_ratchet concept (KB, confidence 6.0) creates a structural floor for agricultural commodity prices that feeds directly into the structural inflation decomposition (Claim 7). **Specific new connection**: the working_capital_inflation_channel means commodity re-inflation transmits to corporate cash flows faster than to reported earnings, creating a stealth tightening channel that could partially substitute for the impaired interest rate channel.

### Labor Market Dynamics (KB confidence 6.5)
The wage-price spiral analysis (Claim 6) depends critically on labor market tightness and unit labor cost dynamics. The structural vs. cyclical decomposition (Claim 7) attributes ~0.2-0.3pp of above-target inflation to labor market tightness — the primary cyclical component that monetary policy can address. **Specific new connection**: if AI adoption eventually reduces services sector labor demand (the 5-15 year horizon), the initial impact may be disinflationary through wages but inflationary through the political channel (populist response → fiscal expansion → demand-driven inflation). This creates a non-monotonic relationship between AI adoption and inflation.

### Deglobalization (KB confidence 6.0)
Tariff pass-through and supply chain restructuring costs are central to Claim 4 (goods disinflation exhaustion) and Claim 7 (structural inflation decomposition). Deglobalization is the primary swing variable for whether goods contribute +0.3-0.5pp or -0.2-0.0pp to inflation over the next 3-5 years. **Specific new connection**: deglobalization → higher import prices → stronger CPI → tighter Fed → stronger dollar → more EM pressure → more deglobalization pressure (as EM countries pursue self-sufficiency). This is a positive feedback loop that could shift the structural inflation floor higher over a multi-year horizon.

### Stock-Bond Correlation (iter_0019/0020, KB confidence varies)
The inflation regime is the primary determinant of correlation regime. Per KB concept taylor_rule_inflation_coefficient (confidence 8.0), φ_π > 1.5 generates negative correlation (bonds hedge), φ_π < 1.0 generates positive correlation (bonds amplify). My estimate of φ_π ≈ 1.0-1.3 places us in the "danger zone" where the correlation regime is unstable and regime-dependent — directly connecting this inflation regime analysis to the correlation framework. **Specific implication**: the asymmetric reaction function (Claim 10) means the correlation regime will also be asymmetric — more likely positive correlation during inflation scares (Fed slow to tighten) and negative correlation during growth scares (Fed quick to ease).

### Fiscal Dominance (KB framework, 2.0-2.5/5 signals triggered)
The fiscal dominance constraint is the binding mechanism that prevents the Fed from closing the last mile through brute force rate hikes. The self-referential interest rate channel (each 100bp adds ~$250-320B debt service, offsetting 10-25% of contractionary intent) means that aggressive tightening partially finances itself. **Specific new connection**: the inflation regime determines whether fiscal dominance worsens — above-target inflation that isn't controlled by monetary policy allows nominal GDP growth to partially offset debt dynamics, creating a perverse equilibrium where "tolerable" above-target inflation serves both fiscal sustainability and financial stability objectives. This may be the implicit equilibrium the Fed is navigating toward.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the true steady-state shelter/OER inflation rate in the post-pandemic housing market?** I estimate 3.5-4.0%, but this depends on housing supply elasticity, construction technology, and demographic trends (millennial household formation peaking vs. immigration policy) that I cannot precisely calibrate. A 0.5pp error in this estimate changes the structural inflation decomposition materially.

2. **How will the Fed framework review (expected 2025-2026) modify the reaction function?** If the review produces a formal acknowledgment that 2% is a "range" (e.g., 1.5-2.5%), this would validate my asymmetric reaction function claim and make the de facto regime shift explicit. If the review reaffirms strict 2% targeting, the credibility test becomes whether the Fed actually tightens to achieve it — and at what cost.

3. **What is the true tariff pass-through elasticity in a world of concentrated retail (Walmart, Amazon, Costco) with significant monopsony power?** Academic estimates of 0.3-0.5x pass-through are based on pre-2020 market structures. The concentration of retail may either increase pass-through (fewer competitors to undercut) or decrease it (retailers absorb margin compression to maintain volume). This is a first-order uncertainty for the goods re-inflation thesis.

4. **Can the Fed credibly tighten financial conditions through macro-prudential tools if the interest rate channel remains impaired?** Potential tools include: tighter bank capital requirements (Basel III endgame), margin requirements, LTV limits, or direct guidance to GSEs. If these tools are effective, they represent an alternative transmission channel that could resolve the last-mile problem without higher rates. If they are politically infeasible or too slow, the transmission impairment persists.

5. **What is the threshold at which inflation expectations de-anchor irreversibly vs. remaining in a "stretched but anchored" state?** The Coibion-Gorodnichenko threshold (75-100bp sustained divergence for >6 months) is the best available estimate, but it has never been tested in a regime with social media amplification of price signals, algorithmic price-setting by retailers, and real-time inflation data (Truflation, PriceStats) that could accelerate expectations formation. The de-anchoring process may be faster and more binary than historical analogues suggest.

6. **How does the structural inflation decomposition change if AI productivity gains materialize faster than the 5-15 year base case?** My framework assigns low probability to this, but if (for example) AI radically reduces healthcare administrative costs within 3 years, the structural component could shrink meaningfully. This is the key downside risk to my above-target inflation thesis.

7. **Is the BoJ normalization impact priced into the UST term premium already?** The gradual nature of BoJ normalization means markets may have already adjusted positioning. If so, the exogenous shock thesis (Claim 12) is weaker. The counter-argument is that markets have priced BoJ normalization multiple times and been wrong (reversed), so the actual event carries additional term premium because of the embedded "this time is different" discount.

8. **How should we decompose the information content of breakeven inflation when TIPS liquidity is structurally impaired?** TIPS bid-ask spreads have widened post-2022, and the TIPS market is dominated by institutional holders with specific hedging mandates (pension funds, insurance companies) that may not reflect marginal inflation views. If breakeven levels are partially driven by liquidity premia rather than inflation expectations, my mispricing claim (Claim 5) needs to be adjusted for the liquidity component.
