## HIGH_CONFIDENCE

### 1. Services inflation persistence is the central regime problem, with no historical precedent for resolution without recession or productivity revolution

**Confidence: 9/10**

**Originating agents:** generalist_01 (9/10), generalist_02 (8/10), macro_strategist_01 (8/10), rates_strategist_02 (7/10), credit_analyst_01 (8/10), credit_analyst_02 (6/10)

**Surviving evidence:** generalist_02's base-rate finding is the anchor: in 5 completed post-1960 episodes of elevated services inflation, the rate for services CPI returning to ≤3% without a recession is **0/5**. The two partial successes (1991, 2008) both required recessions opening significant output gaps. macro_strategist_01 provides the theoretical underpinning via the NKPC with Calvo pricing — services unit labor cost growth at ~3.0-4.0% fully explains persistence at ~3.5-4.0%, and resolution requires either wage deceleration (labor market softening), productivity acceleration (AI, but timeline too long), or margin compression (limited remaining scope). The agents disagree on *mechanism* (labor costs vs. transmission failure vs. PE pricing power) but converge on the *fact* of persistence and its dominance in the inflation outlook. The pair_0 debate confirmed this as the strongest joint finding. Services ex-shelter at ~4-5% is the binding constraint, not broad-based inflationary pressure.

### 2. Goods disinflation is essentially complete, with asymmetric risk tilted toward re-inflation

**Confidence: 8/10**

**Originating agents:** generalist_01 (9/10), generalist_02 (8/10), macro_strategist_01 (7/10), rates_strategist_02 (7/10), credit_analyst_01 (8/10), commodities_analyst_02 (7/10)

**Surviving evidence:** Core goods CPI at -1% to +0.5% represents the resolution of identifiable supply shocks (supply chain normalization, inventory restocking, energy mean-reversion). generalist_02's three-phase sequencing template places the economy in "late Phase 2 / early Phase 3" where goods disinflation has exhausted and services persistence becomes the dominant residual — a pattern consistent across all 6 historical episodes studied. The next marginal move in goods is upward from tariffs (0.3-0.8pp direct CPI impact depending on scope per Amiti-Redding-Weinstein 2019 calibration), deglobalization friction, and structural commodity price floors. credit_analyst_01 adds a critical distinction: supply-normalization disinflation (2022-24) compresses credit spreads, while demand-destruction disinflation widens them — the current market is not discriminating between these mechanisms. The pair_0 debate validated the framing against a biased extrapolation of 2022-24 as template.

### 3. Shelter/OER lag resolution will provide 40-80bp of mechanical CPI relief by late 2026, but this is necessary and insufficient for returning to 2%

**Confidence: 9/10**

**Originating agents:** macro_strategist_01 (9/10), generalist_02 (8/10), generalist_01 (7/10), rates_strategist_02 (7/10), credit_analyst_01, credit_analyst_02 (7/10)

**Surviving evidence:** The BLS methodology lag of 12-18 months is well-documented and largely mechanical. Private rent indices (Zillow at ~3.0-3.5% YoY, CoreLogic similar) lead CPI shelter by this lag. With shelter at ~36% of CPI, deceleration from ~5.0% to ~3.5-4.0% contributes 40-80bp of headline disinflation (agents' estimates converge on this range). All four debates confirmed agreement. generalist_02 provides the critical historical warning: the 1983 and 1995 precedents show OER-driven disinflation masked ongoing services ex-shelter inflation that re-emerged once the measurement lag expired. rates_strategist_02 adds that steady-state shelter inflation is structurally higher than pre-pandemic (~3.5-4.0% vs. 3.0%) due to housing supply deficit (3.5-5.5M units), mortgage lock-in freezing turnover, and insurance cost escalation. **The signpost to watch: "mission accomplished" narrative once core CPI prints below 2.5% on OER drag — this is the 1995 trap.**

### 4. Wage-price spiral risk is low (<10-15% probability) due to absent institutional prerequisites

**Confidence: 9/10**

**Originating agents:** challenger_02 (8/10), macro_strategist_01 (8/10), generalist_01 (8/10), generalist_02 (7/10), rates_strategist_02 (8/10), credit_analyst_01 (7/10)

**Surviving evidence:** Universal convergence across all agents and all four debates. The conjunctive conditions framework requires: (a) unemployment sustained below ~3.8%, (b) wage growth >5%, (c) pricing power for full pass-through, (d) expectations de-anchoring. Currently 0/3-4 conditions met. challenger_02's historical falsification is definitive: true wage-price spirals (where real wage growth exceeds productivity AND firms pass through AND expectations ratchet iteratively) have occurred only twice in the US post-war period (1966-69, 1973-75). Institutional prerequisites are absent: union density ~10% vs. 25-30% in the 1970s, COLA coverage <10% vs. >60%. The 2021-23 natural experiment — where 2/3 conditions were temporarily met without triggering a spiral — provides additional validation. **However, this does NOT mean inflation persistence is impossible** — it operates through alternative channels (fiscal, commodity, shelter, expectations drift) that do not require the spiral mechanism.

### 5. AI-driven productivity gains will not resolve services inflation within the 2026-2028 policy-relevant horizon

**Confidence: 8/10**

**Originating agents:** macro_strategist_01 (6/10), generalist_02 (6/10), rates_strategist_02 (8/10), generalist_01 (6/10), credit_analyst_01 (7/10)

**Surviving evidence:** Five agents converge, all four debates confirmed agreement. generalist_02 provides the tightest evidence: three major productivity revolutions with measurable services CPI impact (1920s electrification, 1990s IT, 2010s mobile/cloud) each required 5-8 years from broad enterprise adoption. Even compressing the historical lag by 50% yields 2027 at the edge of relevance. macro_strategist_01 formalizes this via the Solow paradox/Brynjolfsson J-curve: the current "installation phase" generates investment demand (inflationary) before productivity supply (disinflationary). The net near-term effect of AI on aggregate inflation is ambiguously neutral-to-positive, not negative. rates_strategist_02 adds the most original implication: AI-optimism may lead to premature Fed easing via embedded lower r* estimates, with the equity wealth channel (the one channel operating in reverse) amplifying the demand effect. The pair_0 debate correctly noted the macro-inflationary claim is overstated ($150B capex = 0.5% GDP, too small to move the needle alone), but the near-term disinflation claim is equally unsupported.

### 6. Inflation passthrough is regime-dependent and non-linear (bimodal)

**Confidence: 9/10**

**Originating agents:** commodities_analyst_02 (7/10), credit_analyst_01 (7/10), generalist_01 (7/10), credit_analyst_02 (7/10) | **Prior KB:** confidence 9

**Surviving evidence:** This builds on the highest-confidence prior finding. commodities_analyst_02 quantifies the food channel: a 30% agricultural shock produces 0.3-0.5pp in DM under anchored conditions vs. 0.8-1.5pp under unanchored — a 2-3x regime multiplier. credit_analyst_01 frames the same insight through credit: moderate inflation (2-4%) is credit-neutral-to-positive, but above 4% becomes credit-destructive as central bank response overwhelms nominal growth. generalist_01 applies it to breakevens: passthrough is 0.1-0.3pp when anchored vs. 0.5-0.8pp when de-anchored. The pair_1 debate confirmed this as one of the strongest joint findings — linear models are structurally wrong. The practical implication: the current "anchored but strained" regime means small shocks can produce outsized effects if they push the system across the regime boundary.

## MODERATE_CONFIDENCE

### 7. The effective Taylor Rule coefficient has declined to ~1.0-1.3, near the indeterminacy boundary

**Confidence: 7/10**

**Originating agents:** rates_strategist_02 (8/10), macro_strategist_01 (7/10), challenger_02 (7/10)

**Surviving evidence:** Revealed-preference extraction: the Fed began cutting in September 2024 with core PCE at ~2.7%. The pace and forward guidance imply an effective φ_π of 1.0-1.3, down from 1.8-2.0 during the hiking cycle. The Clarida-Galí-Gertler framework shows φ_π < 1 generates multiple equilibria. challenger_02 correctly warns this is not a stable structural parameter — it varies by estimation period, cycle position, and FOMC composition. The pair_3 debate resolved this productively: the *direction* matters even if the point estimate is imprecise, and the probability of φ_π > 1.5 is low under current revealed preferences. However, the Fed's state-contingent reaction function (aggressive when inflation high, accommodative when near target) means the indeterminacy result from time-invariant models overstates the risk. **Downgraded from Agent A's 8/10 because the asymmetric reaction function partially addresses the indeterminacy concern.**

### 8. Structural inflation has shifted upward by 30-80bp (new effective floor ~2.3-2.8% core PCE)

**Confidence: 6/10**

**Originating agents:** generalist_01 (7/10), macro_strategist_01 (5/10), generalist_02 (5/10), rates_strategist_02 (5/10)

**Surviving evidence:** Four agents converge directionally but with wide uncertainty bands. Enumerated structural components: housing supply deficit (~10-20bp per macro_strategist_01), deglobalization/tariffs (~10-20bp), healthcare/insurance dynamics (~10-15bp). The ranges overlap: generalist_01 at 50-100bp, macro_strategist_01 at 30-50bp, generalist_02 at ~40bp (40% structural × ~100bp above target). The pair_0 debate correctly noted generalist_02's methodology is cruder but more honest about uncertainty, while generalist_01 is more actionable. **The direction is high-confidence (~8/10) — some structural shift has occurred. The magnitude is genuinely uncertain (~5/10), hence the composite.** The decomposition methodology (Beveridge-Nelson, UC-SV, analogue-weighted) produces wide confidence intervals (+/-20-30bp). challenger_02's warning about overconfidence in point estimates applies directly here.

### 9. Breakeven inflation markets underprice right-tail inflation risk, though the point-estimate magnitude is uncertain

**Confidence: 6/10**

**Originating agents:** generalist_01 (7/10), macro_strategist_01 (6/10), rates_strategist_02 (6/10), generalist_02 (6/10)

**Surviving evidence:** Four agents argue breakevens are directionally too low. The strongest argument is macro_strategist_01's Fisher decomposition showing a negative inflation risk premium (-10 to -20bp) inconsistent with the asymmetric upside risk from tariffs, fiscal trajectory, and Phillips Curve convexity. generalist_02's probability-weighted analogue analysis yields expected 5Y breakeven of ~2.33% (approximately current pricing) but with bimodal distribution — making the *optionality* cheap even if the *level* is approximately fair. **Both debates that addressed this (pair_0, pair_3) converged: the directional underpricing of right-tail protection survives, but the specific magnitude (15-50bp per rates_strategist_02) does not survive challenger_02's liquidity premium critique (TIPS liquidity premium ranges -80bp to +40bp, making any level claim fragile).** The actionable conclusion: inflation optionality offers asymmetric risk/reward regardless of the point-estimate debate.

### 10. The Phillips Curve is operating in its convex region, making last-mile disinflation disproportionately costly

**Confidence: 6/10**

**Originating agents:** macro_strategist_01 (6/10), credit_analyst_02 (8/10 on covenant-lite aspect), rates_strategist_02 (connected)

**Surviving evidence:** macro_strategist_01 provides the theoretical grounding (Ball-Mankiw-Romer 1988, Hazell et al. 2022): near full employment, the sacrifice ratio rises from 1.5-2.0 (for 3.0%→2.5%) to 3.0-5.0 (for 2.5%→2.0%). credit_analyst_02 provides the complementary credit-channel explanation: covenant-lite (>90% of leveraged loans) with 15-40% EBITDA addbacks delays the default-transmission channel by 12-24 months, extending the period of above-target inflation. The pair_2 debate found these explanations complementary, not competing. **Confidence limited by identification problem: the "immaculate disinflation" could alternatively reflect a flat curve with supply normalization rather than a convex curve.** challenger_02's open question about whether the Phillips Curve slope is itself endogenous to the anchoring state deepens the uncertainty.

### 11. The credit maturity wall ($900B-$1.5T, 2025-2028) is inflation-regime-dependent and underpriced at current spreads

**Confidence: 7/10**

**Originating agents:** credit_analyst_01 (8/10), credit_analyst_02 (9/10 on CLO mechanics, 7/10 on maturity wall)

**Surviving evidence:** The maturity wall is near-arithmetic certainty. Refinancing math: median B-rated credit issued at 5.5-7.0% faces refinancing at 8.5-11.0%, a 40-60% cash interest expense increase pushing coverage below 1.5x (historical default acceleration threshold). credit_analyst_02 adds the CLO plumbing: inflation regime determines whether CLO arbitrage works (SOFR ~3.5% = viable) or breaks (SOFR ~5.5% = dead), and post-reinvestment CLO expirations ($250-350B over 18 months) remove the marginal bid for ~65-70% of institutional leveraged loans. credit_analyst_01's scenario-weighted default rate (4.3% expected vs. 2.5% market-implied) suggests meaningful mispricing, though the pair_1 debate correctly noted the specific gap is sensitive to subjective probability weights. **The binary framing (credit_analyst_02's "no intermediate outcome") was refuted in pair_2 — partial shelter normalization + modest Fed cuts is a plausible intermediate path. But the directional mispricing claim survives.**

### 12. The Kalecki fiscal channel (deficits at 6-7% GDP) is partially offsetting monetary tightening and suppressing default rates

**Confidence: 6/10**

**Originating agents:** generalist_01 (8/10), credit_analyst_01 (6/10)

**Surviving evidence:** The accounting identity is unassailable: Profits = Investment + Deficits + Net Exports - Savings. Federal deficits at ~$1.8-2.0T annually mechanically support corporate revenues. generalist_01 quantifies the offset: every 100bp of rate hikes generates ~$250-320B in additional government interest payments, of which 10-25% recycles as spending. credit_analyst_01 estimates 100-150bp of spread suppression, though the pair_1 debate correctly flagged this as residual-based attribution with false precision (could be 50bp or 200bp). The pair_0 debate identified this as generalist_01's most important unique contribution — a concrete mechanism missing from historical analogues. **Confidence limited because the magnitude is model-dependent and the KB rates underlying concepts at 6-7/10.**

### 13. The Fed's reaction function has a revealed asymmetry biasing long-run inflation above target

**Confidence: 7/10**

**Originating agents:** rates_strategist_02 (7/10), generalist_02 (connected via 1966-69 analogue)

**Surviving evidence:** rates_strategist_02's revealed-preference evidence: easing signaled at core PCE ~3.2% (2023), tightening delayed until ~5.2% (2022) — a ~200bp asymmetry. The institutional incentive is clear: political cost of recession exceeds political cost of moderately above-target inflation. The steady-state implication: inflation fluctuates in a 2.3-3.0% band rather than the target 2.0%. generalist_02's 1966-69 analogue reinforces this — premature easing during an inflation stall produced a second wave 3/3 times historically. The pair_3 debate validated this as surviving challenger_02's critiques because it's based on observable Fed behavior, not estimated parameters. **This may be the most policy-relevant finding in the entire synthesis: the 2% target may be operationally non-binding.**

### 14. The 1966-69 "first wave" is the most relevant historical analogue, though institutional differences limit direct applicability

**Confidence: 6/10**

**Originating agents:** generalist_02 (7/10)

**Surviving evidence:** Five-feature match: (1) initial shock subsidence without resolution, (2) fiscal expansion during disinflation (Great Society ≈ IRA/CHIPS), (3) labor market remaining tight, (4) central bank premature pivot, (5) services inflation sticky at 3.5-4.5%. The pair_0 debate reduced the similarity score from 8.0/10 to 6.5-7.0 — the 2024 Fed explicitly communicated restrictive stance with data-dependence, unlike the 1967-68 Fed operating under direct political pressure with no inflation-targeting framework. **The analogue is valuable as a heuristic and source of base rates (especially the 0/5 services disinflation finding and the conditional second-wave base rate), but the unique combination of mortgage lock-in, AI, deglobalization, and fiscal expansion means no single analogue fully applies.** generalist_01's treatment of the regime as novel with historical elements is more epistemically sound.

### 15. Agricultural supply shocks are primary inflation regime catalysts for EM economies but secondary for DM

**Confidence: 7/10 (EM), 5/10 (DM)**

**Originating agents:** commodities_analyst_02 (8-9/10)

**Surviving evidence:** The arithmetic is irrefutable: food CPI weights of 30-50% in EM vs. 10-15% in DM create a structural wedge. A 30% agricultural shock produces 1.5-3.0pp in EM vs. 0.3-0.5pp in DM under anchored conditions. Export ban reflexivity (2007-08 rice panic: prices tripled on 3-4% production shortfall) amplifies shocks non-linearly. The biofuel mandate structural link (40% of US corn, 30% of Brazilian sugarcane to fuel) creates a post-2005 energy-food correlation (0.4-0.6 vs. near-zero pre-2005) that didn't previously exist. The pair_1 debate correctly assessed: "Agent A dominates the EM conversation... Agent B correctly identifies that services at 60%+ of core PCE is simply the bigger number" for DM. Current stocks-to-use ratios (wheat ~27%, corn ~26%, soybeans ~28%) are below 10-year averages but above stress zones, warranting monitoring not alarm.

## LOW_CONFIDENCE

### 16. "Expectations drift" (sub-threshold gradual ratchet) is a more relevant risk than formal de-anchoring

**Confidence: 5/10**

**Originating agent:** generalist_02 (7/10)

The distinction between drift (gradual 25-50bp ratchet below Coibion-Gorodnichenko threshold, operating through firm pricing behavior and consumer acceptance) and de-anchoring (discontinuous >75bp sustained divergence) fills a genuine analytical gap. The 1966-69 and 2004-07 precedents show drift occurring without triggering aggressive policy response. Michigan 5Y-10Y at 3.0-3.2% may represent exactly this. However, the mechanism is less well-documented than the spiral or de-anchoring frameworks, and the pair_0 debate found only generalist_02 developing this concept. Needs corroboration from behavioral finance or survey-based research.

### 17. Private credit's rate-insensitive deployment creates a structural leak in monetary transmission

**Confidence: 4/10**

**Originating agent:** credit_analyst_02 (6/10)

The directional claim is valid: committed capital with 5-7 year fund lifecycles continues deploying regardless of rate environment. But the pair_2 debate devastated the magnitude estimate: private credit at $200-300B/yr is ~5-8% of total US non-financial credit creation (not 15-20% of non-bank credit as Agent claimed), implying neutral rate adjustment of perhaps 5-10bp, not 50-100bp. The novel implication that rate insensitivity cuts both ways (easing also less stimulative) is genuinely interesting but undeveloped.

### 18. CLO OC test mechanics create non-linear stress amplification at specific thresholds

**Confidence: 6/10**

**Originating agent:** credit_analyst_02 (8/10)

The 7.5% CCC-bucket threshold triggering mandatory cash diversion is a contractual fact, and the 2020 COVID episode provided partial validation. The pair_2 debate identified this as "the single most valuable novel insight" — a concrete, deterministic mechanism invisible to macro-only frameworks. However, this remains a single-agent contribution with limited cross-validation. The question is whether enough simultaneous downgrades accumulate across the CLO market to trigger systemic effects.

### 19. BoJ normalization is an underpriced exogenous shock to the global inflation regime

**Originating agent:** rates_strategist_02 (5/10)

The mechanism is sound (~$1.6T in foreign holdings, 10-15% repatriation = 15-30bp term premium), but BoJ normalization has been priced and repriced multiple times without the predicted capital flow disruption materializing at scale. The pair_3 debate assessed this as a "chronic condition (gradual repatriation) rather than an acute shock." The cross-border CLO AAA demand channel (credit_analyst_02's open question) adds a secondary vector but remains speculative.

### 20. PE-backed services sector consolidation creates a feedback loop sustaining services inflation

**Confidence: 4/10**

**Originating agent:** credit_analyst_02 (6/10)

Directionally interesting — PE concentration in healthcare, insurance brokerage, and business services creates oligopolistic pricing power in services-CPI-heavy sectors. But the pair_2 debate found PE's share of services-sector employment is "still <10%," insufficient to be a primary driver of aggregate 3.5-4.0% services inflation. macro_strategist_01's NKPC explanation fits the data without requiring this channel. Valuable as a second-order effect in specific sub-markets, not as a regime-level driver.

## REFUTED

### R1. Biofuel mandate as a meaningful structural inflation regime driver for DM

**Killed by:** challenger_02 (Claim 5), confirmed in pair_3 debate

Corn ethanol's contribution to headline CPI is approximately 0.05-0.12pp. While the mechanical claim about the RFS allocating ~36-40% of US corn to ethanol is correct, the quantitative impact is a rounding error in a $28T economy. The narrative fallacy converts a modest structural price floor in one commodity into a sweeping "fiscal dominance" and "inflation regime" claim. **The biofuel-energy-food correlation (0.4-0.6 post-2005) is real and worth tracking as a tail-risk amplifier, but it is not regime-level for DM inflation.**

### R2. Private credit raises the neutral rate by 50-100bp

**Killed by:** pair_2 debate

credit_analyst_02 inflated the denominator effect by using non-bank credit as the reference base rather than total credit. Private credit at $200-300B/yr is ~5-8% of total non-financial credit creation, not 15-20%. The directional claim (private credit reduces monetary policy effectiveness) survives; the magnitude is overstated by ~5-10x. Revised estimate: 5-15bp, not 50-100bp.

### R3. Binary "no intermediate outcome" for shelter/maturity wall interaction

**Killed by:** pair_2 debate

credit_analyst_02's claim that shelter resolution either arrives in time (benign) or doesn't (catastrophic) with no middle path was refuted by the straightforward observation that partial shelter normalization to ~4.0% combined with modest 50-75bp Fed cuts is a perfectly plausible intermediate outcome. The maturity wall is a distribution of maturities, not a cliff, and amend-and-extend activity partially addresses it pre-emptively.

### R4. El Niño as "the single most underpriced inflation regime risk factor"

**Killed by:** pair_1 debate

The superlative is unjustifiable. commodities_analyst_02 admitted the pricing comparison is *estimated* rather than *observed* (confidence 6/10). The meteorological-yield linkages are solid, but the pricing assertion is a guess. Downgraded to: "El Niño is a meaningful but difficult-to-price inflation tail risk" — defensible but less interesting.

### R5. Food-away-from-home (FAFH) as a regime-relevant hidden agricultural channel in services

**Killed by:** pair_1 debate

commodities_analyst_02 quantified the impact at 0.15-0.25pp contribution to services CPI from a hypothetical 20% agricultural commodity price increase — below most measurement error thresholds. Analytically creative framing (agricultural costs "misattributed" as demand-driven services inflation) but the magnitude cannot independently shift an inflation regime or alter a central bank's reaction function.

### R6. Services inflation mean-reverts within 18-30 months of goods disinflation absent recession (challenger_02's Claim 4)

**Partially killed by:** pair_3 debate, generalist_02's base-rate evidence

challenger_02 cited "5 of 7 post-1960 episodes" of services mean-reversion, but the analogues (1991-93, 2001-03, 2008-10) all involved recessions or significant demand destruction. The mean-reversion wasn't passive — it was forced. Without recession, the base rate is 0/5. challenger_02's claim of recency bias in services persistence overestimates the degree to which history supports non-recessionary resolution.

## KEY_DISAGREEMENTS

### D1. What drives services inflation persistence: labor costs (NKPC) vs. transmission impairment vs. credit market structure?

- **macro_strategist_01:** Fundamentally a labor cost phenomenon through NKPC Calvo pricing. Demand-side, amenable to monetary policy.
- **rates_strategist_02:** Fundamentally a transmission failure — the interest rate channel at 60% effectiveness, mortgage lock-in, reverse wealth effects. Not a pricing puzzle but a plumbing problem.
- **credit_analyst_02:** Covenant-lite and EBITDA addbacks delay the credit channel by 12-24 months, extending above-target inflation.

**Status:** The pair_2 debate found these complementary at different levels of analysis: macro_strategist_01 is right about the aggregate mechanism, rates_strategist_02 is right about why it's slower to resolve this cycle, credit_analyst_02 is right about a specific structural change. The productive synthesis: services inflation is a labor cost phenomenon (macro_strategist_01) operating through an impaired transmission mechanism (rates_strategist_02) with delayed credit-channel feedback (credit_analyst_02). The practical question — can monetary policy resolve it without recession? — remains unresolved.

### D2. How much of above-target inflation is structural vs. cyclical?

- **rates_strategist_02:** 60-70% structural (supply-side), only 30-40% cyclical
- **macro_strategist_01:** 60-70% cyclical (demand-driven), 30-40% structural
- **generalist_02:** ~40% structural, ~60% cyclical

**Status:** The two most methodologically rigorous agents (macro_strategist_01, rates_strategist_02) reach nearly opposite conclusions. The divergence stems from classification: rates_strategist_02 classifies housing supply deficit and deglobalization as "structural" (monetary policy cannot fix them), while macro_strategist_01 classifies residual labor tightness and fiscal impulse as "cyclical" (they will respond to policy). Both are right about their specific components; the disagreement is about which components dominate. **This is the highest-priority question for future research — it determines whether the inflation regime is amenable to conventional policy or requires structural reform.**

### D3. Is the current inflation regime historically precedented or genuinely novel?

- **generalist_02:** The 1966-69 "first wave" is the primary analogue (similarity 7-8/10)
- **generalist_01:** The regime is potentially unprecedented — no clean analogue for the combination of mortgage lock-in + AI + deglobalization + fiscal expansion
- **challenger_02:** The KB anchors too heavily to the 2021-23 episode; historical base rates show similar conditions resolved multiple times

**Status:** The pair_0 debate found generalist_01's "novel with historical elements" framing more epistemically sound than generalist_02's single-analogue approach, but generalist_02's base-rate discipline is irreplaceable for constraining overconfident narratives. challenger_02's critique that prior breakeven levels near current readings didn't trigger de-anchoring is legitimate but doesn't address the transmission-impairment argument. **Resolution path: use the 1966-69 template for base rates and sequencing (generalist_02), the cross-asset framework for market-level analysis (generalist_01), and the transmission-impairment lens for understanding why this cycle is structurally slower (rates_strategist_02).**

### D4. Are current credit spreads mispriced relative to the inflation regime?

- **credit_analyst_01:** IG OAS at 90-110bp and HY at 300-350bp embed a disinflationary glide path with 65-70% probability that is inconsistent with structural inflation persistence. Expected HY default rate 4.3% vs. 2.5% market-implied.
- **credit_analyst_02:** CLO arbitrage viability is a binary function of the inflation regime — functioning at SOFR ~3.5%, dead at SOFR ~5.5%.
- **challenger_02:** The scenario probability weights underlying these mispricing claims are inherently overconfident.

**Status:** Both credit analysts find directional mispricing but the pair_1 debate showed the specific magnitude is sensitive to subjective probability weights — shift disinflationary from 55% to 65% and the default-rate gap nearly vanishes. challenger_02's meta-critique (scenario probabilities at 12+ month horizons have Brier scores of 0.3-0.4) applies. **The directional finding (spreads are complacent relative to the inflation regime uncertainty) survives; the specific alpha estimates do not.**

### D5. Is the Fed implicitly accommodating a 2.5-3.0% inflation norm?

- **rates_strategist_02:** Yes — the asymmetric reaction function (easing trigger at ~100bp above target, tightening at ~300bp) produces a de facto 2.3-2.8% average
- **macro_strategist_01:** The Fed faces a choice between accepting this or tightening further; the framework review will determine which
- **challenger_02:** We cannot determine the Fed's true tolerance from a single cycle's revealed preferences

**Status:** This is partly an empirical question (what will the Fed do?) and partly a structural question (what can the Fed do given fiscal constraints?). generalist_01's observation that equity multiples, credit spreads, and real yields embed contradictory assumptions about the answer makes this the key unresolved cross-asset variable. **Flag for monitoring through the expected Fed framework review (2025-2026).**
