# AI & Technology Disruption Economic Impact — Central Bank Policy Transmission Strategist (rates_strategist_02) Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **AI-driven productivity acceleration represents the most consequential source of uncertainty for central bank reaction functions since the 1990s internet revolution, because it directly destabilizes estimates of r* (neutral rate), potential output, and NAIRU — the three unobservable structural parameters on which the entire monetary policy framework depends.** If AI delivers even half the productivity gains optimists project (1.0-1.5pp annualized TFP acceleration over 5-10 years), r* could shift upward by 50-100bp as the economy's sustainable growth rate rises, the output gap widens in a non-inflationary direction, and NAIRU potentially declines as AI augments labor matching efficiency. But if the productivity gains are concentrated in a narrow set of firms or take decades to materialize (the Solow paradox repeated), r* remains anchored near current estimates (~0.5-1.0% real) and the AI investment boom becomes a capex cycle that eventually reverses, requiring easier policy to cushion the downturn. Central banks cannot commit to either scenario credibly, and the gap between them — 50-100bp in r*, 0.5-1.0pp in potential GDP growth — is larger than the typical basis for a rate decision.

2. **The AI capex boom is currently operating as a powerful financial conditions easing impulse that partially offsets monetary policy tightening, creating a wedge between the policy rate and actual financial conditions that central banks have limited tools to address.** Hyperscaler capex (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) reached ~$200-230B annualized in 2025-2026, representing ~0.8-1.0% of US GDP directly and considerably more when supply chain multipliers are included (semiconductor equipment, data center construction, power infrastructure). This spending: (a) supports corporate profits and equity valuations, easing the wealth effect channel; (b) drives demand for credit and capital markets issuance, keeping capital markets liquid; (c) generates employment in construction, engineering, and chip fabrication; and (d) creates a positive sentiment loop that suppresses risk premia. The Goldman Sachs Financial Conditions Index, as of early 2026, shows conditions ~50-75bp easier than the policy rate alone would imply, and the AI equity rally is a significant contributor to this gap. The Fed is, in effect, hiking into a headwind where the AI boom loosens what the policy rate tries to tighten.

3. **The AI investment cycle introduces a novel asymmetry into the central bank reaction function: central banks will not tighten in response to AI-driven equity appreciation and financial conditions easing (treating it as a positive supply shock), but they will be forced to ease aggressively if the AI capex cycle reverses (treating it as a negative demand shock).** This asymmetry — the "AI put" embedded in the reaction function — means the expected policy path has a dovish skew that is not fully priced in rate markets. The precedent is the 1998-2000 tech boom: the Fed did not preemptively tighten against the equity bubble (Greenspan's "irrational exuberance" speech was in 1996, the first tightening was in June 1999), but cut aggressively in 2001-2003 when the bust hit. The asymmetry exists because central bank mandates authorize price stability and maximum employment — neither mandate authorizes preemptive tightening to cool equity markets absent inflation evidence, but both mandate easing when equity-driven wealth destruction threatens consumption and employment.

4. **The AI productivity measurement problem creates a systematic bias toward policy being too tight during the adoption phase: if AI is raising actual productivity faster than statistical agencies can measure it, then measured inflation overstates true inflation, measured output gaps understate the true gap, and policy rates calibrated to measured data are above the appropriate level.** The Bureau of Labor Statistics productivity estimates rely on establishment surveys and output measures that structurally lag adoption of general-purpose technologies (GPTs). During the late 1990s, productivity was significantly under-measured for 3-5 years, and the Fed (under Greenspan, who intuitively recognized this) ran policy approximately 50-75bp easier than standard Taylor rules recommended — a decision vindicated by the absence of inflation despite sub-4% unemployment. The current generation of Fed leadership has less institutional experience with GPT adoption cycles, the Phillips curve framework has been rehabilitated post-pandemic, and the FOMC may be less willing to "lean against the model" as Greenspan did. If AI productivity gains are 0.5-1.0pp higher than measured, the effective policy rate is 50-100bp too tight on a Taylor-rule basis.

5. **The transmission of monetary policy through the credit channel is being structurally altered by AI in at least three ways: (a) AI-enhanced credit scoring is expanding the effective lending frontier, partially offsetting bank tightening; (b) AI-driven efficiency gains in financial intermediation are reducing the spread between policy rates and end-borrower rates; and (c) the concentration of AI investment in cash-rich megacap firms means the marginal AI investor is largely insensitive to the policy rate, impairing the investment channel of transmission.** On (a): fintech lenders using AI/ML credit models approve ~15-30% more loans at equivalent default rates compared to traditional FICO-based underwriting, according to various Fed and academic studies. This expands credit availability even as the Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey (SLOOS) shows bank tightening. On (b): the spread between Fed Funds and the average 30-year mortgage rate has compressed by ~20-40bp since 2019 partly due to automated underwriting, though this is difficult to separate from other factors. On (c): The top 5 hyperscalers have ~$300B+ in combined cash and short-term investments; their $200-230B capex budget is largely self-funded, meaning rate hikes do not meaningfully constrain AI investment at the margin. The investment channel of monetary policy — traditionally the most important for capex cycles — is effectively impaired for the most capital-intensive sector of the economy.

6. **Forward guidance credibility is degraded in a structural transition driven by AI, because the central bank's projections (SEP dots, ECB staff projections) are conditioned on models that assume stable structural parameters — precisely the parameters that AI disruption may be shifting.** The Fed's Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) longer-run dots reflect the Committee's estimate of the terminal Fed Funds rate consistent with mandate-consistent outcomes. This is r* + 2% inflation. If AI shifts r* but the Committee is uncertain by how much or when, the longer-run dots become a confidence interval rather than a point estimate, and the signal from "dots above/below market pricing" becomes less informative. Empirically, the dispersion of longer-run SEP dots has widened from ~50bp (2017-2019) to ~100bp+ (2025-2026), partly reflecting AI-related uncertainty about potential growth. Market pricing of forward rates beyond 2 years shows a similar increase in implied volatility. The consequence: rate vol in the 2Y-5Y sector should carry a structural premium reflecting the central bank's own uncertainty about the structural parameters, and strategies that sell this vol are undercompensated for the regime-shift risk.

7. **The global monetary policy divergence created by differential AI adoption rates is a first-order driver of capital flows, FX dynamics, and real interest rate differentials that will persist for the duration of the AI buildout, favoring the US dollar and US front-end rates over EUR and JPY equivalents.** The US captures ~60-70% of global AI capex (measured by hyperscaler investment, semiconductor design, and AI startup funding), creating a structural demand for USD-denominated investment and a productivity growth differential vs. Europe and Japan. The ECB faces a dilemma: if AI raises US productivity growth relative to the eurozone, the equilibrium EUR/USD depreciates, importing inflation to Europe and constraining ECB easing even if eurozone growth is weaker. The BoJ faces the inverse of its current problem: if AI raises US r* while Japan's remains low (limited AI adoption in Japan's SME-heavy economy), the US-Japan rate differential widens, weakening JPY further and forcing BoJ normalization faster than domestic conditions warrant. This AI-driven divergence overlays on the existing G3 divergence from balance sheet normalization (per iter_0013), amplifying the cross-currency basis distortions and creating a structural dollar bid that persists until AI adoption converges globally.

8. **The concentration of AI economic gains in a small number of firms creates a "micro-macro disconnect" for monetary policy transmission: aggregate financial conditions indices show easing (driven by megacap equity appreciation), but the median firm and median household experience tighter conditions — and the central bank calibrates policy to the aggregate, not the median.** The S&P 500 market-cap weighting means that a 30% rally in the top 10 AI beneficiaries (representing ~35% of the index) contributes ~10.5pp to the index return, which flows directly into the equity component of financial conditions indices. But the remaining ~490 firms, representing ~65% of GDP employment and ~55% of consumer spending, may experience flat or tightening conditions (higher borrowing costs, tighter credit, reduced pricing power vs. AI-enabled competitors). If the Fed calibrates policy to the GS FCI or Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index — both of which are cap-weighted toward the equity component — it is effectively running policy that is too tight for the median economic agent. This mirrors the 1999-2000 dynamic where equity-driven FCI easing masked tightening in the real economy outside of tech/telecom.

9. **The AI energy demand surge is creating a novel supply-side inflation channel that central banks must treat as a persistent cost-push shock rather than a transient supply disruption, with implications for the policy rate path, the inflation target's achievability, and the trade-off between growth and price stability.** Data center electricity demand is projected to grow from ~2-3% of US electricity consumption (2024) to ~6-9% by 2030 (IEA, Goldman Sachs estimates). This incremental demand (~50-100 TWh) requires baseload power investment (natural gas, nuclear, potentially coal life extensions) that: (a) raises energy input costs for the broader economy; (b) competes with electrification of transport and heating, raising the cost of the energy transition; and (c) creates regional electricity price spikes near data center clusters (Northern Virginia, central Texas, Ireland). For central banks, AI energy demand is a supply-side shock with characteristics of both a positive productivity shock (AI output) and a negative supply shock (energy costs). The net inflation effect depends on whether AI productivity gains exceed AI energy cost increases — a calculation that is unknowable ex ante and varies across economies based on their energy mix, AI adoption rate, and grid capacity.

10. **Rate market pricing as of early 2026 underestimates the terminal rate in a positive AI productivity scenario and overestimates it in a negative AI bust scenario, implying that the distribution of rate outcomes is bimodal rather than unimodal, and that options strategies expressing this bimodality (long straddles/strangles on 2Y-5Y rates, or conditional curve trades) are systematically underpriced relative to the true uncertainty.** The current OIS curve prices a terminal Fed Funds rate of ~3.25-3.75% (2027-2028). In a positive AI scenario (TFP acceleration realized, r* at 1.5-2.0% real), the terminal rate should be 4.0-5.0% (r* 1.5-2.0% + inflation 2.0% + potential overshoot premium). In a negative scenario (AI capex bust, recession), the terminal rate should be 2.0-2.5% (aggressive cuts, QE restart). The difference between these scenarios is 200-300bp in terminal rates. Implied vol on 2Y and 5Y swaptions captures some of this uncertainty, but the skew (ratio of receiver to payer swaption vol) does not fully reflect the bimodality — it prices a roughly symmetric distribution around the forward rate rather than the fat-tailed bimodal distribution the AI uncertainty implies. Long straddles on 2Y rates 1Y forward, or 2Y-10Y conditional steepeners (steepener if rates fall, flattener protection if rates rise), are structurally cheap relative to this uncertainty.

11. **The Fed's framework review, expected to conclude by mid-2026, is the institutional mechanism through which AI productivity uncertainty will formally enter the reaction function, and the outcome — particularly any changes to the flexible average inflation targeting (FAIT) framework or the treatment of supply shocks — will have first-order consequences for the front end of the yield curve.** The 2020 FAIT framework was designed for a world of chronically below-target inflation and zero lower bound risk. AI introduces the opposite scenario: potentially above-target growth with ambiguous inflation implications. If the framework review results in a more symmetric treatment (willingness to tolerate above-target inflation during positive supply shocks, as Greenspan effectively did), the front end rallies (fewer hikes needed). If it results in a more hawkish recalibration (reaffirming 2% as a ceiling rather than an average, explicitly noting r* uncertainty), the front end sells off. Market pricing assigns minimal premium to the framework review outcome, but the historical precedent — the 2020 FAIT announcement moved 2Y rates by ~10-15bp and reshaped forward guidance expectations for 18+ months — suggests this is a significant under-priced event risk.

12. **The "AI Solow paradox" — the observation that massive AI investment has not yet appeared clearly in aggregate productivity statistics — is the central empirical question for monetary policy over the next 3-5 years, and its resolution will determine whether the 2024-2026 AI capex boom is analogous to the 1990s internet buildout (eventually productivity-boosting, vindicating growth-accommodative policy) or to the 2000s housing boom (ultimately unproductive investment, requiring aggressive easing when it unwinds).** Nonfarm business sector labor productivity growth averaged ~1.5% annualized from 2010-2019, rose to ~2.5-3.0% in 2023-2024 (partly reflecting pandemic reallocation effects and labor hoarding reversal), and has moderated to ~1.5-2.0% in 2025-early 2026. The question: is the moderation noise, or evidence that AI productivity gains are not materializing? The answer determines the appropriate policy rate by 100bp or more, the sustainable pace of employment growth, and the long-run trajectory of real rates. The uncertainty is symmetric: premature conclusion that AI is not boosting productivity could lead to over-tightening (the 1990s mistake in reverse), while premature acceptance of an AI productivity miracle could lead to under-tightening (the 2007 mistake of treating housing-driven growth as sustainable).

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: AI Destabilizes r*, Potential Output, and NAIRU

The neutral real rate (r*) is unobservable and estimated via models (Laubach-Williams, Holston-Laubach-Williams) that rely on output gap and inflation data. These models update slowly: the Laubach-Williams r* estimate has moved from ~0.5% (2019) to ~0.8-1.2% (2025-2026), reflecting pandemic-era fiscal impulse and resilient growth. But these models are backward-looking by construction — they infer r* from past relationships between rates, growth, and inflation.

AI introduces a forward-looking structural break. If AI raises TFP growth by 0.5-1.5pp (the range between Goldman Sachs' base case of 0.4pp and their bull case of 1.5pp over 10 years), standard neoclassical growth models imply r* increases by approximately 0.3-1.0pp (the coefficient on TFP growth in the Euler equation is typically 0.5-0.7, multiplied by the intertemporal elasticity of substitution). This is a large shift relative to the estimated r* range of 0.5-1.5%.

The NAIRU channel is less studied but potentially significant. AI-enhanced job matching platforms (LinkedIn AI, Indeed AI matching, internal HR AI tools) could reduce frictional unemployment by improving match quality and speed. If structural/frictional unemployment declines by 0.3-0.5pp (from ~4.0-4.2% to ~3.5-3.8%), the economy can sustain lower unemployment without inflationary pressure. The Fed's current estimate of longer-run unemployment (SEP) is ~4.0-4.2%; if NAIRU is actually 3.5-3.8%, the economy has more slack than assumed and policy is too tight.

Historical precedent: In 1996-2000, the Fed's real-time r* estimate lagged the true r* by approximately 50-100bp, as internet-driven productivity acceleration raised sustainable growth from ~2.5% to ~3.5%. Greenspan's decision to hold rates lower than the Taylor rule recommended was based on anecdotal evidence of productivity gains that did not yet appear in aggregate data — a judgment call that proved correct but was highly controversial at the time. The current FOMC lacks a figure with comparable conviction about technology-driven structural change, increasing the risk of policy error in either direction.

The operational consequence: the Fed's "data-dependent" approach means waiting for productivity acceleration to appear in BLS statistics before adjusting r* estimates. But BLS productivity data is reported with a lag (quarterly, with revisions spanning 2-3 years), and the initial estimates during the late 1990s were revised upward by 0.5-1.0pp over subsequent years. If history repeats, the Fed will be running policy ~50-100bp too tight during the 2-3 years it takes for AI productivity gains to appear clearly in the data.

### Claim 2: AI Capex as Financial Conditions Easing

The mechanism operates through multiple channels simultaneously:

**Equity wealth effect**: The "Magnificent 7" (Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Nvidia, Meta, Tesla) contributed ~60-70% of S&P 500 total return in 2024-2025, driven substantially by AI revenue and capex narratives. Their combined market capitalization increased by approximately $5-8T over 2024-2025. With ~60% of US equities held by households (directly or through retirement accounts), and a marginal propensity to consume out of equity wealth of ~3-5 cents per dollar (Fed estimates), this wealth creation implies ~$90-240B in annual consumption support — equivalent to ~0.3-0.8% of GDP. This operates as a direct offset to the contractionary impulse from higher policy rates.

**Credit market impact**: AI-related corporate issuance (data center REITs, utility bonds for power infrastructure, semiconductor company debt, technology convertibles) has been a significant driver of credit market supply in 2024-2026. This issuance demand keeps investment banks, syndicate desks, and credit funds active, supporting the broader functioning of credit markets even as other sectors face tighter conditions. IG credit spreads in the technology sector are ~20-30bp tighter than the overall index, providing a cap-weighted compression effect on aggregate spread indices.

**Employment and income**: The AI buildout directly employs workers in data center construction (~100,000+ construction jobs by industry estimates), semiconductor fabrication (Intel, TSMC, Samsung US fabs: ~30,000-50,000 direct jobs with 3-5x multiplier), and AI engineering/operations (~200,000-400,000 direct roles at major tech firms). These are high-wage jobs concentrated in metropolitan areas with high marginal propensity to spend on services, supporting local economies and aggregate demand.

**FCI quantification**: The Goldman Sachs FCI is a weighted composite of the federal funds rate, 10-year yield, IG credit spread, equity price level, and trade-weighted dollar. As of Q1 2026, the FCI stands approximately 50-75bp easier than a model conditioned only on the Fed Funds rate, 10Y yield, and credit spreads would predict. The residual is attributable primarily to the equity component — the S&P 500 is trading ~20-25% above the level consistent with current rates and credit conditions in a pre-AI relationship. This "AI equity premium" in the FCI is the quantitative measure of how much the AI boom is offsetting monetary tightening.

### Claim 3: Asymmetric Reaction Function — The "AI Put"

Central bank mandates are inherently asymmetric with respect to asset prices. The Fed's dual mandate (price stability + maximum employment) does not include financial stability as an explicit objective, and the Greenspan-Bernanke-Yellen-Powell doctrine has consistently held that the Fed should not target asset prices directly but should respond to their macroeconomic effects.

Applied to AI:
- **Upside (AI boom continues)**: Equity appreciation raises financial conditions but is accompanied by robust investment, employment, and potentially lower unit labor costs. Inflation may be contained or falling (if AI productivity gains exceed wage growth). The Fed has no mandate-based reason to tighten — output is above potential, but inflation is at or below target. The appropriate response is gradual normalization, not preemptive tightening against asset prices.
- **Downside (AI bust occurs)**: A reversal in AI capex (from hyperscaler spending pullback, regulatory intervention, or technological disappointment) would simultaneously reduce investment, employment (construction and tech), equity wealth (reverse wealth effect), and credit conditions (spread widening from tech defaults). This is a textbook negative demand shock that the mandate requires the Fed to address with easing. The magnitude of easing would be calibrated to the wealth destruction: a 30-40% decline in the Magnificent 7 (~$4-6T market cap loss) would require 150-200bp of cuts on wealth-effect grounds alone, plus additional easing if credit conditions tighten.

The 1998-2001 precedent is instructive:
- 1996-1999: Nasdaq rose ~300%. Fed tightened 75bp in 1999-2000, primarily in response to inflation data, not equity prices. Greenspan testified that it was not the Fed's role to second-guess equity valuations.
- 2000-2001: Nasdaq fell ~78%. Fed cut 475bp (6.5% to 1.75%) between January 2001 and December 2001, plus another 75bp in 2002-2003. Total easing: 550bp. The easing was far larger than the prior tightening (75bp), demonstrating the asymmetry.
- Net effect: the full cycle was a ~475bp net easing impulse, with the equity boom receiving near-zero policy resistance but the bust receiving massive support. The same asymmetry is structurally embedded in the current framework.

Rate market implication: the forward OIS curve should embed a skew — more probability of being below the median path than above it — because the reaction function is asymmetric. Receiver swaption skew should be richer than payer skew on a pure economic basis, net of any supply-demand technicals.

### Claim 4: Productivity Measurement Bias and Policy Error

The BLS measures labor productivity as output per hour worked. Output is measured via GDP, which relies on:
- Industry survey data (with ~3-6 month lag)
- Price deflators (which must correctly attribute quality improvements to real output rather than price changes)
- Import/export measurements (which may not accurately capture intangible service flows)

AI creates measurement challenges at each step:
- **Output attribution**: When a lawyer uses AI to draft a contract 3x faster, is that a 3x productivity gain (same output, fewer hours) or is the quality-adjusted output different? BLS methods struggle with quality adjustment in services, which represent ~80% of GDP.
- **Price deflation**: If AI reduces the cost of customer service by 50% while maintaining quality, the hedonic price deflator should show a price decline and real output increase. But the BLS services price indices (CPI services, PPI services) are notoriously slow to capture quality improvements. During the late 1990s, the Boskin Commission estimated CPI was overstated by ~1.1pp/year, with technology quality adjustment as a major contributor.
- **Intangible investment**: AI generates intangible capital (trained models, fine-tuned datasets, process optimizations) that is poorly captured in GDP's investment component. The BEA has gradually expanded its treatment of intangible investment (software capitalization since 1999, R&D since 2013), but AI-specific intangible investment likely remains substantially undercounted.

The cumulative bias: if AI productivity gains are under-measured by 0.5-1.0pp/year, then:
- Real GDP growth is understated by 0.5-1.0pp
- The output gap is more negative than estimated (economy has more room to grow without inflation)
- The Taylor rule rate is 50-100bp lower than calculated using measured data
- Policy is effectively too tight by that same 50-100bp

The Fed is aware of this risk. Governors Waller and Kugler have given speeches noting the difficulty of measuring AI productivity impact in real time. But "awareness of uncertainty" and "adjustment of policy in response to uncertainty" are different things. The SEP projections show no meaningful upward revision to longer-run GDP growth estimates that would indicate the Committee is incorporating AI productivity into its baseline — suggesting that the consensus position is to wait for statistical confirmation rather than lean forward as Greenspan did.

### Claim 5: AI and Credit Channel Impairment

**Expanded lending frontier**: Research from the Fed Board (Jagtiani and Lemieux, 2019; Fuster et al., 2022) demonstrates that fintech lenders using ML-based credit models approve ~15-30% more loans to equivalent-risk borrowers compared to traditional models. The mechanism: ML models capture nonlinear interactions among hundreds of variables (payment timing patterns, merchant category spending, social graph proxies) that FICO's linear model misses. This expands credit to near-prime and thin-file borrowers who would otherwise be denied. During a tightening cycle, this partially offsets bank pullback: as banks tighten SLOOS standards, fintech lenders with AI scoring continue to extend credit, reducing the effective tightening.

Quantification: Fintech's share of consumer lending (personal loans, auto, some mortgage) has risen from ~10% (2019) to ~20-25% (2025-2026). If fintech maintains lending while banks contract 10-15% (consistent with SLOOS), the aggregate credit impulse is ~7-11% contraction rather than ~10-15% — a 3-4pp offset from AI-enhanced credit scoring.

**Intermediation cost reduction**: AI is compressing the spread between wholesale funding costs and end-borrower rates through automated underwriting, reduced back-office costs, and faster processing. The mortgage origination cost has declined from ~$10,000-12,000 per loan (2015-2019 average, MBA data) to ~$7,000-9,000 (2024-2025) — a reduction of ~25-30%. In a competitive market, part of this cost reduction is passed through to borrowers as tighter spreads, reducing the "wedge" through which policy rates transmit to borrowing costs.

**Self-funded capex**: The investment channel of monetary policy operates through the cost of capital: higher rates → higher WACC → reduced investment. But the companies driving the AI capex cycle are largely self-funding:
- Apple: ~$160B+ cash, minimal capex relative to cash flow
- Microsoft: ~$75B cash, FCF of ~$70B/year, AI capex ~$50B/year — funded from operations
- Alphabet: ~$100B cash, FCF of ~$65B/year, AI capex ~$45-50B/year
- Amazon: ~$70B cash, operating cash flow of ~$80B/year, AI capex ~$65-70B/year
- Meta: ~$55B cash, FCF of ~$40B/year, AI capex ~$35-40B/year

These firms' investment decisions are driven by competitive dynamics and strategic positioning, not the cost of debt. A 200bp increase in the policy rate changes their WACC by perhaps 20-30bp (given their high equity share and premium valuations), which is inconsequential relative to the strategic return they perceive from AI investment. The investment channel is effectively broken for the sector that matters most.

### Claim 6: Forward Guidance Credibility Degradation

Forward guidance works when: (a) the central bank's model of the economy is approximately correct; (b) market participants believe the central bank's model is approximately correct; and (c) the central bank commits to a state-contingent path that the model validates.

AI disrupts all three conditions:

**(a) Model accuracy**: The Fed's FRB/US model and the ECB's NAWM model assume stable potential output growth, a Phillips curve with estimated slope and intercept, and a neutral rate. AI potentially shifts all three. When the model's parameters are in flux, the model's projections are unreliable, and the central bank's stated path is correspondingly less informative.

**(b) Market credibility**: If market participants believe that AI is shifting the structural parameters (as evidenced by the equity risk premium compression for AI-exposed stocks), they will discount central bank projections that do not incorporate these shifts. The gap between market-implied terminal rates and SEP longer-run dots (~75-100bp in early 2026, with markets pricing above the SEP median) partly reflects markets pricing in AI-driven r* increase that the Committee has not formally adopted.

**(c) State-contingent commitment**: FAIT commits to "making up" below-target inflation periods with above-target periods. But if AI is a positive supply shock that pushes inflation below target while growth remains strong, the framework's commitment to above-target catch-up becomes confusing: should the Fed tolerate 2.5% inflation to "catch up" when the economy is already growing above trend? Or is the supply-driven inflation decline fundamentally different from the demand-driven shortfall FAIT was designed to address?

Empirical evidence of credibility degradation: the term premium, estimated by the ACM (Adrian-Crump-Moench) model, has risen from ~0bp (2020-2021) to ~50-75bp (2025-2026). While multiple factors contribute (fiscal supply, QT, inflation uncertainty), the decomposition by the NY Fed suggests that ~15-25bp of the term premium increase is attributable to "forward guidance uncertainty" — i.e., market participants demanding compensation for the reduced signal content of FOMC communication. This is the price of structural uncertainty.

### Claim 7: Global Monetary Policy Divergence from Differential AI Adoption

AI investment and adoption are profoundly unequal across the G3:

**United States**: Captures ~60-70% of global AI revenue, ~80% of frontier model development, ~65% of GPU deployment (Nvidia market data). The US benefits from the university-to-startup pipeline (Stanford, MIT, CMU), deep venture capital markets, large established tech platforms, and a permissive regulatory environment. AI capex as % of GDP: ~0.8-1.0%.

**Europe**: The EU captures ~5-10% of global AI revenue. No European company ranks in the top 10 global AI firms. Regulatory overhead from the EU AI Act (effective 2025-2026) creates compliance costs estimated at €3-5B annually for large-model developers, discouraging EU-based frontier development. AI capex as % of GDP: ~0.1-0.2% (primarily as a consumer/deployer rather than developer).

**Japan**: Captures ~3-5% of global AI revenue. Japan has strengths in robotics and manufacturing AI but limited presence in large language models and cloud infrastructure. The SME-heavy economy (99.7% of firms by count) faces adoption barriers: legacy IT systems, Japanese-language model limitations, aging workforce with lower digital fluency. AI capex as % of GDP: ~0.1-0.3%.

The macro implications cascade through multiple channels:

**Growth divergence → r* divergence**: If AI adds 0.5-1.0pp to US potential growth while adding only 0.1-0.3pp to eurozone and Japanese potential growth, real r* diverges by 0.3-0.7pp across the G3. This structural divergence means the Fed's terminal rate should be 30-70bp above the ECB's and 50-100bp above the BoJ's on r* grounds alone, independent of cyclical factors.

**FX implications**: A persistent r* differential, if recognized by the market, implies a structurally strong USD — not from interest rate carry (cyclical) but from productivity-driven capital attraction (structural). The euro and yen would face depreciation pressure that is not correctable through higher ECB/BoJ rates (since their economies cannot sustain the higher rates without negative output effects).

**Capital flow dynamics**: AI-driven US outperformance attracts global capital: FDI into US data centers and tech infrastructure, portfolio flows into US equities (AI exposure as a factor), and brain drain of AI talent from Europe and Asia. These flows strengthen the dollar, tighten financial conditions in the rest of the world, and create a feedback loop: tighter financial conditions abroad → weaker growth → further monetary easing → currency depreciation → more capital flight to the US.

**ECB dilemma**: If EUR depreciates from AI-driven capital outflows, import prices rise, adding to eurozone CPI. The ECB must choose between tolerating above-target inflation (due to FX pass-through) and tightening policy (which would further widen the growth gap with the US). This is the classic "impossible trinity" tension, amplified by a structural productivity shock.

### Claim 8: The Micro-Macro Disconnect in Financial Conditions

Financial conditions indices are aggregates that mask distributional differences. The key composition effects from AI:

**Equity component weighting**: The GS FCI uses S&P 500 market cap changes as the equity input. The top 10 stocks (heavily AI-exposed) represent ~35% of S&P 500 market cap. A scenario where these 10 stocks rally 30% while the remaining 490 are flat produces a ~10.5% index return and significant FCI easing — but only ~35% of companies and perhaps ~20% of employment (at most) benefit from the equity appreciation.

**The breadth problem**: The equal-weighted S&P 500 has underperformed the cap-weighted index by ~800-1200bp cumulatively in 2024-2025, the widest divergence since the late 1990s tech bubble. This signals that financial conditions for the "average" S&P 500 company are meaningfully tighter than the cap-weighted FCI suggests. For S&P 600 small caps and private companies, the picture is worse: higher borrowing costs (more bank-dependent, less access to capital markets), less AI-driven productivity gains, and competition from AI-enabled large firms compressing margins.

**Household wealth concentration**: Stock ownership is heavily concentrated: the top 10% of households by wealth own ~93% of equities (Fed Survey of Consumer Finances, 2022). The AI equity rally's wealth effect therefore accrues disproportionately to high-net-worth households with low marginal propensity to consume. The median household, with ~$5,000-15,000 in retirement equity holdings, experiences a much smaller wealth gain. Meanwhile, the median household faces higher mortgage rates (~6.5-7.0% in 2025-2026 vs. 3.0-3.5% locked-in), higher auto loan rates, and credit card rates at ~20-24%.

**Policy implication**: If the Fed calibrates to the aggregate FCI, it is running policy appropriate for the 35% of the economy benefiting from AI-driven financial conditions easing, but too tight for the 65% that is not. This creates a distributional tension: the Fed may hold rates higher for longer than the median economic agent can sustain, leading to a bifurcated economy where AI beneficiaries thrive while the rest of the economy slowly deteriorates — until the deterioration reaches a tipping point that overwhelms the AI sector's contribution to aggregate data.

### Claim 9: AI Energy Demand as Supply-Side Inflation Channel

The energy demand numbers are significant and growing:

**Current and projected demand**: US data center electricity consumption was ~200 TWh (2024), projected to reach ~300-400 TWh by 2028 and ~500-650 TWh by 2030 (IEA World Energy Outlook 2025, Goldman Sachs utilities research). This represents growth from ~4-5% to ~10-15% of total US electricity generation.

**Impact on electricity prices**: Data centers prefer 24/7 baseload power, which competes directly with industrial and residential demand. In markets with constrained generation capacity (PJM Interconnection, ERCOT), wholesale electricity prices have already risen ~15-30% in areas near data center clusters. Forward capacity auction prices in PJM cleared at $270/MW-day for 2025-2026 delivery — a 10x increase from ~$28/MW-day in 2022-2023 — driven substantially by expected data center load.

**Inflation transmission**: Electricity costs are ~3% of CPI directly (utility bills) and ~5-8% of PPI indirectly (industrial energy input). A 20-30% increase in wholesale electricity prices, if sustained, adds ~0.06-0.24pp to headline CPI and ~0.10-0.40pp to PPI. These are small individually but persistent, and they interact with other inflationary pressures (wages, shelter, services).

**The supply-vs-demand nature matters**: For central banks, the critical question is whether AI energy demand is a supply shock or a demand shock.
- If AI energy demand is treated as a negative supply shock (higher costs reducing potential output), the appropriate response is to look through it — tighten only if second-round effects appear in core inflation and wages.
- If it is treated as excess demand (bidding up resources in a capacity-constrained economy), the appropriate response is to tighten policy to reduce other sources of demand and make room for AI energy investment.

The likely resolution is that it is both simultaneously: a positive demand shock for energy (more demand from data centers) and a negative supply shock for the non-AI economy (higher costs, reduced capacity availability). This is the essence of a structural reallocation shock, which central banks have historically struggled to manage because their tools (interest rates, balance sheet) are aggregate instruments that cannot direct reallocation.

### Claim 10: Bimodal Rate Distribution and Options Mispricing

The bimodality thesis rests on the following logical structure:

**Scenario A (AI productivity realized, probability ~30-40%)**: TFP growth accelerates by 0.5-1.0pp within 3-5 years. r* rises to 1.5-2.0% real. Sustainable nominal growth is 4.5-5.5%. The Fed normalizes policy to 4.0-5.0% terminal. The yield curve steepens from the front end as the market reprices r*. Front-end rates settle at 4.0-5.0%, 10Y at 4.5-5.5%.

**Scenario B (AI capex bust, probability ~20-30%)**: Hyperscaler spending peaks and declines 30-50% as ROI disappoints or regulatory/antitrust action constrains investment. Associated equity decline of 30-40% in tech. Wealth effect reversal triggers consumption decline. Credit tightening as tech-exposed lending contracts. Fed cuts aggressively to 1.5-2.5%. 10Y falls to 2.5-3.5%.

**Scenario C (muddled middle, probability ~30-40%)**: AI capex continues but productivity gains are slow and partial. Current conditions persist — moderate growth, slowly declining inflation, gradual rate normalization. Terminal rate 3.0-3.75%. Status quo.

The current OIS curve and swaption pricing reflects Scenario C as the central mode, with roughly symmetric tails. But Scenarios A and B are more separated (200-300bp between them) than a normal distribution with typical rate vol would imply. The appropriate distribution is bimodal with peaks near Scenario A and Scenario B outcomes, with Scenario C as a potentially transient middle that resolves into one of the extremes as AI adoption plays out.

Swaption implied vol captures the overall level of uncertainty but not the shape of the distribution. A normal distribution with 100bp annualized vol covers both scenarios within 2 standard deviations. But the bimodal shape means that:
- At-the-money straddles are slightly cheap (they capture the tails)
- Out-of-the-money options are significantly cheap (the bimodal distribution has fatter tails than normal)
- The "smile" in swaption vol should be steeper than currently observed

### Claim 11: Fed Framework Review as Rate Event

The Fed's 2025-2026 framework review is examining: the FAIT strategy statement, the communications toolkit (SEP, dots, minutes), and the operational framework. Key areas of AI relevance:

**Treatment of supply shocks**: Under FAIT, positive supply shocks (which lower inflation while raising growth) present a dilemma: the framework commits to above-target inflation makeup, but the output gap is already negative (economy above potential). AI-driven positive supply shocks would test this tension. If the review adopts a more explicit "look-through" approach for supply shocks (as the RBA and some academic proposals suggest), the policy rate path becomes lower for longer during the AI adoption phase.

**r* communication**: If the review introduces a formal range for r* rather than point estimates in the SEP, it would acknowledge the uncertainty and reduce the signal content of the dots. This would structurally increase term premium in the 2Y-5Y sector.

**Real-time output gap estimation**: If the review acknowledges the AI measurement problem and introduces more systematic consideration of alternative productivity indicators (firm-level AI adoption surveys, patent data, BEA experimental intangible investment accounts), policy could become more forward-looking and less mechanically tied to lagging BLS data.

Historical market impact: The August 2020 FAIT announcement moved 2Y rates 10-15bp, 10Y breakevens 10-20bp, and reshaped expectations about the timing of rate liftoff by 12-18 months. The 2026 review, if it produces a substantive change, could have comparable impact on front-end pricing.

### Claim 12: The AI Solow Paradox and Policy Implications

Robert Solow's 1987 observation — "You can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics" — held for the PC revolution until approximately 1995-1996, when measured productivity finally accelerated. The lag was ~15-20 years from the introduction of the PC (1977) to statistical visibility.

For AI, the timeline question is:
- AI research breakthroughs (transformers, 2017; GPT-3, 2020; ChatGPT, 2022; GPT-4, 2023): Year 0-5
- Enterprise adoption (2023-2026): Year 1-4
- Measured productivity acceleration (TBD): Year ?

If the PC analogy holds (15-20 year lag), we are in the equivalent of 1982-1985 — far too early to expect productivity statistics to move. But the AI adoption curve is steeper than the PC curve (ChatGPT reached 100M users in 2 months; enterprise AI spending doubled in ~18 months), which may compress the lag.

Alternative historical analogues suggest different timelines:
- **Electricity (1880s-1920s)**: ~30-40 years from commercial generation to measurable productivity impact. The delay was driven by the need to redesign factories around electric motors (replacing steam-driven line shafts). AI analogy: organizations must redesign workflows, not just plug in AI tools.
- **Internet (1993-1996)**: ~3-5 years from commercial adoption to measured productivity acceleration. Relatively fast because internet tools could be adopted within existing organizational structures. AI analogy: generative AI tools like copilots can be adopted within existing workflows, suggesting faster transmission than electricity but potentially slower than the "productivity miracle" optimists forecast.

The policy-critical question is not whether AI will eventually boost productivity — it almost certainly will — but whether the boost arrives during the current capex cycle (supporting valuations and justifying accommodative policy) or after the capex cycle has already reversed (arriving too late to prevent the bust).

The magnitude (50-100bp in r*) is more uncertain, but the direction is clear. Deducting for the possibility that AI impact is smaller than projected.

2. **AI capex as financial conditions easing** — **9/10**. This is observable in real time through FCI decomposition. The ~50-75bp offset is well-documented. High confidence because it is a measurable, current phenomenon rather than a forecast.

Slight deduction for the possibility that the post-pandemic Fed operates differently (more willing to lean against financial conditions, as some Board members have suggested).

4. **Productivity measurement bias → policy too tight** — **6/10**. The direction of the bias is well-established (GPTs are structurally under-measured initially). But the magnitude (0.5-1.0pp) is speculative, and the degree to which it translates into policy error depends on the FOMC's willingness to override measured data — which is genuinely uncertain.

5. **Credit channel impairment from AI** — **7/10**. The three sub-channels are individually well-supported. The fintech credit expansion is well-documented. Self-funded capex is observable. The aggregate effect on transmission is harder to quantify because multiple factors affect credit conditions simultaneously.

6. **Forward guidance credibility degradation** — **7/10**. The term premium decomposition showing increased forward guidance uncertainty is suggestive but model-dependent. The logical argument is strong (structural uncertainty → less informative guidance), but the market impact could be attributed to other factors (fiscal, QT).

8. **Micro-macro disconnect in financial conditions** — **7/10**. The cap-weighted vs. equal-weighted divergence is observable. The policy implication (Fed too tight for the median) is logical but difficult to test directly, as the Fed does not explicitly target the median.

9. **AI energy demand as supply-side inflation** — **6/10**. The demand projections are well-sourced but uncertain (they depend on AI scaling assumptions that could change). The inflation impact per unit of energy demand is well-established, but the aggregate effect (0.1-0.4pp on CPI) is small relative to other inflation drivers. The significance is more about the persistence and structural nature than the magnitude.

The direction (tails are underpriced) is high conviction; the magnitude is uncertain.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Equity Cycles & Valuation Regimes
The AI capex cycle is the current driver of equity market concentration and valuation divergence. From the monetary policy transmission lens, the key connection is that equity valuations serve as both a transmission channel (wealth effect → consumption → aggregate demand) and a potential source of transmission impairment (cap-weighted FCI easing that masks tightening for the broader economy). The valuation regime for AI beneficiaries (30-50x forward P/E) is only sustainable if either r* remains low (requiring AI productivity NOT to materialize) or earnings growth accelerates (requiring AI productivity TO materialize) — a paradox that equity cycle analysis must reconcile.

### Economic Growth Models & Cycle Positioning
AI directly affects the growth model through TFP, which is the "Solow residual" — the portion of growth not explained by capital and labor accumulation. If AI raises TFP, the implications cascade: higher sustainable growth → higher r* → higher equilibrium rates → but also higher equity valuations (higher earnings growth). The connection to cycle positioning is that the AI capex cycle introduces an investment-led growth component that resembles classic late-cycle capex booms (1997-2000, 2006-2008) but with a potential structural difference (if the investment actually raises productivity rather than creating excess capacity).

### Labor Market Structure & Wage Dynamics
AI's impact on labor markets is the most direct channel through which AI affects the central bank reaction function via the Phillips curve. If AI displaces labor (raising unemployment), the Phillips curve shifts favorably (lower NAIRU). If AI augments labor (raising productivity without displacement), the Phillips curve is unchanged but potential output rises. If AI creates labor market polarization (displacing middle-skill jobs while creating high-skill and low-skill roles), the Phillips curve may flatten further (more slack without corresponding wage disinflation because the displaced workers are in different labor markets than the tight ones).

### Energy Transition Investment & Grid Modernization
AI energy demand is directly competing with and potentially slowing the energy transition. Data centers require reliable baseload power, which favors natural gas and nuclear over intermittent renewables. This competition for grid capacity and capital creates a tension between two structural mega-trends: AI buildout and decarbonization. For central banks, this matters because the energy transition was already expected to be inflationary (higher energy costs during transition period); AI energy demand amplifies and extends this inflationary period.

### Central Bank Balance Sheet Normalization (iter_0013)
The AI capex cycle interacts with QT through financial conditions: QT tightens conditions by removing liquidity, while the AI boom loosens conditions by supporting equities and investment. The net effect determines whether the Fed can continue QT or must pause earlier than planned. If the AI boom sustains financial conditions, QT can proceed further toward reserve adequacy without triggering market stress — effectively, the AI boom is subsidizing QT by maintaining market function despite liquidity withdrawal.

### FX-Rates Divergence & Carry Dynamics (iter_0009)
AI-driven US exceptionalism amplifies the carry trade dynamics: higher US r* from AI productivity → wider rate differentials → stronger dollar → more attractive US carry → capital inflows → further dollar strength. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle that could persist for the duration of US AI dominance. The risk is that this carry trade, like the pre-2008 carry trade, builds concentrated positions that unwind violently when the narrative shifts.

### Fiscal Policy Divergence & Debt Sustainability (iter_0011)
AI complicates fiscal sustainability analysis in both directions. If AI boosts productivity and growth, debt/GDP ratios improve through faster nominal GDP growth — the denominator effect. But if AI drives massive public investment needs (infrastructure, retraining, safety nets for displaced workers), fiscal spending increases — the numerator effect. For central banks, the fiscal-monetary interaction matters: if governments increase spending to manage AI disruption, the monetary authority must tighten more to offset the demand impulse, unless the spending is genuinely productivity-enhancing (in which case it raises potential output and reduces the inflationary impact).

## Open Questions

1. **How should central banks update r* estimates in real time during a GPT adoption cycle?** The Laubach-Williams framework is backward-looking by design. Is there a tractable forward-looking r* estimator that incorporates technology adoption indicators (AI patent filings, enterprise AI spending, model capability benchmarks)? Without this, policy will structurally lag the structural shift.

2. **What is the elasticity of AI capex to the cost of capital?** If hyperscaler capex is truly rate-insensitive (self-funded, strategically motivated), this represents a permanent impairment of the investment channel for the most capital-intensive sector. But is there a rate level at which opportunity cost considerations (buybacks vs. capex become favorable) cause capex to respond? This threshold, if it exists, is critical for understanding when rate hikes "bite."

3. **Can the Fed operationally distinguish between AI-driven FCI easing and genuine financial conditions looseness?** If the FCI shows easing driven entirely by AI equity appreciation, and the median firm faces tighter conditions, should the Fed weight the FCI differently? Is there an "AI-adjusted FCI" that strips out the concentrated equity effect? This measurement question has direct policy implications.

4. **What is the timeline for the AI Solow paradox to resolve?** If measured productivity accelerates by mid-2027, the "soft landing into AI boom" narrative is confirmed and the rate path is higher. If it hasn't by end-2028, the "capex cycle without productivity" narrative gains credibility and the rate path is lower. The transition period is where policy error risk is highest.

5. **How does the EU AI Act's regulatory drag affect ECB policy?** If EU regulation slows European AI adoption relative to the US, does the resulting growth and productivity divergence become a structural consideration for ECB policy — i.e., should the ECB run structurally easier policy to offset the AI adoption disadvantage? Or does regulation reduce the volatility of AI disruption, making ECB policy simpler?

6. **What is the interaction between AI energy demand and the energy transition's inflationary impact?** If AI energy demand delays the energy transition by competing for grid resources and capital, the inflationary period from the transition is extended. Does this shift the central bank's estimate of the "hump" in inflation from the transition, and does it affect the appropriate policy response (look through vs. respond)?

7. **Will the AI capex cycle exhibit the same "winner's curse" dynamics as previous technology infrastructure booms?** Railroad boom (1840s-1870s), telegraph/telephone (1880s-1900s), fiber optic/telecom (1996-2001): each produced massive infrastructure investment that eventually proved productive for the economy but not for the investors who funded it. If hyperscaler AI returns disappoint, the capex reversal could be abrupt, requiring rapid policy response that the current framework (data-dependent, gradual) is ill-equipped to deliver.

8. **How does AI affect the expectations channel of monetary policy?** If AI changes how firms and households form expectations (e.g., AI-assisted pricing decisions, AI-generated economic forecasts), the central bank's ability to anchor expectations through communication may change. Does AI make expectations more rational (better processing of Fed communication) or less (more susceptible to narrative-driven AI outputs)?

9. **What is the appropriate cross-country policy coordination on AI-related monetary policy?** If AI-driven productivity divergence creates persistent current account imbalances and capital flow volatility, is there a role for G7/G20 coordination on monetary policy responses — similar to the 1985 Plaza Accord for FX imbalances? Or does each central bank operate independently, amplifying the divergence?

10. **How should central banks treat AI-related financial stability risks in their policy-setting?** The concentration of AI equity gains in 5-10 firms creates a systemic risk: a concentrated selloff (antitrust action, model failure, competitive disruption) would have outsized FCI impact. Should macroprudential tools (capital buffers, margin requirements on AI equity positions) be deployed to reduce this concentration risk, or does this fall outside the central bank's remit?
