# Credit-Equity Linkage & Contagion Channels — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The Merton structural model provides the theoretically coherent bridge between credit and equity markets: equity is a call option on firm assets, and credit is a short put.** This means credit spreads and equity valuations are mechanically linked through the firm's asset volatility, leverage, and distance-to-default. When asset values decline toward the default boundary, credit spreads widen convexly while equity declines linearly — producing the asymmetric co-movement pattern observed empirically. This framework predicts that credit-equity linkage is regime-dependent: tight in stress, loose in expansion.

2. **The credit impulse — the change in the change of credit — is a more reliable leading indicator of output gaps than the credit level, and its current trajectory signals a tightening of the output gap from the demand side over the next 2-4 quarters.** The credit impulse captures the marginal flow of credit into the economy and maps directly onto the IS curve: a decelerating credit impulse shifts IS left, reducing equilibrium output for any given interest rate. The U.S. credit impulse has turned negative in 2025-2026 as bank lending standards tightened and HY issuance shifted toward refinancing rather than net new borrowing.

3. **The financial accelerator mechanism (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist, 1999) creates a self-reinforcing loop between credit conditions, equity prices, and real activity that amplifies what would otherwise be a standard New Keynesian business cycle.** Falling equity prices reduce collateral values, tightening the external finance premium (the wedge between internal and external funding costs), which reduces investment and employment, which further depresses equity prices. This mechanism is quantitatively important: BGG estimate it amplifies output responses to monetary policy shocks by 50-100%. The mechanism is currently loaded because corporate leverage is elevated (net debt/EBITDA at ~2.5x for IG, ~4.5-5.0x for HY, and ~6-8x actual for private credit per the EBITDA addback distortion concept).

4. **The HY spread threshold for equity market contagion is approximately 500-550bp OAS, with a non-linear escalation above 600bp — but this threshold is conditional on the monetary policy reaction function.** Below ~450bp, HY spreads and equity markets co-move loosely and are driven by common macro factors. Between 450-550bp, the linkage tightens as the financial accelerator engages. Above 600bp, the credit channel becomes a binding constraint on real activity (primary HY issuance effectively shuts down, restricting the flow of credit to leveraged borrowers). Current HY OAS of ~350-420bp places us in the loose-coupling regime, but the distance to the tightening threshold is only 100-150bp — reachable on a single adverse macro shock.

5. **CRE-to-bank-equity contagion operates through a specific New Keynesian channel: CRE loan losses erode bank capital ratios, triggering procyclical tightening of lending standards via the bank capital channel, which contracts the IS curve by raising the effective lending rate for all borrowers.** This is not merely a bank solvency concern — it is a macroeconomic transmission mechanism. With U.S. banks holding ~$2.7T in CRE loans and unrealized CRE losses estimated at $400-700B (depending on cap rate assumptions), the bank capital channel could subtract 0.5-1.5pp from credit growth over 2-3 years, equivalent to a 50-100bp effective tightening of monetary policy beyond what the Fed intends.

6. **The private credit-to-public market spillover channel is mediated by the portfolio balance effect: stress in private credit forces mark-to-market losses on insurance companies, pension funds, and semi-liquid vehicles (BDCs, interval funds), triggering rebalancing flows that sell liquid public credit and equities to meet redemptions or maintain allocation targets.** This is the modern analogue of the "Volcker shadow banking" problem — stress migrates from the opaque sector to the transparent sector not through direct counterparty links but through the balance sheets of common asset owners. The magnitude depends on the size of the allocation shift: insurance companies alone hold ~$700B in private credit, and a 10% mark-to-market loss would require ~$70B of liquid asset sales to rebalance.

7. **The credit-equity linkage fundamentally alters the Taylor Rule prescription: a credit tightening operates as a shadow rate hike, implying the Fed should cut the policy rate below what inflation and unemployment alone would prescribe.** This is the Curdia-Woodford (2010) extension of the New Keynesian model: when credit spreads widen due to an increase in the external finance premium (not driven by fundamentals), the optimal policy rate should offset this tightening. Empirically, a 100bp widening in HY spreads is equivalent to approximately 25-50bp of Fed funds rate tightening. The implication is that the Fed's actual reaction function includes financial conditions as a de facto third mandate — and the credit-equity contagion channel is the mechanism through which financial conditions enter the Taylor Rule.

8. **The current macro regime exhibits a dangerous asymmetry: credit-equity linkage is loose on the upside (credit tightness is not binding in expansion) but will tighten sharply on the downside, creating a non-linear cliff risk that standard VAR models underestimate by construction.** This asymmetry arises because the Merton model payoff structure is inherently non-linear (the put option embedded in credit is far out-of-the-money in expansion but moves toward at-the-money as asset values decline). Combined with the financial accelerator's feedback loop and the maturity wall's time-varying refinancing pressure, the system has more downside convexity than upside. This is the credit-equity equivalent of the NKPC convexity concept: the sacrifice ratio is low when the economy is above potential but escalates non-linearly as it approaches potential.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Merton model as the structural bridge
The Merton (1974) framework and its extensions (Black-Cox 1976, Leland 1994, CreditGrades) treat the firm's equity as a European call option on its assets with a strike price equal to the face value of debt. Credit spreads are the price of the implicit put option sold by debtholders. This framework generates several testable predictions: (a) credit spreads should correlate with equity volatility (confirmed empirically: Collin-Dufresne, Goldstein & Martin 2001 find equity volatility explains 25-30% of credit spread variation); (b) the correlation between credit returns and equity returns should increase in stress (confirmed: Campbell & Taksler 2003 document this asymmetry); (c) the mapping should be stronger for HY than IG (confirmed: HY is closer to the default boundary, so the put option is closer to at-the-money). The framework is not perfect — the "credit spread puzzle" documents that Merton models underpredict observed spreads by 50-100bp for IG, attributable to liquidity and tax premia — but it provides the correct structural intuition for the credit-equity linkage.

### Claim 2: Credit impulse as output gap leading indicator
The credit impulse was formalized by Biggs, Mayer & Pick (2009) and has since been validated by the BIS as a leading indicator of GDP growth. The mechanism is IS-curve economics: what matters for demand is the flow of new borrowing (which adds to spending power), not the stock of debt. The second derivative — the impulse — captures whether the flow is accelerating or decelerating. Current data: the Fed's Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey (SLOOS) shows net tightening of lending standards for C&I loans persisting through 2025, with the percentage of banks tightening still above the percentage easing (though the spread has narrowed from its 2023 peak). HY gross issuance has been robust, but net issuance (gross minus maturities and calls) has been flat to negative as the maturity wall forces refinancing activity. The KB's maturity_wall_2025_2027 concept (confidence 10) confirms that $900B-$1.1T of corporate debt comes due, with the majority being refinanced rather than adding net credit to the system. This means the credit impulse is flat to negative even though gross issuance headlines look healthy — a critical distinction.

### Claim 3: Financial accelerator amplification
The BGG (1999) financial accelerator is a core mechanism in DSGE models used by the Fed (FRB/US, SIGMA) and the ECB (NAWM). The external finance premium — the wedge between the cost of internal funds and external borrowing — is endogenous to the firm's net worth, which is primarily determined by equity prices for public firms and by collateral values for smaller firms. Quantitatively, BGG calibrate the elasticity of the external finance premium to the leverage ratio at approximately 0.05 (a 1% increase in leverage raises the external finance premium by 5bp). With corporate leverage elevated — the KB's ebitda_addback_distortion concept (confidence 7) documents actual leverage at 7.5-8.5x for leveraged borrowers vs. reported 6x — the financial accelerator is more loaded than headline leverage data suggests. The amplification factor (the ratio of the output response with vs. without the accelerator) is estimated at 1.5-2.0x for investment shocks and 1.2-1.5x for monetary policy shocks in standard calibrations. This means a credit shock that reduces investment by 1% would reduce output by 1.5-2.0% through the accelerator, compared to only 1% in a frictionless model.

### Claim 4: HY spread threshold for equity contagion
The non-linear threshold is derived from multiple empirical observations: (a) primary market access: HY new issuance effectively shuts down when OAS exceeds ~600bp, as documented by Gilchrist & Zakrajsek (2012) and observable in issuance data during 2008, 2011, 2015-16, and March 2020. When primary markets close, borrowers cannot refinance, and the maturity wall becomes a solvency wall. (b) Credit-equity beta: regression analysis of HY index returns on S&P 500 returns shows the beta is approximately 0.3-0.4 when HY OAS is below 400bp but rises to 0.6-0.8 above 500bp and approaches 1.0 above 700bp (Bai, Collin-Dufresne & Goldstein 2019). (c) The Fed reaction function creates a put: the Fed's financial conditions monitoring and its history of responding to credit stress (2018 Q4, March 2020) means that the threshold is partially endogenous — the Fed will likely ease before spreads reach levels that shut down primary markets. The KB's cross_asset_growth_rate_divergence concept (confidence 7) notes that rates markets price 75-100bp of easing, which would compress spreads before the 600bp threshold is reached — unless the easing is blocked by inflation (the stagflationary bifurcation scenario).

### Claim 5: CRE-to-bank-equity contagion
The bank capital channel has been extensively studied in the New Keynesian literature (Van den Heuvel 2002, Gertler & Kiyotaki 2010). The mechanism: CRE loan losses reduce bank capital → banks constrained by regulatory capital requirements (CET1 ratio targets of 10-13% for large banks) tighten lending standards → credit supply contracts → investment and consumption decline → output falls. The current state: U.S. banks hold ~$2.7T in CRE loans (Fed H.8 data), concentrated in regional and community banks where CRE represents 30-50% of total loans (vs. 10-15% for money center banks). CRE prices have declined 15-30% from peak depending on property type (office worst, multifamily more resilient). The key empirical question is how much of this loss has been realized vs. deferred through extend-and-pretend. The FDIC reported ~$72B in non-current CRE loans as of Q4 2025, but this likely understates true impairment due to maturity extension practices. If unrealized losses of $400-700B are eventually recognized, regional bank CET1 ratios would decline by 200-500bp, bringing many below the stress capital buffer — triggering mandatory lending contraction. This is a slow-moving contagion channel (2-5 year timeline) but a quantitatively significant one.

### Claim 6: Private credit spillover via portfolio balance
The portfolio balance channel is a standard New Keynesian open-economy mechanism (Tobin 1969, modernized by Vayanos & Vila 2021 in the QE context) applied to cross-sector contagion. Insurance companies are the largest institutional holders of private credit (~$700B), followed by pension funds (~$300B) and retail semi-liquid vehicles (BDCs, interval funds, ~$300-400B). The mechanism: private credit marks are smoothed (mark-to-model, quarterly reporting lags) — the KB's private_credit_opacity concept (confidence 7.5) establishes this. When fundamental deterioration eventually forces marks lower (either through defaults, rating agency downgrades, or regulatory pressure), the portfolio owners face allocation drift: private credit's share of their portfolio shrinks, requiring either (a) selling liquid assets to maintain portfolio weights, or (b) accepting an underweight — which risk management frameworks typically resist. For insurance companies subject to risk-based capital (RBC) requirements, the mechanism is even more direct: downgraded private credit assets attract higher capital charges, forcing sales of other assets to maintain surplus ratios. The March 2023 SVB episode demonstrated a milder version of this channel: unrealized losses on one asset class (held-to-maturity bonds) spilled over to equity prices across the banking sector via the common balance sheet channel.

### Claim 7: Credit conditions and the modified Taylor Rule
The Curdia-Woodford (2010) extension of the standard New Keynesian model adds a credit spread term to the Taylor Rule: r* = r_natural + φ_π(π - π*) + φ_y(y - y*) - φ_s(s - s*), where s is the credit spread and s* is its "natural" level consistent with full information frictionless markets. The additional term captures the idea that credit spread widening driven by financial frictions (not fundamentals) has contractionary effects that monetary policy should offset. Empirically, the Fed has behaved consistently with this rule: the 2018 Q4 pivot (spreads widened ~200bp → Powell shifted from "long way from neutral" to "patient"), the March 2020 response (emergency rate cuts and QE when spreads blew out), and the 2023 pause (banking stress caused implicit tightening that the Fed acknowledged as a substitute for additional rate hikes). The Gilchrist-Zakrajsek (2012) excess bond premium (EBP) — the component of credit spreads not explained by expected defaults — is the empirical proxy for this financial friction term. The EBP predicts GDP growth at 1-4 quarter horizons with an R-squared of 15-25%, comparable to the term spread's predictive power.

### Claim 8: Asymmetric downside convexity
The non-linearity in credit-equity linkage is a structural feature of the Merton model: the delta of the embedded put option increases as the firm approaches the default boundary. At the macro level, this means the credit-equity correlation is state-dependent. In the current state (HY OAS ~350-420bp, output gap near zero, default rates ~2-3%), the system is in the low-correlation, low-convexity regime. But the phase transition to the high-correlation, high-convexity regime is abrupt rather than gradual. Historical episodes confirm this: in 2007, HY OAS went from ~300bp in June to ~800bp by November — a 500bp widening in 5 months — with the credit-equity correlation jumping from ~0.3 to ~0.8 over the same period. Linear VAR models trained on the full sample (which is dominated by the low-correlation regime) will systematically underestimate the tail risk. The KB's risk_appetite_regime_sequencing concept (confidence 9) captures this: the transition from complacent carry to anxiety to panic is sequential but accelerating. The maturity_wall_2025_2027 (confidence 10) introduces a time-varying catalyst: refinancing pressure mechanically increases over the next 18 months, reducing the distance to the high-convexity regime.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Risk Appetite Regimes (iter_0001)
- **Risk appetite regime sequencing** (confidence 9): The credit-equity linkage is the transmission mechanism through which risk appetite regime transitions propagate. The complacency → anxiety transition is signaled by HY spread widening without equity follow-through; the anxiety → panic transition is signaled by the credit-equity correlation jumping to 0.7+. The financial accelerator explains *why* the transitions accelerate once started.
- **Correlation regime shifts** (confidence 9): The credit-equity linkage is a primary driver of the shift from negative to positive stock-bond correlation. In the disinflationary scenario, credit stress → equity decline → flight to quality → bonds rally (negative correlation). In the stagflationary scenario, credit stress → equity decline AND inflation persistence → bonds sell off (positive correlation). The macro regime determines which correlation pathway activates.
- **Basis trade fragility** (confidence 9): Credit market stress that triggers de-risking flows can spill into the Treasury basis trade via two channels: (a) hedge funds that run both credit and basis trade positions face cross-margining and may unwind basis trades to meet credit margin calls; (b) a flight to quality that compresses Treasury yields can widen the basis, stressing levered positions.

### Economic Growth Models (iter_0002)
- **Fiscal deficit Kalecki channel** (confidence 9): The fiscal channel currently suppresses credit-equity contagion by sustaining corporate profits and reducing default risk. The Kalecki identity shows that the 6-7% fiscal deficit flows directly to the corporate sector as profits, reducing the probability of hitting the Merton default boundary. Fiscal consolidation would remove this floor, activating the credit-equity contagion channel. This is the key state variable: as long as the fiscal deficit sustains profits, the Merton model's distance-to-default remains wide and credit-equity linkage stays loose.
- **Cross-asset growth rate divergence** (confidence 7): The credit-equity linkage provides the mechanism through which the divergence resolves. If rates markets are correct (growth disappoints), the credit impulse weakens, HY spreads widen toward the 500bp threshold, and the financial accelerator drags equities toward the rates-implied valuation. If equities are correct (growth holds), credit conditions remain benign and spreads stay compressed. Credit spreads are the arbiter between the two narratives.
- **EBITDA addback distortion** (confidence 7): The addback distortion means the financial accelerator is more loaded than observable leverage ratios suggest. True leverage of 7.5-8.5x (vs. reported 6x) implies that the Merton distance-to-default is significantly narrower than model estimates based on reported financials. This reduces the gap between "credit-equity linkage is loose" and "credit-equity linkage has tightened," making the phase transition closer than headline data suggests.
- **Output gap measurement error** (confidence 9): The credit impulse provides an independent cross-check on the output gap: if the credit impulse is negative while traditional output gap estimates show a positive gap, it suggests demand-side deterioration that traditional measures may be missing. This is particularly valuable given the mean absolute revision of 1.0-2.0pp in real-time output gap estimates.

### Labor Market Dynamics (iter_0003)
- **Credit leads labor sequencing** (confidence 9): This concept is the direct empirical validation of the macro framework's prediction: in the New Keynesian model with financial frictions, credit conditions lead real activity because the external finance premium is a forward-looking price that responds to expected future cash flows, while labor data is a lagging indicator of realized output. The 3-12 month lead of credit spreads over the Sahm Rule is the observed manifestation of the financial accelerator operating faster than the labor market adjusts.
- **Labor hoarding cliff risk** (confidence 5): The credit-equity linkage amplifies the cliff risk: if credit conditions tighten (HY spreads breach 500bp), firms that were hoarding labor face both a revenue decline and a financing cost increase simultaneously. The external finance premium rises precisely when firms need to access credit to bridge the revenue shortfall, forcing more aggressive layoffs than a pure demand shock would imply. This is the financial accelerator mechanism applied specifically to the labor hoarding problem.

### Credit Cycles (depth_level 3, confidence 6.0)
- The credit impulse, the maturity wall, and the bank capital channel are all components of credit cycle dynamics. The credit-equity linkage is the cross-asset manifestation of the credit cycle — it is the mechanism through which credit cycle turning points become equity market events. The KB's credit_leads_labor_sequencing concept establishes the sequencing; this analysis provides the structural framework (Merton + BGG + modified Taylor Rule) that explains *why* credit leads.

### Real Estate Cycles (depth_level 2, confidence 5.5)
- CRE-to-bank-equity contagion (Claim 5) is the primary linkage between real estate cycles and financial markets. The New Keynesian framework predicts that CRE shocks operate as a negative wealth shock to bank balance sheets, which contracts credit supply to all sectors — not just real estate. This cross-sectoral contamination is the distinguishing feature of the bank capital channel vs. a sectoral reallocation.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the current distance-to-default for the aggregate HY market, adjusted for EBITDA addback distortions?** The standard KMV/CreditGrades models use reported leverage, which the KB establishes is understated by 15-40%. Recalculating distance-to-default with true leverage would give a more accurate estimate of how close the system is to the Merton threshold.

2. **How does the private credit market's lack of continuous mark-to-market alter the financial accelerator dynamics?** In the standard BGG model, the feedback loop operates through market prices. If $1.5T+ of credit is marked to model with quarterly lags, does this dampen the accelerator (because marks don't move) or amplify it (because the eventual mark-down is larger and more abrupt)?

3. **Is the Fed's implicit financial conditions target — the "Fed put" — priced into credit spreads in a way that distorts the Gilchrist-Zakrajsek EBP signal?** If markets expect the Fed to respond to credit stress, the EBP may understate the true financial friction because spreads never reach levels they would without the expected policy response. This creates moral hazard and potentially reduces the signaling value of the very indicator the Fed uses to calibrate its response.

4. **What is the elasticity of the CRE extend-and-pretend channel to interest rate levels?** If rates remain elevated, extend-and-pretend becomes more expensive for banks (they are extending at higher rates with lower property values as collateral). Does this mean the CRE-to-bank contagion channel is rate-level dependent, creating an interaction between monetary policy and financial stability that the standard Taylor Rule doesn't capture?

5. **Can the credit impulse be decomposed into bank credit vs. non-bank credit components, and do they have different GDP-predictive power?** The shift of credit intermediation from banks to private credit and direct lending means the aggregate credit impulse may miss sectoral shifts in credit allocation. If private credit is growing (substituting for bank credit) but bank credit is contracting, the net impulse could be flat while the composition shifts in a way that matters for financial stability.

6. **What is the correlation between private credit marks and public equity prices at the common-owner level?** Insurance companies and pension funds hold both. If the correlation is low (because private marks are smoothed), does the portfolio balance channel only activate when marks are eventually adjusted — creating a delayed, discontinuous contagion rather than the continuous adjustment assumed in standard models?

7. **How does the 2025-2027 maturity wall interact with the credit-equity linkage threshold?** If maturity wall refinancing occurs at spreads below 500bp, the wall is manageable and the credit-equity linkage stays loose. If macro deterioration pushes spreads above 500bp before the bulk of refinancing is completed, the maturity wall becomes the catalyst for the phase transition to the high-convexity regime. What is the probability distribution over this timing intersection?
