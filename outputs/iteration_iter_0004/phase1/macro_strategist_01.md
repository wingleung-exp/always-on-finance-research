# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are fundamentally driven by the interaction of the output gap, the monetary policy stance relative to the Taylor Rule, and inflation expectations—not by market sentiment in isolation.** The risk-on/risk-off (RORO) framework, while empirically observable in asset correlations, is a reduced-form description of underlying macro state transitions between demand-driven expansions, supply shocks, and policy-induced tightening.

2. **The current macro regime (Q1 2026) is best characterized as "late-cycle risk appetite with fragile underpinnings": the output gap is near zero or slightly positive, real policy rates remain restrictive relative to most r-star estimates, and risk appetite persists primarily due to forward-looking expectations of monetary easing and AI-driven earnings optimism.** This creates a regime where risk appetite is high but structurally vulnerable to disappointment on either the growth or the rate-cut front.

3. **Risk appetite regime shifts are state-dependent on the source of the shock—demand vs. supply—and the degree of monetary policy accommodation available.** A demand shock (recession) with ample policy space produces a "V-shaped" risk-off/risk-on transition (e.g., 2020). A supply shock with constrained policy space produces a prolonged negative stock-bond correlation breakdown and an extended risk-off regime (e.g., 2022). The current environment sits uncomfortably between these archetypes.

4. **Contagion velocity in modern market structure has increased due to three structural changes: (i) the compression of dealer balance sheets under Basel III/IV, reducing market-making capacity; (ii) the rise of passive and systematic strategies that amplify momentum in both directions; and (iii) the retailization of previously institutional asset classes (private credit, interval funds), creating untested liquidity mismatch channels.** These structural shifts mean that the transmission from a macro shock to cross-asset contagion is faster than in pre-GFC market structures.

5. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades follow a predictable New Keynesian sequence: (i) an adverse shock widens the output gap or shifts inflation expectations; (ii) uncertainty about the central bank reaction function raises term premium and rate volatility; (iii) higher rate volatility tightens Value-at-Risk constraints on leveraged intermediaries; (iv) forced deleveraging transmits from rates to credit to equities; (v) the feedback loop amplifies via the financial accelerator (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist, 1999) as tightening financial conditions widen the output gap further.** This cascade is not random—it has a causal structure rooted in the interaction of monetary policy, intermediary balance sheets, and the real economy.

6. **The Taylor Rule provides a natural framework for identifying risk appetite regime boundaries.** When the actual policy rate is substantially below the Taylor-prescribed rate (as in 2021), excess monetary accommodation fuels a risk-on regime characterized by compressed risk premia, reach-for-yield behavior, and rising leverage. When the policy rate overshoots the Taylor prescription (as arguably in late 2023/early 2024), it creates a "tight money" regime that, with a lag, erodes risk appetite through its effect on credit availability, corporate earnings, and housing. The transition between these regimes—not the level of rates per se—is what triggers the most violent cross-asset moves.

7. **The probability distribution over risk appetite regimes for the next 12 months is asymmetric: the base case (55%) is a gradual transition toward neutral risk appetite as rate cuts materialize but growth decelerates; the upside (20%) is an extended risk-on driven by AI productivity gains lifting potential output; the downside (25%) is a sharp risk-off triggered by a policy error (rates held too high into an incipient recession) or an external shock (geopolitical, fiscal) that overwhelms the policy put.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Macro Foundations of Risk Appetite
The RORO literature (e.g., Bekaert, Hoerova, and Lo Duca, 2013) identifies VIX decomposition into risk aversion and uncertainty components as the empirical workhorse. From a New Keynesian perspective, these map onto identifiable macro states: risk aversion rises when the expected output gap deteriorates and agents revise down their consumption Euler equations, while uncertainty rises when the central bank reaction function becomes ambiguous (e.g., during regime transitions between hiking and cutting). The IS curve provides the demand-side channel (interest-rate-sensitive spending declines as real rates rise), and the Phillips Curve provides the inflation channel (cost-push shocks create a negative risk premium for bonds that historically accompanies risk-off for equities). The NK model insists these are not independent—the joint behavior of growth, inflation, and policy determines the risk appetite regime.

### Claim 2: Current Regime Assessment
**Output gap**: The US economy appears near potential as of early 2026. Labor markets are balanced (unemployment near 4.0-4.2%), consistent with most NAIRU estimates. GDP growth has decelerated from the above-trend pace of 2023-2024 toward a 1.5-2.0% range, closer to consensus estimates of potential growth.

**Policy stance**: The Fed funds rate, even after cuts that began in late 2024, remains in a range that many estimates of r-star (Holston-Laubach-Williams at ~0.7-1.0% real, Lubik-Matthes somewhat higher) would characterize as mildly restrictive. The real federal funds rate (deflated by core PCE) remains above most neutral rate estimates by 50-100bp.

**Forward expectations**: Market pricing implies additional cuts, and the equity market—particularly the AI/technology complex—is priced for a soft landing plus secular productivity acceleration. This creates a "risk-on conditional on the future path" configuration that is inherently fragile: any disruption to the expected easing path (hotter-than-expected inflation, tariff-induced supply shock) removes the key prop.

### Claim 3: State-Dependent Regime Transitions
The NK framework makes a sharp distinction between demand and supply shocks because the central bank's optimal response differs:
- **Demand shock** (recession): Inflation falls, output falls. The Taylor Rule prescribes aggressive easing. The central bank's ability to cut rates and provide forward guidance shortens the risk-off duration. Historical analogues: 2001, 2008-09, 2020.
- **Supply shock** (cost-push): Inflation rises, output falls. The Taylor Rule creates a trade-off—the central bank faces a "divine coincidence" violation (Blanchard and Galí, 2007). Risk-off is prolonged because the policy put is constrained. Historical analogue: 2022.
- **Mixed/ambiguous shock**: The worst case for risk appetite stability, because it creates maximum uncertainty about the reaction function. The current tariff/deglobalization environment has elements of both supply and demand disruption, making regime identification difficult.

### Claim 4: Structural Acceleration of Contagion
Three empirically documented structural changes:

(i) **Dealer balance sheet compression**: Primary dealer assets relative to outstanding securities have declined significantly since pre-GFC levels. Adrian and Shin (2010, 2014) document how dealer leverage is procyclical. Basel III leverage ratios and supplementary leverage ratio (SLR) requirements have structurally reduced dealers' willingness to absorb selling pressure. The September 2019 repo spike and the March 2020 Treasury market dislocation are evidence that the market's shock-absorption capacity has diminished.

(ii) **Passive/systematic amplification**: Index funds, risk parity, volatility-targeting, and CTA strategies now represent a substantially larger share of equity and rates AUM than a decade ago. These strategies are mechanically procyclical: vol-targeting strategies sell risky assets when realized volatility rises, creating the feedback loop described in Brunnermeier and Pedersen (2009) between market liquidity and funding liquidity. The August 2024 VIX spike and yen carry unwind demonstrated this amplification in practice.

(iii) **Retailization liquidity mismatch**: As documented in the knowledge base (retailization_liquidity_risk concept), $200B+ in semi-liquid private credit vehicles offer quarterly redemption against 5-7 year underlying loan maturities. This creates a classic Diamond-Dybvig (1983) bank-run vulnerability in a new venue. The Minsky cycle dynamics (minsky_cycle_demographic_conditioning concept) suggest that prolonged risk-on regimes encourage exactly this kind of maturity transformation.

### Claim 5: The Cascade Mechanism
The financial accelerator (Bernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist, 1999) is the canonical NK model of how financial frictions amplify macro shocks. The sequence:

1. An adverse shock widens the output gap → corporate cash flows decline → external finance premium rises.
2. Rising uncertainty about the policy reaction function → term premium increases (Adrian, Crump, Moench, 2013) → rate volatility rises.
3. Higher rate volatility → VaR and risk-budgeting constraints bind → leveraged intermediaries reduce positions.
4. Forced selling in rates → spillover to credit (rising spreads) → spillover to equities (multiple compression).
5. Tighter financial conditions (Goldman Sachs FCI, Chicago Fed NFCI) feed back into the IS curve → wider output gap → loop continues.

This is not merely theoretical. The March 2020 cascade (Treasury → credit → equities → EM) demonstrated the mechanism at speed, arrested only by the Fed's overwhelming balance sheet intervention. The key NK insight is that the cascade has a causal macro structure: it begins with an output gap shock and is mediated by the policy reaction function.

### Claim 6: Taylor Rule as Regime Boundary
The Taylor Rule (Taylor, 1993; modified versions per Taylor, 1999) prescribes:

i_t = r* + π_t + 0.5(π_t − π*) + 0.5(y_t − y*)

When i_actual << i_Taylor, monetary policy is excessively accommodative. This manifests as:
- Compressed term premium (lower compensation for duration risk)
- Compressed credit spreads (reach-for-yield across the credit spectrum)
- Elevated equity multiples (lower discount rate in the Gordon growth model)
- Rising leverage (debt issuance is cheap, equity buybacks are financed)

These are precisely the conditions that characterize risk-on regimes. The transition to risk-off typically occurs when the gap narrows—either because the Fed tightens (the 2022 episode) or because inflation rises enough that the Taylor-prescribed rate jumps discontinuously (supply shock). The NK framework thus provides a structural, not ad hoc, account of why risk appetite regimes persist and why they transition.

### Claim 7: Probability Distribution
- **Base case (55%): Gradual normalization.** The Fed delivers 2-3 additional cuts in 2026, bringing the policy rate closer to neutral. Growth decelerates but remains positive. Risk appetite transitions from elevated to neutral. Spreads widen modestly (HY +50-75bp), equity multiples compress 1-2 turns, rate volatility (MOVE index) declines.
- **Upside (20%): Extended risk-on.** AI productivity gains materialize faster than expected, lifting potential output and corporate earnings. The output gap remains near zero despite above-trend growth because the supply side expands. This is the "roaring 20s" analogue—risk premia stay compressed, multiples expand, and the Taylor Rule validates accommodative policy because inflation remains anchored.
- **Downside (25%): Sharp risk-off.** Tariff escalation creates a stagflationary impulse. The Phillips Curve steepens as inflation expectations de-anchor. The Fed faces a divine coincidence violation: it cannot ease to support growth without validating higher inflation. This is the worst scenario for risk appetite because the policy put is disabled. Historical analogue: the 1970s oil shocks, though the institutional framework is stronger today.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Volatility regimes**: Risk appetite regime shifts are the macro cause; volatility regime shifts are the market-microstructure effect. The MOVE index (rates vol) is the leading indicator because it reflects uncertainty about the central bank reaction function, which is the proximate driver of risk appetite in the NK framework. VIX follows as the equity market reprices the output gap consequences.

**Credit cycles**: The credit cycle is the amplification mechanism for risk appetite regime shifts, not the trigger. In the Minsky framework (compatible with NK via the financial accelerator), the credit cycle determines the economy's vulnerability to a risk appetite shift: late-cycle credit extension (tight spreads, weak covenants, high leverage) means that a given negative shock produces a larger cascade. The knowledge base's credit cycle research (depth_level 3, confidence 6.0) provides the foundation for assessing current vulnerability.

**Equity cycles**: Equity valuations embed risk appetite via the equity risk premium. The NK framework links equity cycles to risk appetite through the discount rate channel (r* + term premium + equity risk premium) and the cash flow channel (output gap → corporate earnings). Current AI-driven earnings optimism may be decoupled from the macro cycle, creating basis risk between equity-implied risk appetite and credit-implied risk appetite.

**FX regimes**: The dollar smile framework (Jen, 2001) maps directly to risk appetite regimes: the dollar strengthens in risk-off (flight to quality), weakens in goldilocks risk-on (capital flows to EM for higher returns), and strengthens again in US-exceptionalism risk-on (capital flows to US for growth). The current regime—where the dollar benefits from both rate differentials and AI-driven growth expectations—is an unusual confluence.

**Monetary policy**: The central bank reaction function is the single most important determinant of risk appetite regime duration and transition dynamics. Ambiguity about the reaction function (is the Fed data-dependent? What data? Does it weight financial conditions?) is itself a regime variable. The "Fed put" is the mechanism by which the central bank truncates the left tail of risk-off outcomes.

**Fiscal policy**: Fiscal impulse is increasingly relevant to risk appetite regimes as government deficits crowd into the Treasury supply picture. A fiscal-dominance scenario (where the central bank is constrained by the government's financing needs) would structurally alter the risk appetite framework by disabling the policy put for a different reason than supply-side inflation.

**Demographics**: The knowledge base's stock_bond_correlation_regime concept (confidence 8) establishes that aging-driven labor scarcity biases toward positive stock-bond correlation during stress, which directly impacts the severity of risk-off episodes for 60/40 portfolios. The retailization_liquidity_risk concept (confidence 6) identifies a novel contagion channel through semi-liquid private credit vehicles held by aging investors.

## Open Questions

1. **How should we calibrate the Taylor Rule gap as a leading indicator of risk appetite transitions?** The optimal specification (original Taylor 1993 vs. modified Taylor 1999 vs. inflation-forecast-based variants) matters for signal quality, and the choice of r-star estimate dominates the output. A 50bp difference in r-star can flip the signal from "accommodative" to "restrictive."

2. **Has the "Fed put" strike price moved, and if so, how does this alter the risk appetite regime distribution?** If the Fed's willingness to intervene is now conditional on inflation being below target (i.e., the put only exists when inflation is low), the tail risk distribution in the risk-off regime is materially different from 2010-2019.

3. **Is there an empirically stable relationship between financial conditions indices (GS FCI, Chicago Fed NFCI) and the output gap, or has this relationship shifted post-pandemic?** If the transmission from financial conditions to real activity has weakened (perhaps because household balance sheets are stronger), then the financial accelerator cascade is dampened and risk-off episodes may be shallower.

4. **What is the contagion velocity from private credit/retailized vehicles to public markets?** The retailization_liquidity_risk channel is theoretically compelling but has never been tested under stress. Would gates and redemption queues in semi-liquid private credit vehicles trigger secondary effects in public HY markets, or would the illiquidity act as a containment mechanism?

5. **How do we model the interaction between tariff-induced supply shocks and risk appetite when the standard demand-shock framework doesn't apply?** The current macro environment features both potential demand destruction (from tariff costs to consumers) and supply disruption (from input cost increases). The NK model's divine coincidence violation under supply shocks means the policy reaction is ambiguous—and policy ambiguity is the primary accelerant of risk-off transitions.

6. **What is the structural break test for the claim that contagion velocity has permanently increased?** It is possible that the March 2020 and August 2024 episodes reflect unusual circumstances (pandemic, yen carry concentration) rather than a permanent structural shift in market microstructure. A rigorous NK approach would want to distinguish between parameter instability and regime change before concluding that the financial system is permanently more fragile.

7. **How should cross-country monetary policy divergence be incorporated into a risk appetite regime framework?** The Fed, ECB, and BOJ are at different points in their policy cycles. Divergence creates carry trade opportunities that themselves become sources of risk appetite (yen carry → risk-on) and vulnerability (yen carry unwind → risk-off). The Mundell-Fleming framework suggests that policy divergence under flexible exchange rates transmits through the exchange rate, but the scale of modern carry trades may overwhelm the textbook adjustment mechanism.
