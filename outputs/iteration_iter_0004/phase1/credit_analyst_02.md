# Credit-Equity Linkage & Contagion Channels — Structured & Private Credit Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The Merton model framework, while theoretically elegant, systematically understates credit-equity linkage in practice because it ignores the structural plumbing of modern credit markets — CLO mechanics, private credit opacity, and covenant-lite documentation create non-linear transmission channels that the simple equity-volatility-to-credit-spread mapping cannot capture.**

2. **The dominant contagion direction has reversed: in 2008, credit stress transmitted to equities through bank balance sheets; in the current cycle, the primary contagion channel runs from credit market structure (maturity wall, CLO overcollateralization test failures, private credit NAV markdowns) to equity markets through (a) bank equity impairment from CRE/private credit losses and (b) forced selling cascades triggered by CLO structural features.**

3. **Private credit-to-public market spillover is the most underpriced contagion channel. The $1.7T+ private credit market is invisible to HY spread indices, public default statistics, and standard financial conditions indices — meaning that credit stress can accumulate to critical mass in private markets before any public market signal fires. When the signal finally arrives (via BDC markdowns, interval fund gate activations, or bank provisioning), the equity market reaction will be disproportionate to the apparent "new" information because the true credit deterioration has been building for 12-24 months.**

4. **CLO overcollateralization (OC) test mechanics create a discrete, non-linear contagion channel from credit to equities. When CCC-bucket holdings breach the 7.5% threshold in a CLO, the manager must divert cash flows from equity tranches to de-lever senior tranches. This is not a smooth function — it is a step function that, when triggered across the $1T+ CLO market, simultaneously (a) destroys CLO equity returns (hitting bank and asset manager equity valuations), (b) creates forced selling pressure in the leveraged loan market, and (c) widens spreads in a self-reinforcing loop.**

5. **The HY spread threshold for equity market contagion is regime-dependent, but the structural evidence suggests ~500bp OAS on the HY index is the approximate tipping point where credit stress becomes self-reinforcing and equity markets must reprice. Below 500bp, credit stress is absorbable; above 500bp, the maturity wall becomes a solvency wall, CLO arbitrage economics invert, and the credit-equity feedback loop activates. Currently, HY OAS sits near 300bp (as of early 2026), meaning the market is pricing in essentially zero probability of this scenario.**

6. **The maturity wall of $900B-$1.1T in leveraged credit maturing 2025-2027 is the clock on credit-equity contagion. This is not a prediction about whether contagion will occur — it is a statement about when the refinancing test must be passed. At current spread levels, the wall is manageable. At 500bp+ OAS with rates at 4.5%+, the all-in refinancing cost for a typical B-rated borrower reaches 10-11%, implying debt service consumes 70-80% of actual (addback-adjusted) cash EBITDA. This is the mechanism through which credit market structure forces itself onto equity valuations.**

7. **EBITDA addback distortions (15-40% inflation of reported leverage metrics) mean that credit-equity linkage models calibrated on reported leverage ratios systematically understate the true fragility of the leveraged loan universe. A borrower reported at 6x leverage is likely at 7.5-8.5x on cash EBITDA — the equity cushion is thinner than it appears, making the Merton-model distance-to-default calculation dangerously optimistic.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 (Merton model inadequacy):** The Merton model treats a firm's equity as a call option on its assets, with credit spreads derivable from equity volatility and leverage. This works in theory for a single firm with transparent balance sheet. But the modern leveraged credit ecosystem has three features that break this mapping: (a) covenant-lite structures (>90% of leveraged loans) mean distressed borrowers can avoid default for 12-24 months longer than covenanted structures would allow, creating a temporal disconnect between equity signals and credit events; (b) private credit borrowers (~$1.7T) are not in any public equity or credit index, so the Merton framework has no observable inputs; (c) CLO structural mechanics create forced-selling dynamics that are unrelated to any individual firm's fundamentals. The Merton model sees the firm; the structural credit analyst sees the plumbing through which distress propagates.

**Claim 2 (Reversed contagion direction):** In 2007-2008, the contagion ran: subprime credit losses → bank balance sheet impairment → equity market crash → credit tightening → real economy. Banks were the nexus. Today, banks hold substantially less leveraged credit on-balance-sheet (post-Dodd-Frank), but they have significant exposure through (a) CRE loans (especially regional banks with 30-50% CRE concentration), (b) warehouse lines for CLO formation, (c) fund financing to private credit vehicles, and (d) residual CLO equity holdings. The contagion path now runs: credit market structural stress (maturity wall, CLO OC failures) → bank CRE/credit losses → bank equity decline → tightening of warehouse lines and fund financing → amplification of original credit stress. Additionally, ~$300B+ in BDC/interval fund/non-traded REIT retail assets create a second channel: NAV markdowns → retail redemptions → forced selling → loan market dislocation → wider equity market confidence impact.

**Claim 3 (Private credit spillover):** The knowledge base already documents private credit opacity (confidence 7.5) and private credit rate insensitivity. The spillover channel is not about private credit losses per se — it is about the information shock when those losses become visible. Private credit NAVs are manager-determined, updated quarterly, and smoothed. PIK toggle usage (estimated at 5-8% of private credit portfolios, up from ~2% in 2022) allows interest income to be accrued without cash collection, masking deterioration. When a large BDC or interval fund is forced to mark down — whether by auditor intervention, a failed credit facility covenant, or a realization event — the market suddenly recalibrates its estimate of the entire private credit universe. This is a classic information cascade / adverse selection problem. The equity market impact arrives not through direct losses but through uncertainty repricing: if this one fund's NAV was overstated by 15%, what about the rest?

**Claim 4 (CLO OC test mechanics):** CLO structures typically have an OC test that limits CCC-rated holdings to ~7.5% of the portfolio (by par value). When breached, excess CCC holdings are carried at market value rather than par, which mechanically fails the OC test and diverts cash flows from junior to senior tranches. With ~$1T in US CLO assets, even a moderate wave of downgrades to CCC (e.g., if 5% of BB-rated CLO holdings are downgraded) would trigger widespread OC failures. The forced selling that follows is procyclical: CLO managers sell CCC-rated loans at distressed prices, which depresses recovery assumptions for other holders, which triggers further markdowns, which impairs CLO equity returns, which reduces appetite for new CLO formation, which removes the single largest source of demand for leveraged loans. This is the structural credit-equity linkage that Merton cannot model.

**Claim 5 (500bp threshold):** The 500bp threshold is derived from the intersection of two structural constraints: (a) CLO arbitrage economics — CLO equity tranches typically need a spread differential of ~200bp between asset yield and liability cost to generate adequate returns. At 500bp OAS on the HY index, CLO AAA spreads widen to ~180-200bp (from current ~130bp), and the arbitrage collapses, shutting down new CLO formation; (b) refinancing feasibility — at 500bp OAS with a 5-year Treasury at ~4%, a B-rated borrower faces an all-in coupon of ~9-10%, which is manageable for some but fatal for the most leveraged. The 500bp level is approximate and regime-dependent, but it represents the zone where structural market mechanics shift from self-correcting to self-reinforcing.

**Claim 6 (Maturity wall as clock):** The maturity wall data is arithmetic, not debatable (KB confidence 9). The question is whether borrowers can refinance at acceptable costs. At current spreads (~300bp OAS), the answer is yes for most. The KB documents that at rates of 4.5-5.0%, the drag is ~20-35bp on GDP with $30-50B/yr in additional interest costs. The credit-equity linkage arises because the maturity wall forces a binary outcome: either borrowers refinance (at higher costs, compressing equity valuations through reduced earnings and buybacks) or they cannot (defaults spike, recovery rates are impaired by covenant-lite structures, and equity markets reprice the credit cycle). The maturity wall is therefore the structural deadline by which credit-equity contagion must either occur or be avoided.

**Claim 7 (EBITDA addback distortion):** ECB and Fed analyses have documented the 15-40% range of EBITDA addback inflation (KB confidence 7). This directly affects credit-equity linkage because equity analysts typically use reported EBITDA to calculate EV/EBITDA multiples, while credit analysts (ideally) adjust for addbacks. The result is a persistent divergence between equity-market-implied leverage and actual leverage. When this divergence is resolved — typically in a downturn when projected synergies and cost savings fail to materialize — both equity and credit markets must reprice simultaneously, creating the correlated sell-off that credit-equity linkage models are designed to predict but typically underestimate.

This is a self-reinforcing loop that is visible *today* — not a hypothetical.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the actual current CCC-bucket utilization across the CLO market?** If the average CLO is at 4% CCC holdings, the buffer is comfortable. If it's at 6.5%, one moderate downgrade wave triggers the cascade. This data exists (from Intex, LCD, JPMorgan CLO research) but is not freely available.

2. **What is the true mark-to-recovery gap in private credit?** The KB documents the range as 5-25% — this is too wide to be actionable. Narrowing this range requires bottom-up analysis of private credit portfolio company financials, which are not publicly disclosed.

3. **How will semi-liquid private credit vehicles (interval funds, BDCs, non-traded REITs) behave in a stress scenario?** These vehicles have ~$300B+ in retail assets with quarterly or semi-annual liquidity. Historically, they have not been tested in a severe downturn under current AUM levels. Gate activations would trigger an information cascade.

4. **What is the empirical relationship between CLO formation volume and leveraged loan spreads?** CLOs have been the single largest buyer of leveraged loans (~65-70% of new issuance). If CLO formation stalls (due to arbitrage economics inverting), what happens to the equilibrium spread? Estimates range from 50-150bp of widening, but this has not been tested at scale.

5. **How does the private credit "extend-and-pretend" playbook interact with the maturity wall clock?** Private credit lenders can and do amend terms, add PIK toggles, and extend maturities — effectively buying time. But this does not eliminate the refinancing need; it defers and potentially compounds it. The question is whether this deferral mechanism can push enough of the maturity wall beyond the cycle window to prevent contagion.

6. **What are the actual bank exposures to private credit through warehouse lines, subscription facilities, and NAV lending?** These indirect exposures are poorly disclosed. If bank balance sheets are more exposed to private credit than reported (through off-balance-sheet facilities, fund financing, and residual positions), the CRE-to-bank-equity contagion model understates the true risk.

7. **Can public equity market signals (CDS-implied equity volatility, bank equity sub-sector performance, BDC discount-to-NAV) serve as early warning indicators for the private credit information cascade?** BDC trading at persistent 10-15% discounts to NAV may be a market-based signal that public investors do not trust private credit marks — but the signal-to-noise ratio is unclear.
