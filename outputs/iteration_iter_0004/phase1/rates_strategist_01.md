# Risk Appetite Regimes — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Term premium decomposition is the indispensable analytical step for interpreting risk appetite regime shifts in rates — not because it is the "best" single indicator, but because without it, yield curve movements are uninterpretable and frequently misleading.**

2. **The structural term premium regime has shifted from negative (2015–2021, central-bank-suppressed) to durably positive (2023–present), driven by the mutually reinforcing interaction of QT, fiscal supply, and widened inflation uncertainty. This is the highest-conviction rates regime call and it reshapes how every other risk appetite signal should be read.**

3. **Yield curve regime states (bull/bear × flat/steep) produce distinct risk appetite signals only after decomposition into expectations-driven vs. term-premium-driven moves. The 2s10s steepening-means-risk-on heuristic is now more dangerous than useful — late-2023 proved term-premium-driven steepening accompanies equity selloffs, not rallies.**

4. **The positive stock-bond correlation regime — now in its fourth year — is structurally linked to term premium dynamics and represents the single most consequential regime shift for multi-asset portfolio construction. In this regime, "risk-off = buy bonds" is invalid, and risk appetite drawdowns produce simultaneous losses across both asset classes.**

5. **The treasury basis trade (~$800B–1T notional, 20–50x levered) has converted the Treasury market from a regime stabilizer into a regime amplifier. In risk-off episodes, forced basis trade unwinds produce Treasury selling — the opposite of flight-to-quality — creating a reflexive feedback loop that accelerates cross-asset de-risking.**

6. **Fiscal impulse has supplanted central bank balance sheet policy as the dominant structural driver of term premium and, by extension, the risk appetite regime. The 6–7% deficit-to-GDP during expansion is the load-bearing floor under current risk appetite; fiscal consolidation is the most underpriced risk to the prevailing regime.**

7. **The 5y5y forward term premium, while model-dependent in level, is directionally informative as a structural regime barometer because it strips near-term policy noise. Its shift from negative to persistently positive captures the transition from "central bank backstop" regime to "fiscal credibility dependent" regime.**

8. **Foreign official reserve manager retreat ($200–400B aggregate reduction over the past decade, with China from ~$1.3T peak to ~$770B) is a slow-moving but irreversible structural headwind that raises baseline term premium and makes the market more sensitive to marginal domestic demand.**

9. **Repo market and funding plumbing are the transmission layer through which rates-market stress propagates into cross-asset risk appetite regime shifts. Sept 2019 and March 2020 demonstrated that when overnight funding seizes, the distinction between "safe haven" and "risk asset" collapses because Treasuries serve both roles simultaneously.**

10. **No single indicator — including term premium — achieves R² above ~0.15 for predicting risk appetite regime transitions. The defensible approach is a composite framework that combines term premium dynamics, credit signals (structural lending terms, CLO arbitrage economics), and funding market conditions, weighted by regime phase.**

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Decomposition as analytical necessity (evolved from iter_0003 Claim 1):**

The refined claim is structural, not superlative: any interpretation of yield curve movements without expectations/term-premium decomposition is unactionable. The late-2023 episode is canonical: 2s10s steepened from –100bp to –15bp, which a naive framework reads as "risk-on." In fact, most of the steepening was term-premium-driven (10Y sold off on fiscal supply + Fitch downgrade + BoJ policy shift), and equities sold off alongside. If the equity risk premium simultaneously widens (because higher discount rates compress multiples), both legs lose. The iter_0001 demographic analysis on labor scarcity creating persistent supply-side inflation pressure provides structural support for this correlation regime persisting. This is not merely a statistical observation — it invalidates the core hedging assumption of the dominant portfolio construction framework.

Removal of the fiscal impulse (austerity, spending cuts, or higher taxes) would simultaneously: raise term premium (market doubts credibility of consolidation) or collapse risk appetite (if consolidation is credible, the private sector income support vanishes). ## Connections to Other Topics

- **Credit markets:** Credit structural terms (covenants, addbacks, PIK toggle usage) are upstream of credit spreads as risk appetite barometers (iter_0003 synthesis, credit_analyst_02). Term premium and credit are linked via the discount rate channel: rising term premium compresses equity valuations, which tightens the credit-equity basis, which can trigger credit repricing. The "term premium up, spreads tight" combination (late 2023) is inherently unstable and resolves via either term premium mean-reversion or spread widening.

- **Private credit opacity:** The $1.7–2.0T private credit AUM with mark-to-model valuation (iter_0003 synthesis, credit_analyst_02) is invisible to term premium models but interacts through CLO warehousing and insurance company investment portfolios. If private credit NAVs face a reckoning, forced selling of liquid Treasuries by insurance companies would directly hit term premium.

- **Monetary policy transmission:** In the current positive-term-premium regime, the Fed's influence over long-end rates is diminished. Rate cuts primarily move the front end; the back end responds to fiscal and supply dynamics. This means the Fed's ability to ease financial conditions via conventional policy is structurally weaker — monetary policy "pushes on a string" at the long end when fiscal supply dominates.

- **Equity risk premium:** Term premium and equity risk premium are substitutes in investor asset allocation. A durable 50bp+ term premium regime should, all else equal, compress equity multiples by ~1.5–2.0 turns (PE ratio), as bonds offer more competitive risk-adjusted returns. This is the primary channel through which rates regime shifts affect equity valuations.

- **Dollar and EM contagion:** Term premium differentials drive carry flows. US term premium rising relative to peers strengthens the dollar via capital attraction, which tightens financial conditions for EM borrowers with $13T in offshore dollar-denominated debt (Bruno & Shin mechanism from iter_0003 synthesis). This is an underappreciated risk appetite transmission channel.

- **Fiscal sustainability / demographics:** The iter_0001 analysis established that demographic-driven entitlement spending is the structural engine behind rising duration supply. This iteration's fiscal impulse claim (Claim 6) is the cyclical expression of that structural force. The two are the same story at different time horizons: demographics set the trend, election-cycle fiscal decisions set the volatility around it.

- **Systematic/passive market structure:** Vol-targeting strategies ($300B+ AUM) and passive flows create reflexive dynamics that accelerate term premium moves in both directions. The 0DTE vol supply that structurally suppresses VIX (iter_0003 synthesis, challenger_01) means that VIX will understate the true fragility of risk appetite in a positive-term-premium regime — MOVE (rates vol) is a more reliable thermometer of duration risk appetite.

## Open Questions

1. **Model arbitration for term premium:** ACM and Kim-Wright diverge by 50–100bp at times. Can a model-agnostic regime classifier be constructed (e.g., principal component of multiple model estimates) that is robust to individual model specification errors? This is the single most important methodological question for operationalizing the decomposition framework.

2. **QE resumption / YCC threshold:** At what term premium level or degree of market dysfunction does the Fed intervene? The March 2020 response (unlimited QE when the Treasury market ceased functioning) and the March 2023 BTFP (immediate response to banking stress) suggest the Fed reacts to market functioning, not to yield levels per se. If so, the relevant question is: at what point does basis trade unwinding or repo stress trigger the intervention, and does that cap the structural term premium shift?

3. **Stock-bond correlation regime persistence:** Is the positive correlation a permanent feature of a supply-side inflation world, or does it revert once inflation expectations re-anchor? The answer determines whether 60/40 and risk parity are permanently impaired or temporarily dislocated. Monitoring: real-time inflation expectations decomposition, rolling 60-day stock-bond correlation, and the relative contribution of demand vs. supply shocks to inflation prints.

4. **Fiscal consolidation probability and transmission:** If deficit reduction occurs (spending cuts, revenue increases, or DOGE-style efficiency measures), what is the transmission to term premium — does it fall (lower supply) or rise (if markets doubt credibility of consolidation, or if the growth impact of austerity widens credit spreads)? The Eurozone 2010–2013 experience suggests credibility matters more than the arithmetic.

5. **Private credit → Treasury market linkage:** Insurance companies (major holders of both private credit and Treasuries) could become a contagion channel. If private credit writedowns trigger insurance company portfolio rebalancing, how large is the forced selling of liquid Treasuries, and does this create a term premium spike that feeds back into broader risk appetite? No data exists to quantify this channel.

6. **Basis trade sizing and concentration:** Current estimates ($800B–1T) are extrapolated. CFTC data on leveraged fund net short futures positioning provides partial visibility, but the repo financing side is opaque. A concentrated blowup (one or two large funds) versus distributed unwinding would produce very different term premium dynamics. What is the Herfindahl index of basis trade participation?

7. **Composite indicator construction:** The meta-finding that no single indicator achieves R² > 0.15 demands a practical follow-up: what is the optimal weighting scheme across term premium, credit structural terms, CLO arbitrage economics, and repo conditions? Should the weights be regime-phase-dependent (late-cycle vs. early-cycle)? Backtesting this across the 1998–2025 sample is the most actionable research question.
