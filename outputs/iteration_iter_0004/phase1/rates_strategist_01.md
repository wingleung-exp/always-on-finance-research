# Credit-Equity Linkage & Contagion Channels — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The yield curve term premium is the primary transmission mechanism linking credit and equity stress, not the level of rates itself.** Rising term premium compresses equity multiples and widens credit spreads simultaneously through a common discount-rate channel, while changes in expected short rates (monetary policy expectations) affect credit and equity differently and with variable lags.

2. **The 5y5y forward rate encodes critical information about the terminal cost of capital that drives both credit refinancing feasibility and equity valuation ceilings.** When the 5y5y forward rises above ~4.5%, it signals a structural repricing of neutral rates that makes the $900B-$1.1T maturity wall (2025-2027) significantly more damaging and compresses equity duration premia — creating a joint tightening channel that operates independently of Fed policy.

3. **Credit-equity contagion follows a yield-curve-mediated sequence: curve inversion first (expectations channel), then bear steepening (term premium channel), then credit spread blowout, then equity drawdown.** This sequencing is observable across the 1990, 2000-01, and 2007-09 cycles, with the bear steepener phase being the most dangerous for both credit and equity because it signals that the market is simultaneously pricing recession risk AND demanding compensation for fiscal/supply risk.

4. **The basis trade ($800B-$1T notional, 50-100x leverage) creates a novel and untested credit-equity contagion channel through Treasury market dysfunction.** A basis trade unwind forces Treasury selling that disrupts the risk-free rate benchmark, propagating stress from the rates market into credit spreads (via widening swap spreads and funding costs) and equity markets (via volatility-of-volatility and risk parity deleveraging) simultaneously.

5. **Treasury supply dynamics (issuance, auction performance, foreign official demand) now function as an independent credit-equity linkage channel that did not exist at this scale before the post-2020 fiscal expansion.** When term premium rises due to supply concerns rather than growth expectations, it produces a "bad" tightening that hits credit (higher refinancing costs without growth support) and equity (multiple compression without earnings growth) asymmetrically harder than rate-expectations-driven tightening.

6. **The current yield curve shape (inverted-to-flat at the front end, steepening at the long end) is consistent with late-Regime-1/early-Regime-2 risk appetite positioning, where credit and equity linkage is weakest — but the transition to Regime 2 will dramatically tighten the credit-equity correlation from ~0.3 to ~0.8 within 2-4 months.**

7. **Private credit's $1.7T opacity creates a "shadow term premium" that is not captured by any standard term structure model (ACM, Kim-Wright), meaning that conventional yield curve decompositions systematically understate the true cost of capital facing the corporate sector and therefore underestimate credit-equity contagion risk.**

8. **The HY OAS-to-VIX ratio is the single best real-time indicator of credit-equity regime, with a ratio above 25 signaling credit leading (contagion flows from credit to equity) and below 15 signaling equity leading (contagion flows from equity to credit via the Merton structural channel).**

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Term Premium as Primary Transmission Mechanism

The term premium component of yields represents the compensation investors demand for bearing duration risk — it is driven by inflation uncertainty, fiscal supply expectations, central bank balance sheet policies, and foreign demand. When term premium rises, it simultaneously:
- Raises the discount rate applied to equity cash flows (compressing P/E multiples, especially for long-duration growth stocks)
- Widens credit spreads because the opportunity cost of holding risky debt increases and the discount rate applied to recovery values rises
- Tightens financial conditions through mortgage rates and corporate bond yields

Crucially, a 50bp rise in the 10-year yield driven by term premium expansion has very different credit-equity implications than a 50bp rise driven by higher expected short rates. The latter reflects anticipated growth (which supports earnings and cash flows), while the former reflects pure risk compensation (which is a headwind to both credit and equity without any growth offset). Historical decomposition using the Adrian-Crump-Moench model shows that the 2013 Taper Tantrum, the 2018 Q4 selloff, and the 2023 Q3 term premium surge all produced synchronized credit-equity weakness precisely because the yield move was term-premium-driven.

### Claim 2: 5y5y Forward as Joint Constraint

The 5y5y forward rate (the market's expectation of the 5-year rate five years from now) strips out cyclical monetary policy expectations and approximates the long-run neutral rate plus long-run inflation expectations plus long-run term premium. When this rate moved from ~2.5% in 2021 to ~4.0-4.5% in 2023-2025, it signaled a regime shift in the structural cost of capital.

For the credit market: the maturity wall of $900B-$1.1T in 2025-2027 must refinance at coupons 300-500bp higher than original issuance. If the 5y5y forward stays above 4.5%, this refinancing occurs at structurally elevated rates regardless of Fed cuts, because the long end of the curve is anchored by term premium and neutral rate expectations. The credit cycle indicator framework already identifies an effective stall speed of 1.5-2.0% GDP (vs. standard 0-0.5%) after adjusting for covenant-lite delay and EBITDA addback distortion — a high 5y5y forward compounds this vulnerability.

For the equity market: the 5y5y forward directly maps to the equity risk premium calculus. When the "risk-free" rate 5 years forward is 4.5%, the required equity return is ~10-11%, which compresses forward P/E multiples to ~15-17x from the 20-22x sustained during the zero-rate era.

### Claim 3: Yield-Curve-Mediated Contagion Sequence

Historical pattern across three recession episodes:
- **Phase A (12-24 months before recession):** Yield curve inverts as the market prices Fed overtightening. Credit spreads remain tight because inversion reflects expectations, not term premium. Equity markets may continue rallying.
- **Phase B (6-12 months before recession):** Curve begins bear steepening — the long end sells off as term premium rises (fiscal concerns, inflation uncertainty, or foreign selling). This is the critical phase where credit-equity correlation spikes. HY OAS begins widening (the KB's credit-equities-labor sequencing monitor places this at 3-12 months before labor deterioration).
- **Phase C (0-6 months before recession):** Credit spread blowout, with equities pricing 60-80% of eventual drawdown. The curve is now bull steepening as the front end drops on rate cut expectations.

The bear steepener phase (B) is uniquely dangerous because it represents a "worst of both worlds" — the long end is repricing term premium upward while the economy is already decelerating. This produces correlated credit-equity stress without the safety valve of lower long-term rates.

### Claim 4: Basis Trade Contagion Channel

The $800B-$1T basis trade (per KB: confidence 9/10) represents leveraged hedge fund positions exploiting the Treasury cash-futures basis. At 50-100x leverage, even small mark-to-market losses trigger forced selling of Treasuries. This creates a novel contagion pathway:

1. Basis trade unwind → Treasury selling → yields spike, swap spreads widen
2. Wider swap spreads → higher funding costs for banks and dealers → reduced market-making capacity
3. Reduced liquidity → wider bid-ask spreads in credit markets → forced selling by levered credit funds
4. Treasury volatility spike → VIX spike via volatility-of-volatility → equity risk parity and CTA deleveraging

The KB relationship (basis_trade_fragility → correlation_regime_shifts, confidence 8) confirms this mechanism. The key insight from a rates strategist perspective is that the basis trade makes the *Treasury market itself* a source of contagion rather than a safe haven — fundamentally altering the traditional flight-to-quality dynamic that historically *reduced* credit-equity correlation during stress.

### Claim 5: Supply-Driven Term Premium as Credit-Equity Channel

Post-2020 fiscal expansion has created Treasury issuance levels ($2T+ annual net issuance) that structurally elevate term premium. This is qualitatively different from growth-driven or policy-driven yield increases:

- **Growth-driven yield rise:** Credit earnings improve, equity earnings improve, spreads may tighten — *positive* for both
- **Policy-driven yield rise (hawkish Fed):** Front-end moves more, credit tightens via funding costs, equity derates but earnings intact — *negative but manageable*
- **Supply-driven term premium rise:** Long end moves more, no growth offset, fiscal concerns may signal future austerity — *negative for both credit AND equity*

The KB's fiscal_impulse_trajectory → risk_appetite_regime_sequencing relationship (confidence 9) confirms that fiscal dynamics are now the primary driver of regime transitions. When term premium rises due to supply concerns (e.g., August 2023 Fitch downgrade / Treasury refunding announcement), credit and equity sell off together with high correlation because there is no offsetting fundamental story.

### Claim 6: Current Regime Positioning

The five-regime risk appetite model (KB confidence: current assessment "Late Regime 1, expects transition to Regime 2 in 12-24 months") maps to specific yield curve behaviors:

- **Regime 1 (Complacent Carry):** Curve relatively flat, term premium compressed, credit-equity correlation low (~0.3). Investors are positioned for carry in both credit (tight spreads) and equity (buyback-supported) — they behave as independent risk factors.
- **Regime 2 (Early Stress):** Curve steepens via term premium, credit-equity correlation jumps to ~0.8 as the common discount-rate factor dominates. This is the transition where the "diversification" between credit and equity allocations fails.

The current HY OAS of 350-420bp (40th-55th percentile per the KB) is consistent with late Regime 1, but the credit cycle indicator framework warns that after adjusting for covenant-lite delays and EBITDA addbacks, the cycle is more advanced than visible. The yield curve will be the first instrument to detect the regime transition — specifically, watch for a sustained rise in 10-year term premium above 50bp (ACM model) while the front end is unchanged or falling.

### Claim 7: Shadow Term Premium from Private Credit

Standard term structure models (ACM, Kim-Wright) estimate term premium from observable Treasury yields and macro variables. They do not capture:
- $1.7T of private credit that is invisible to market-based financial conditions indices (KB: private_credit_opacity, confidence 7.5)
- Manager-smoothed NAVs that mask true credit risk (KB: leading_indicator_degradation → private_credit_opacity, confidence 7)
- $200-300B/yr deployment from committed capital pools that is rate-insensitive (KB: private_credit_rate_insensitivity, confidence 6)

This creates a "shadow term premium" — the true cost of capital in the economy is higher than what the Treasury yield curve implies, because private credit carries significant illiquidity and default risk premia that are not reflected in public markets. When stress materializes, the *revelation* of this shadow term premium produces a sudden repricing of credit risk that spills into equity markets through the Merton structural channel (corporate balance sheets are weaker than reported, equity values drop to reflect true leverage).

### Claim 8: HY OAS-to-VIX Ratio as Regime Indicator

The ratio of HY OAS (in basis points) to VIX (index level) captures the relative pricing of credit risk versus equity risk:

- **Ratio > 25 (HY OAS = 500, VIX = 18):** Credit is pricing more stress than equity → contagion is credit-led (e.g., energy credit crunch 2015-16, where HY sold off well before the S&P 500)
- **Ratio < 15 (HY OAS = 300, VIX = 22):** Equity is pricing more stress than credit → contagion is equity-led (e.g., COVID crash March 2020, where equity volatility spiked before credit spreads fully repriced)
- **Ratio 15-25:** Balanced regime, credit-equity correlation moderate

From a rates perspective, the ratio also maps to curve dynamics: credit-led stress episodes tend to produce bear steepening (supply/fiscal concern), while equity-led stress episodes tend to produce bull steepening (flight-to-quality demand for Treasuries). This distinction is critical for positioning because the hedge portfolio (long Treasuries) only works in equity-led stress — in credit-led stress, Treasury term premium may rise alongside credit spreads.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Credit Cycles (confidence 6.0, depth 3):** The term premium decomposition adds a *why* to the credit cycle's *what*. Rising term premium is the mechanism through which credit conditions tighten even when the Fed is on hold — the long end of the curve drives refinancing costs, and term premium expansion signals that the market is demanding more compensation for uncertainty. The maturity wall framework (KB confidence 10) is the specific vulnerability that term premium acts upon.

**Equity Cycles (confidence 5.0, depth 2):** The equity cycle's valuation ceiling is set by the yield curve's term structure. P/E expansion requires either falling yields (lower discount rates) or rising earnings expectations. When term premium is the driver of yield changes, there is no earnings offset — making term premium the binding constraint on equity multiples. The Merton model connection works bidirectionally: falling equity values (higher leverage) widen credit spreads, but the trigger for equity declines is often term premium repricing.

**Real Estate Cycles (confidence 5.5, depth 2):** CRE-to-bank-equity contagion (identified as a knowledge gap) operates through the yield curve: CRE is highly duration-sensitive, and when term premium rises, CRE cap rates must widen, causing mark-to-market losses that flow through bank balance sheets into bank equity prices. The 5y5y forward rate directly drives CRE capitalization rates, making it the most important single variable for the CRE-bank-equity transmission channel.

**Risk Appetite Regimes (confidence 7.9, depth 1):** The yield curve is the earliest and most granular indicator of risk appetite regime transitions. The term premium component specifically captures the shift from Regime 1 (compressed term premium, carry-favorable) to Regime 2 (rising term premium, carry-hostile). The five-regime model's "fiscal impulse trajectory" as key discriminant maps directly to Treasury supply dynamics and their impact on term premium.

**Monetary Policy Transmission:** Rate expectations (the non-term-premium component of yields) capture how effectively monetary policy is transmitting into financial conditions. Private credit's rate insensitivity (KB confidence 6) means that the expectations channel is weakened — a 150bp cut may lower 2-year yields but not reduce private credit's cost of capital, limiting the credit-equity relief that rate cuts normally provide.

**Fiscal Policy & Sovereign Risk:** Treasury supply dynamics are the nexus where fiscal policy meets financial markets. Persistent deficits → elevated issuance → higher term premium → tighter financial conditions → weaker credit and equity markets. This is the Minsky-Kalecki fiscal channel (KB framework) operating through the term structure.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the current ACM/Kim-Wright term premium estimate, and how has it evolved since the August 2023 peak?** The analysis depends critically on whether term premium is currently rising, stable, or falling. The New York Fed publishes ACM daily — the current level and trajectory would validate or invalidate several claims.

2. **Has the basis trade grown or shrunk since the KB's $800B-$1T estimate?** If basis trade notional has declined due to regulatory pressure or margin increases, the contagion channel may be less potent than assessed. Conversely, if it has grown, the risk is larger.

3. **What is the actual correlation between 10-year term premium changes and HY OAS changes on a rolling 3-month basis?** This would directly test Claim 1. If the correlation is low, the term premium transmission mechanism may be less important than argued.

4. **How does the CLO structure's AAA-to-equity waterfall interact with term premium changes?** CLO AAA spreads are partially driven by term premium (duration risk), while CLO equity is driven by default risk. The differential sensitivity may create a "tranche dislocation" that amplifies credit stress in non-linear ways.

5. **Is the 5y5y forward rate actually a stable predictor of equity multiples, or did the 2010-2021 era (with QE suppressing term premium) create a misleading relationship?** The 5y5y-equity linkage may be regime-dependent, working in "normal" rate environments but breaking down during QE/QT regimes.

6. **What fraction of private credit's $1.7T is floating-rate, and how does this change the term premium transmission?** If private credit is predominantly floating-rate, it is more sensitive to the front end (policy expectations) than the long end (term premium), which would alter the contagion channel.

7. **How has the growth of passive/ETF credit investing changed the credit-equity linkage?** ETF flows create a mechanical linkage (equity-like trading of credit instruments) that may tighten credit-equity correlation structurally, independent of fundamental channels.

8. **What would a "successful" Fed intervention (standing repo facility activation, potential Treasury purchases) do to term premium and the basis trade contagion channel?** The KB notes Fed tools are "untested during high inflation" — if the Fed intervenes to suppress term premium, does it break the credit-equity contagion or merely delay it?
