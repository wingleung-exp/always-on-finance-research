# Risk Appetite Regimes — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite is an endogenous output of balance sheet dynamics, not an exogenous psychological input.** The Minsky process — where successful debt validation breeds more aggressive leverage — mechanically generates rising risk appetite during stability. The credit system manufactures risk appetite through the feedback loop of lending → spending → income → profit validation → more lending. This is not a behavioral claim about sentiment; it is a structural claim about cash-flow accounting.

2. **Risk appetite regimes map onto a refined four-phase Minsky classification: hedge-dominant, speculative-transitional, speculative-mature, and Ponzi-fragile — with sector-specific heterogeneity as the critical diagnostic feature.** Different sectors of the economy can and do simultaneously occupy different Minsky stages. The 2006 regime was Ponzi in household mortgage credit but hedge-to-speculative in corporate balance sheets; the current (Q1 2026) regime is hedge in households, speculative-mature in public corporate credit, and likely Ponzi-fragile in segments of private credit. A monolithic "risk-on/risk-off" reading systematically misidentifies which sector will crack.

3. **The sectoral financial balances identity (Private + Government + Foreign ≡ 0) imposes a hard constraint on the duration and sustainability of any risk appetite regime.** When the US private sector is running a financial deficit (net borrowing exceeds net saving), the regime is load-bearing on government deficits and/or capital inflows. Current US fiscal deficits of ~6-7% of GDP during a non-recessionary economy are the single largest support beam under current risk appetite. This is arithmetic, not opinion. The corollary: fiscal consolidation is the most underpriced risk to the current regime because it mechanically drains the net financial assets sustaining private sector leverage.

4. **The credit impulse (second derivative of credit growth relative to GDP) is the most upstream leading indicator of risk appetite regime transitions, superior to VIX, raw spreads, or survey sentiment — but must be read alongside the Kalecki profit equation to distinguish self-validating from self-liquidating credit.** The credit impulse captures the actual flow of new purchasing power; the Kalecki equation (Profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports + Capitalist Consumption − Worker Saving) reveals whether that credit is generating the profits needed to service it. When credit impulse is positive but the Kalecki-implied profit flow is weakening (e.g., fiscal consolidation reduces the government deficit term), the regime is running on credit fumes — a Ponzi condition by definition.

5. **Central bank policy modulates the tempo but does not determine the regime — a claim I now refine from iter_0003 to acknowledge that rate policy has genuine traction in hedge-phase economies, while having diminishing influence in speculative and Ponzi phases.** The 2022-2023 hiking cycle demonstrably crushed risk appetite in rate-sensitive pockets (crypto, SPACs, unprofitable tech). But the 2003-2007 episode remains the devastating counterexample for any rate-centric view: the Fed hiked from 1% to 5.25% while risk appetite escalated to extreme levels via endogenous credit creation in structured products. The resolution is phase-dependent: rate policy matters most in hedge phases (where borrowers are margin-sensitive) and least in Ponzi phases (where asset-price expectations dominate cash-flow calculations).

6. **The current regime (Q1 2026) exhibits a dangerous sectoral divergence: household balance sheets in hedge mode mask the speculative-to-Ponzi transition occurring in leveraged corporate credit and private credit, creating a false sense of aggregate stability.** Household mortgage DSRs remain historically moderate (post-2008 deleveraging held). But nonfinancial corporate debt/GDP is elevated at ~50%+, covenant-lite exceeds 80% of institutional loans, B-rated leverage at 5.5-6.0x in public markets and an estimated 6.5-7.5x in private credit (possibly 8-10x on true cash earnings after EBITDA addback adjustments). The household strength is the reason aggregate indicators read "moderate" — but the crisis won't come from households this time.

7. **The Kalecki profit equation provides the fundamental feedback mechanism that validates or liquidates speculative positions, making fiscal trajectory the transmission channel between macro policy and financial fragility.** If Profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports + Capitalist Consumption − Worker Saving, then the current ~6-7% fiscal deficit is directly sustaining corporate profits at levels that validate current spread compression and leverage ratios. A fiscal consolidation from 6% to 3% of GDP — even phased over two years — would remove approximately $500-700B annually from the Kalecki profit flow. This is the mechanism by which austerity causes a Minsky moment: it doesn't just "reduce demand" in the Keynesian multiplier sense — it eliminates the profit flows that service existing debt, converting speculative units into Ponzi units overnight. The Eurozone 2010-2013 episode is the definitive case study.

8. **Private credit ($1.7-2.0T AUM) represents the most significant blind spot in the Minsky classification of the current regime, functioning as a hidden Ponzi layer whose mark-to-model accounting delays but amplifies eventual regime transitions.** PIK toggles, NAV lending against illiquid marks, and EBITDA addback inflation (reported 6.5x leverage potentially masking 8-10x on cash earnings) are structurally Ponzi characteristics: they require continued access to new capital and favorable marks rather than cash-flow adequacy to service existing obligations. This generation of private credit has never experienced a full-cycle stress test. The semi-liquid vehicles ($100B+ in non-traded BDCs and interval funds) introduce a contagion channel from retail redemption dynamics into institutional credit markets that did not exist in prior cycles.

9. **The regime transition asymmetry (risk-off 3-5x faster than risk-on) is not a behavioral anomaly but a structural consequence of balance sheet mechanics — and systematic/passive strategies have compressed the transition timeline by approximately 40% since 2015.** The Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist financial accelerator (collateral value decline → credit constraint tightening → further asset price decline) provides the micro-foundation. Adrian and Shin's procyclical leverage mechanism (VaR-based → mechanical deleveraging in drawdowns) provides the market structure channel. Passive share rising from ~20% (2007) to 50%+ (2025) of equity AUM, vol-targeting/risk-parity strategies ($300-500B), and ETF authorized participant liquidity withdrawal in stress have compressed the median transition from ~45 trading days pre-2010 to ~22 trading days post-2017. The implication: there is less time to recognize and act on Minsky transitions than in any prior cycle.

10. **The stock-bond correlation shift toward positive territory in supply-side inflation regimes fundamentally disrupts the traditional risk-off playbook and represents a structural, not cyclical, change tied to the demographic labor scarcity supply shock identified in iter_0001.** When the dominant macroeconomic risk shifts from demand-side (where bonds rally in risk-off, providing hedging) to supply-side (where bonds sell off simultaneously with equities), the entire multi-asset portfolio construction paradigm calibrated to negative stock-bond correlation becomes a source of risk rather than mitigation. The 2022 episode (equities -25%, bonds -15%) is not an outlier but a feature of the new regime. In a positive correlation environment, the traditional "risk-off = buy bonds" trade is invalidated, and the absence of a reliable risk-off hedge amplifies the Minsky transition dynamics by eliminating the natural dampener.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Endogenous Risk Appetite:**
Minsky's *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy* (1986) provides the theoretical architecture; Adrian & Shin (2010) provide the micro-foundations via procyclical leverage. Every major credit cycle since 1980 exhibits the signature: low realized volatility → VaR-based capacity expansion → more lending → more spending → lower volatility → further capacity expansion. The 2004-2007 period is canonical: CDO issuance accelerated precisely because realized defaults were low, which was because the credit-financed spending was sustaining the income that serviced the debt. Risk appetite was manufactured by the system, not imported from animal spirits. The BIS's own procyclicality research confirms mark-to-market accounting + VaR-based risk management = mechanical leverage expansion in calm markets. This claim achieved 9/10 consensus in iter_0003 synthesis and was unchallenged across all four debates.

**Claim 2 — Sector-Specific Minsky Staging:**
The iter_0003 synthesis confirmed this as one of the strongest novel contributions, with the pair_2 debate finding the sector-specific framework "decisively stronger" than the monolithic treatment. Current sector classification:
- *Households*: Hedge mode. Post-2008 deleveraging sustained. Mortgage DSR at historical moderate levels. LTV ratios constrained by post-GFC lending standards. This is the legacy of the 2008 Minsky moment — the sector that broke was forced through a full deleveraging cycle.
- *Public corporate credit*: Speculative-mature. Income covers interest but refinancing is required (large maturity walls 2025-2027). Leverage elevated but not yet Ponzi because cash flows still cover current debt service at current rates. The BBB cliff risk remains: $3.5T+ of BBB-rated debt, any significant downgrade wave forces selling by investment-grade mandated holders.
- *Private credit*: Ponzi-fragile in segments. PIK toggles, amendment activity surging (est. 30-40% of 2021-2022 vintage per credit_analyst_02), EBITDA addbacks potentially inflating from reported 6.5x to effective 8-10x. Income does not cover full debt service for a material fraction; continued capital availability and favorable marks are required. This is the textbook Ponzi definition.
- The four-phase refinement (adding speculative-transitional) captures the 2014-2019 corporate trajectory: initial leverage build → rate sensitivity → refinancing dependence → the mature speculative state where only an exogenous catalyst or endogenous overextension triggers the Ponzi transition.

**Claim 3 — Sectoral Balances Constraint:**
Godley's identity is accounting fact: Private Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance ≡ 0. Current US approximate balances: Government deficit ~6-7% GDP, foreign surplus (capital account deficit) ~3-4% GDP, leaving the private sector running a financial surplus of ~2-3% GDP. This surplus is what allows the private sector to accumulate net financial assets even as corporate leverage rises — the household surplus more than offsets corporate net borrowing. If the government deficit contracts to 3% GDP (a moderate consolidation), and the foreign balance stays roughly stable, the private sector surplus compresses to near zero or deficit. At that point, the aggregate private sector can no longer net save while simultaneously increasing leverage — the math simply doesn't permit it. The Eurozone 2010-2013 austerity episode provides the devastating real-world experiment: governments slashed deficits while private sectors were still deleveraging → private sector income collapsed → debt/GDP ratios worsened despite austerity → the Minsky transition accelerated rather than stabilized. This was the single highest-convergence macro claim in iter_0003 synthesis (both macro strategists agreed despite disagreeing on virtually everything else).

**Claim 4 — Credit Impulse + Kalecki:**
The credit impulse (Biggs, Mayer & Pick, 2009) leads real activity by 2-4 quarters because new credit = new deposits = new spending = new income in the endogenous money framework. But the credit impulse alone doesn't tell you whether the credit is self-validating. The Kalecki profit equation fills this gap: if the credit-financed spending is generating profits (because fiscal deficits, investment, and net exports are large enough), the credit is validated and the speculative regime sustains. If credit impulse is positive but the Kalecki profit sources are weakening, you have credit expansion that is destroying its own validation — the definition of a Ponzi spiral. Current reading: credit impulse has been modestly positive through late 2025/early 2026, sustained largely by the fiscal deficit term in the Kalecki equation. The credit impulse data comes from the Fed's Z.1 release (quarterly, ~10-week lag — a genuine limitation), BIS credit statistics, and ECB quarterly sector accounts. The iter_0003 synthesis rated the Kalecki framework at 5/10 partly due to single-agent advocacy and operationalization challenges. I maintain it at 7/10: the framework is an accounting identity (irrefutable) with causal power demonstrated in Japan post-1990 and Eurozone 2010-2013 — the uncertainty is in the transmission lag, not the direction.

The resolution: rate policy has genuine traction when the dominant borrowing sector is in hedge or early-speculative phase (rate-sensitive by construction), and diminishing traction as the system migrates toward Ponzi finance (where expected capital gains, not debt service costs, drive behavior). Currently, households are in hedge mode and would be rate-sensitive to further tightening; corporate leveraged credit is in speculative-mature mode where refinancing cost matters but is buffered by still-adequate cash flows; private credit Ponzi-fragile segments are least rate-sensitive because they are dependent on continued capital availability rather than cost of capital.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Fiscal Policy & Sectoral Balances**: Inseparable from risk appetite assessment. The sectoral balances identity makes fiscal trajectory the single most important macro input to regime sustainability. Fiscal consolidation during a speculative-to-hedge transition is procyclical by construction — it removes the net financial asset flow sustaining private balance sheets. The current US deficit (~6-7% GDP) is anomalous for a non-recessionary economy and its potential contraction is the primary risk to the current regime.

- **Credit Markets & Spreads**: Credit spreads are a lagging indicator of Minsky transitions — they compress throughout the speculative phase and blow out only after the transition is underway. The credit impulse leads spreads by 2-4 quarters. CLO formation economics (60-70% of institutional loan demand) create a mechanical demand floor that compresses spreads independently of genuine risk assessment — this is why structural lending terms (covenants, addbacks, PIK) are superior to spread levels as risk appetite indicators, as credit_analyst_02 demonstrated in iter_0003.

- **Monetary Policy Transmission**: Risk appetite regimes determine whether monetary policy transmits. In hedge-dominant regimes, rate changes have direct traction on debt service costs. In speculative/Ponzi regimes, endogenous credit creation can overwhelm policy signals. This creates the paradox where central banks appear powerful in stable periods (when they aren't needed) and impotent in crisis (when they are). The "pushing on a string" asymmetry — rate hikes transmit reliably, rate cuts don't when balance sheets are impaired — is the Keynesian liquidity trap restated in Minsky terms.

- **Demographics (iter_0001)**: Labor scarcity as a supply-side structural shock biases the stock-bond correlation positive (claim 10), eliminates the traditional risk-off hedge, and creates the inflation persistence that constrains the Fed put. The demographic dependency ratio inflection (workers/retirees from 4:1 to 2.8:1 by 2035) mechanically increases age-related fiscal spending by 3-5% GDP, making deficit contraction politically and economically difficult — which paradoxically supports the fiscal deficit that sustains current risk appetite. The tension between demographically-mandated spending growth and deficit-reduction political pressure is a structural source of regime uncertainty.

- **Term Premium & Rates**: The structurally higher term premium (iter_0003 synthesis at 8/10) — driven by QE withdrawal, fiscal supply pressure, and widened inflation distribution — is the rates market expression of the same sectoral balance dynamics. Higher term premium means higher refinancing costs for the corporate maturity wall, accelerating the speculative → Ponzi transition for marginal borrowers. The basis trade ($800B-1T notional, 20-50x levered) is the leveraged expression of risk appetite in rates markets and a potential Minsky amplifier if unwound.

- **Currency & Balance of Payments**: The dollar as reflexive risk appetite amplifier (Bruno & Shin 2015, via $13T offshore dollar-denominated debt) connects the foreign sector balance to Minsky dynamics. A risk-off event that strengthens the dollar tightens financial conditions globally via the EM debt burden channel, creating a feedback loop that the sectoral balance identity captures but monolithic risk frameworks miss. If the US current account deficit narrows (reducing the foreign sector surplus), it mechanically squeezes either the domestic private sector or government sector balance — a Godley-style adjustment that constrains risk appetite from the external account.

## Open Questions

1. **How to operationalize the Kalecki profit equation as a real-time indicator?** The components (investment, government deficit, net exports, capitalist consumption, worker saving) are available in national accounts data but with significant lags and revision uncertainty. Can a monthly or high-frequency proxy be constructed? NIPA corporate profits data is quarterly with revisions; BEA investment data similar. A credit-impulse-adjusted Kalecki indicator might be constructable from higher-frequency credit data (weekly bank lending surveys, monthly BIS credit data) combined with fiscal outflow data from Treasury statements. This is the most important analytical development needed to make the framework actionable.

2. **What is the critical threshold for fiscal deficit contraction that triggers the sectoral balance squeeze?** The Eurozone 2010-2013 case involved rapid consolidation from ~6% to ~3% in 2-3 years. The US 1937 case involved a swing from deficit to surplus. Is there a non-linear threshold, or is the transmission proportional? The answer likely depends on the starting private sector financial balance and the speed of adjustment — a gradual 50bp/year contraction may allow private balance sheet adjustment, while a sudden 200bp shock may not.

3. **Can the private credit Ponzi classification be validated with observable data before a crisis?** Potential proxies: CLO equity tranche spreads (reflecting underlying loan quality expectations), private credit fund redemption queue lengths, PIK-toggle activation rates (available from some data providers), and the gap between private and public loan pricing for the same or comparable borrowers. None of these are comprehensive, but a composite could provide earlier warning than waiting for default data.

4. **How does the AI capex cycle interact with the Minsky framework?** If AI investment is generating genuine productivity gains, it validates speculative positions through the Kalecki equation (higher investment → higher profits). If it is primarily a capex arms race financed by cash balances of mega-caps, it sustains the income flow but doesn't create the broad-based profit validation needed for the wider leveraged credit market. The critical question: is AI capex funded by internal cash flow (hedge finance) or by leverage (speculative/Ponzi)? Current evidence suggests mega-cap AI investment is largely cash-flow financed (hedge), but the downstream ecosystem (AI startups, data center REITs, GPU leasing companies) may be heavily leveraged — a sectoral divergence within the tech sector itself.

5. **Has systematic strategy growth permanently altered Minsky cycle dynamics, or merely compressed them?** If passive/systematic share continues rising (60%+ → 70%+), does the mechanical deleveraging channel eventually become so dominant that it functions as a circuit-breaker (forced selling is so fast that it clears the market before contagion builds) or as an amplifier (compression below some critical threshold creates flash-crash-type dynamics that overwhelm fundamental stabilizers)? The March 2020 V-shaped recovery suggests the former; but that episode featured the fastest and largest policy response in history (unlimited QE within weeks). The counterfactual — compressed Minsky transition *without* overwhelming policy response — has not been tested.

6. **What is the current state of the NBFI (non-bank financial intermediation) Minsky cycle?** Minsky's original framework centered on banks. The migration of credit intermediation to CLOs, private credit funds, mortgage REITs, and leveraged ETFs has shifted much of the speculative/Ponzi activity outside the regulated banking perimeter and outside the lender-of-last-resort safety net. The flow-of-funds data captures some of this but with significant classification challenges and lags. The critical unknown: if the Minsky moment originates in NBFI, does the Fed have the tools and willingness to backstop entities that are not deposit-taking institutions? The BTFP (March 2023) suggests willingness; the legal authority for broader NBFI backstops is uncertain.
