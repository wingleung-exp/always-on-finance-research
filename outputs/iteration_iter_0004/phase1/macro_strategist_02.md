# Credit-Equity Linkage & Contagion Channels — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Credit and equity markets are not independent asset classes but co-determined outputs of a single underlying process: endogenous credit creation and its impact on the Kalecki-Levy profit identity.** The Merton model's structural insight — that equity is a call option on firm assets while credit is a put — is directionally correct but fundamentally incomplete because it treats asset values as exogenous, when in fact they are endogenously determined by the credit cycle itself.

2. **The primary causal direction in credit-equity linkage runs from credit to equity, not the reverse.** Bank lending creates deposits (endogenous money), which fund investment and consumption, which generate the corporate profits that validate equity valuations. Equity prices are downstream of credit conditions in a way that mainstream finance — which treats both as independent reflections of "fundamental value" — systematically fails to model.

3. **The current credit-equity linkage is operating in a Minsky "speculative" phase where the Kalecki fiscal channel is masking the deterioration of credit quality, producing an artificial decorrelation between credit spreads and equity risk premia.** HY OAS at 350-420bp and S&P at ~21x forward P/E are not independently rational assessments — they are jointly sustained by the 6-7% fiscal deficit flowing through the Kalecki profit identity. Remove the fiscal support and both reprice simultaneously.

4. **CRE-to-bank-equity contagion operates through a balance sheet channel that is fundamentally Minskyan: banks holding CRE loans at book value are in a "speculative" financing position where they depend on refinancing (rolling over) loans to borrowers whose cash flows no longer cover debt service.** The $900B-$1.1T maturity wall concentrates the potential for a discrete regime shift where mark-to-market losses force equity dilution or failure.

5. **The HY spread widening threshold for equity market contagion is not a fixed number but is regime-dependent, determined by the Minsky stage of the cycle and the magnitude of the Kalecki fiscal buffer.** In the current regime, with fiscal deficits sustaining profits, the threshold is higher than historical norms (~550-600bp OAS vs. the historical ~450bp). But this higher threshold is itself a fragility signal — it means more damage is accumulating before the signal fires.

6. **Private credit-to-public market spillover will not operate through traditional spread contagion but through a Godley-style sectoral balance disruption: the $1.7T private credit market sits outside the financial conditions indices, and its stress will manifest as a sudden deterioration in the private sector financial balance when PIK toggles, EBITDA addbacks, and covenant-lite structures simultaneously fail to suppress defaults.** This is a flow-of-funds problem, not a price contagion problem.

7. **The credit impulse — the change in the change of credit — is the correct leading indicator for both credit spread movements and equity market regime shifts, and it is currently sending a divergent signal from equity market pricing.** The credit impulse has decelerated materially from its 2023-2024 peak even as equity valuations have continued to expand, creating a Godley-style inconsistency that must resolve through either credit re-acceleration or equity repricing.

8. **Mainstream structural models of credit-equity linkage (Merton, reduced-form, copula-based) all fail because they lack a banking sector and treat the money supply as exogenous.** Any model that does not include endogenous credit creation, sectoral balance constraints, and the Minsky instability dynamic will systematically underestimate tail correlations between credit and equity in downturns and overestimate diversification benefits.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Credit and Equity as Co-Determined Outputs of Endogenous Credit Creation

The Merton (1974) structural model correctly identifies the accounting relationship between equity (a residual claim on assets) and credit (a senior claim). When asset values decline, equity absorbs losses first while credit spreads widen — a genuine structural insight. However, the Merton model takes asset values, asset volatility, and interest rates as given parameters, when in the Post-Keynesian framework these are all endogenously determined by the credit creation process itself.

As Minsky argued, the key dynamic is that credit expansion raises asset values (by funding purchases of assets), which raises collateral values, which enables further credit expansion — the procyclical feedback loop that Minsky identified as the core mechanism of financial instability. Asset values are not exogenous "fundamentals" perturbed by random shocks; they are the product of credit flows. The Merton model captures a snapshot of the credit-equity relationship at a point in time but misses the dynamic process through which both are jointly determined.

Empirically, the correlation between credit spreads and equity prices is itself time-varying and regime-dependent. In the "hedge" and early "speculative" Minsky phases, credit and equity can diverge substantially (credit remaining benign while equity rallies). In the late "speculative" and "Ponzi" phases, correlations spike toward unity. This regime-switching correlation is inexplicable in a Merton framework (where it should be stable given stable fundamentals) but is a natural prediction of the Minsky model.

The practical implication: cross-asset strategies that assume stable credit-equity correlations based on the Merton model (as many structured products and risk models do) are systematically mispricing tail risk.

### Claim 2: Credit-to-Equity Causal Direction via Endogenous Money

This claim follows directly from endogenous money theory (Moore, 1988; Lavoie, 2014). In the orthodox loanable funds model, savings fund investment, and both credit and equity markets are simply allocating a fixed pool of savings. In this world, causation is ambiguous — equity declines could reduce wealth and hence savings and hence credit availability, or credit tightening could reduce investment and hence earnings and hence equity prices.

In the Post-Keynesian framework, banks create money ex nihilo when they extend loans. Credit creation is the primary impulse; deposits, income, profits, and hence equity valuations are the consequence. The sequence is: banks extend credit → borrowers spend → spending becomes income for others → income supports profits (Kalecki) → profits validate equity valuations. This is not a theoretical curiosity — it is the operational reality of how the monetary system functions, as confirmed by the Bank of England's 2014 Quarterly Bulletin on money creation.

The existing KB entry on `credit_leads_labor_sequencing` (confidence 9) provides strong empirical support: across the 2000-01, 2007-09, and 1990 episodes, credit spreads widened 3-12 months before equities priced the drawdown, and equities priced 60-80% of eventual drawdowns before unemployment confirmed recession. This sequencing is predicted by the endogenous money view: credit conditions deteriorate first because banks tighten lending standards before the income/profit consequences play out in equity markets and labor markets.

The causal primacy of credit does not mean equity-to-credit feedback is absent. Equity market declines can tighten financial conditions (reducing collateral values, triggering margin calls, impairing bank equity) and thereby restrict credit supply. But this is a second-round feedback effect operating through the credit channel, not an independent causal force. The initiating impulse is almost always in credit markets. This is why Minsky focused on the financing structure of the economy, not on equity prices per se.

### Claim 3: Kalecki Channel Masking Credit-Equity Fragility

The existing KB documents the Kalecki profit channel (confidence 8) and the Minsky-Kalecki synthesis framework. The key insight for credit-equity linkage is: the fiscal deficit at ~6-7% of GDP is mechanically sustaining corporate profits via the Kalecki-Levy identity (Profits = Investment + Government Deficit - Household Savings - Foreign Surplus). This profit flow supports both equity valuations (via the P/E denominator) and credit quality (via interest coverage ratios).

The artificial decorrelation between credit spreads and equity risk premia can be seen in the data that the KB has already flagged as `cross_asset_pricing_inconsistency`: equities price robust consumption at ~4.0% unemployment while rates price 75-100bp of easing. Credit (HY OAS 350-420bp) leans toward the equity-side narrative. This inconsistency is not merely a difference of opinion across markets — it reflects the Kalecki channel propping up both credit and equity simultaneously, preventing the normal Minsky dynamics from surfacing in observable prices.

The danger is that this is exactly the pattern Minsky predicted in the "speculative" phase: surface-level stability concealing growing fragility. The `ebitda_addback_distortion` (confidence 7) and `covenant_lite_default_delay` (confidence 7) entries document how true leverage is 15-40% higher than reported and defaults are delayed 12-24 months. These distortions are invisible in the credit-equity price relationship but represent latent contagion potential.

When the Kalecki channel weakens — whether through fiscal consolidation, trade balance shifts, or rising household savings — profits decline, interest coverage deteriorates, and the artificial decorrelation between credit and equity collapses. The `minsky_kalecki_credit_timing` framework predicts this occurs within 4-6 quarters of fiscal consolidation exceeding 2pp GDP.

### Claim 4: CRE-to-Bank-Equity Contagion as a Minsky Channel

Commercial real estate lending is a textbook Minsky problem. CRE loans were underwritten during a period of low interest rates and high property valuations — the "hedge" financing phase where rental income comfortably covered debt service. As rates rose and property values declined (particularly in office sectors where remote work structurally reduced demand), many CRE borrowers shifted from "hedge" to "speculative" (needing to refinance to meet debt service) or even "Ponzi" (needing property value appreciation just to avoid negative equity).

Banks holding CRE loans face a classic Minskyan dilemma: marking the loans to market would reveal equity impairment, but extending and pretending allows them to maintain book value marks. This "extend and pretend" strategy is itself a speculative financing position — the bank is betting that time will heal the problem. The maturity wall concentrates the moment when this bet is tested.

The contagion to bank equity operates through several reinforcing channels:
- **Direct balance sheet**: loan loss provisions reduce reported earnings and erode equity capital
- **Regulatory capital**: increased risk-weighted assets from CRE downgrades consume capital buffers
- **Funding costs**: widening CDS spreads for exposed banks increase wholesale funding costs
- **Deposit flight**: SVB-style confidence dynamics can rapidly compound the problem for banks with concentrated CRE exposure

Regional and mid-size banks are most exposed: CRE constitutes 28-30% of total loans for banks below the top 25, versus ~6-8% for the largest banks. This concentration means that CRE stress doesn't need to be systemic to trigger sectoral equity contagion — a handful of regional bank failures could propagate through the KBW Regional Banking Index to broader financial sector equity, and from there through interbank credit conditions.

The critical Post-Keynesian insight is that this is not just a pricing problem — it is a flow-of-funds problem. Banks reducing CRE lending to preserve capital also reduce credit creation in the broader economy, which reduces income, which reduces the profits that service other loans. The contagion is not merely through prices but through the withdrawal of endogenous money creation.

### Claim 5: Regime-Dependent HY Spread Threshold

The question "at what HY OAS level do equities reprice?" is poorly framed from a Post-Keynesian perspective because it implicitly assumes a stable structural relationship between credit spreads and equity valuations. The Minsky framework tells us this relationship is inherently unstable — it depends on the financing structure of the economy, which changes through the cycle.

Empirical evidence from prior episodes:
- **2007-2008**: HY OAS broke above 500bp in summer 2007; equities peaked in October 2007. The lag was ~4 months, but equities did not fully reprice until HY OAS exceeded 800bp (March 2008 Bear Stearns).
- **2015-2016**: HY OAS hit 600bp (energy-driven); equities corrected ~15% (August 2015 - February 2016) but no recession followed as the Minsky dynamic was localized to energy.
- **2020**: HY OAS spiked from 350bp to 1100bp in three weeks; equities repriced simultaneously — a compressed Minsky unwind driven by exogenous shock.
- **2022**: HY OAS rose to ~500bp; equities fell ~25% — but this was rate-driven, not credit-driven, and did not trigger classic Minsky dynamics.

The pattern suggests that the OAS threshold itself depends on the macro-financial regime:
- With a large Kalecki fiscal buffer (current regime): the threshold is elevated because fiscal-sustained profits keep interest coverage ratios from deteriorating even as spreads widen. Estimate: ~550-600bp OAS before equity contagion becomes self-reinforcing.
- Without the fiscal buffer (post-consolidation): the threshold drops to historical norms of ~450bp because profit erosion compounds spread widening.
- In a Ponzi phase (late-stage leverage with refinancing dependence): the threshold drops further to ~400bp because marginal borrowers cannot absorb even modest spread widening.

The perverse implication: the higher threshold in the current regime means that more credit damage must accumulate before equity markets register the problem. This is a variant of Minsky's core insight — stability (in the form of a higher pain threshold) is itself destabilizing because it encourages greater risk-taking during the calm.

### Claim 6: Private Credit Spillover as a Sectoral Balance Disruption

The existing KB extensively documents private credit opacity (confidence 7.5): $1.7T in assets outside public indices, mark-to-model practices, PIK toggle usage, EBITDA addback distortion. The question is how this stress transmits to public markets.

The mainstream view focuses on price contagion — if private credit losses emerge, they might widen public HY spreads through fear of correlation. This is possible but misses the more fundamental transmission mechanism.

From a Godley sectoral balance perspective, private credit plays a specific role in the flow of funds: it channels savings (from pension funds, insurance companies, endowments, and increasingly retail investors via interval funds and BDCs) into corporate borrower spending. This credit flow supports private sector spending, which through the Kalecki identity supports aggregate profits.

When private credit stress materializes — through rising defaults, PIK-to-cash conversion failures, or investor redemptions — the disruption is to the flow of funds itself:

1. **Lender-side**: investors in private credit vehicles (pensions, insurers, endowments) face realized losses or restricted liquidity. This reduces their capacity for portfolio allocation elsewhere, including public equities.
2. **Borrower-side**: firms that relied on private credit for operating or refinancing lose access to that funding. They must either tap public markets (increasing supply pressure on public credit) or curtail spending (reducing income flows to other sectors).
3. **Sectoral balance impact**: the private sector financial surplus (currently ~2-3% of GDP) deteriorates because credit flows that were sustaining spending dry up while debt service obligations remain. This is precisely the dynamic Godley identified as a pre-recession signal.

The key point is that the transmission is through real economic flows — spending, income, and profits — not merely through sentiment or price contagion. This makes the private credit-to-public market spillover potentially more severe than a standard credit spread widening episode, because it operates on the same Kalecki-Levy profit channel that is currently propping up both credit and equity valuations.

The semi-liquid vehicle question (interval funds, BDCs) adds a Minsky dimension: these vehicles have offered investors the illusion of liquidity while holding fundamentally illiquid assets. This is a "speculative" financing position at the fund level — the fund depends on continued investor confidence to avoid forced selling. A wave of redemptions could force asset sales at fire-sale prices, providing the mark-to-market event that has been suppressed by mark-to-model practices.

### Claim 7: Credit Impulse Divergence as Leading Indicator

The credit impulse — defined as the change in the flow of new credit as a proportion of GDP — is theoretically grounded in the endogenous money view: it is the acceleration in credit creation that drives changes in aggregate demand, not the level of credit outstanding (Biggs, Mayer & Pick, 2009). When the credit impulse is positive and rising, demand is accelerating; when it turns negative, demand is decelerating regardless of the absolute level of credit.

The credit impulse in the US peaked in late 2023/early 2024 as post-pandemic credit normalization played out and banks tightened standards following the SVB episode. Since then, while total credit outstanding has continued to grow, the rate of new credit creation has decelerated — the second derivative has turned. Meanwhile, equity valuations have continued to expand, driven by the AI narrative and the persistent Kalecki fiscal channel.

This divergence between decelerating credit impulse and expanding equity valuations is a Godley-style inconsistency: if credit creation is the upstream driver of profits (via endogenous money → spending → income → profits), then a sustained deceleration in credit creation must eventually feed through to slower profit growth, undermining the earnings denominator of equity valuations.

The divergence can persist as long as the Kalecki fiscal channel compensates for weakening private credit creation. But this means equity valuations are increasingly dependent on a single support — fiscal deficits — rather than being validated by organic private sector credit expansion. This is a fragility signal in the Minsky sense: the system is becoming more dependent on a specific condition (continued fiscal expansion) to maintain its current trajectory.

### Claim 8: Structural Model Failure Without Banking and Endogenous Money

This is a foundational methodological claim. The Merton structural model, reduced-form credit models (Jarrow-Turnbull, Duffie-Singleton), and Gaussian copula models used for CDO pricing and credit-equity correlation all share a common deficiency: they take the macroeconomic environment as given and model credit and equity within that environment. None of them include:

- A banking sector that creates money endogenously
- A sectoral balance constraint requiring all financial flows to balance
- A Minsky instability dynamic where financing structures evolve endogenously through the cycle
- A Kalecki profit identity linking macro flows to micro fundamentals

The consequence is that these models estimate credit-equity correlations using historical data that is dominated by tranquil periods (the "hedge" and early "speculative" phases of Minsky cycles), then assume those correlations are structural. In fact, the correlations are regime-dependent: they are low in tranquil periods (when credit and equity are responding to different sectoral dynamics) and spike toward unity in stress periods (when the common upstream driver — credit creation — dominates all asset prices simultaneously).

This is not merely a theoretical objection. It has practical consequences:
- **Risk models** that use copula-based credit-equity correlation underestimate portfolio tail risk
- **Credit-equity arbitrage strategies** (long equity, short credit or vice versa) are exposed to correlation regime shifts
- **Structured products** (CLO equity tranches, synthetic CDOs) that depend on diversification across credit and equity are fragile precisely when diversification is needed most
- **Financial conditions indices** that weight credit spreads and equity prices independently misread the joint dynamics

Godley's stock-flow consistent (SFC) models and Keen's Minsky models are methodologically superior because they include a banking sector, endogenous credit creation, and the accounting constraints that bind credit and equity markets together. Their practical limitation — computational complexity and calibration difficulty — does not invalidate their theoretical superiority.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the true credit impulse reading when private credit flows are included?** Official credit data undercounts the $1.7T private credit market. If private credit growth is decelerating faster than public credit data suggest, the credit impulse divergence from equity valuations may be even larger than observable data show. This is a critical data gap.

2. **Can the Kalecki fiscal channel sustain equity valuations indefinitely even as private credit deteriorates?** There is a theoretical possibility that sufficiently large fiscal deficits can permanently offset private credit cycle dynamics — essentially, that the government can replace private credit creation as the driver of profits. If true, the credit-equity linkage weakens; if false (as I expect), it merely delays and amplifies the eventual contagion.

3. **How do passive equity flows and systematic strategies alter the credit-to-equity transmission mechanism?** The growth of passive investing, vol-targeting, and CTA strategies since 2008 has changed the microstructure of equity markets. These flows may accelerate the credit-to-equity transmission (through mechanical selling triggered by volatility spikes) or delay it (through persistent inflows regardless of fundamentals). The Post-Keynesian framework has not fully incorporated these structural changes.

4. **What is the distribution of the private sector financial surplus?** The Godley framework shows the aggregate private sector is in surplus at 2-3% GDP. But if this surplus is concentrated in the top wealth decile while the bottom 50% are in financial deficit, the credit-equity contagion could propagate through consumer credit stress even while the aggregate balance appears healthy. The sectoral balance identity is necessary but not sufficient — distributional data is needed.

5. **How does the international dollar funding system interact with domestic credit-equity linkage?** The Eurodollar system creates dollar-denominated credit outside the US banking system. Stress in this offshore dollar credit market could feed back to US equity markets through funding channels that are poorly captured by domestic flow-of-funds data. Godley's framework would need to be extended to include the foreign banking sector's role in US credit creation.

6. **Is the current Minsky classification of the overall economy at "speculative" stage correct, or are we already seeing Ponzi characteristics in specific sectors (CRE, private credit, AI capex) while the aggregate remains at "speculative"?** The existing `minsky_ai_capex_classification` concept in the KB suggests sectoral heterogeneity. If multiple sectors are simultaneously at Ponzi stage while the aggregate appears speculative, the contagion potential is higher than the aggregate classification implies — a compositional fallacy in reverse.
