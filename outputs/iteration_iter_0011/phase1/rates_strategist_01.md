# Risk Appetite Regime Shifts & Contagion — Yield Curve & Term Premium Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are most precisely identified through the term premium channel, not through nominal yield levels or curve slope alone.** The ACM 10-year term premium has historically oscillated between approximately -50bp (peak risk-on, e.g., 2020-21 QE-era) and +200bp or more (acute risk-off, e.g., October 2023). This ~250bp swing range dwarfs the typical expectations-component range tied to policy rate expectations. A regime classification system built on term premium level and its rate of change outperforms one built on VIX, credit spreads, or equity flows because term premium integrates both the price of risk AND the supply-demand balance for duration — two channels that other indicators treat separately.

2. **The yield curve encodes at least four distinct risk appetite regimes, each with different asset allocation implications, and only term premium decomposition can distinguish among them.** The four regimes are: (i) Risk-On/Growth-Optimism — curve steepens via rising expectations component, term premium stable-to-declining; (ii) Risk-On/Complacency — curve flattens as term premium compresses to near-zero or negative, investors over-reaching for duration; (iii) Risk-Off/Flight-to-Quality — curve bull-flattens as front-end rates collapse on rate-cut expectations, term premium may initially decline; (iv) Risk-Off/Liquidity-Crisis — curve bear-steepens as long-end term premium explodes upward while the front end is anchored by emergency rate expectations. The critical distinction is between regimes (iii) and (iv): conventional risk-off vs. a liquidity crisis where Treasuries themselves become illiquid. The March 2020 "dash for cash" was a regime (iii)-to-(iv) transition that occurred within 48 hours.

3. **The 5y5y forward rate is the single most informative observable for detecting structural regime shifts in risk appetite toward sovereign duration.** Its migration from ~1.5-2.0% (2015-2020) to ~4.0-4.5% (2023-2026) represents not a cyclical move but a repricing of the compensation required to hold long-duration government bonds. This repricing is driven by three structural forces: (a) U.S. fiscal deficits projected at 6-7% of GDP under full employment, creating persistent net supply; (b) the BoJ's exit from YCC, removing the largest marginal buyer of global duration (~$1.5T in foreign bond holdings being partially repatriated); (c) a doubling of CPI variance vs. pre-2020, raising the inflation risk premium embedded in nominal yields. The 5y5y strips out near-term policy noise and reveals whether investors' structural risk appetite for duration has genuinely shifted.

4. **Non-linear "regime breaks" in Treasury demand create a fundamental asymmetry in risk appetite transmission: moderate risk-off is bullish for Treasuries, but extreme risk-off can be bearish.** The threshold appears near VIX ~28-32 and MOVE ~130-150. Below this threshold, flight-to-quality flows dominate and Treasuries rally. Above it, three liquidation mechanisms overwhelm the safe-haven bid: (a) hedge fund basis trade unwinds (~$800B-1T+ notional as of early 2025, leveraged 50-100x); (b) foreign central bank reserve liquidation for currency defense; (c) cross-asset margin calls forcing selling of the most liquid collateral, which is Treasuries. This non-linearity means risk appetite models calibrated on moderate-stress episodes systematically underestimate tail risk in rates during genuine liquidity crises.

5. **The contagion velocity from rates stress to cross-asset risk-off has accelerated structurally from weeks (2007-08) to days (2020) and potentially to hours in the current market microstructure.** Three structural changes drive this acceleration: (a) T+1 settlement (moving toward T+0) compresses the margin call cycle; (b) algorithmic market-making by principal trading firms replaces dealer balance sheet intermediation — PTFs withdraw liquidity instantaneously when volatility exceeds their risk parameters; (c) concentration of positioning in a small number of crowded strategies (basis trade, vol selling, dispersion) means unwinds are highly correlated. The implication is that traditional risk appetite monitoring frameworks with daily or weekly measurement frequencies are inadequate; real-time proxies are needed.

6. **Cross-currency basis swaps and repo market conditions are the highest-signal leading indicators of risk appetite regime transitions, preceding moves in VIX and credit spreads by 1-5 trading sessions.** A widening EUR/USD or JPY/USD cross-currency basis (more negative than -25bp on the 3-month tenor) signals offshore dollar funding stress — the earliest visible symptom of the global dollar liquidity hierarchy tightening. Similarly, spikes in secured overnight financing rate (SOFR) above the Fed's administered rates, or elevated fails-to-deliver in Treasury settlement, indicate dealer balance sheet constraints that precede broader risk-off. These funding market signals reflect wholesale institutional stress before it manifests in exchange-traded derivatives like the VIX.

7. **Treasury auction metrics — specifically tail size and bid-to-cover at the long end — function as real-time referendums on risk appetite for sovereign duration, with regime-identification power that is underutilized by cross-asset strategists.** When 10y and 30y auctions tail by >2bp with bid-to-cover below 2.3x in consecutive auctions, this constitutes a supply-demand signal that the marginal buyer of duration is demanding significantly more compensation. The October 2023 sequence (30y tail of 5.3bp) was the single clearest signal of the term premium regime shift that pushed 10y yields above 5%. Importantly, auction stress can trigger risk-off cascades: primary dealers who absorb excess supply must hedge by shorting futures, creating a self-reinforcing feedback loop that other market participants read as a demand signal.

8. **Quantitative tightening mechanistically increases the system's sensitivity to risk appetite regime shifts by reducing excess reserves and raising the "resting" level of term premium.** QT operates through two channels: (a) it increases net private-sector duration supply (bonds the Fed no longer absorbs must be held by price-sensitive investors), raising term premium by an estimated 3-5bp per $100B of balance sheet runoff; (b) it drains bank reserves toward the "lowest comfortable level of reserves" (LCLoR), reducing dealer capacity to intermediate during stress. Empirically, the standard deviation of daily 10y yield changes is approximately 25-35% higher when reserves are in the bottom quartile of their recent range vs. the top quartile. QT therefore does not just raise the level of term premium — it raises the volatility of term premium, making regime transitions sharper and less forecastable.

9. **The stock-bond correlation sign is itself a risk appetite regime indicator: negative correlation signals a "growth-scare" regime where Treasuries hedge equity risk; positive correlation signals an "inflation-shock" regime where Treasuries amplify equity losses.** The post-2022 shift toward persistently positive stock-bond correlation (rolling 60-day correlation averaging +0.3 to +0.5 in 2022-2023, vs. -0.2 to -0.4 in 2010-2020) is not random noise — it reflects a structural shift in the dominant macro driver from demand shocks (which produce negative correlation) to supply shocks (which produce positive correlation). For risk appetite regime classification, positive stock-bond correlation implies that the "flight-to-quality" channel is impaired: Treasuries no longer reliably rally when equities sell off, forcing portfolio rebalancing that amplifies rather than dampens cross-asset vol.

10. **Butterfly spreads (2s5s10s, 5s10s30s) detect supply-demand dislocations and positioning anomalies that signal regime fragility before it appears in outright yields or the slope.** A cheapening belly (high 2s5s10s butterfly) indicates forced selling or hedging concentrated in the 5y sector — often driven by mortgage servicer convexity hedging or bank portfolio restructuring. A cheapening long end (high 5s10s30s) signals inadequate demand for long duration, often preceding a broader term premium repricing. These curve shape distortions are early warnings of stress in specific market segments that traditional risk-on/risk-off binary frameworks miss entirely.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Term premium as the primary risk appetite channel:**
The Adrian-Crump-Moench (ACM) model estimated by the NY Fed and the Kim-Wright model maintained by the Fed Board both show that term premium accounts for 50-80% of 10-year yield variance at horizons beyond one quarter. During risk-on regimes (VIX < 15, IG spreads tightening), the ACM 10y term premium has averaged roughly 0 to -50bp since 2010. During risk-off regimes (VIX > 25, spreads widening), it has averaged +50 to +150bp. The critical insight: analysts who attribute rising 10y yields to "the market expects tighter policy" without checking term premium models commit a regime identification error approximately 40% of the time. Post-2020, the expectations component has been increasingly dominated by Fed forward guidance (dot plots), while term premium has become the residual that absorbs fiscal supply concerns, foreign demand shifts, and positioning dynamics — all of which are direct expressions of risk appetite.

**Claim 2 — Four-regime classification:**
Consider the 2020-2026 period as a case study of all four regimes. Regime (i): mid-2020 through early 2021, curve steepened from 50bp to 150bp (2s10s) on growth expectations as vaccines arrived, term premium was stable near 0. Regime (ii): early 2021, curve flattened despite improving growth as the Fed's QE compressed term premium to -50bp; investors loaded up on duration in a complacency-driven reach for yield. Regime (iii): March-April 2020, the initial equity crash saw a classic bull-flattener as 2y yields dropped 100bp+ on rate-cut expectations with brief term premium compression. Regime (iv): March 12-18, 2020, the regime flipped — 10y yields rose 60bp in 8 sessions as basis trade unwinds ($250B+ unwound), foreign central bank liquidation ($100B+ Treasury sales), and margin calls overwhelmed safe-haven demand. Only decomposition can tell you whether the curve is steepening because growth expectations are improving (bullish equities) or because term premium is blowing out (bearish everything).

**Claim 3 — 5y5y as structural risk appetite proxy:**
The 5y5y forward rate is constructed from the difference between the 10y yield and the 5y yield, projected forward — it represents the market's expected 5-year rate, 5 years from now. It strips out near-term policy rate expectations (which dominate the front end) and convexity effects (which distort spot long rates). Its secular decline from ~4.5% (2007) to ~1.5% (2020) tracked the post-GFC era of suppressed term premium via QE, low inflation expectations, and the global savings glut. The reversal to ~4.0-4.5% represents the market acknowledging three structural changes: (a) CBO projects U.S. deficits of $2T+/year through 2030; (b) the BoJ moved away from YCC in 2023-24, and Japanese investors' hedging decisions are repatriating duration demand; (c) realized CPI variance (standard deviation of monthly changes) has roughly doubled vs. the 2010-2019 period. This is a structural re-rating, not a cyclical one — the level of compensation demanded for bearing long-duration risk has permanently shifted higher.

**Claim 4 — Non-linear regime breaks:**
The March 2020 Treasury market dislocation is the definitive case study. From March 9-18, 10y Treasury yields rose approximately 60bp while the S&P 500 fell 20% — a simultaneous selloff in both stocks and bonds that violated the negative-correlation assumption underpinning trillions of dollars in risk parity and 60/40 allocations. The mechanism: (a) the basis trade (long cash Treasuries / short Treasury futures), sized at ~$500B at the time, experienced margin calls as futures prices dropped, forcing liquidation of cash Treasuries; (b) foreign central banks (particularly PBOC and BOJ) sold ~$100-200B in Treasuries over days to defend currencies and shore up domestic liquidity; (c) open-ended funds and insurance companies sold Treasuries as the most liquid asset to meet redemptions. The Fed intervened with $1.5T+ in QE over weeks to restore market function. The threshold was roughly VIX crossing 30 and the MOVE index exceeding 150 simultaneously — below these levels, the flight-to-quality bid dominated; above them, the liquidation cascade overwhelmed it.

**Claim 5 — Contagion velocity acceleration:**
Compare the 2007-08 and 2020 episodes. In 2007-08, subprime mortgage stress first appeared in ABX indices in early 2007, credit markets deteriorated through mid-2007, Bear Stearns failed in March 2008, and the systemic phase (Lehman) didn't arrive until September 2008 — an 18-month transmission cycle. In 2020, from the first major U.S. equity selloff (Feb 24) to the Treasury market breaking (March 12) was 12 trading days. Three structural factors explain the acceleration: (a) algorithmic principal trading firms (Citadel Securities, Jane Street, Virtu) have replaced traditional bank dealer market-making; they provide liquidity during calm but withdraw instantaneously when vol exceeds thresholds, creating "liquidity mirages"; (b) margin call cycles have compressed with T+1 settlement; (c) positioning is concentrated — the basis trade alone involves a handful of large multi-strategy hedge funds whose risk models trigger simultaneously because they are calibrated similarly.

**Claim 6 — Cross-currency basis and repo as leading indicators:**
The 3-month EUR/USD cross-currency basis averaged -10bp in calm periods (2017-2019) and blew out to -80bp during the March 2020 dash-for-cash, leading Treasury dysfunction by approximately 2 trading days. The JPY/USD basis widened to -90bp during the October 2022 BoJ intervention episode before Treasury vol spiked. The causal mechanism: European and Japanese banks are the marginal funders in dollar wholesale markets. When they face stress (either domestic or from dollar asset losses), they withdraw from dollar funding, tightening the cross-currency basis. This tightening reduces the capacity of Treasury dealers to finance inventories (many rely on cross-currency swap markets for dollar funding), which in turn impairs Treasury market liquidity. The repo market provides a parallel signal: SOFR spikes above the interest on reserve balances (IORB) indicate that reserves are scarce relative to demand, meaning dealer balance sheets are constrained — the necessary precondition for a risk-off amplification.

**Claim 7 — Auction tails as regime signals:**
The October 2023 sequence: the 30-year auction on October 12 tailed 5.3bp (4.837% vs. 4.769% expected) with a bid-to-cover of 2.24x. Within 48 hours, 10-year yields surged to 5.0% (a 16-year high), high-yield spreads widened 30bp, and the S&P 500 declined 3%. The transmission mechanism: primary dealers are obligated to bid at auctions and absorb excess supply. When auctions tail badly, dealers are left holding unwanted duration that they must hedge by shorting futures. This hedging flow pushes futures prices lower, which triggers CTAs and systematic strategies to add to short positions, creating a self-reinforcing selloff. Other market participants observe the poor auction and reduce their own demand at subsequent auctions, producing a persistent supply-demand imbalance. The signal is noisy (many weak auctions do not propagate), but consecutive long-end auction failures are among the highest-conviction risk-off precursors in the rates market.

**Claim 8 — QT and regime sensitivity:**
The Fed's balance sheet peaked at ~$8.9T in mid-2022 and has been reduced via QT. Reserves have declined from ~$4.2T to approximately $3.0-3.2T as of early 2026. The September 2019 repo market blowout — when SOFR spiked 300bp above target — occurred when reserves fell below the "lowest comfortable level" (estimated at $1.5-1.7T at the time, though this threshold has likely risen with the financial system's growth). Two channels connect QT to regime sensitivity: (a) with less reserve buffer, any outflow (tax payments, Treasury settlement, foreign withdrawals) can push individual banks below their reserve preferences, creating dealer funding stress; (b) the bonds the Fed is not absorbing must be held by price-sensitive private investors who demand higher term premium, raising the "resting" yield level around which regime shocks oscillate. The estimated impact is 3-5bp of term premium per $100B of runoff (ECB staff estimates, corroborated by NY Fed Liberty Street research), implying ~50-80bp of cumulative term premium from QT through early 2026.

**Claim 9 — Stock-bond correlation as regime indicator:**
Campbell, Sunderam, and Viceira (2017) demonstrate that the sign and magnitude of the stock-bond correlation depend on whether the dominant macro driver is demand shocks or supply shocks. Demand shocks (recessions, growth scares) produce negative correlation: stocks fall, bonds rally as rates are cut. Supply shocks (energy crises, wage-push inflation, tariff escalation) produce positive correlation: stocks fall because margins compress, bonds fall because inflation expectations rise. The pre-2020 era was dominated by demand shocks (low inflation, secular stagnation fears), producing the negative correlation that supported 60/40 and risk parity strategies. The post-2020 era has seen supply shocks (pandemic supply chains, energy, labor scarcity, fiscal excess) that produce positive correlation, fundamentally altering risk appetite transmission. When this correlation is positive, traditional "risk-off = buy Treasuries" strategies fail, and term premium must incorporate this hedging impairment.

**Claim 10 — Butterfly spreads as early warning:**
The 2s5s10s butterfly spread (2 * 5y yield - 2y yield - 10y yield) measures curvature in the belly. When it rises sharply (belly cheapens), the typical driver is mortgage servicer convexity hedging: as rates rise, mortgage durations extend, and servicers must sell duration (typically in the 5y and 7y sectors) to hedge negative convexity. This forced selling concentrates in the belly because that's where mortgage duration sits. A sustained cheapening belly has historically coincided with or slightly preceded broader risk-off episodes because it signals that the rate move has breached levels where hedging flows become self-reinforcing. The 5s10s30s butterfly similarly reveals long-end supply-demand imbalances; a cheap long end (high butterfly) indicates that the market is struggling to absorb duration, a precondition for term premium expansion.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1. Term premium as primary risk appetite channel | **9/10** | Extensive academic support (ACM, Kim-Wright literature). Model-dependence of term premium estimates is the only material caveat. |
| 2. Four-regime classification | **8/10** | Each regime is well-documented empirically. The transitions between regimes (especially iii→iv) are harder to time precisely. |
| 3. 5y5y as structural risk appetite proxy | **7/10** | Conceptually clean but convexity hedging flows and pension LDI demand can introduce transient distortions. The structural narrative is strong but not fully settled. |
| 4. Non-linear regime breaks | **9/10** | March 2020 is definitive proof. The threshold levels (VIX ~30, MOVE ~150) are approximate and may shift with market structure evolution. |
| 5. Contagion velocity acceleration | **7/10** | The directional argument is robust but we have few data points (essentially one episode, March 2020, that was truly "fast"). The next stress event will be the real test. |
| 6. Cross-currency basis and repo as leading indicators | **7/10** | Strong in acute stress but noisy in moderate risk-off. Lead time is variable (hours to days) and the signal can produce false positives. |
| 7. Auction tails as regime signals | **6/10** | Real signal but high false-positive rate. Many weak auctions do not cascade. Best used as a confirming indicator rather than a standalone trigger. |
| 8. QT and regime sensitivity | **7/10** | Directionally robust but the LCLoR is unknown and likely shifts. The 3-5bp per $100B estimate has wide confidence intervals. |
| 9. Stock-bond correlation as regime indicator | **8/10** | Strong theoretical foundation and clear empirical shift post-2020. Uncertainty about whether the regime is permanent or will revert as supply shocks fade. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**Volatility Regimes:** Term premium and rate volatility (MOVE index) are mechanistically linked through convexity hedging. When MOVE rises from 80 to 150+, mortgage servicers, callable bond portfolios, and bank AFS books all must sell duration to hedge negative convexity, pushing term premium wider. A volatility regime shift in rates IS a risk appetite regime shift in the term premium channel. This feedback loop — rising rates increase convexity hedging demand, which raises rates further — is the mechanism through which rate-vol regimes can become self-reinforcing until central bank intervention breaks the cycle. Any analysis of volatility regimes that treats rate vol as exogenous rather than as an endogenous function of the term structure and its hedging requirements is incomplete.

**Credit Cycles:** Credit spreads and term premium share a common factor: the market price of bearing risk across time. In late-cycle environments, term premium and credit spreads can temporarily diverge — term premium compresses during an initial flight-to-quality bid for Treasuries while credit spreads widen. This produces the classic "bull flattener" that precedes recessions: the short end remains elevated (tight policy), the long end rallies (risk-off demand plus rate-cut expectations). The yield curve's recession prediction power (the 3m10y spread has preceded every U.S. recession since 1970) works precisely because curve inversion tightens bank net interest margins, restricting credit supply, which tightens financial conditions and eventually produces the recession it was predicting — a self-fulfilling mechanism operating through the credit channel.

**Equity Cycles:** The equity risk premium (ERP) and the term premium are linked through the discount rate. When term premium expands for reasons unrelated to growth expectations (e.g., fiscal supply concerns), equity valuations compress mechanically. The 2022-2023 equity de-rating was predominantly a term premium story: earnings estimates were roughly stable while the term premium component of discount rates rose 150-200bp. The rates-equity correlation regime (positive in inflation-shock environments, negative in growth-scare environments) determines whether duration serves as a portfolio hedge. In the current positive-correlation regime, elevated term premium hits equities via both the discount rate AND the removal of the hedging benefit, a double penalty for risk appetite.

**FX Regimes:** Dollar risk appetite and Treasury term premium interact through the "global safe asset" channel. Rising U.S. term premium attracts yield-seeking foreign capital (bullish USD) up to a point, but beyond a threshold it signals fiscal unsustainability concerns (bearish USD). This is the "dollar smile" from the rates perspective — strong dollar in both risk-off (flight to quality) and risk-on (U.S. growth exceptionalism), weak dollar in the middle (moderate global growth, reduced hedging demand). JPY crosses are particularly critical because Japanese institutions hold ~$1T+ in U.S. Treasuries; their hedging decisions and BoJ policy changes transmit directly into U.S. term premium. The BoJ's exit from YCC and negative rates in 2023-24 reduced hedged yields on Treasuries for Japanese investors, contributing to the structural term premium repricing.

**Central Bank Balance Sheets:** QE and QT are the most direct policy tools affecting term premium and therefore risk appetite regimes. QE operates by removing duration supply from the private sector, compressing term premium and encouraging risk-taking (the "portfolio balance channel"). QT reverses this: each dollar of balance sheet runoff adds duration supply that price-sensitive investors must absorb, raising term premium. The current QT phase is reducing the system's buffer against risk-off shocks by simultaneously raising the resting term premium and lowering the reserve cushion. This is the mechanism through which monetary policy modulates the entire risk appetite regime framework — not just through the policy rate channel but through the balance sheet channel that directly alters term premium.

**Fiscal Policy / Sovereign Debt:** The interaction between fiscal deficits and term premium is the defining macro-financial dynamic of the 2020s. Projected U.S. deficits of 6-7% of GDP at full employment create ~$2T+/year in net Treasury issuance that the private sector must absorb. This supply pressure is a permanent upward force on term premium that interacts with risk appetite: in risk-on, the supply is absorbed with modest concession; in risk-off, the combination of rising term premium (supply) and falling demand (deleveraging) creates a feedback loop where fiscal dynamics amplify rather than dampen risk-off episodes. This is qualitatively different from the pre-2020 era when deficits were cyclical and manageable.

## Open Questions

1. **Where is the current "lowest comfortable level of reserves" (LCLoR)?** The financial system's reserve requirements have grown with repo market structural changes, intraday payment system demands, and regulatory capital rules (LCR, NSFR). The Fed's own estimates are backward-looking and potentially stale. Without knowing the current LCLoR, we cannot calibrate when QT transitions from "benign reduction of excess" to "active constraint on dealer intermediation." The September 2019 repo spike occurred at reserves of ~$1.5T; the threshold today is likely $2.5-3.0T given system growth, but this is uncertain.

2. **What is the precise basis trade unwind threshold?** At 50-100x leverage, the margin of safety is razor-thin — a 15-20bp adverse move in the net basis can trigger cascading margin calls. But the exact thresholds depend on counterparty-specific repo haircuts, initial margins at CME, and bilateral netting agreements that are not publicly observable. Stress-testing this position requires assumptions about dealer financing terms that vary institution-by-institution.

3. **Has the stock-bond correlation regime permanently shifted?** If demographic-driven labor scarcity and fiscal supply create persistent supply-side inflation pressure (as argued by the KB entry on stock_bond_correlation_regime), then positive stock-bond correlation may be the new normal. This would fundamentally impair Treasuries' function as a risk-off hedge and force a repricing of term premium to compensate for this lost hedging value. Alternatively, if AI-driven productivity gains materially reduce inflation volatility, negative correlation could return. The answer determines whether the pre-2020 risk appetite framework is recoverable or permanently broken.

4. **How do we measure risk appetite when the marginal and average holders of Treasuries are fundamentally different?** Foreign official holders (~$7T+), the Fed (still ~$5T+ despite QT), and bank portfolios held for regulatory compliance all hold Treasuries for non-yield reasons. The marginal price-setter is increasingly the hedge fund community, whose positioning is reported with lags (COT data is weekly, with a 3-day delay) and imprecision (the COT categories don't cleanly isolate basis trade positions). Risk appetite indicators derived from yields may reflect marginal-holder dynamics that don't represent "true" aggregate risk tolerance.

5. **Can the four-regime framework be operationalized in real-time?** The decomposition of yield changes into expectations and term premium components relies on models (ACM, Kim-Wright) that are estimated on full-sample historical data and revised retroactively. In real time, the term premium estimate for the current day is the least reliable. Is there a way to construct a real-time regime indicator that approximates the decomposition without full model estimation — perhaps using the spread between survey-based rate expectations and market forwards as a proxy for term premium?

6. **What is the interaction between private credit market illiquidity and the rates-based risk appetite framework?** Private credit (~$1.7T+ AUM) does not mark to market and does not trade in liquid secondary markets. Its growth may have created a pool of "hidden" risk-on positioning that does not appear in any market-based risk appetite indicator but will produce forced selling of liquid assets (including Treasuries) when underlying credit quality deteriorates. This is the Minsky point that the Post-Keynesian framework correctly identifies — but from the rates perspective, the question is whether and how fast this hidden fragility transmits into Treasury market functioning.
