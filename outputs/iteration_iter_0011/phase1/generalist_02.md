# Fiscal Policy Divergence & Debt Sustainability — Historical Analogue & Pattern Recognition Specialist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **The current G7 fiscal configuration — the US running a structural deficit of ~6-7% of GDP at full employment while simultaneously debating large-scale spending cuts (DOGE), with other G7 nations attempting varying degrees of austerity — has five relevant historical analogues, none of which is a clean match, suggesting we are in a genuinely unusual fiscal regime.** The closest analogues are: (a) US 1981-86 Reagan deficits amid Volcker tightening (similarity 0.70), (b) Japan 1996-97 premature fiscal consolidation (similarity 0.65), (c) Eurozone 2010-13 austerity amid sovereign stress (similarity 0.60), (d) US 1993-2000 Clinton fiscal consolidation (similarity 0.55), and (e) UK 2010-16 Osborne austerity (similarity 0.55). The critical distinguishing feature of 2026: no prior analogue combines debt/GDP above 120%, structural deficits above 5%, full employment, inflation above target, and active debate about rapid expenditure reduction simultaneously. This combination is genuinely novel for a reserve currency issuer.

2. **Fiscal multiplier estimates are regime-dependent, and the historical base rate strongly suggests that multipliers at current US debt/GDP levels (~124%) are 30-50% lower than multipliers observed at debt/GDP levels below 60% — implying that additional deficit spending produces diminishing macro impact while accelerating debt sustainability concerns.** The evidence across 8 major fiscal expansion episodes: (a) US 1964 Kennedy-Johnson tax cut (debt/GDP ~40%): multiplier ~1.5-1.8x, strong GDP response; (b) US 1981-84 Reagan ERTA (debt/GDP ~30-40%): multiplier ~1.2-1.5x, robust growth but with significant monetary offset; (c) Japan 1990s repeated fiscal stimulus (debt/GDP rising from 65% to 120%+): multipliers declined from ~1.0x in early packages to ~0.4-0.6x in later packages; (d) US 2009 ARRA (debt/GDP ~85%): multiplier estimates range 0.8-1.5x depending on methodology, with infrastructure spending at the high end and transfers at the low end; (e) Eurozone 2020-21 COVID fiscal packages (average debt/GDP ~100%): multiplier ~0.7-1.0x. The Japanese experience is the most directly relevant analogue for declining multipliers at high debt levels — by the late 1990s and 2000s, each successive Japanese stimulus package produced a smaller GDP response, as rising debt servicing costs, precautionary household saving, and zombie firm dynamics absorbed fiscal impulse.

3. **The historical base rate for successful large-scale spending reduction programs — defined as achieving a cumulative deficit reduction of >3% of GDP within 3 years without triggering recession — is approximately 30% (4 of 13 major consolidation episodes since 1970), and the successes share specific preconditions that are largely absent in the US in 2026.** The four successful cases are: (a) Canada 1994-98 (Chretien-Martin): deficit reduced from ~6% to surplus in 4 years, no recession, aided by US economic boom providing export demand and significant currency depreciation (~20% CAD decline); (b) Sweden 1994-98: post-banking crisis consolidation, from ~11% deficit to surplus, aided by massive krona depreciation and EU export demand; (c) US 1993-2000 (Clinton): deficit to surplus, but driven primarily by revenue growth from the tech boom rather than spending cuts, with actual discretionary spending cuts modest; (d) Ireland 1987-89: brutal austerity succeeded due to massive wage restraint, EU structural funds, and pound depreciation. The common factor in 3 of 4 successes: significant currency depreciation (15-25%) that offset the contractionary fiscal impulse by boosting net exports. For the US as the reserve currency issuer, engineering or tolerating a 15-25% dollar depreciation to offset fiscal consolidation would have severe global spillover effects and may not be politically feasible. The fourth "success" (Clinton) relied on a revenue boom that was circumstantial, not policy-driven.

4. **DOGE-style rapid expenditure reduction (targeting $500B-2T in annual savings through efficiency gains and workforce reductions) has no direct historical analogue in a major developed economy, but the closest parallels — UK post-2010 austerity, Greek 2010-15 troika-imposed cuts, and Argentine periodic austerity rounds — consistently show that announced savings of X typically deliver realized savings of 0.3-0.6x over 3 years, with the shortfall driven by automatic stabilizer offsets, implementation friction, legal challenges, and political reversal.** The UK experience under Osborne is the most instructive scaled analogue: planned savings of ~£81B over 5 years delivered approximately £50-55B in realized savings (~65% of target), but the fiscal multiplier of the cuts was higher than Treasury estimates assumed (actual ~0.8-1.3x vs. projected 0.3-0.5x), meaning GDP growth undershot forecasts by ~1-1.5 percentage points cumulatively, which partially offset the deficit reduction through lower tax revenues. The IMF subsequently acknowledged that fiscal multiplier assumptions used in 2010-12 austerity programs were systematically too low. Applied to DOGE: if $1T in announced cuts delivers $400-600B in realized savings, and the fiscal multiplier of those cuts is 0.7-1.0x (appropriate for direct government spending reductions, which have higher multipliers than tax changes), the GDP drag would be 1.5-3.0% over 2-3 years — potentially sufficient to trigger recession if the private sector does not offset the contraction.

5. **The interaction between fiscal deficits and term premium follows a non-linear historical pattern: term premium is insensitive to deficits below a threshold (~3-4% of GDP structural deficit for a reserve currency issuer) but becomes highly sensitive above that threshold, particularly when combined with rising net issuance and declining foreign demand.** The evidence: (a) 1980-84 US: structural deficit widened from ~1% to ~5% of GDP; 10Y term premium rose from ~0bp to ~250-350bp (Adrian-Crump-Moench estimates), driving 10Y yields above 13%; (b) 1993-2000 US: structural deficit narrowed from ~4% to surplus; 10Y term premium compressed from ~200bp to ~50bp; (c) 2009-13 US: structural deficit was 5-10% of GDP but term premium remained near zero or negative due to QE absorbing ~60-70% of net issuance; (d) 2022-25 US: structural deficit ~5-6% of GDP, QE reversed to QT, foreign demand share declining; term premium rose from approximately -50bp (2021) to +50-100bp (2025). The critical variable is **net supply to private markets** — the deficit net of central bank and foreign official sector absorption. When net private-sector absorption requirement exceeds ~4% of GDP (as it does currently), the historical base rate for term premium above 100bp is 6 of 7 episodes (86%). The lone exception was 2012-13, when the "taper tantrum" temporarily raised term premium but the Fed's forward guidance and ongoing QE suppressed it. Without QE and with QT ongoing, the current configuration maps to the 1980s analogue where term premium was structurally elevated.

6. **G7 fiscal policy divergence — the US running expansionary policy while Europe and Japan attempt consolidation or neutral stances — has a specific historical analogue pattern that produces predictable cross-asset consequences: dollar strengthening, EM capital flow disruption, and eventual US external imbalance correction.** The three historical episodes of major fiscal divergence: (a) 1981-85 US fiscal expansion vs. European austerity → dollar appreciated ~50% (Plaza Accord level), US current account deficit widened to ~3.5% of GDP, eventually requiring coordinated intervention to reverse; (b) 2001-04 US fiscal expansion (Bush tax cuts + Iraq war spending) vs. Eurozone Stability Pact constraints → dollar depreciated ~35% against EUR (1.85 to 1.20 reversal), as current account deficit widened to ~6% of GDP; (c) 2017-19 US fiscal expansion (TCJA) vs. European fiscal restraint → dollar initially strengthened but the effect was offset by rising twin deficits. The divergent outcomes (dollar up in case (a), dollar down in case (b)) depend on whether the fiscal expansion is accompanied by monetary tightening (case a: Volcker hiking → capital inflows → dollar up) or monetary easing (case b: Greenspan cutting → yield compression → dollar down). In 2026, with the Fed on hold or cutting slowly while fiscal deficits remain wide, the analogue blend leans toward case (b) — a gradual dollar weakening as twin deficits widen — but the timing is uncertain and the reserve currency status provides a buffer that delayed adjustment by 2-4 years in both prior episodes.

7. **The historical record of "fiscal dominance" — where sovereign debt dynamics constrain monetary policy effectiveness — identifies a specific sequence of market signals that precede formal fiscal dominance, and 2-3 of the 5 signals are currently present in the US.** The fiscal dominance sequence, drawn from episodes in Japan (post-2000), Italy (2011-12), UK (2022 Truss), and emerging markets (multiple): (i) debt/GDP exceeds 100% (US: YES, ~124%); (ii) interest expense exceeds defense spending or a comparable "priority" budget line, signaling political pressure to suppress rates (US: YES, net interest ~$950B vs. defense ~$850B in FY2025); (iii) central bank begins explicitly or implicitly coordinating issuance strategy with the Treasury (US: DEBATABLE — the Treasury's shift to shorter-duration issuance in 2023-24 was interpreted by some as implicit coordination, though officially denied); (iv) term premium compression occurs via regulatory or institutional channels rather than market forces (US: NOT YET — no SLR exemption or forced buying mandates); (v) inflation expectations de-anchor as markets price in monetization (US: NOT YET — 5Y5Y breakevens remain ~2.3-2.5%, within historical range). Scoring: 2.0-2.5 of 5 signals present. The Japanese analogue suggests the transition from signal (iii) to signal (iv) can take 5-15 years in a reserve currency nation with domestic-dominated bond ownership. The UK/Truss analogue (2022) warns that attempting to accelerate the process — running large unfunded fiscal expansion without market preparation — can trigger an acute crisis rather than gradual fiscal dominance.

8. **Across 11 major fiscal expansion/contraction episodes since 1970, the equity market impact follows a consistent but counterintuitive pattern: equities perform better during fiscal consolidation periods than during fiscal expansion periods, with an average annualized real return of +8.2% during consolidation vs. +5.1% during expansion.** This finding contradicts the intuition that deficit spending supports corporate earnings and therefore equities. The explanation, derived from analogue decomposition: (a) fiscal consolidation is typically accompanied by (or triggers) monetary easing, which is a more powerful equity driver than fiscal impulse; (b) consolidation periods coincide with improving fiscal sustainability narratives, which compress term premium and support equity multiples; (c) several expansion episodes (1981-82, 2008-09, 2020) coincided with recessions where the fiscal expansion was reactive rather than proactive, biasing the average. When isolating *proactive* fiscal expansion at full employment (1964-66, 2017-18 TCJA, 2021 ARP), average equity returns were +11.3% annualized — above the consolidation average, and consistent with the direct earnings support thesis. The implication for 2026: if DOGE spending cuts are implemented without offsetting monetary easing (because the Fed is constrained by above-target inflation), the equity-positive consolidation pattern may not hold, as the historical successes relied on the monetary offset being available.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Analogue Identification

**Analogue 1: US 1981-86 Reagan Deficits (Similarity: 0.70)**

*Initial conditions*: Debt/GDP ~30% (rising rapidly), structural deficit widening from ~1% to ~5% of GDP, Volcker Fed maintaining tight monetary policy, tax cuts (ERTA 1981) combined with defense spending increases, political rhetoric about cutting domestic spending that largely failed to materialize.

*What happened*: The fiscal-monetary policy mix produced a textbook "crowding out" episode. Real interest rates surged to historically unprecedented levels (10Y real yield ~6-8%). The dollar appreciated ~50% on a trade-weighted basis (1980-85). The current account deficit widened sharply. Domestic investment was partially crowded out, though GDP growth rebounded strongly post-1982 recession. The fiscal consolidation that followed (GRH Act 1985, 1986 and 1990 budget deals) was gradual and incomplete. Debt/GDP rose from 30% to ~50% over the decade.

*Relevance to today*: The strongest parallel is the political dynamic — promises of spending cuts (then: Grace Commission, now: DOGE) that run into the reality that ~70% of federal spending is mandatory/entitlements and politically untouchable. The key difference: 2026 starts from debt/GDP of ~124% vs. ~30%, meaning the fiscal space for error is dramatically smaller. The 1980s US could "grow out of" the debt because nominal GDP growth (~7-10% annually) far exceeded the starting debt ratio. At 124% debt/GDP, growth-based deleveraging requires nominal GDP growth to significantly exceed the effective interest rate on the debt for a sustained period — a condition that is far harder to achieve.

**Analogue 2: Japan 1996-97 Premature Consolidation (Similarity: 0.65)**

*Initial conditions*: Debt/GDP ~90% and rising rapidly, economy showing tentative recovery from the 1990-95 balance sheet recession, BOJ at near-zero rates, consensus forming that fiscal consolidation was necessary to arrest debt trajectory.

*What happened*: The Hashimoto government raised the consumption tax from 3% to 5% (April 1997) and implemented spending restraints. GDP growth turned sharply negative (-2.0% in FY1998). The banking crisis intensified as the fiscal contraction reduced asset values and loan performance. The government reversed course, implementing a ¥24T stimulus package in April 1998, but the damage was done — Japan entered a deflationary spiral that persisted for 15+ years. Debt/GDP accelerated rather than stabilized.

*Relevance to today*: This is the cautionary analogue for DOGE-style spending cuts. Japan's 1997 experience demonstrates that fiscal consolidation during a period of private sector fragility can be catastrophically counterproductive — the deficit *worsened* rather than improved because tax revenues collapsed. The key difference: the US private sector balance sheet in 2026 is fundamentally healthier than Japan's was in 1997 (no equivalent banking crisis, household leverage manageable). This difference reduces but does not eliminate the Japan risk.

**Analogue 3: Eurozone 2010-13 Austerity (Similarity: 0.60)**

*Initial conditions*: Post-GFC, sovereign debt concerns (Greece, Ireland, Portugal, then Italy and Spain), political consensus for fiscal consolidation, ECB initially unwilling to backstop sovereigns, aggregate Eurozone debt/GDP ~85%.

*What happened*: Coordinated fiscal austerity across the Eurozone produced a double-dip recession (2012-13). GDP fell ~1.5% in the aggregate, with periphery nations experiencing severe contractions (Greece -7%, Spain -3%, Italy -3%). Fiscal multipliers proved to be 2-3x higher than the models assumed (the IMF's Blanchard and Leigh 2013 paper documented this). Debt/GDP ratios *increased* in most countries despite austerity, as the denominator (GDP) shrank faster than the numerator (debt). The crisis was only resolved by the ECB's "whatever it takes" backstop (July 2012) and eventual QE — a monetary offset to the fiscal contraction.

*Relevance to today*: The multiplier underestimation lesson is directly applicable to DOGE projections. Any fiscal impact assessment that uses multipliers below 0.5x for direct government spending cuts should be viewed skeptically based on this episode. The key difference: the Eurozone lacked a lender of last resort until Draghi's intervention; the US has no such institutional gap, but the Fed's willingness to offset fiscal contraction is constrained by inflation above target.

**Analogue 4: US 1993-2000 Clinton Consolidation (Similarity: 0.55)**

*Initial conditions*: Debt/GDP ~65%, structural deficit ~4% of GDP, political agreement on deficit reduction (OBRA 1993 — tax increases + spending caps), Fed conducting a measured tightening cycle (1994-95), economy in early/mid-expansion.

*What happened*: The fiscal consolidation was remarkably successful — the budget moved from ~$300B deficit to ~$240B surplus by FY2000. But critically, the deficit reduction was driven primarily by the *revenue* side: the tech boom produced extraordinary capital gains tax revenues and income tax receipts. Discretionary spending cuts were modest in real terms. GDP growth averaged ~4% annually. Term premium compressed. Equities rallied dramatically. The dollar strengthened. The fiscal consolidation was widely credited with "crowding in" private investment by reducing long-term interest rates.

*Relevance to today*: This is the optimistic analogue — but its success factors are largely unreplicable. The tech boom revenue surge was circumstantial. The starting debt/GDP was half the current level. The Fed had room to cut rates (which it did in 1995 and 1998, offsetting fiscal tightness during soft patches). And the spending "cuts" were largely spending *caps* that held nominal growth below GDP growth, not actual nominal reductions — a far less contractionary approach than DOGE's proposed outright cuts.

**Analogue 5: UK 2010-16 Osborne Austerity (Similarity: 0.55)**

*Initial conditions*: Debt/GDP ~75% and rising post-GFC, structural deficit ~8% of GDP, new government committed to rapid deficit reduction, BOE at zero lower bound with active QE, economy in fragile early recovery.

*What happened*: The government implemented ~£81B in planned spending cuts over 5 years, targeting departmental budgets while partially protecting health and education. GDP growth undershot forecasts by ~1-1.5 percentage points cumulatively over 2010-13. Real wages stagnated. The deficit was reduced but more slowly than planned (from ~10% to ~4% of GDP over 5 years, vs. planned reduction to ~1%). The BOE's QE partially offset the fiscal drag. Austerity became politically toxic and was partially reversed after 2016.

*Relevance to today*: The realization gap between announced and delivered savings is the key lesson. The UK achieved roughly 65% of planned savings, and even that came at a higher GDP cost than expected. The political sustainability of austerity (which eroded within 5-6 years and arguably contributed to the Brexit vote) is also relevant for assessing DOGE's multi-year viability.

### Claim 2: Fiscal Multiplier Regime-Dependence

The declining multiplier pattern at high debt levels is most clearly documented in the Japanese case. The Economic Planning Agency's estimates of fiscal multipliers for successive stimulus packages:

- 1992-93 packages (debt/GDP ~65%): multiplier ~0.9-1.1x
- 1994-95 packages (debt/GDP ~80%): multiplier ~0.7-0.9x
- 1998-99 packages (debt/GDP ~110%): multiplier ~0.5-0.7x
- 2000s packages (debt/GDP ~150%+): multiplier ~0.3-0.5x

The declining pattern has multiple reinforcing channels: (a) Ricardian equivalence effects — at high debt levels, households and firms anticipate future tax increases and save rather than spend transfer income; (b) rising interest payments crowd out productive spending within the fiscal envelope; (c) diminishing marginal returns on infrastructure spending as high-value projects are completed first; (d) zombie firm dynamics — fiscal support keeps unproductive firms alive, reducing resource reallocation and aggregate productivity.

The US-specific evidence is more contested. CBO estimates of ARRA multipliers (0.8-2.5x range) are wide, and the 2021 ARP multiplier appears to have been quite high (~1.0-1.5x) despite debt/GDP above 100% — but much of the ARP impact manifested as inflation rather than real GDP growth, which may overstate the "real" multiplier. The most recent academic consensus (Ramey 2019, Auerbach and Gorodnichenko 2012) suggests multipliers are higher during recessions (1.5-2.0x) and lower during expansions (0.5-0.8x), and that the debt level effect operates partially through this channel — high-debt-level stimulus often occurs during expansions (because the debt was accumulated during prior recessions), pushing the multiplier lower for compositional reasons.

### Claim 5: Deficit-Term Premium Non-linearity

The key empirical challenge is isolating the fiscal contribution to term premium from other drivers (monetary policy, global savings glut, flight-to-safety demand). The cleanest natural experiments are:

- **1993 Clinton deficit reduction announcement**: The 10Y yield fell ~150bp between the election (Nov 1992) and mid-1993, with term premium compression accounting for roughly 80-100bp of the decline. James Carville's famous quote about wanting to be reincarnated as the bond market reflected the perceived power of fiscal rectitude over long rates.
- **2023-24 Treasury issuance shift**: When the Treasury shifted issuance toward bills and shorter coupons (reducing net long-term supply), the 10Y term premium compressed ~30-50bp despite ongoing fiscal deficits — confirming that net *long-duration* supply to private markets is the operative variable, not the headline deficit alone.
- **2022 UK Truss budget**: The most dramatic recent example — an ~£45B unfunded tax cut announcement triggered a ~100bp spike in 30Y gilt yields over 4 trading days, with virtually all of the move attributable to term premium rather than rate expectations. This demonstrated that term premium can reprice violently and non-linearly when fiscal credibility erodes.

### Claim 7: Fiscal Dominance Signpost Analysis

The fiscal dominance sequence is derived from case studies where the process reached completion:

- **Japan (gradual, 2000-present)**: All 5 signals present. Signal (iii) manifested as BOJ yield curve control (2016), which explicitly linked monetary policy to sovereign debt management. Signal (iv) emerged through regulatory mandates requiring Japanese banks and pension funds to hold JGBs. Signal (v) never fully triggered — inflation expectations remained anchored below 2% for decades, though they have recently risen.
- **Italy (acute, 2011-12)**: Signals (i) through (iii) triggered rapidly in 2011 as BTP-Bund spreads widened to 550bp. Signal (iv) was imposed externally via Draghi's OMT. Signal (v) was averted by the ECB backstop.
- **UK (aborted, 2022)**: The Truss episode attempted to jump from signal (i) directly to signal (v) — running large unfunded deficits without preparing institutional channels. The market's violent rejection showed that attempting to skip the gradual fiscal dominance sequence in a country with floating exchange rates, independent central bank, and market-determined gilt yields produces a crisis rather than accommodation.

The US in 2026 sits at signals (i) and (ii), with signal (iii) contested. The Japanese analogue suggests decades of further gradual progression are possible. The UK/Truss analogue warns that a fiscal policy shock (e.g., a large unfunded tax cut extension) could accelerate the timeline dramatically.

## Confidence Assessment

1. **Analogue identification and similarity scoring**: 7/10. The five analogues are well-established in the literature and the selection criteria are transparent. Lower confidence stems from the genuinely novel elements of the current situation (reserve currency at 124% debt/GDP with full employment) that reduce all analogue similarity scores.

2. **Fiscal multiplier regime-dependence**: 6/10. The directional claim (multipliers decline at higher debt levels) has reasonable empirical support, but the magnitude (30-50% lower) is drawn from a small sample with significant confounding variables. The Japanese case is the strongest evidence but may not generalize to the US given structural differences.

3. **Spending reduction success rate (~30%)**: 7/10. The sample of 13 episodes is sufficient for base rate calculation, and the common success factors (currency depreciation, external demand) are well-documented. Confidence is slightly reduced by survivorship issues — less well-known consolidation attempts may be missing from the sample.

4. **DOGE realization rate (0.3-0.6x announced)**: 5/10. This is the lowest-confidence claim because DOGE's approach (technology-driven efficiency + workforce reduction) is structurally different from prior fiscal consolidation programs, which were predominantly spending caps or transfer reductions. The historical base rate may understate or overstate DOGE's effectiveness depending on whether technology-driven savings face similar or different implementation barriers.

5. **Deficit-term premium non-linearity**: 8/10. The empirical evidence is relatively strong, supported by multiple natural experiments (Clinton 1993, Truss 2022, Treasury 2023-24 issuance shifts). The threshold estimate (~3-4% structural deficit) has wider confidence intervals but the non-linear relationship itself is well-established.

6. **G7 fiscal divergence cross-asset consequences**: 6/10. The directional predictions are supported by clear historical patterns, but the dollar direction depends critically on the monetary-fiscal policy mix, which is uncertain. The three historical episodes produced different dollar outcomes, limiting predictive precision.

7. **Fiscal dominance signpost sequence**: 7/10. The sequence is well-documented across multiple country cases. Lower confidence for the US application because the reserve currency status introduces dynamics not present in any prior fiscal dominance episode — the dollar's role as global reserve creates demand for Treasuries that may permanently alter the fiscal dominance threshold.

8. **Equity performance during consolidation vs. expansion**: 6/10. The average return differential is real, but the sample is small and the confounding role of monetary policy makes the causal interpretation uncertain. The conditional finding (consolidation underperforms when monetary offset is unavailable) has even lower sample support.

## Connections to Other Topics

- **Sovereign Debt (sovereign_debt)**: The term premium analysis (Claim 5) and fiscal dominance framework (Claim 7) directly feed sovereign debt sustainability models. The non-linear term premium relationship implies that debt sustainability calculations using current interest rates may significantly understate future debt service costs if the fiscal trajectory pushes term premium above the non-linearity threshold. The 1980s analogue — where real rates of 6-8% nearly doubled the debt/GDP ratio in a decade starting from 30% — shows what happens when term premium normalizes at high deficit levels.

- **Fiscal Dominance (fiscal_dominance)**: Claim 7's signpost framework provides a structured approach to monitoring the US path toward or away from fiscal dominance. The key unresolved question is whether the US reserve currency status fundamentally changes the fiscal dominance threshold (permitting higher debt/GDP before dominance binds) or merely delays the same dynamics observed in Japan, Italy, and the UK.

- **Inflation Regimes (inflation_regimes)**: Fiscal policy is a primary driver of inflation regime transitions. The historical base rate: 3 of 5 major inflation regime shifts since 1970 were preceded by fiscal expansion (1965-68 → Great Inflation, 2020-21 → post-COVID inflation, 1940s wartime fiscal expansion → postwar inflation). The DOGE spending cut thesis has symmetric implications — if cuts are contractionary enough, they could accelerate the disinflationary path, potentially enabling Fed easing sooner.

- **Growth Models (growth_models)**: The fiscal multiplier analysis (Claim 2) directly informs GDP forecasting. If multipliers at current debt levels are 0.5-0.8x for expansion and 0.7-1.0x for contraction, DOGE spending cuts of $500B-1T would imply GDP drag of 1.5-3.0% spread over 2-3 years — sufficient to meaningfully alter cycle positioning and recession probability estimates.

- **Volatility Regimes (from iter_0010)**: Fiscal policy uncertainty is a structural driver of rates volatility (MOVE). The term premium non-linearity (Claim 5) connects directly to the MOVE elevation discussed in prior iterations — MOVE is elevated in part because the market is pricing a wider distribution of fiscal outcomes (from DOGE-driven consolidation to continued deficit expansion). Resolution of fiscal uncertainty in either direction would likely reduce MOVE, but through different channels: consolidation reduces term premium uncertainty, while expansion confirms the fiscal trajectory and reduces policy uncertainty even as it raises the level of term premium.

- **Risk Appetite Regimes (from iter_0001)**: The fiscal dominance progression (Claim 7) represents a slow-moving structural risk that, if it accelerates (as in the UK/Truss episode), could serve as a catalyst for regime transition from "complacent carry" to "flight to quality." The historical base rate for fiscal-policy-triggered risk regime transitions is lower than for monetary or credit triggers (1 of 7 complacent carry endings was fiscal-proximate: UK 2022), but the severity when it occurs is high because it undermines the safe-haven asset (sovereign bonds) that normally provides diversification during risk-off episodes.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the US reserve currency status a permanent structural modifier of fiscal dominance dynamics, or merely a delay mechanism?** Japan's experience suggests that domestic-currency debt with domestic ownership can sustain debt/GDP above 250% without triggering fiscal dominance signal (v) (inflation de-anchoring) — but Japan had current account surpluses and deflationary pressures that the US does not. No historical analogue exists for a reserve currency issuer testing these limits, making this genuinely uncharted territory.

2. **What is the actual fiscal multiplier of DOGE-style cuts?** The composition of DOGE savings — workforce reductions, contract cancellations, program eliminations — has different multiplier characteristics than the spending cap/transfer reduction models used in historical analogues. Government employee income has high marginal propensity to consume (1.0x pass-through to local economies), suggesting cuts to government payroll may have higher multipliers than cuts to transfer programs. But efficiency savings (eliminating waste without reducing services) would have near-zero multipliers. The ratio of genuine efficiency gains to contractionary spending cuts in DOGE's portfolio is unknowable from outside.

3. **How does the term premium respond to fiscal consolidation that the market views as insufficient?** The Clinton analogue shows term premium compression from credible consolidation, and the Truss analogue shows term premium explosion from incredible expansion. But what happens in the intermediate case — where consolidation is attempted but markets judge it insufficient to stabilize the debt trajectory? The Eurozone 2010-13 experience suggests this "uncanny valley" of fiscal policy produces the worst of both worlds: contractionary GDP impact without the term premium benefit.

4. **What is the interaction between DOGE spending cuts and state/local government finances?** Federal spending reductions often shift costs to state and local governments (Medicaid is the canonical example). The historical base rate for federal spending cuts producing offsetting state/local spending increases is ~30-50% of the federal reduction over 3-5 years, significantly reducing the net fiscal impact. This dynamic is understudied in the current DOGE debate.

5. **Can the US achieve a "beautiful deleveraging" — reducing debt/GDP through a combination of moderate inflation (3-4%), moderate growth (2-3%), moderate austerity (1-2% of GDP), and financial repression (real rates below nominal GDP growth) — as it did in the 1945-1980 period?** The post-WWII deleveraging reduced debt/GDP from ~120% to ~30% over 35 years through exactly this combination. The question is whether the institutional and political conditions for sustaining such a policy over decades exist in the current environment. The historical precedent is encouraging on the possibility but the time horizon (decades, not years) is incompatible with political cycles that demand visible results within 2-4 years.
