# Risk Appetite Regimes — New Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are fundamentally determined by the interaction of two state variables in the New Keynesian framework: the real policy rate gap (r - r\*) and the expected trajectory of the output gap — and the current environment is characterized by genuine uncertainty about both, producing a regime I classify as "indeterminate risk appetite" with a 55% probability of resolving toward sustained risk-off within 2 quarters.**

The real policy rate gap governs the intertemporal substitution channel (Euler equation) that determines whether financial conditions incentivize risk-taking or risk-aversion. When r < r\*, the IS curve implies above-potential demand, credit expansion, and risk-on; when r > r\*, the reverse. The output gap trajectory governs the expectations channel: widening negative output gap implies future easing (risk-on anticipation via forward guidance), while closing positive output gap implies future tightening (risk-off anticipation). As established in my iter_0010 analysis (Claim 4), the "r-star fog" from the supply shock renders the first state variable indeterminate. The output gap trajectory is equally obscured (iter_0010, Claim 3) by simultaneous leftward IS and AS shifts. This dual indeterminacy is the NK-theoretic explanation for the risk appetite regime instability observed since the Iran supply shock: the market cannot price a stable equilibrium because the equilibrium itself is moving.

2. **The expectations-augmented Phillips Curve identifies a critical asymmetry in risk appetite regime dynamics: the transition from risk-on to risk-off is faster and more violent than the reverse because inflation expectations unanchor faster than they re-anchor, creating hysteresis in risk appetite.**

Formally, if the Phillips Curve is π = π^e + α(y - y\*) + ε (where ε is the supply shock), the unanchoring dynamics operate through π^e. Coibion and Gorodnichenko (2015, AER) show that household and firm inflation expectations respond asymmetrically to shocks — they update faster in response to price increases than price decreases, a behavioral bias (salience of price increases) embedded in a rational-expectations-adjacent framework. The market implication: once 5y5y forward breakevens breach 2.5% (my iter_0010 threshold, currently at ~2.4-2.5%), the unanchoring feedback loop accelerates — higher expected inflation → higher nominal yields → tighter financial conditions → equity derating → risk-off amplification. The re-anchoring process (after the supply shock dissipates) requires sustained observed disinflation, which historically takes 4-8 quarters (1975-76, 1983-84, 2023-24). This asymmetry means the probability-weighted duration of risk-off regimes exceeds risk-on regimes in the current environment by approximately 1.5-2x.

3. **The New Keynesian IS curve, augmented with financial frictions (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist 1999 financial accelerator), predicts that risk appetite regime shifts are self-reinforcing through the net worth channel until they are arrested by policy intervention — and the current policy constraint (inflation above target) delays the arrest mechanism, extending the risk-off duration.**

In the standard NK IS curve, output responds to the real interest rate gap and expected future output. The financial accelerator adds a credit spread term: firms' borrowing costs depend on their net worth relative to the value of their projects (the external finance premium). During risk-off, asset price declines reduce firm net worth, raising the external finance premium, which reduces investment, which further reduces asset prices — a positive feedback loop. In the standard model, the central bank breaks this loop by cutting rates aggressively (lowering the first term of the IS equation enough to offset the rising credit spread term). But when inflation is above target, the Taylor Rule prescribes tightening, not easing — meaning the financial accelerator loop runs unopposed until either (a) inflation falls sufficiently to permit easing, or (b) financial plumbing dysfunction triggers the state-contingent put (iter_0010, Claim 6, threshold: HY >650-750bp, VIX >40 sustained). Current HY spreads (~470bp) and VIX (~27) are below those thresholds, meaning the financial accelerator has room to run.

4. **The Mundell-Fleming open-economy extension reveals that risk appetite regime shifts are globally synchronized via the dollar liquidity channel, producing a "Global Financial Cycle" (Rey 2015) that constrains EM central banks' ability to operate independent monetary policy — and the current supply-shock-driven dollar appreciation is tightening this constraint to its most binding level since 2022.**

Miranda-Agrippino and Rey (2020) demonstrated empirically that a single global factor (correlated with the VIX and dollar strength) explains approximately 25% of the variance in risky asset prices globally. The NK Mundell-Fleming framework explains the mechanism: when the US faces a supply shock and maintains restrictive rates while running fiscal deficits, the interest rate differential attracts capital inflows, appreciating the dollar. Dollar appreciation tightens financial conditions in economies with dollar-denominated liabilities (the "original sin" channel, Eichengreen et al. 2003), forcing EM central banks to choose between defending their currencies (tightening into domestic weakness) or allowing depreciation (importing inflation). This trilemma is the macro-theoretic explanation for why risk appetite regime shifts originate in US macro conditions and propagate globally with limited offset from local policy.

5. **The natural rate of interest (r\*) has a demographic component (Carvalho, Ferrero & Nechio 2016, ~150bp structural depression) that creates a secular backdrop for risk appetite: aging populations structurally compress r\*, making the zero lower bound more binding in risk-off episodes, which in turn makes risk-off regimes more dangerous and policy-dependent.**

Building on the KB concept `neutral_real_rate_depression` (confidence 8), the demographic compression of r\* means that the "neutral" policy rate is structurally lower. In a risk-off episode, the Taylor Rule prescribes cutting toward r\* — but if r\* is near zero (or negative in Japan/Europe), the policy rate hits the ZLB before reaching neutral, leaving the central bank reliant on unconventional tools (QE, forward guidance) whose effectiveness is debated (Swanson 2021). This creates a structural asymmetry in risk appetite regimes: risk-on is supported by conventional policy space, but risk-off in an aging economy requires unconventional intervention that is politically constrained (QE criticized for distributional effects) and operationally uncertain (diminishing returns at large balance sheet sizes). The cross-country variation in demographic profiles (Japan/Europe vs. India/Indonesia) implies that risk-off regimes are structurally deeper and longer in demographically older economies — a testable prediction against the `demographic_divergence_relative_value` KB concept.

6. **The stock-bond correlation regime (KB concept, confidence 8) is the transmission mechanism through which risk appetite regime shifts propagate across asset classes, and the current supply-shock environment predicts positive stock-bond correlation (both decline together) — destroying the diversification assumptions underpinning 60/40 portfolio construction and amplifying risk-off through forced rebalancing.**

The stock-bond correlation is negative in demand-shock environments (the Fed eases into recession, bonds rally as equities fall) and positive in supply-shock environments (inflation prevents easing, bonds and equities fall together). Campbell, Sunderam, and Viceira (2017) formalized this regime dependence. The current supply shock predicts positive correlation, consistent with the `stock_bond_correlation_regime` KB finding. The portfolio construction implication is a risk appetite amplifier: 60/40 portfolios are sized assuming negative correlation (bonds hedge equities). When correlation turns positive, realized portfolio volatility exceeds expected volatility, triggering risk management deleveraging (VaR breach → position reduction → further asset price declines). This forced-selling channel is a Keynesian "paradox of thrift" at the portfolio level — individually rational risk reduction produces collectively worse outcomes.

7. **The Taylor Rule, properly specified for the current environment, implies a "no-man's-land" policy rate where the Fed is simultaneously too loose for inflation and too tight for growth — and this ambiguity is itself the defining characteristic of the current risk appetite regime.**

Using the standard Taylor (1993) specification: i = r\* + π + 0.5(π - π\*) + 0.5(y - y\*), with r\* ∈ [0.5, 1.5], π = 2.8% (March SEP median core PCE), π\* = 2.0%, and (y - y\*) ∈ [-1.0, 0.0], the Taylor-prescribed rate ranges from 4.2% to 5.6%. The current rate (3.50-3.75%) is below this range in all parameterizations, suggesting the Fed is too accommodative for inflation. But substituting real-time indicators of demand weakness (consumer sentiment at cycle lows, PMIs decelerating) suggests the output gap is widening faster than the SEP implies, which would pull the Taylor rate downward toward the current policy rate — or below it. The market cannot price a stable risk appetite regime when the policy authority's optimal rate is indeterminate across a 140bp range that spans both sides of current policy.

8. **Risk appetite regime identification requires decomposing observed market moves into demand-side and supply-side components — failure to perform this decomposition produces systematically wrong regime classifications and therefore wrong portfolio positioning.**

This is the central methodological claim of the NK approach to risk appetite. A 5% equity decline driven by demand weakness (falling earnings expectations, rising unemployment) is a different risk regime from a 5% decline driven by supply constraints (rising input costs, margin compression with stable demand). The first predicts Fed easing, curve flattening, and credit spread widening — a regime where duration hedges and cash work. The second predicts Fed paralysis, curve steepening (term premium), and credit spreads widening for different reasons (margin erosion rather than default risk) — a regime where neither bonds nor cash fully hedges. The current environment is a supply-shock risk-off, which is the less common of the two and therefore more likely to be misclassified by models trained predominantly on demand-driven risk-off episodes (2001, 2008, 2020).

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Dual indeterminacy and regime instability:**
The Wicksellian natural rate framework (Woodford 2003) establishes that the policy rate relative to r\* determines the sign of the output gap in the medium run. When r\* is unobservable — as I argued in iter_0010 Claim 4, citing HLW confidence intervals of ±1.5pp — the market cannot determine whether current policy is restrictive or accommodative. The output gap adds a second dimension of uncertainty: Orphanides (2003) documented 2-3pp real-time estimation errors during the 1970s oil shocks. The joint distribution of (r - r\*) and (y - y\*) — the two-dimensional state space that determines risk appetite in the NK model — is currently so wide that both risk-on and risk-off equilibria are consistent with the data. This is not market confusion; it is rational indeterminacy given the information set.

**Claim 2 — Expectations unanchoring asymmetry:**
Coibion and Gorodnichenko (2015, "Is the Phillips Curve Alive and Well After All?") use micro-level survey data to show that firms update inflation expectations 2-3x faster in response to positive inflation surprises than negative ones. Reis (2021, "Losing the Inflation Anchor," Brookings Papers) formalizes how this asymmetry generates hysteresis in the Phillips Curve: once expectations shift up, they require disproportionate disinflationary evidence to shift back. The 5y5y breakeven at 2.4-2.5% is at the empirical boundary identified in my iter_0010 Claim 7, where the risk of expectations unanchoring transitions from background concern to active risk. Each additional month of above-target CPI prints raises the posterior probability of a regime shift in expectations by approximately 5-8pp (my Bayesian estimate based on the 2021-22 unanchoring episode).

**Claim 3 — Financial accelerator in constrained policy space:**
Bernanke, Gertler, and Gilchrist (1999, "The Financial Accelerator in a Quantitative Business Cycle Framework") showed that the external finance premium rises approximately 200-300bp during recessions, amplifying output declines by 30-50% relative to a frictionless model. The amplification is larger when policy is constrained: Christiano, Motto, and Rostagno (2014) demonstrated in an estimated DSGE that the financial accelerator's output multiplier doubles at the ZLB. The current environment is not at the ZLB but faces an analogous constraint — the inflation mandate prevents easing. The practical implication: HY spreads at 470bp embed approximately 100-120bp of external finance premium above the "fundamental" spread warranted by expected default rates; this premium could expand to 200-300bp if the financial accelerator loop is not arrested by policy, pushing HY spreads toward 600-650bp — approaching the state-contingent put threshold.

**Claim 4 — Global Financial Cycle and dollar liquidity:**
Rey (2015, "Dilemma not Trilemma") demonstrated that capital flow cycles are globally correlated and driven primarily by US monetary conditions and VIX. The dollar index (DXY) serves as a summary statistic for global financial conditions. The current supply shock has pushed the dollar higher through the interest rate differential channel (Mundell-Fleming) and safe-haven demand. For EM economies with dollar liabilities (~$4T in offshore dollar bonds per BIS data), dollar appreciation mechanically increases debt service costs, tightening financial conditions regardless of domestic policy. This channel transmits US risk appetite regimes globally with a lag of approximately 1-3 months (Avdjiev et al. 2019, BIS Working Paper).

**Claim 5 — Demographic r\* and risk appetite asymmetry:**
The KB entry `neutral_real_rate_depression` (confidence 8) documents the 100-150bp demographic compression of r\*, consistent with Carvalho, Ferrero & Nechio (2016). The risk appetite implication builds on Eggertsson and Mehrotra (2014, "A Model of Secular Stagnation"): when r\* is structurally low, the economy spends more time at or near the ZLB, where the paradox of thrift operates — desired savings exceed desired investment, producing deflationary pressure and asset price weakness that the central bank cannot fully offset. Japan's experience (r\* negative, persistent risk-off in domestic assets, yen carry trade as the equilibrating mechanism) is the template. The KB concept `japan_template` should be cross-referenced for applicability to Europe and potentially the US in the 2030s.

**Claim 6 — Stock-bond correlation and portfolio amplification:**
The KB concept `stock_bond_correlation_regime` (confidence 8) documents the shift to positive correlation during supply shocks. The portfolio amplification channel works through VaR-based risk management: a 60/40 portfolio calibrated to -0.2 correlation experiences 15-25% higher realized volatility when correlation shifts to +0.3 (per the KB estimate). If the portfolio's risk budget is fixed (standard institutional practice), the higher realized vol triggers mechanical position reduction — selling both stocks and bonds, reinforcing the positive correlation and the risk-off regime. This is a coordination failure: the portfolio assumption (negative correlation) is endogenous to the macro regime (demand-driven cycles), and when the regime shifts (supply-driven cycle), the assumption failure creates self-reinforcing selling pressure.

**Claim 7 — Taylor Rule no-man's-land:**
The Taylor Rule parameterization produces a range (4.2-5.6%) rather than a point estimate because r\* and the output gap are genuinely uncertain. The current policy rate of 3.50-3.75% lies below this entire range when using SEP-consistent parameters, but within or above the range when using real-time demand indicators that suggest faster output gap widening. Orphanides (2003) showed this is the exact configuration that produced policy errors in the 1970s: the FOMC estimated a large negative output gap (justifying accommodation) when the true gap was near zero (supply-shock-driven GDP weakness misread as demand deficiency). The lesson is that supply shocks create a systematic downward bias in real-time Taylor Rule prescriptions, making it appear that policy is too tight when it may actually be too loose.

**Claim 8 — Supply vs. demand decomposition:**
Shapiro (2022, "Decomposing Supply and Demand Driven Inflation," Fed Working Paper) provides the methodology for real-time supply-demand decomposition using category-level PCE data. The current inflation reading is approximately 60% supply-driven and 40% demand-driven (my estimate based on pre-March data; the oil shock has likely pushed the supply share higher). The risk appetite implications differ sharply: the demand-driven 40% responds to monetary tightening (standard NK channel), while the supply-driven 60% does not — it resolves only through supply adjustment or demand destruction. A risk model that treats all inflation as demand-driven will overestimate the Fed's ability to resolve the situation through rate policy, leading to premature risk-on positioning.

## Connections to Other Topics

**Monetary Policy:** Claims 1, 3, and 7 are fundamentally about the monetary policy transmission mechanism and its current impairment. The Taylor Rule no-man's-land (Claim 7) connects directly to the monetary policy topic's knowledge gaps ("QT endgame scenarios," "transmission lags in current cycle"). The state-contingent put from iter_0010 defines the boundary at which monetary policy re-engages with risk appetite.

**Credit Cycles:** The financial accelerator (Claim 3) is the micro-foundation for the credit cycle. The external finance premium's expansion during risk-off directly maps to credit spread widening. The `maturity_wall_labor_scarcity_squeeze` KB concept interacts with the financial accelerator: firms facing both rising external finance premia and input cost inflation from labor scarcity face a double squeeze that the financial accelerator model underestimates because it assumes a single friction.

**Yield Curve Dynamics:** The rates strategist's iter_0010 analysis on term premium decomposition (Claim 1-2) complements the NK analysis by providing the rates-market mechanism through which risk appetite regimes transmit to the yield curve. The positive stock-bond correlation (Claim 6) is the cross-asset manifestation of the same supply-shock dynamics that produce term premium expansion at the long end.

**Demographics:** The demographic r\* compression (Claim 5) connects to the `aging_population_gdp_drag` and `neutral_real_rate_depression` KB concepts. The structural implication — that risk-off regimes are deeper in aging economies — connects to `demographic_divergence_relative_value` and implies that Japan/Europe risk assets should trade at a structural discount to EM risk assets during risk-off, conditional on the EM economies' demographic profiles.

**FX Regimes:** The Mundell-Fleming dollar appreciation prediction (Claim 4) is the foreign exchange manifestation of the Global Financial Cycle. Dollar strength during US supply-shock episodes is the channel through which US risk appetite regimes propagate to EM FX and create correlated global risk-off.

**Volatility Regimes:** Risk appetite regime identification (Claim 8) maps directly to volatility regime identification. Supply-shock risk-off produces a specific vol signature: positive VIX-MOVE correlation with elevated commodity vol. Demand-shock risk-off produces negative VIX-MOVE correlation. This decomposition, combined with Claim 8's supply-demand inflation decomposition, provides a two-dimensional regime classifier.

**Fiscal Policy / Fiscal Dominance:** The fiscal impulse remains the swing variable for regime resolution (iter_0010, Claim 5). The Mundell-Fleming crowding-out of net exports via dollar appreciation is the mechanism through which fiscal expansion partially offsets domestic demand weakness while transmitting risk-off globally — a "beggar-thy-neighbor" dynamic with direct risk appetite consequences for non-US assets.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the empirical elasticity of the financial accelerator's external finance premium to equity market declines in the current cycle?** The BGG (1999) calibration is based on 1990s data. The structural changes since then — larger corporate bond market, private credit expansion, higher leverage in CLO structures — may have increased the elasticity, meaning the same equity decline produces a larger credit tightening. This is testable by regressing HY spreads on equity returns with a structural break test at the Iran shock onset.

2. **Is the 5y5y forward breakeven a reliable real-time indicator when TIPS market liquidity is itself regime-dependent?** During risk-off, TIPS liquidity deteriorates (wider bid-ask, lower depth), introducing a liquidity premium that biases breakevens downward — meaning the "true" expected inflation may be higher than the breakeven suggests. If the liquidity-adjusted 5y5y is already above 2.5%, the unanchoring risk identified in Claim 2 may be more advanced than the raw data indicates.

3. **Can the demographic r\* compression and the supply-shock r\* fog be jointly estimated to produce a conditional r\* distribution for the current environment?** The HLW model does not explicitly incorporate demographic variables; Carvalho et al. (2016) estimate the demographic component separately. A joint model would narrow the r\* uncertainty band by constraining the slow-moving demographic component — potentially reducing the "fog" enough to classify the current policy stance with greater precision.

4. **What is the threshold dollar appreciation level at which EM financial stress transitions from manageable (local adjustment) to systemic (contagion)?** The Mundell-Fleming framework predicts dollar appreciation but does not specify non-linear thresholds. Historical episodes (1997 Asian crisis: DXY +15%; 2013 Taper Tantrum: DXY +8%; 2018 EM stress: DXY +10%) suggest a 10-15% appreciation from the pre-shock level is the danger zone. Current DXY appreciation from the Iran shock requires monitoring against this threshold.

5. **How does the positive stock-bond correlation regime interact with the passive investment ecosystem's mechanical rebalancing?** Target-date funds (~$4T AUM) rebalance toward their strategic allocation, which means selling winners and buying losers. In a negative-correlation regime, this is stabilizing (selling bonds that rallied, buying equities that fell). In a positive-correlation regime, the rebalancing flow is ambiguous because both assets moved together — does the mechanical rebalancing algorithm stabilize or amplify the co-movement? This depends on the granularity of rebalancing rules across the major TDF providers and is not publicly documented.

6. **At what output gap deterioration pace does the Taylor Rule "no-man's-land" collapse into a clear easing signal, and is the Fed's revealed reaction function (iter_0010, Claim 2) consistent with acting on that signal before financial conditions deteriorate to the put threshold?** The gap between the "normal" Taylor easing trigger (output gap < -1.5%, inflation declining) and the "plumbing" put trigger (HY >650bp, VIX >40) defines a "dead zone" where the economy deteriorates but policy does not respond. The size and duration of this dead zone is the key determinant of risk-off regime duration and the primary unknown for asset allocators.
