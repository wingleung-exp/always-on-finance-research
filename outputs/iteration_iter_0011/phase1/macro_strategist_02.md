# Risk Appetite Regime Shifts & Contagion — Post-Keynesian Macro Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Risk appetite regimes are endogenous to the credit cycle, not exogenous shocks to be modeled as random switches.** The Minsky cycle—hedge → speculative → Ponzi finance—is the generative mechanism behind what markets label "risk-on" and "risk-off." Risk appetite does not fluctuate randomly; it is manufactured by the credit creation process itself.

2. **The sectoral financial balances identity predicts which risk appetite regimes are sustainable and which must reverse.** A "risk-on" regime that requires the domestic private sector to run persistent financial deficits (spending more than income, financed by rising debt) is inherently self-limiting. The reversal to "risk-off" is not a mood swing but a balance-sheet constraint becoming binding.

3. **"Risk-off" episodes are fundamentally liquidity crises in which the hierarchy of money reasserts itself.** When risk appetite collapses, agents scramble up the liquidity pyramid—from equities to corporate bonds to Treasuries to central bank reserves to cash. This hierarchy (identified by Perry Mehrling and rooted in Minsky's work) determines contagion pathways with far more precision than correlation-based models.

4. **The velocity of modern contagion is governed by the shadow banking system's reliance on mark-to-market collateral chains.** Repo haircuts, margin calls, and collateral revaluation create a pro-cyclical amplification mechanism that Minsky could not have fully anticipated but that is entirely consistent with his framework. The leverage embedded in dealer balance sheets and hedge fund basis trades determines how quickly a localized credit event becomes systemic.

5. **Liquidity withdrawal cascades follow a predictable sequence: first private credit markets freeze, then interbank/repo markets seize, and only then do public equity markets crash.** The observable risk-off in equity VIX typically lags the real fragility event in credit markets by days to weeks. Traditional risk-on/risk-off indicators watching equities and FX are lagging, not leading.

6. **Central bank interventions do not eliminate Minsky cycles; they displace them temporally and across sectors.** Each intervention (QE, emergency facilities, yield curve control) creates a new generation of speculative and Ponzi positions structured around the expectation of continued intervention, raising the eventual amplitude of the next risk-off event.

7. **The private debt-to-GDP ratio and the credit impulse are the most reliable leading indicators of risk appetite regime transitions.** When private debt growth decelerates after an extended boom (credit impulse turns negative), a risk-off transition becomes highly probable within 4–8 quarters regardless of what equity market sentiment indicators show.

## Evidence & Reasoning

**Claim 1 — Endogeneity of risk appetite to credit:**
Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis (1986, *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*) argues that stability itself is destabilizing. During tranquil periods, borrowers and lenders progressively relax safety margins, shifting from hedge finance (income covers principal and interest) to speculative finance (income covers interest only, requiring rollover) to Ponzi finance (income covers neither, requiring asset appreciation). What markets call "risk-on" is simply the aggregate expression of this portfolio migration toward fragility. The shift to "risk-off" occurs when cash flow validation fails at the margin. This is not a regime "switch" in the Markov sense—it is the endogenous exhaustion of a debt-driven expansion.

**Claim 2 — Sectoral balances as sustainability filter:**
Wynne Godley's sectoral balances framework (the identity: Private Balance + Government Balance + Foreign Balance ≡ 0) provides a macroeconomic accounting constraint. During prolonged risk-on regimes, the private sector typically moves into deficit (borrowing to invest and consume). If the government is simultaneously pursuing austerity (surplus or reduced deficit) and the current account is in deficit, then the private sector deficit becomes arithmetically larger. The U.S. private sector ran persistent deficits from 1996–2006; this was *identical* to the "Great Moderation" risk-on regime. When private balance sheets could no longer sustain this, the 2008 crisis was the forced reversal—a "risk-off" that was entirely predictable from the flow-of-funds data. Godley himself warned of this in his Levy Institute reports as early as 2000.

**Claim 3 — Hierarchy of money in risk-off:**
Mehrling's "money view" framework shows that financial instruments exist in a hierarchy: at the top, central bank money (reserves); below it, bank deposits; below that, money market instruments; then bonds; then equities and real assets. In risk-on, agents happily hold lower-quality instruments. In risk-off, they flee upward. This explains why correlations spike during crises—it is not that asset classes suddenly become correlated, but that every agent is simultaneously trying to move up the same liquidity pyramid. This framework explains why U.S. Treasuries rally in crises (flight to the top of the pyramid), why the dollar strengthens (the global hierarchy has the Fed at the apex), and why emerging market currencies collapse (at the bottom of the global hierarchy).

**Claim 4 — Collateral chains and contagion speed:**
Manmohan Singh (IMF) has documented how the same collateral is pledged and re-pledged ("rehypothecated") multiple times in repo and derivatives markets, creating a "collateral velocity" that amplifies both expansion and contraction. When a risk-off event triggers haircut increases, the collateral chain contracts multiplicatively. Pozsar's "shadow banking" mapping shows that entities like money market funds, securities lenders, and dealer banks form a chain of interlocking balance sheets where one entity's asset is another's collateral. This is Minsky's insight transposed to wholesale funding markets. The speed of the 2020 Treasury market dislocation—Treasuries themselves became illiquid for 48 hours—demonstrated that even the apex of the liquidity hierarchy can be temporarily impaired when dealer balance sheet capacity is exhausted.

**Claim 5 — Credit leads, equities lag:**
Empirically, high-yield credit spreads (ICE BofA HY OAS) began widening in June 2007, seven months before the equity peak. Leveraged loan markets froze in July 2007. The commercial paper market seized in August 2007. The equity market did not peak until October 2007 and did not experience its real crash until September 2008. Similarly, in 2020, credit markets (investment-grade ETF dislocations, CP market stress) showed distress before equities experienced their sharpest declines. The VIX, widely used as a "fear gauge," is primarily an equity derivative—it measures options-implied volatility on equities, not the underlying credit and funding fragility that generates the risk-off event.

**Claim 6 — Central bank displacement of cycles:**
Post-GFC monetary policy illustrates this perfectly. QE compressed term premia and credit spreads, encouraging the migration from hedge to speculative finance in corporate credit markets (the BBB cliff, covenant-lite loans, leveraged CLOs). The 2020 intervention—where the Fed purchased corporate bonds for the first time—validated the "Fed put" and accelerated the Ponzi dynamic in crypto, SPACs, and unprofitable tech. The subsequent tightening cycle of 2022–2023 revealed the fragility (SVB, Signature Bank, Credit Suisse) that the prior intervention had incubated. Each cycle did not eliminate risk; it merely shifted it to new sectors and new balance sheets.

**Claim 7 — Private debt and credit impulse as leading indicators:**
Steve Keen's empirical work (building on Minsky) demonstrates that the change in private debt (credit impulse) is strongly correlated with changes in unemployment and aggregate demand. Keen's model correctly identified the Australian, Irish, and U.S. housing bubbles by tracking private debt-to-GDP ratios exceeding historical norms. The BIS publishes the credit-to-GDP gap (deviation from trend) which serves as a similar early warning indicator—it flashed red for the U.S. in 2006, Spain in 2007, China in 2016, and Canada/Australia in 2018–2019. When the credit impulse turns negative, it means new borrowing is insufficient to service existing debt plus fund new spending, initiating the Minsky reversal.

## Confidence Assessment

| Claim | Confidence | Justification |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| 1. Endogeneity of risk appetite | **9/10** | Core Minskyan insight with overwhelming empirical support across multiple cycles. Slight uncertainty only because exogenous shocks (pandemics, wars) can accelerate or delay the endogenous process. |
| 2. Sectoral balances predict reversals | **8/10** | The accounting identity is irrefutable; the difficulty is in timing. Godley's 2000 warnings were correct but early by 7 years. The framework identifies *what* is unsustainable, not precisely *when* it breaks. |
| 3. Liquidity hierarchy governs risk-off | **8/10** | Well-supported theoretically (Mehrling, Minsky) and empirically (every crisis sees the same flight pattern). Deducted for partial uncertainty about how CBDCs or stablecoins might alter the hierarchy. |
| 4. Collateral chains determine contagion speed | **7/10** | Strong evidence (Singh, Pozsar, 2020 Treasury dislocation) but the shadow banking system is opaque by nature. Data limitations reduce confidence in precise mechanism mapping. |
| 5. Credit leads equities in risk-off | **8/10** | Robust empirical pattern across 2001, 2007–08, 2020 episodes. Some regime-specific variation: purely equity-driven events (1987) did not follow this sequence. |
| 6. Central banks displace cycles | **9/10** | This is almost tautological from a Minsky perspective—suppressing the corrective mechanism guarantees the re-emergence of fragility. Empirical support from every post-GFC episode. |
| 7. Private debt/credit impulse as leading indicators | **8/10** | BIS credit gap and Keen's debt models have strong track records. Deducted because the indicator gives direction but not precise timing, and financial innovation can temporarily extend debt sustainability. |

## Connections to Other Topics

**→ Volatility Regimes:** What mainstream finance calls a "low-vol regime" is what we call the euphoric phase of the Minsky cycle. Compressed volatility is not a regime to be modeled statistically—it is a *symptom* of expanding leverage and risk-taking. The transition from low-vol to high-vol is the Minsky moment itself. Any volatility regime model that does not incorporate private sector leverage dynamics is essentially curve-fitting noise.

**→ Credit Cycles:** This is the primary causal driver. Risk appetite regimes *are* credit cycles viewed from the asset-pricing side. The credit cycle analysis should be the foundation upon which risk appetite regime classification is built, not a separate topic. The BIS credit-to-GDP gap, high-yield spread dynamics, and covenant quality deterioration are the fundamental indicators—risk-on/risk-off in equities and FX are merely their reflection.

**→ Equity Cycles:** Equity valuations during risk-on regimes are supported by two Minskyan mechanisms: (a) the Kalecki profit equation, which shows that private sector deficit spending (investment exceeding saving) generates corporate profits mechanically, and (b) financial asset price appreciation supported by leveraged speculation. When either mechanism exhausts, equity valuations must correct. The commonly cited "equity risk premium" is meaningless without reference to the underlying debt structure supporting corporate cash flows.

**→ FX Regimes:** The global dollar system creates a hierarchical structure where risk-off strengthens the dollar (top of the global money hierarchy) and weakens peripheral currencies. Carry trades (borrowing in low-yield currencies to invest in high-yield ones) are a pure expression of risk-on Minsky dynamics in FX—they work until the reversal, at which point their unwinding amplifies the risk-off cascade. The yen carry trade unwind of August 2024 is a textbook example.

**→ Rates/Term Premium:** Risk appetite regimes profoundly affect the term structure. In risk-on, term premia compress as leveraged investors reach for yield and duration. In risk-off, the flight to safety initially compresses short-end yields (rate cut expectations) while term premia can either collapse (safe haven bid) or spike (if the sovereign itself is the source of fragility, as in the 2022 UK gilt crisis). The distinction between currency issuers and currency users is critical here—risk-off dynamics for U.S. Treasuries are fundamentally different from those for Italian BTPs.

## Open Questions

1. **Has the structure of passive investing and ETF-ification altered the Minsky cycle's timing and amplitude?** Index funds do not perform credit analysis; they simply allocate flows. Does this extend the risk-on phase (flows continue regardless of fundamentals) while making the eventual reversal sharper (no discretionary buyers during the liquidation)?

2. **How should we classify the current (2025–2026) private sector financial position?** The U.S. household sector deleveraged significantly post-GFC but corporate leverage (particularly in leveraged loans and private credit) expanded aggressively. Is the locus of Minsky fragility now primarily in corporate balance sheets and private credit funds rather than household mortgage debt?

3. **Does the global dollar shortage thesis (Pozsar, Zoltan) imply a structural bias toward risk-off outside the U.S.?** If the global financial system requires a continuous supply of dollar-denominated safe assets that the U.S. fiscal trajectory may not sustainably provide, is there a permanent fragility embedded in the system?

4. **What is the Minsky classification of the current private credit boom?** Private credit's opacity (no mark-to-market, illiquid, concentrated) makes it extremely difficult to assess whether we are in hedge, speculative, or Ponzi territory. This is the single largest blind spot in current financial fragility assessment.

5. **Can flow-of-funds data at quarterly frequency capture risk appetite regime shifts that now occur in days or hours?** The Godley framework operates at macroeconomic frequency, but modern market microstructure transmits shocks at millisecond speed. Is there a way to bridge this gap, perhaps through high-frequency proxies for sectoral balance dynamics (daily credit flows, repo market data, dealer positioning)?

6. **What role do fiscal deficits play in sustaining the current risk-on regime?** Large U.S. fiscal deficits are, by sectoral identity, supporting private sector surpluses. If fiscal consolidation occurs, does risk-off become mechanically inevitable absent a swing in the external balance?
