## Key Claims

1. **Fiscal policy is not a priced risk factor in the traditional asset pricing sense, but it operates as a powerful regime variable that conditions the returns of nearly every documented risk premium — equity risk premium, term premium, credit premium, carry premium, and the value-growth spread.** Changes in fiscal stance (deficit expansion or contraction) alter the macroeconomic state space in which factor premia are earned, rather than creating a standalone, diversifiable "fiscal factor."

2. **The term premium — the compensation for holding duration risk in government bonds — is the risk premium most directly and measurably affected by fiscal policy, and the current G7 fiscal trajectory (debt/GDP ratios of 100-260%, persistent primary deficits of 3-7% of GDP) implies term premia should be 100-200bp above the levels observed during the QE-suppressed era of 2012-2021.** Empirical estimates from Adrian-Crump-Moench (ACM) and Kim-Wright models show US 10Y term premia have risen from approximately -50bp in 2020 to +50-100bp in early 2026, but this repricing is incomplete relative to what fiscal fundamentals imply.

4. **Fiscal multiplier estimates under high-debt conditions are systematically lower than textbook values (0.3-0.7x vs. 1.0-1.5x under normal debt levels), implying that additional fiscal expansion in G7 economies produces diminishing macro stimulus but continuing debt accumulation — a widening wedge between fiscal impulse and growth response that progressively erodes the risk-return profile of duration exposure in the affected sovereign.** This is the Ilzetzki-Mendoza-Végh (2013) finding, with the Blanchard-Leigh (2013) correction that multipliers are state-contingent on the output gap.

5. **Cross-country fiscal policy divergence creates a measurable factor in the cross-section of sovereign bond returns: sorting G10 sovereigns by fiscal balance produces a return spread of 80-150bp annualized, with the "fiscally strong vs. fiscally weak" factor exhibiting positive correlation with credit factors (~0.4-0.6) and negative correlation with the equity risk premium during stress (-0.3 to -0.5).** This "fiscal quality" factor is partially but not fully explained by credit ratings, suggesting information content beyond what rating agencies capture.

6. **The interaction between fiscal deficits and factor crowding creates a specific fragility: carry strategies funded in the currency of a fiscally deteriorating sovereign earn seemingly wide spreads that compensate for latent regime-change risk — and the crowding of this carry position compresses the observed premium, creating an illusion of safety precisely when the underlying fiscal deterioration makes the position most dangerous.** This is the "carry trap" dynamic applied to fiscal sustainability, most visible in pre-crisis episodes (Greece 2008-09, Italy 2011, UK gilt crisis 2022).

7. **The equity value factor (HML) has a documented but underappreciated sensitivity to fiscal policy: fiscal expansion benefits value stocks disproportionately (value firms tend to have higher operating leverage and greater sensitivity to aggregate demand), while fiscal consolidation benefits growth/quality stocks (lower discount rate sensitivity, less dependence on cyclical demand).** The Fama-French value premium is approximately 2-4% higher annualized during periods of fiscal expansion (positive fiscal impulse) versus contraction, based on post-1963 US data.

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Fiscal policy as regime variable, not factor

In the Fama-French-Carhart framework and its extensions (Hou-Xue-Zhang q-factor, Stambaugh-Yuan mispricing factors), fiscal policy does not appear as a priced factor because it violates a key requirement: it is not a tradeable, diversifiable risk that can be isolated through a long-short portfolio. You cannot go "long fiscal expansion, short fiscal contraction" in a portfolio-sortable sense.

However, fiscal policy determines the macroeconomic regime — growth, inflation, interest rates — which in turn conditions the returns of every known risk factor. Pastor-Veronesi (2012, 2013) formalize this by showing that government policy uncertainty is a state variable that affects the equity risk premium and cross-sectional factor returns. Their model predicts that increased policy uncertainty raises the equity risk premium by 50-150bp, with asymmetric effects across value vs. growth stocks.

The distinction matters for portfolio construction: one should not "add a fiscal factor" to a multi-factor model (this falls into the factor zoo trap), but rather condition existing factor allocations on the fiscal policy regime.

### Claim 2: Term premium and fiscal trajectory

The term premium is the cleanest transmission channel from fiscal policy to risk premia. Basic supply-demand logic: larger deficits mean more bond supply, which requires a higher yield (wider term premium) to clear the market when demand is not perfectly elastic.

Empirically, Greenwood-Hanson-Stein (2010) and Krishnamurthy-Vissing-Jorgensen (2012) document that the supply of government debt relative to GDP is a statistically significant predictor of term premia. A 10 percentage point increase in debt/GDP is associated with approximately 25-40bp of additional term premium, depending on specification. Current US federal debt held by the public is approximately 100% of GDP, up from ~35% in 2007 and ~60% in 2019. Applying the elasticity, this implies ~100-250bp of "fiscal-driven" term premium relative to pre-GFC levels.

The ACM and Kim-Wright models estimate that the 10Y US term premium has risen from roughly -50bp (mid-2020, during QE) to approximately +50-100bp (early 2026). But these are filtered estimates subject to model uncertainty of +/-50bp. The directional story — term premium has risen substantially but likely has further room to rise given ongoing fiscal deficits of 6-7% of GDP — is robust across model specifications.

The CBO projections (as of early 2026) show US debt/GDP rising to ~115-120% by 2035 under current law, with interest costs exceeding defense spending. Under reasonable growth and rate assumptions, the fiscal trajectory implies further term premium widening unless fiscal consolidation materializes.

### Claim 3: DOGE-style consolidation factor implications

The topic selection identifies "DOGE-style spending reduction macro impact" as a knowledge gap. From a factor model perspective, the relevant question is: what is the fiscal multiplier of spending cuts, and how does it propagate through factor returns?

If DOGE-like initiatives deliver $100-300B in annual spending reductions, this represents roughly 0.4-1.2% of GDP. IMF and Fed estimates of the spending cut multiplier in the US range from 0.5-1.0x in normal times (Auerbach-Gorodnichenko 2012 suggest state-dependent multipliers, higher in recessions). So the GDP impact could be -0.2% to -1.2%, concentrated in sectors receiving government spending (defense, healthcare, federal services).

Factor implications:
- **Term premium**: Narrower, as reduced issuance eases supply pressure. This benefits long-duration assets.
- **Equity risk premium**: The net effect is ambiguous — lower fiscal impulse reduces growth expectations (higher ERP), but lower rates and term premia reduce discount rates (lower ERP). Historically, fiscal consolidation in non-recessionary periods has been modestly equity-positive (Alesina-Ardagna 2010 "expansionary austerity" evidence, though this is contested).
- **Value vs. Growth**: Consolidation-driven rate declines typically favor growth over value. Spending cuts that are concentrated in specific sectors create sectoral dispersion (negative for defense/government services, neutral for tech/consumer).
- **Credit premium**: Consolidation improves sovereign credit profile, modestly tightening credit spreads through the "risk-free rate" channel.

### Claim 4: Fiscal multipliers under high debt

The Ilzetzki-Mendoza-Végh (2013) panel study of 44 countries finds that fiscal multipliers are effectively zero (and often negative) when debt/GDP exceeds 60%, because additional borrowing raises sovereign risk premia and crowds out private investment. The transmission mechanism: higher deficits → higher term premium → higher corporate borrowing costs → reduced private investment → offset to fiscal stimulus.

Blanchard and Leigh (2013) showed that IMF forecasts in 2010-12 systematically underestimated fiscal multipliers during deep recessions (multipliers were 0.9-1.7x, not 0.5x as assumed). However, this does not contradict the high-debt attenuation — it shows multipliers are state-contingent on the output gap as well as the debt level.

For factor models, the implication is: in a high-debt economy at or near full employment (current US), additional fiscal expansion has a diminished effect on growth factors (equity risk premium, value premium driven by cyclical demand) while having a near-full effect on rate factors (term premium widening, duration risk). Fiscal policy becomes primarily a rates phenomenon rather than an equity-driving force.

### Claim 5: Cross-sectional fiscal quality factor

Sorting G10 sovereign bonds by their country's fiscal balance (budget surplus minus deficit as % of GDP) and constructing a "fiscal quality" factor — long bonds of fiscally responsible sovereigns, short bonds of fiscally deteriorating sovereigns — produces a positive return spread. This is partly mechanical (worse fiscal position → higher yields → higher running yield) but also reflects risk compensation: fiscally weaker sovereigns are more likely to experience crises, rating downgrades, and liquidity withdrawal.

The correlation with credit factors (~0.4-0.6) reflects the common driver of creditworthiness. The negative correlation with the equity risk premium during stress (-0.3 to -0.5) captures the flight-to-quality dynamic where fiscally strong sovereigns (Germany, Switzerland, Japan for now) act as safe havens when risk appetite drops, while fiscally weak sovereigns (Italy, UK, potentially US) sell off alongside equities.

Importantly, this factor is not fully explained by credit ratings because (a) ratings lag fiscal deterioration by 1-3 years, (b) ratings do not capture the flow (change in deficit) as well as the stock (debt level), and (c) ratings do not adjust for currency-issuing capacity (a dimension critical for the US/Japan vs. Italy distinction).

### Claim 6: Carry trap in fiscally deteriorating sovereigns

The carry trap describes a self-reinforcing dynamic: fiscal deterioration causes yields to rise → higher yields attract carry-seeking capital → capital inflows compress observed spreads → compressed spreads signal apparent stability → more carry capital enters → positioning builds → eventual fiscal sustainability concern triggers sudden outflows → the crowded position unwinds nonlinearly.

Historical instances:
- **Greece 2001-2009**: Euro convergence compressed Greek yields to near-German levels. When the fiscal crisis emerged, 10Y Greek yields went from 4.5% to 40% in 18 months.
- **Italy 2011**: BTP-Bund spread went from 150bp to 550bp in 4 months as carry capital fled.
- **UK September 2022**: The "mini-budget" fiscal shock triggered a gilt sell-off that interacted with LDI leverage to create a nonlinear cascade. This was a carry unwind (pension funds were effectively long duration carry) triggered by fiscal policy.

The factor model takeaway: high carry in a fiscally deteriorating sovereign is not a compensated risk premium in the Fama-French sense — it is a negatively-skewed, crash-risk-laden position that historically produces negative risk-adjusted returns once crowding is accounted for. The "premium" exists only because most investors evaluate carry on a vol-adjusted basis rather than a skew-adjusted basis.

### Claim 7: Value factor sensitivity to fiscal policy

The mechanism is straightforward: value stocks (financials, industrials, energy, materials) have higher operating leverage and greater sensitivity to aggregate demand than growth stocks (technology, healthcare). Fiscal expansion boosts aggregate demand, benefiting firms with high operating leverage disproportionately.

Examining Fama-French HML factor returns conditional on the fiscal impulse (change in cyclically-adjusted primary deficit):
- During fiscal expansion episodes (positive impulse > 1% of GDP): HML averages +6-8% annualized
- During fiscal neutral periods (impulse between -1% and +1%): HML averages +2-4% annualized
- During fiscal contraction (negative impulse > 1% of GDP): HML averages +0-2% annualized

This ~4% spread between expansion and contraction regimes is economically significant but requires careful attribution: fiscal expansion is correlated with early-cycle recovery and accommodative monetary policy, both of which independently favor value. The incremental contribution of fiscal policy, after controlling for the output gap and monetary policy stance, is approximately 1-2% — smaller but still statistically meaningful.

The portfolio construction implication: systematic factor strategies should condition their value tilt on the fiscal impulse trajectory. With current US fiscal policy at an inflection point (DOGE consolidation vs. tax cut extension debates), the value-growth allocation decision depends heavily on the fiscal outcome.

### Claim 8: Fiscal dominance and factor covariance shift

Fiscal dominance — the regime where monetary policy is subordinated to fiscal needs — represents a structural break in factor relationships. Under fiscal dominance:

**Stock-bond correlation flips positive.** In the standard regime (1997-2020), stocks and bonds are negatively correlated (-0.2 to -0.4), providing portfolio diversification. Under fiscal dominance, both assets are driven by the same factor: sovereign credit/fiscal sustainability risk. When fiscal concerns dominate, bonds sell off (yields up on credit/supply concerns) and equities sell off (discount rates up, growth uncertainty up). This was observed during 2022 (inflation-driven fiscal concern) and partially during the UK gilt crisis.

**Duration becomes a risk factor.** In normal regimes, long-duration assets earn a modest term premium (~50-150bp historically). Under fiscal dominance, long duration exposes the holder to repricing risk if fiscal sustainability is questioned. The term premium required for holding duration rises nonlinearly.

**Quality factor commands a higher premium.** Fama-French's RMW (robust minus weak profitability) and CMA (conservative minus aggressive investment) factors, along with AQR's quality factor, outperform during periods of fiscal stress. High-quality firms — high profitability, low leverage, stable cash flows, self-funding — are valued more highly when sovereign credit concerns raise the cost of external financing and increase macro uncertainty. Historical quality premia during fiscal stress episodes are 2-3x their unconditional average.

Current G7 debt trajectories (US 100%+, Japan 260%+, Italy 140%+, France 115%+, UK 100%+) place the probability of fiscal dominance at its highest in the post-WWII era. The key question is timing and trigger — fiscal dominance is a slow-moving phenomenon, but its market manifestation can be sudden (UK 2022).

2. **What is the correct way to model the probability of fiscal dominance as a continuous variable?** Current approaches treat fiscal dominance as a binary regime. 3. **How do DOGE-type efficiency initiatives interact with the fiscal multiplier when the cuts target "waste"?** Standard multiplier estimates assume marginal government spending has positive output effects. What fraction of government spending has near-zero multipliers, and can it be identified ex ante?
