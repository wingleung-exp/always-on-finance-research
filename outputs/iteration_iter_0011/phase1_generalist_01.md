# Fiscal Policy Divergence & Debt Sustainability — Cross-Asset Strategist Analysis

## Key Claims

1. **Fiscal policy is the single most important cross-asset variable in the current regime.** The US fiscal deficit at 6-7% of GDP simultaneously determines equity earnings trajectories (via Kalecki profit identity), rates volatility (via $2T+ annual issuance), dollar strength (via Mundell-Fleming capital account dynamics), credit spread compression (via profit-sustained coverage ratios), and commodity demand (via fiscal-supported consumption). No other single variable propagates across all five major asset classes with this magnitude.

2. **Cross-asset markets are currently telling an internally inconsistent fiscal story.** Equity markets price continued fiscal generosity (S&P 500 earnings growth 8-10% annualized implying persistent deficit spending), while long-end rates are embedding a fiscal supply premium (30Y term premium ~50-80bp above pre-2022 norms). Credit markets side with equities (HY OAS ~300-350bp, consistent with fiscal profit support), but FX markets are beginning to price fiscal fragility (USD weakness against EUR and gold despite rate differentials favoring dollar). This four-way disagreement — equities/credit bullish vs. rates/FX cautious — is the defining cross-asset tension of Q1-Q2 2026.

3. **G7 fiscal divergence is the widest since post-WWII demobilization and is generating persistent cross-asset relative value opportunities.** The US runs a structural deficit of 6-7% GDP vs. the Euro Area at ~3%, Japan at ~4% (though Japan's government-sector balance is muddied by BOJ monetization), and the UK at ~4-5%. This divergence creates a structural relative value regime: US equities outperform on the Kalecki earnings channel, US Treasuries underperform on fiscal supply, and the dollar faces a fundamental tug-of-war between capital inflows (equity outperformance attracting flows) and fiscal debasement concerns (growing debt/GDP trajectory).

4. **The stock-bond correlation regime is fiscally determined — and we are in a transition zone.** In disinflationary regimes, stock-bond correlation is negative (bonds hedge equity drawdowns). In inflationary regimes, the correlation flips positive (bonds amplify equity losses). The current fiscal regime introduces a *maturity-dependent bifurcation*: the 2Y-SPX correlation is -0.25 to -0.35 (reflecting Fed reaction function dominance at the front end), while the 30Y-SPX correlation is +0.05 to +0.15 (reflecting fiscal supply dynamics at the long end). This bifurcation makes traditional 60/40 portfolio construction unreliable — duration is not a monolithic hedge; it is a maturity-contingent bet on the relative dominance of monetary vs. fiscal policy.

5. **Fiscal consolidation (whether via DOGE spending cuts, TCJA partial expiration, or debt ceiling enforcement) exceeding 2pp of GDP would trigger a correlation regime shift with cascading cross-asset consequences.** The Kalecki channel reversal would hit equities (earnings deceleration), widen credit spreads (profit-funded coverage ratios collapse), flatten the yield curve (lower growth expectations), strengthen the dollar (less twin deficit pressure), and suppress commodity demand. Critically, this would be a *benign* correlation regime from a portfolio perspective — stock-bond correlation would likely turn more negative (growth scare mode), restoring diversification benefits. The worse outcome is fiscal crisis (forced consolidation via market discipline), which would produce positive stock-bond correlation with both falling — the stagflationary regime that destroys diversified portfolios.

6. **The fiscal multiplier under current debt levels (120%+ debt/GDP) is lower than consensus assumes, creating an asymmetric risk profile for cross-asset positioning.** Empirical estimates of fiscal multipliers at high debt/GDP levels (>90%) cluster around 0.4-0.7x, well below the 1.0-1.5x multipliers assumed in CBO scoring and most macro models calibrated to low-debt environments. The implication: fiscal consolidation may be less contractionary than feared (positive for duration, less negative for equities), but continued deficit spending is also less stimulative than priced (the marginal dollar of deficit generates less growth, creating a fiscal treadmill where larger deficits are needed to sustain the same growth rate).

7. **The credit-equity basis is currently pricing fiscal policy inconsistently, creating a specific cross-asset mispricing.** Equity-implied credit spreads (derived from Merton model calibration to equity volatility and leverage) suggest HY spreads should be ~50-75bp wider than observed, while actual credit spreads imply an equity vol surface that is ~2-3 vols too low. This basis exists because equities are pricing the Kalecki profit channel (fiscal support to earnings) while credit is pricing the same channel (fiscal support to coverage ratios) but with different assumptions about fiscal sustainability. When fiscal policy is the dominant driver of both assets, the credit-equity basis becomes a measure of relative fiscal optimism — and right now, credit is marginally more fiscally optimistic than equities.

8. **The interaction between fiscal deficits and the term premium is the most underappreciated cross-asset transmission channel.** Every $100B of incremental Treasury supply is associated with approximately 5-8bp of term premium expansion at the long end (estimates from ACM decomposition and Kim-Wright models). At $2T+ annual issuance, the fiscal supply channel is adding 100-160bp of term premium to 10Y+ yields relative to a balanced-budget counterfactual. This term premium increase mechanically reduces equity multiples through the discount rate channel — a 100bp rise in the risk-free discount rate implies roughly a 1.5-2x P/E compression for the S&P 500 at current earnings duration. The market is currently resolving this tension by assuming a terminal fiscal equilibrium that allows both high multiples and high term premium — a scenario that is internally inconsistent over a 3-5 year horizon.

9. **Gold and Bitcoin are functioning as fiscal policy barometers, and their persistent strength signals that real money is hedging a fiscal tail risk that rates and credit markets are not yet pricing.** Gold at $3,000+/oz and Bitcoin above $80K despite positive real rates represents a structural allocation toward fiscal debasement hedges. Traditional cross-asset models (gold inversely correlated with real rates) broke down in 2023-2025, and the residual — the component of gold price unexplained by real rates and the dollar — is best explained by sovereign fiscal sustainability concerns, particularly central bank reserve diversification away from Treasuries (global central bank gold purchases running at 1,000+ tonnes/year since 2022).

10. **The optimal cross-asset expression of the fiscal policy view is not a directional bet but a relative value position that benefits from the resolution of the current cross-asset inconsistency, regardless of direction.** The trade structure should be: long assets that benefit from fiscal clarity (vol sellers in specific corridors), short assets that are mispricing the asymmetry (long-end duration at insufficient term premium), and overweight assets that benefit under both fiscal paths (gold, select EM assets with fiscal discipline, short-duration credit).

## Evidence & Reasoning

### Claim 1: Fiscal Policy as Dominant Cross-Asset Variable

The Kalecki-Levy profit identity (Profits = Investment + Government Deficit + Net Exports − Household Saving + Dividends) is an accounting identity — it is mathematically certain, not a behavioral hypothesis. At 6-7% of GDP (~$1.8-2.0T), the US fiscal deficit is the largest single component sustaining aggregate corporate profits. Stripping fiscal demand contribution, private-sector growth is only 1.0-1.5% — below stall speed for most business cycle models.

The cross-asset propagation channels are:
- **Equities:** Fiscal → profits → EPS growth (8-10% annualized through 2024-2025 despite restrictive monetary policy)
- **Credit:** Fiscal → profits → interest coverage → spread compression (HY default rates <2% vs. cycle-average 3.5%)
- **Rates:** Fiscal → $2T+ annual issuance → term premium expansion → MOVE index elevation
- **FX:** Fiscal → twin deficits → Mundell-Fleming strong dollar prediction (confirmed but now weakening)
- **Commodities:** Fiscal → demand support → prevents commodity demand destruction → inflationary passthrough amplification

No other single macro variable — not Fed policy, not China growth, not AI productivity — simultaneously drives returns in all five major asset classes. This makes fiscal trajectory the primary monitoring variable for cross-asset allocation.

### Claim 2: Cross-Asset Inconsistency

The current cross-asset configuration contains a specific inconsistency:

| Asset Class | Fiscal Implied | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| S&P 500 (5,500+) | Deficit persists at 6-7% | Bullish fiscal |
| HY OAS (~300-350bp) | Profit support continues | Bullish fiscal |
| 30Y UST yield (4.5%+) | Fiscal supply premium embedded | Cautious on fiscal sustainability |
| USD (DXY weakening vs. EUR, gold) | Twin deficit concern emerging | Cautious on fiscal path |
| Gold ($3,000+/oz) | Debasement/reserve diversification | Bearish fiscal sustainability |

Equities and credit are pricing fiscal continuation; rates, FX, and gold are pricing fiscal unsustainability. One side must be wrong. Historical precedent from the 2010-2011 European sovereign debt episode suggests that rates and FX markets lead in pricing fiscal regime shifts, with equities and credit lagging by 2-4 quarters. If this pattern holds, the equity/credit optimism is the mispricing.

However, there is a crucial difference: the US issues the world's reserve currency and the Fed retains credibility to backstop dysfunction, creating a much higher threshold for fiscal market discipline than for peripheral European sovereigns. The US fiscal trajectory is unsustainable in an arithmetic sense (debt/GDP rising indefinitely at current policy) but not in a near-term crisis sense (the dollar's reserve status creates a self-reinforcing demand for Treasuries).

### Claim 3: G7 Fiscal Divergence

Current fiscal balances (2025-2026 estimates):
- **US:** -6.5% to -7.0% of GDP (structural deficit ~5.5%)
- **Euro Area:** -3.0% to -3.5% (with Germany at ~-2% and France/Italy at ~-4.5%)
- **Japan:** -4.0% to -4.5% (net of BOJ-held debt, effective deficit is lower)
- **UK:** -4.5% to -5.0%
- **Canada:** -1.5% to -2.0%

The US stands out: its deficit is 2-3x the G7 average, and unlike previous episodes of high deficits (2009-2010, 2020-2021), there is no recession or pandemic justifying the spending. The US is running a wartime deficit in a peacetime economy.

Cross-asset implications of G7 divergence:
- **Equity relative value:** US equities benefit more from fiscal support (Kalecki channel is ~4x larger per unit of GDP than European equivalents) → overweight US vs. Europe on pure fiscal basis
- **Rates relative value:** UST term premium exceeds Bund term premium by the widest margin since the Euro's creation → UST underperformance relative to Bunds should persist as long as fiscal divergence continues
- **FX:** Mundell-Fleming predicts USD strength (capital inflows chasing US growth/yield), but the twin deficit channel predicts USD weakness (current account + fiscal deficit) → the dollar is caught between these competing forces, explaining range-bound trading with elevated vol

### Claim 4: Maturity-Dependent Correlation Bifurcation

This builds on a concept established in earlier iterations (fiscal_monetary_dissonance, confidence 6.5). The standard stock-bond correlation framework has three regimes:
1. **Disinflationary risk-off:** Stocks fall, bonds rally → negative correlation → diversification works
2. **Inflationary risk-off:** Stocks fall, bonds fall → positive correlation → diversification fails
3. **Risk-on:** Stocks rally, bonds stable/fall → weak positive correlation

The fiscal regime introduces a fourth dimension: maturity-dependent splitting. At the front end (2Y), the Fed's reaction function dominates — if equities sell off, the market prices rate cuts, creating negative correlation. At the long end (30Y), fiscal supply dominates — even during equity selloffs, long bonds may fail to rally because term premium (reflecting fiscal sustainability concerns) overwhelms the flight-to-quality bid.

Observable evidence:
- 2Y UST-SPX 60-day rolling correlation: -0.25 to -0.35 (standard risk-off)
- 30Y UST-SPX 60-day rolling correlation: +0.05 to +0.15 (fiscal regime)

Portfolio implications are severe: a 60/40 portfolio using aggregate bond indices (which blend front-end and long-end duration) appears diversified but is actually running a maturity basis risk. In a fiscal-supply-driven selloff, the long-end component of the bond allocation amplifies rather than hedges the equity drawdown.

### Claim 5: Fiscal Consolidation Scenarios and Correlation Regime Shift

Three fiscal consolidation pathways are plausible over a 12-18 month horizon:

**Pathway A — DOGE-driven spending cuts (15-25% probability):**
Discretionary spending cuts of $200-500B/year would represent 0.7-1.8pp of GDP fiscal consolidation. If concentrated in transfer payments rather than government investment, the Kalecki channel impact is ~0.5-0.7x the headline figure (transfers have lower profit pass-through than government investment). Cross-asset impact: S&P 500 earnings decelerate to 3-5% growth, HY spreads widen 50-100bp, UST bull flattening (front end follows growth deceleration, long end rallies on lower supply), USD strengthens (lower twin deficit).

**Pathway B — TCJA partial expiration / debt ceiling binding (20-30% probability):**
Failure to extend TCJA provisions would raise effective tax rates by 1.5-2.0pp of GDP. Unlike spending cuts, tax increases directly hit the after-tax profit line in the Kalecki identity while leaving pre-tax demand intact. This is worse for equity markets (direct earnings hit) but less disruptive for the credit cycle (aggregate demand support partially preserved). Cross-asset impact: S&P 500 -10-15% re-rating, credit spreads widen modestly (30-50bp HY), rates rally (growth fears), USD mixed.

**Pathway C — Market-imposed fiscal discipline / fiscal crisis (5-10% probability but catastrophic):**
A failed Treasury auction, rating downgrade (US already lost AAA from S&P and Fitch), or foreign buyer strike forces abrupt fiscal adjustment. This is the tail risk scenario that produces positive stock-bond correlation with both falling — equities crash on growth fears while bonds sell off on credit/supply concerns. Gold spikes. VIX and MOVE both explode. This is the scenario that gold/$3K and central bank reserve diversification are hedging.

**Status quo continuation (40-50% probability):**
Deficit persists at 6-7% via continuing resolutions and legislative gridlock. Kalecki channel sustains earnings. MOVE remains elevated on supply uncertainty. The cross-asset inconsistency persists but does not resolve — this is the "muddle through" scenario that favors carry and relative value.

### Claim 6: Fiscal Multipliers at High Debt Levels

The fiscal multiplier — the GDP impact per dollar of deficit spending — is a function of the debt/GDP starting point, the monetary policy stance, and the composition of spending:

| Study/Source | Debt/GDP Threshold | Estimated Multiplier |
|---|---|---|
| Ilzetzki, Mendoza, Végh (2013) | >60% | 0.5x (avg), negative at >90% |
| Huidrom et al. (2020, IMF) | >70% | 0.4-0.6x in expansions |
| Auerbach & Gorodnichenko (2012) | Regime-dependent | 0.0-0.5x in expansions, 1.0-1.5x in recessions |
| Ramey (2019 survey) | Various | 0.6-1.0x central estimate |
| CBO (2024 scoring) | N/A (assumes ~1.0x) | 0.8-1.2x depending on type |

At 120%+ debt/GDP, the US is operating in the range where fiscal multipliers are demonstrably lower than unity. The implication is asymmetric:
- **Continued spending** is less stimulative than priced (equities overpricing the Kalecki channel on the margin)
- **Fiscal consolidation** is less contractionary than feared (the multiplier works in both directions — withdrawal of a low-multiplier dollar costs less growth than assumed)

This creates a specific cross-asset setup: the market is overpricing both the benefit of continued spending (equities too high) and the cost of consolidation (too much risk premium in rates). A convergence trade — long rates / short equity overweight — exploits this asymmetry.

### Claim 7: Credit-Equity Basis Mispricing

The credit-equity basis measures the gap between equity-implied credit spreads (from Merton structural models) and actual credit spreads. Currently:

- **Equity-implied HY spread:** ~375-425bp (derived from equity vol, leverage, and asset value)
- **Actual HY OAS:** ~300-350bp
- **Basis:** Actual spreads ~50-75bp tighter than equity-implied

This basis persists because:
1. Credit markets have more structural demand (insurance companies, pension funds with regulatory mandates to hold bonds)
2. The CLO machine compresses spreads through structured demand
3. Credit investors are pricing the Kalecki fiscal floor more aggressively than equity investors

From a fiscal policy perspective, the basis reveals that credit is more confident in fiscal continuation than equities. If fiscal consolidation materializes, credit spreads should widen more than equities sell off (closing the basis from the credit side). If the fiscal status quo persists, equity vol should compress (closing the basis from the equity side).

The trade: if you are bearish on fiscal sustainability, express it through credit (wider spreads) rather than equity (selling stocks), because credit has more to reprice.

### Claim 8: Term Premium and Fiscal Supply

The term premium — the compensation investors demand for bearing duration risk beyond expected rate path — has become predominantly a fiscal variable:

- **ACM model estimate (10Y term premium):** ~60-100bp (as of Q1 2026)
- **Pre-2022 average (2015-2021):** ~0 to -20bp
- **Fiscal supply contribution (estimated):** ~100-160bp at current issuance pace

The channel: $2T+ annual net issuance → price-inelastic supply → term premium expansion → higher discount rates → equity multiple compression. Each 25bp of term premium expansion reduces the "fair value" S&P 500 P/E by ~0.3-0.5x (at current earnings duration of ~18-20 years for the index).

But the market is currently running two contradictory discounting frameworks:
- **Equity DCF models** use a terminal risk-free rate of ~3.5-4.0% (assuming fiscal normalization)
- **Treasury markets** price a terminal 10Y of ~4.5%+ (embedding fiscal supply persistence)

If Treasury markets are right, equity valuations need to adjust downward by roughly 8-12% to reconcile. If equity markets are right (fiscal normalization occurs), term premium should compress 80-120bp — a massive duration rally.

### Claim 9: Gold and Bitcoin as Fiscal Barometers

Gold's relationship with real rates broke down beginning in 2023:
- **Traditional model:** Gold = f(real rates⁻, USD⁻, inflation expectations⁺) → R² historically ~0.7-0.8
- **Post-2023 model:** R² has dropped to ~0.3-0.4 using traditional factors
- **Residual (unexplained component):** ~$400-600/oz at current prices

The residual is best explained by:
1. **Central bank purchases:** 1,000+ tonnes/year since 2022 (primarily China, India, Turkey, Poland) — motivated by de-dollarization and US fiscal concerns
2. **Sovereign wealth fund allocation shifts:** Away from UST toward gold and alternative reserves
3. **Retail/institutional "fiscal insurance" demand:** Gold ETF inflows reaccelerating after 2024

Bitcoin's correlation with gold has increased from near-zero (2020) to ~0.3-0.4 (2025-2026), suggesting both are functioning as fiscal debasement hedges. This is a cross-asset signal that should not be dismissed: real-money allocators (central banks, sovereigns, institutional investors) are paying a carry cost to hold zero-yielding assets when real rates are positive — behavior that only makes sense if they assign material probability to fiscal regime deterioration.

### Claim 10: Optimal Cross-Asset Expression

The core insight from the cross-asset consistency check is that fiscal policy resolution — in either direction — reduces cross-asset dispersion. The current wide disagreement between asset classes is itself a source of relative value. The optimal positioning framework:

**Instruments that benefit from resolution regardless of direction:**
- Long volatility on assets with compressed vol (VIX puts, credit index options)
- Long gold (benefits from fiscal crisis; retains value under orderly consolidation)
- Short-duration credit (minimal duration risk, benefits from Kalecki channel while fiscal persists)

**Instruments that benefit from fiscal continuation (status quo):**
- Overweight US equities vs. European equities (Kalecki channel differential)
- Carry trades with fiscal-supported demand floors (EM local currency where host country runs surpluses)
- Short long-end UST vs. long front-end UST (curve steepening if supply persists)

**Instruments that benefit from fiscal consolidation:**
- Long duration (30Y UST) — term premium compression
- Long USD — twin deficit reduction
- Short HY credit — spread widening as Kalecki floor weakens

## Connections to Other Topics

### Sovereign Debt (confidence 5.0, depth 2)
The fiscal deficit analysis directly feeds sovereign debt sustainability. The key connection: at 120%+ debt/GDP and 6-7% primary deficit, the US requires nominal GDP growth to exceed the effective interest rate on outstanding debt (r < g) for debt/GDP to stabilize. Currently r ≈ 3.0-3.5% (weighted average cost of debt) and g ≈ 5.0-5.5% (nominal GDP), so r < g holds — but the gap is narrowing as low-coupon pandemic-era issuance rolls off into higher-coupon replacements. The maturity wall for US sovereign debt (when the average interest cost exceeds nominal GDP growth) is the single most important long-term cross-asset risk. *The fiscal analysis here provides the demand-side (Kalecki) framework; sovereign debt sustainability provides the supply-side (debt dynamics) framework.*

### Fiscal Dominance (confidence 5.0, depth 2)
Fiscal dominance occurs when the fiscal authority's financing needs constrain monetary policy — the central bank loses the ability to set rates independently because tightening would bankrupt the sovereign. The US is not in fiscal dominance (the Fed can still hike without triggering a fiscal crisis), but the corridor is narrowing. The cross-asset signature of approaching fiscal dominance: rates vol rises, currency weakens, inflation expectations de-anchor upward, and the term premium embeds a "fiscal put" discount (the market prices in the expectation that the Fed will eventually be forced to accommodate fiscal needs). *This topic is the logical next-depth exploration from the fiscal analysis.*

### Inflation Regimes (confidence 6.0, depth 3)
Fiscal policy determines which inflation regime we inhabit. Persistent fiscal deficits make the inflation regime stickier (fiscal demand prevents the demand destruction that normally resolves supply-driven inflation), raise the neutral rate (more fiscal stimulus requires higher rates to achieve the same restrictiveness), and increase the probability of regime transitions (fiscal exhaustion could trigger a deflationary shock; fiscal expansion could trigger a supply-driven inflation spike via commodity passthrough amplification, as established in KB concept fiscal_deficit_passthrough_amplification at confidence 7). *The stock-bond correlation analysis in Claim 4 is a direct manifestation of the inflation regime connection.*

### Growth Models (confidence 7.4, depth 1)
The Kalecki-Levy framework established in the KB (confidence 9) is the primary analytical bridge between fiscal policy and growth models. The key tension: standard growth models (Solow, NK DSGE) treat fiscal policy as a cyclical stabilizer, while the Kalecki framework reveals that fiscal policy has become a *structural* driver of profits and growth in the current cycle. This distinction matters for cross-asset positioning because cyclical stabilizers mean-revert (so positioning should fade fiscal impact) while structural drivers persist (so positioning should lean into fiscal direction). *The 7.4 confidence on growth models reflects substantial prior work; the fiscal analysis here reinforces the Kalecki channel as the dominant framework.*

### Volatility Regimes (recently analyzed in iter_0010)
The fiscal-vol nexus is one of the most actionable cross-asset connections. As established in the KB relationship cross_asset_vol_divergence ↔ kalecki_fiscal_channel (confidence 8): fiscal spending simultaneously suppresses VIX (via earnings support) and elevates MOVE (via supply uncertainty). This MOVE-VIX divergence is itself a fiscal policy signal. Monitoring: if VIX rises to meet MOVE (equity vol catches up to rates vol), the market is pricing fiscal withdrawal. If MOVE falls to meet VIX (rates vol normalizes), the market is pricing fiscal normalization without disruption. *The vol regime analysis from iter_0010 provides the volatility framework; the fiscal analysis here provides the fundamental driver.*

### FX-Rates Divergence (iter_0009)
The fiscal_monetary_dissonance concept (confidence 6.5) identified the maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation that Claim 4 of this analysis builds on. The FX channel adds another dimension: fiscal divergence between the US and G7 creates persistent carry opportunities (USD-funded carry into EM where fiscal discipline is better) but also tail risk (a US fiscal crisis would cause a violent carry unwind as USD funding costs spike). *The FX analysis provides the currency dimension; the fiscal analysis here provides the fundamental fiscal driver of FX dynamics.*

### Credit-Equity Linkage (explored in iter_0004, iter_0010)
The credit-equity basis (Claim 7) is a direct expression of the credit-equity linkage topic. The Kalecki fiscal channel simultaneously supports both credit (via coverage ratios) and equities (via earnings growth), but with different sensitivities. The established KB concept kalecki_fiscal_channel (confidence 8) identifies that the Kalecki floor is "sufficient for IG/upper HY but insufficient for lower HY/leveraged loans where firm-level interest expense overwhelms aggregate demand support." This heterogeneity within credit is critical for cross-asset positioning: the fiscal bet should be expressed through lower-quality credit (most sensitive to fiscal withdrawal) rather than IG (which has alternative support mechanisms).

### Commodity-Inflation Transmission (iter_0005)
The KB concept fiscal_deficit_passthrough_amplification (confidence 7) establishes that fiscal deficits amplify commodity-to-inflation passthrough by preventing demand destruction. From a cross-asset perspective, this means: commodity price spikes in a high-deficit regime are more inflationary, more persistent, and more damaging to the stock-bond correlation (pushing it more positive). If a commodity supply shock occurs while the US deficit is at 6-7%, the inflation transmission will be 2-3x what standard models predict — a critical scenario for portfolio construction.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the precise fiscal consolidation threshold that triggers the Kalecki channel reversal?** The 2pp of GDP heuristic is widely cited in the KB (confidence 9) but is a rough estimate. Does the composition of consolidation matter? (Transfer cuts vs. government investment cuts vs. tax increases have different Kalecki multipliers.) Does the speed of consolidation matter? (Gradual vs. sudden — analogous to the distinction between orderly and disorderly deleveraging in credit markets.)

2. **What is the political probability and likely magnitude of DOGE-style spending cuts?** This is the key near-term catalyst for fiscal regime change. The announcement effect on markets has been limited so far, but actual implementation could be much larger. Need to assess: (a) legal constraints on executive spending authority, (b) Congressional appetite for spending cuts vs. tax cuts, (c) timeline for any material fiscal impact to flow through to GDP.

3. **How quickly does the Kalecki channel transmit fiscal changes to corporate profits and then to asset prices?** The 4-6 quarter lag estimate in the KB is a heuristic. Cross-asset markets may front-run the actual fiscal impact — equities and credit could reprice well before the fiscal consolidation hits the profit line. What is the anticipatory pricing lead time? Is it different for equities vs. credit vs. rates?

4. **Is the maturity-dependent correlation bifurcation stable or transitional?** If it persists, it fundamentally alters optimal portfolio construction (favor front-end duration over long-end). If it's transitional (resolving toward either fully negative or fully positive stock-bond correlation), the current portfolio implications are temporary.

5. **What is the true fiscal multiplier at 120%+ debt/GDP in an economy with the reserve currency?** The academic estimates (0.4-0.7x) are primarily from non-reserve-currency countries. The US may be different because: (a) the dollar's reserve status creates captive demand for Treasuries, (b) Fed backstop prevents liquidity crises, (c) the US can fund deficits at lower rates than its debt/GDP would normally imply. Does the reserve currency status raise the effective multiplier (because the crowding-out channel is weaker)?

6. **At what debt/GDP level does the US lose its fiscal exceptionalism?** There is some threshold beyond which even the reserve currency issuer faces fiscal market discipline (higher rates, weaker currency, tighter financial conditions). Is it 150%? 180%? Japan at 250%+ suggests the threshold is very high for a country with a captive domestic savings pool — but the US does not have Japan's current account surplus or domestic savings rate. What are the cross-asset signals that would indicate the threshold is approaching?

7. **How should cross-asset portfolios be constructed when the primary risk factor (fiscal policy) is binary and politically determined?** Traditional portfolio optimization assumes continuous, mean-reverting risk factors. Fiscal policy outcomes are discrete (legislation passes or doesn't), binary (consolidation or continuation), and politically determined (not market-driven). This requires a fundamentally different portfolio construction approach — more like options-based contingent claim analysis than mean-variance optimization. What is the right framework?

8. **Does the G7 fiscal divergence create a self-reinforcing dynamic?** US fiscal largesse → US growth outperformance → capital inflows to US → lower US funding costs → easier to run larger deficits → more fiscal largesse. Is this a positive feedback loop that sustains itself until a discontinuous break? Or does the twin deficit channel (fiscal deficit → current account deficit → currency weakness → imported inflation → tighter monetary policy) provide a natural self-correcting mechanism?

9. **How should we weight the gold/Bitcoin signal?** Central bank gold purchases are a strong signal of fiscal concern among sovereign allocators. But central banks may be wrong (they have been wrong about reserve allocation before — e.g., Euro reserves in 2010). 10. **What is the interaction between AI-driven productivity growth and fiscal sustainability?** If AI delivers the 1-2pp productivity growth acceleration that optimists project, it would raise potential GDP growth, increase tax revenues without rate increases, and potentially restore the r < g condition even at higher interest rates.
